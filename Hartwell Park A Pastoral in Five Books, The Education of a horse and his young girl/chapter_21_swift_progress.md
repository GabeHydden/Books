# Chapter 21: Swift Progress
**Book III: Autumn — Partnership**
**POV: Sage**
**Early October 1813**

October arrived with golden light and the smell of wood smoke drifting from distant farmhouses. The harvest was in full swing across the county—Sage could see wagons loaded with grain moving along the roads when she rode out with Riley to check fences or deliver messages to neighboring farms. Hartwell Park's own small harvest had been gathered, the root cellar stocked, the hay barn full to bursting.

And Promise was leading.

Not just tolerating being led, but actually walking beside Rebecca with something approaching confidence. Sage stood at the paddock fence watching this morning's session, her heart full to aching with pride.

"Walk on," Rebecca said, and Promise stepped forward, the lead rope—a proper short lead now, not the longe line—hanging in a gentle curve between them. His head was level, ears relaxed, stride easy. Nothing like the terrified horse who'd knocked Sage down two weeks ago in his desperate rush for safety.

Two weeks. That was all it had taken.

After that first disastrous attempt, they'd gone back to basics. More longe line work in the round pen. Then letting Promise walk himself the last few steps to his paddock while Sage held the line loosely from behind. Then walking beside him with the longe line, no pressure, just presence. Then—just three days ago—the switch to a proper lead rope, short enough that they walked close together.

And now this. Promise following Rebecca's subtle cues, stopping when she stopped, walking when she walked, turning when she turned. Responsive. Willing. Choosing partnership.

"He's ready for more," Rebecca said when she brought him back to the fence where Sage waited. "I think we can start introducing the bit this week."

The bit. Another milestone. Another step toward the day when Sage might actually sit on his back.

"Already?" The word came out before Sage could stop it, and she heard the eagerness in her own voice.

Rebecca's expression flickered—something Sage couldn't quite read. "His foundation is solid. He trusts the halter, trusts being led. The bit is the logical next step."

"I didn't mean—" Sage started, then stopped. She had meant it. Had meant *already* in the sense of *wonderful, so soon, faster than I hoped*. "I'm just excited. He's doing so well."

"He is." Rebecca handed Promise's lead rope to Sage. "Why don't you take him back to his paddock? Practice what he's learned."

Sage's fingers closed around the rope, feeling the weight of it, the connection to Promise's halter. He stood quiet beside her, ears pricked toward his paddock but no tension in his body. Waiting to see what she'd ask.

"Walk on," she said softly.

He walked. Beside her, matching her pace, the rope staying loose between them. Ten steps. Twenty. All the way to his paddock gate without a single surge forward, a single moment of panic.

When she unclipped the lead and let him go, he didn't bolt for the far corner. Just walked calmly to his water trough, drank, then came back to the fence where she stood. Pushed his nose against her shoulder in that gesture that had become his signature—*I see you, you see me, we're in this together.*

"You're remarkable," she told him, rubbing the spot behind his ears that made him lean into her hand. "Do you know that? Six months ago you wouldn't let anyone within ten feet of you. Now look at you."

He didn't understand the words, of course. But he understood the tone, the touch, the warmth of her pride washing over him through all the ways bodies spoke to each other.

---

The days that followed blurred together in a golden haze of progress and autumn beauty.

Promise accepted the bit on his third session—mouthing it uncertainly at first, working his jaw around the strange metal taste, but not fighting, not panicking. By the end of the week he was wearing it for whole sessions, learning that the slight pressure on the corners of his mouth meant the same things the halter pressure had meant. Turn. Stop. Pay attention.

Rebecca started long-lining him—driving from behind with two long reins attached to the bit, teaching him the feel of rein aids that would someday come from a rider's hands. Promise picked it up quickly, his intelligence evident in how fast he connected the new sensations to actions he already knew.

"He wants to learn," Rebecca observed one afternoon as they watched Promise circle the round pen at a steady trot, responding to her voice commands relayed through the long lines. "Some horses just tolerate training. Promise is actively trying to understand."

"He always was smart," Sage said. "Even when he was terrified. You could see him thinking."

"The smart ones are often the most damaged by bad handling. They understand enough to know they're being betrayed."

Sage thought about that as she worked with the younger horses that afternoon. Marcus was coming along nicely—trotting confidently under saddle now, even attempting a few canter strides. Belle, the nervous filly, had finally stopped shying at the mounting block. Oliver had developed a surprisingly pleasant working trot once Sage figured out he needed more leg and less hand.

Everything was progressing. Everything was going well.

She mentioned as much to Riley that evening as they groomed their horses after a long ride checking the far boundaries.

"Promise is going to be ready for backing before winter," she said, unable to keep the excitement from her voice. "At this rate, anyway. Rebecca says his long-lining is as good as horses who've been in training for months."

Riley didn't respond immediately, focused on picking a stone from Clover's hoof. When they looked up, their expression was thoughtful. "That's fast."

"I know! It's amazing. All that foundation work is paying off."

"Mm." Riley set down the hoof pick and straightened. "How many sessions is he doing now? Per day, I mean."

Sage had to think about it. "Morning session with Rebecca, usually. Then I work with him in the afternoon—just leading, grooming, getting him comfortable with more handling. And sometimes Rebecca does a short evening session if there's time."

"Three sessions a day."

"Not always three. Sometimes just two." Sage heard something in Riley's tone and paused. "Is that too much?"

"I don't know. That's Rebecca's call." Riley began brushing Clover's shoulder with long, even strokes. "I just remember when you first came here, Promise was getting maybe one session every other day. Short ones. Five, ten minutes at most."

"That was different. He couldn't handle more then."

"Right. And now he can. That's good." Riley was quiet for a moment. "I just wonder if sometimes we get so caught up in how much a horse can handle that we forget to ask how much they should be handling."

Sage felt a flicker of defensiveness rise in her chest and pushed it down. Riley wasn't criticizing. Just... observing. The way they always observed, quiet and thoughtful.

"I'll mention it to Rebecca," she said.

But somehow, in the rush of the next few days, she didn't. There was always something else to focus on—Marcus's first canter transition, Belle finally letting Sage put the saddle pad on her back, a merchant visiting to buy one of the young horses who'd completed his training. And Promise, always Promise, making progress that seemed almost miraculous.

He was wearing a surcingle now—the broad band around his middle that would accustom him to girth pressure before the saddle came. He barely reacted when Rebecca tightened it, just shifted his weight slightly and continued his long-lining as if nothing had changed.

"I've never seen a horse with his history adapt this quickly," Rebecca said one evening as they turned out the barn lamps. "It's like he was waiting for permission to trust."

"Maybe he was," Sage said. "Maybe all those months of watching, learning—maybe he was just building up the courage."

"Maybe." Rebecca's voice was warm with satisfaction. "He's going to be extraordinary, Sage. When he's ready for a rider, he's going to be something special."

*When he's ready.* Not *if*. That was new. That was progress in itself.

Sage walked back to the house that night with her heart singing. The bruise on her hip from where Promise had knocked her down was long faded. Rebecca's warning—*enthusiasm can make us careless*—felt like something from another time, another situation. They weren't being careless now. They were being thorough. Systematic. Building on success after success.

Everything was going so well.

---

The saddle changed things.

Not the surcingle—Promise had accepted that weeks ago. But the saddle itself, with its weight and its shape and the stirrups that hung against his sides, was different. Sage lowered it onto his back with both hands, moving slowly, letting him see the thing and smell it before it touched him. Rebecca stood at his head, one hand on his halter, watching.

Promise went rigid the moment the saddle settled. Every muscle locked, nostrils flaring. The round pen went very quiet.

"Easy," Sage murmured. She kept one hand on the saddle, the other flat against his shoulder. "That's all. Just weight. Nothing else."

She let it sit there—didn't tighten the girth, didn't touch the stirrups, just let him feel the shape and heft of it on his back while she stood at his side, her hand steady against him. After a full minute his breathing steadied. After two, his head lowered half an inch. After three, he shifted his weight and blew out through his nose.

"Good," Rebecca said from his head. "That's enough for today. Take it off."

Sage lifted the saddle away. Promise shook himself once, all over, the way horses did after something strange had happened, and then turned to nose at Sage's pockets as though nothing remarkable had occurred.

Three days later she was settling the saddle on him with the girth loosely fastened, walking him in circles around the round pen. He kept turning his head to look at the thing on his back—curious more than afraid—and Sage talked to him the whole time, her voice low and steady while Rebecca watched from the center and called adjustments.

Five days after that first saddling, the girth was properly tightened and Promise was trotting with the saddle as though he'd always worn one. The stirrups bumped his sides and he ignored them. The leather creaked and he ignored that too. Sage long-lined him through the paces while Rebecca observed, and his rhythm never faltered.

"He trusts you with it," Rebecca said after the session, while Sage untacked him in the round pen and he stood drowsing in the October sun. "He lets you put it on because it comes from your hands."

Sage rubbed behind his ears, and he leaned into her palm.

---

"He's ready to be backed," Rebecca announced at breakfast on a bright Thursday morning. She said it the way she said most things—flatly, as a conclusion she'd already examined from every angle.

Sage's teacup stopped halfway to her mouth. Jennifer looked up from her medical journal.

"Backed," Sage repeated. "You mean—someone riding him?"

"I mean weight across his back. Leaning over him first, then sitting. The next logical step." Rebecca spread jam on her toast with precise, even strokes. "He's accepted the saddle, the girth, the stirrups. He long-lines beautifully. His trust in handling is solid. There's no reason to wait unless we're waiting out of sentiment rather than judgment."

"That's wonderful." Sage heard the brightness in her own voice and didn't try to contain it. "When?"

"Saturday. Which gives us two more days of saddle work to confirm he's settled." Rebecca set down her knife. "The question is who rides him first."

The words landed in the quiet kitchen. Jennifer's gaze moved between Rebecca and Sage.

"Riley," Rebecca said. "Riley has the most experience backing young horses. The deepest seat. The best instinct for when to sit still and when to bail. If something goes wrong, Riley is most likely to stay safe and keep the horse safe too."

Sage nodded immediately. "Of course. Riley's brilliant with young horses. And Promise knows Riley—they've worked together all autumn."

"Good." Rebecca returned to her toast. "I'll hold the lead rope. You'll be in the round pen too, where Promise can see you. Your presence will help him stay calm."

"He's going to be fine," Sage said. "He's ready. You can see it in how he carries himself now—confident, settled. He trusts us."

Rebecca made a small sound—not quite agreement, not quite dissent. "I'll speak with Riley this morning."

---

Sage found Riley in the tack room that afternoon, cleaning bridles with the methodical care they brought to everything. She'd been bursting to talk about it all morning but had waited, wanting to hear Rebecca's version of the conversation first.

"Rebecca told you about Saturday?" she asked.

"She told me." Riley didn't look up from the bridle in their hands, working saddle soap into the leather with small circles.

"Are you excited? I'm excited. I know I shouldn't be—I should be measured and cautious and all the things Rebecca would want. But Riley, he's going to be *backed*. After everything he's been through. After everything we've—"

"Sage." Riley's voice was quiet enough to stop her. They set down the bridle and looked at her. "He doesn't know me."

"Of course he does. You've worked with him for months. He's comfortable with you."

"He's comfortable with me at a distance. In the round pen, on the other end of a longe line, as part of the scenery while you do the real work." Riley wiped their hands on a cloth, taking their time. "He's never had my weight on him. He's never had anyone's weight on him except the people who hurt him. And now the first person to sit on his back since Thornton's yard is going to be someone who isn't you."

The words settled between them like something with weight.

"Rebecca knows what she's doing," Sage said, though something in Riley's tone had put a hairline crack in her certainty.

"Rebecca knows horses better than anyone alive. I believe that." Riley picked up the bridle again. "I just wonder if Promise would tell us the same thing she's telling us. If we asked him—not with our logic, but with his—whether he'd choose me to be the first one up there."

"Who would he choose?"

Riley looked at her steadily. "You know who."

Sage did know. Had known it in the wordless way you knew things about someone you loved—that Promise's trust was built on her voice, her hands, her smell, her patient hours at his fence. That what he'd learned about humans being safe, he'd learned from her specifically.

But Rebecca had decades of experience. Rebecca had backed hundreds of horses. And Rebecca said Riley.

"You'll be brilliant," Sage said firmly. "You always are with young horses. And I'll be right there. He'll see me and he'll know it's all right."

Riley nodded slowly, returning to the bridle. "I'm sure you're right."

But their hands, usually so steady, pressed too hard on the next stroke, and the saddle soap crumbled.

---

The next two days, Sage worked Promise with the saddle twice each session — morning and afternoon — while Riley watched from the rail.

It was Riley who suggested the weight work. "If I'm going to lean on him Saturday, he should know what that feels like first. Not from me — from you. You're the one he trusts with new things."

So Sage leaned. Carefully, incrementally, the way Rebecca directed. First just her forearms across the saddle while Promise stood on the lead rope. He turned his head to look at her — *what are you doing up there?* — but didn't move. Then more weight, her elbows locked, her feet barely on the ground. Promise shifted beneath her, rebalancing, and stood.

"Good," Rebecca said. "Now bounce. Gently. Let him feel the weight move."

Sage bounced. Promise's ears flicked back and his skin twitched, but he held. She did it again. Again. Each time he settled faster, his body learning that weight-on-back could shift and move without meaning pain.

By Friday afternoon she was hanging from the stirrup on his near side, most of her weight suspended from the saddle, her feet off the ground. Promise stood like stone, his head level, his eye calm. He'd even stopped looking back at her — the weight was just another thing that came from small-human's hands, and small-human's hands had never lied to him.

"He's solid," Riley said from the fence, and there was genuine admiration in it. "You've done beautiful work with him, Sage."

"We all have."

"No." Riley's voice was quiet but certain. "This part was you."

---

Friday evening, Sage stood at Promise's fence in the fading light. He came to her immediately, took his apple, leaned into her hand while she scratched behind his ears.

"Tomorrow," she told him. "Tomorrow someone's going to sit on your back for the first time since before. And it's going to be different from before, I promise you that. Riley is kind and patient and has the best hands of anyone I've ever seen ride. You're going to be fine."

Promise pushed his nose against her shoulder. His coat was thick now with his winter growth coming in, soft under her fingers. His eyes were calm, trusting, seeing only the human who had never hurt him.

"You're ready," she said. "We're all ready."

Behind her, the last light caught the round pen rails where everything would happen tomorrow morning. The saddle hung in the tack room, cleaned and checked. Riley had walked the round pen that afternoon, filling in a divot near the rail, raking the footing smooth.

Everything was prepared. Everything was in order.

Sage kissed Promise's velvet nose, said goodnight, and walked back to the house with her heart so full of hope it ached.
