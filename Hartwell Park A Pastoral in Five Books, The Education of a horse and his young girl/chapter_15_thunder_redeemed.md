# Chapter 15: Thunder Redeemed

The morning Rebecca told Sage she was ready to ride Thunder again, Sage's stomach dropped like a stone.

"Today?" she asked, keeping her voice carefully neutral.

"Today." Rebecca's tone was matter-of-fact, brooking no argument. "You've had eight days to heal. Thunder's mouth is completely recovered. You've ridden Clover successfully. You've groomed Thunder every day without incident. There's no reason to wait longer except fear, and fear that's allowed to take root grows stronger, not weaker."

Sage nodded, though her hands were already trembling. She'd known this moment was coming. Had thought about it every day while grooming Thunder, while watching Riley work him on the longe line, while lying awake at night replaying the rear over and over in her mind. But knowing it was coming and facing it were two different things.

"Riley will supervise," Rebecca continued. "I'll be in the stable yard if needed. But Sage—" She waited until Sage met her eyes. "Thunder has moved on from what happened. The question is whether you have."

***

An hour later, Sage stood in Thunder's stall with his tack, her hands moving through the familiar routine on autopilot while her mind raced. Thunder stood quiet, hip cocked, eyes half-closed. He showed no sign of remembering the rear, no wariness of her presence. Horses didn't hold grudges. Riley had told her that half a dozen times over the past week.

But Sage remembered. Her ribs had only stopped aching two days ago. She remembered the sensation of sliding backward, of nothing to hold onto, of hitting the ground so hard the world went blank. Remembered the look in Thunder's eyes afterward—not mean, not vicious, just afraid because she'd hurt him first.

"I won't do it again," she murmured, smoothing his saddle pad into place. "I promise."

Thunder's ear swiveled back toward her voice but otherwise he didn't react. Just a horse being tacked up. Nothing special. Nothing to fear.

Sage wished she could borrow some of his calm.

***

Riley was waiting in the exercise ring, expression carefully neutral. "How do you feel?"

"Terrified," Sage admitted.

"Good. Fear keeps you honest. It's overconfidence that gets people hurt." Riley walked a slow circle, studying her. "What's your plan for today?"

"Walk and trot work. Transitions. Circles and changes of direction. Nothing fancy. Nothing that pushes him." Sage had rehearsed this answer. "And if he gets frustrated or hot, I stop and regroup instead of pushing through."

"And if you get frustrated?"

"I stop immediately. Dismount if I need to. Walk away before I do something I'll regret."

Riley nodded. "Thunder's not going to test you the way he did that day. He's had easy work for over a week—he's fresh and eager to move. But he will test your confidence. He'll feel if you're uncertain and he'll question whether you can handle him. That's just who Thunder is. He needs a rider who can stay calm and clear even when he's pushing boundaries."

"I understand."

"Then go get your horse."

***

Thunder stood where she'd left him tied in the barn aisle, drowsing in a patch of sun. Sage untied his lead rope, gathered her courage, and led him toward the ring. With each step her heart beat faster, her palms growing damp against the leather. By the time they reached the mounting block, she had to consciously relax her grip on the reins.

Thunder stood perfectly still at the block, waiting. He'd done this hundreds of times. It was routine, boring even. Just another day of work.

Sage put her left foot in the stirrup. Paused, breathing deliberately, feeling the give in her ribs—mostly healed but still tender. If she fell today, it would hurt. If she made another mistake, hurt Thunder again...

*No.* She shook the thought away. Fear of making mistakes would make her tentative, and tentative riding was unclear riding. Thunder needed clarity.

She swung her leg over and settled into the saddle. Thunder shifted his weight slightly, rebalancing under her, but didn't move. Sage gathered her reins, found her stirrups, checked her position. Heels down, shoulders back, seat deep in the saddle. Breathe.

"Walk on," she said quietly, and squeezed with her legs.

Thunder walked.

The simple obedience nearly undid her. He just walked forward in his characteristic ground-covering stride—one-two-three-four—as if the last time she'd ridden him hadn't ended with her in the dirt and him fleeing to the far side of the ring. As if they were starting fresh, clean slate, no grudges.

"Good boy," she managed, voice tight.

They walked the full perimeter of the ring twice before Riley called out: "How does he feel?"

"Forward. Listening." Sage tested a half-halt and Thunder softened immediately, waiting for her next request. "He's giving me everything I ask."

"Then ask for more. But carefully."

Sage took a breath, settled deeper into her seat, and asked for trot. Thunder gave her a perfect transition—no surge, no resistance—and they floated into his big, reaching trot. Sage rose with the rhythm, posting smoothly, feeling his power beneath her but also his willingness. He was working with her, not against her.

Around the ring they went. Once. Twice. Sage felt her body starting to remember what it was like to ride Thunder when she rode him well—the exhilaration of his forward movement, the satisfaction when he responded to her aids precisely. This was what she'd been working toward all summer. Not domination, not forcing compliance, but clear communication where both partners understood each other.

"Serpentine," Riley called.

Sage began weaving Thunder through smooth curves across the ring—left, center, right, back again. She asked with her inside leg, supported with her outside rein, and Thunder bent through each curve like water. When she forgot to ask clearly with her leg and just pulled on one rein, he drifted instead of bending. The mistake was immediately obvious—*that* was pulling, *this* was asking. She corrected, he responded, and they finished the serpentine properly.

"Good," Riley said. "Again, but faster."

The second serpentine at a bigger trot was harder. Thunder's stride covered more ground, the curves came faster, Sage had to think quicker to prepare for each change of direction. But she stayed with him, breathing through the difficulty, trusting her body to remember what her mind had struggled to learn.

When they came back to walk, Sage's legs were shaking—partly from exertion, partly from relief. She'd done it. Ridden Thunder through real work without losing her temper, without yanking on his mouth, without letting fear make her either too harsh or too tentative.

"How do you feel?" Riley asked.

"Like I can breathe again." It was the truth. The knot of dread that had lived in her chest for eight days was finally loosening.

"Want to quit there? End on a good note?"

Sage considered. Part of her wanted exactly that—to dismount now while she was still successful, still confident. But another part remembered Rebecca's words: *Thunder makes you earn every step.* If she quit now because she was afraid of making another mistake, that fear would follow her to the next ride and the next.

"One more thing," she said. "I want to canter."

Riley's eyebrows rose. "You sure?"

"It's where we lost it last time. I need to prove to both of us that I can ride his canter correctly."

Riley studied her for a long moment, then nodded. "One circle each direction. But the second you feel yourself getting tense or unclear, you come back to trot. Understood?"

"Understood."

***

Sage walked Thunder in a small circle to prepare, gathering her reins slightly, sitting deep. Thunder's ears flicked back—*we're doing something different*—and his energy shifted. Not anxious. Just attentive, ready.

She waited until they were on the straight side of the ring, asked with her outside leg, sat deep, and thought: *canter*.

Thunder picked up the left lead smoothly, and suddenly they were flying. His three-beat rhythm was bigger, more powerful than Clover's, but Sage sat quietly and followed the motion. Around the ring they went—one long lap—and Thunder stayed balanced, stayed listening, gave her exactly what she asked for and nothing more.

Coming back to trot felt like landing. Sage's whole body was trembling, but she was grinning.

"Other direction," Riley called. "Then you're done."

The right lead canter was harder—Thunder always found this direction more difficult—but he gave it honestly, and Sage sat deep and quiet and let him find his balance. When she asked for trot again, he came back smoothly, and Sage let the reins go long so he could stretch his neck down, rewarding him for his effort.

"Walk him around the ring on a long rein," Riley said. "Let him cool out. You earned it. Both of you."

***

Sage dismounted on shaking legs, and Thunder immediately dropped his head, nosing at her pockets for treats even though he knew she never carried them while riding. The casual trust of the gesture—*you have pockets, pockets sometimes have apples, worth checking*—made her eyes sting.

"I'm so sorry," she told him quietly, stroking his neck. "For hurting you. For losing control. For being the kind of rider you couldn't trust."

Thunder lipped at her sleeve, unconcerned with apologies. What mattered to him was now: was she going to lead him back to the barn? Was there going to be water? Food? Grooming? Horses lived in the present moment, and right now, in this moment, Sage was just the human who rode him and then got off and sometimes gave scratches behind his ears.

Riley approached, handed Sage Thunder's lead rope. "You did well. Better than well. You rode him the way he needs to be ridden—clear, firm but fair, willing to correct yourself when you made mistakes."

"I was so afraid I'd do it again. Lose my temper. Hurt him."

"But you didn't. You felt yourself getting tense during that first trot and you breathed through it instead of letting it control you. That's growth." Riley paused. "The question now is whether you're ready to try Promise."

The words hung in the air between them. Sage looked at Thunder, then toward the paddock where Promise grazed in the morning sun.

"Not yet," she said quietly. "I need to be absolutely certain I can control myself under pressure. Thunder's testing me was one thing. But Promise..." She shook her head. "Promise has been hurt so badly by riders who lost control. I won't risk being another one. Even for a second."

Riley nodded slowly. "Good answer. The fact that you know you're not ready means you're getting closer to being ready. Real horsemanship is knowing the difference."

***

After cooling Thunder out and putting him away, Sage made her way to Promise's fence despite her exhaustion. Her legs felt like water, her ribs ached dully, but she needed to tell him what had happened even though he couldn't understand the words.

Promise came to the fence immediately, took his apple with the gentle precision she'd come to love. While he crunched, she scratched behind his ears.

"I rode Thunder today," she said. "For real. After the accident. After I hurt him and he hurt me." Promise leaned into her hand, comfortable. "And it was good. I didn't lose control. I didn't let fear make me unclear. I rode him the way he deserved to be ridden."

The stallion finished his apple and pushed his nose against her shoulder—his favorite gesture, the one that meant *here-with-you-comfortable*.

"I'm not ready to ride you yet," Sage continued. "I know that frustrates me, wanting to move faster. But you deserve better than a rider who's still learning not to make terrible mistakes. You deserve someone who's absolutely certain they can stay calm and clear even when you're afraid or testing or confused."

Promise blew out softly through his nose, then turned to graze, staying close to the fence. The easy comfort of his presence—no demands, no judgment, just choosing to be near her—felt like absolution.

Sage leaned her forehead against the fence rail, watching him graze. In the distance, she could see Riley working another horse in the round pen. Rebecca was in the stable yard talking to a delivery man. Thunder whinnied from his paddock, and Whisper answered. The familiar sounds and rhythms of Hartwell Park continuing around her.

A month ago she'd been so desperate to prove herself that she'd pushed Thunder too hard and paid the price. Today she'd proven something differentâ€"that she could fail badly and then do better. That mistakes didn't have to define her if she learned from them. That patience wasn't weakness; it was the hardest kind of strength.

"I'll keep getting better," she promised Promise. "I'll keep learning. And when I'm ready—when I'm absolutely certain I can be the rider you need—we'll try. But not before."

The stallion grazed on, unconcerned with timelines and promises, living in this moment where grass was good and sun was warm and the small-human stood nearby making soft sounds that felt like safety.

And Sage, watching him, felt something settle in her chest. Not confidence exactly. Not yet. But something steadierâ€"the knowledge that she could learn from her worst moments. That falling didn't mean failing as long as she got back up and did better.

Thunder had taught her precision and bravery. Today he'd taught her something else: redemption was possible if you were willing to earn it. One ride, one moment of staying calm, one choice to be better than you'd been before.

She stayed at the fence until her legs stopped shaking, until her heart rate returned to normal, until the last of the fear drained away and left only determination in its place.

Tomorrow she would ride Thunder again. And the day after. And the day after that. Building the foundation of competence and control that Promise would someday need from her.

Not rushing. Not pushing. Just working steadily toward the moment when she would be ready and he would be ready and they could begin the real work together.

The work of building trust between two creatures who'd both learned that humans could hurt them.

The work of proving that partnership was possible even after pain.

The work that required patience, humility, and the willingness to keep trying even when you'd failed.

Sage took a deep breath, feeling the slight pull in her healing ribs, and smiled.

The work continued.

And she was ready for it.
