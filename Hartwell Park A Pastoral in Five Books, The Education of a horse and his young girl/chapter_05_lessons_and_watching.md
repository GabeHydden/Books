### Chapter 5: Lessons and Watching

The second week at Hartwell Park brought weather that finally seemed to believe in spring. The mornings still held a chill, but by mid-morning the sun had genuine warmth to it, and the primroses around Sage's sitting spot had multiplied into small constellations of pale yellow.

Promise had moved closer.

Not dramatically—nothing about this work was dramatic. But on the ninth morning, when Sage took her usual position with her book, Promise was grazing only fifteen feet from the fence instead of his previous twenty. He didn't approach when she sat down, but he didn't retreat either. Just continued pulling up grass, his ears swiveling occasionally in her direction, his body no longer coiled quite so tightly.

"You're boring now," Riley observed, appearing behind Sage with a bucket of grooming tools. "That's good. That's very good."

Sage looked up from her book—she'd started _Robinson Crusoe_ now, finding something oddly appropriate about stories of survival and isolation. "How can you tell?"

"He's eating with his back partially to you. See?" Riley gestured with a curry comb. "If he was still worried, he'd keep you in direct sight always. But he's decided you're predictable. Predictable is safe."

Sage studied Promise's posture. Riley was right—his body was angled slightly away, though one ear still tracked toward her. "So what comes next?"

"More of the same. But also—" Riley paused, seemed to weigh something. "Also, I think it's time you learned to ride."

The words sent an unexpected jolt through Sage's chest. Fear and excitement tangled together, indistinguishable from each other. "Ride?"

"You've been watching these horses for nearly two weeks now. You can read their bodies better than most people who've worked with them for years. You're comfortable around them. Clover lets you groom her. Thunder doesn't push you around." Riley set down the bucket. "And if you're going to work with Promise eventually—really work with him—you need to know what you're asking him to do. Need to understand what it feels like from up there."

Sage's hand went to her throat, that habitual gesture, but she caught herself halfway. Let her hand drop. "I've never—I don't know the first thing about riding."

"That's why it's called learning." Riley's expression was patient. "Come on. Finish your sitting time, then meet me at the mounting block."

***

The letter arrived with the morning post.

Sage was in the kitchen with Jennifer, finishing breakfast, when Rebecca came in from the study carrying a single sheet of paper. She set it on the table in front of Sage without preamble, her expression unreadable.

"This came today. It's from your mother. I've read it—it was addressed to me. You should read it as well."

Sage picked up the letter. Her mother's handwriting—precise, slanted, the product of expensive schooling—filled the page.

*Dear Rebecca,*

*I write to inquire after the health and situation of my daughter, who departed Bath under circumstances I can only describe as distressing. While I appreciate your willingness to receive Sage during what has been a period of considerable nervous agitation, I must now request her return. The season is progressing, and her continued absence from society will only fuel the very speculation I have worked to suppress.*

*I trust you understand that Sage is not yet of age, and that her removal from my household without my formal consent places both of us in an awkward position. I have consulted Mr. Hadley on the legal particulars, and he assures me that a mother's claim is clear in these matters.*

*I do not wish to create unpleasantness. I ask only that Sage be returned to Bath by the end of the month, so that we may address this situation with the discretion it requires. Mr. Pemberton has expressed his willingness to let the matter rest, provided no further accusations are made public.*

*Your affectionate sister,*
*Caroline Morrison*

The words blurred. Sage set the letter down because her hands were shaking too badly to hold it, and pressed her palms flat against the table, feeling the grain of the wood, the solidity of it.

*Mr. Pemberton has expressed his willingness to let the matter rest.*

As though he were the injured party. As though his magnanimity in not pursuing the girl who'd accused him were a gift they should be grateful for.

"Sage." Jennifer's voice, near but not touching. A cup of tea appeared at her elbow—she hadn't seen Jennifer move to make it.

Rebecca had pulled out a chair and sat down across from Sage. She had a piece of paper and a pen in front of her, and she began speaking in the precise, methodical tone she used when working through a problem.

"The legal situation is this. You are seventeen. In England, your mother has custodial authority until you reach majority at twenty-one. However." Rebecca held up one finger. "I am your nearest paternal relation, your father's sister. I can petition for guardianship on grounds of the mother's failure to protect. The assault by Pemberton, your mother's refusal to act, and her subsequent attempt to return you to proximity with your assailant constitute grounds." A second finger. "Additionally, any court proceeding would require your mother to explain publicly why you left Bath, which she manifestly does not want. The threat of legal action is a bluff. She is counting on our fear of scandal matching hers."

Sage stared at the letter on the table. *Nervous agitation.* Her mother had reduced everything—the assault, the bruises, the terror, the months of recovery—to *nervous agitation.*

"She wants me back so she can pretend it didn't happen."

"Yes." Rebecca said it without softening. "That is precisely what she wants."

The rage came then—not the numb, frozen thing Sage had carried since Bath, but something hot and alive, rising from her stomach into her chest. She shoved back from the table, the chair scraping against the flagstones, and stood because sitting felt impossible when her whole body wanted to shake apart.

"I won't go back. I won't."

"No," Rebecca said. "You won't."

Jennifer's hand found Sage's arm—light, brief, withdrawn before Sage could flinch. "You're safe here. The letter changes nothing about that."

"I'll write to your mother," Rebecca said, pulling her paper closer. "And to Mr. Hadley. We'll settle this properly." She paused, studying Sage with that too-direct gaze. "But not today. Today you have work to do."

Sage picked up the letter again. Read it once more. Then folded it, carefully, and set it aside.

"I have a riding lesson," she said. Her voice came out flat and strange.

Jennifer and Rebecca exchanged a glance. "You don't have to—" Jennifer began.

"I want to ride." Sage was already moving toward the door. "I want to do something with my body that isn't shaking."

***

An hour later, Sage stood in her room staring at the trousers laid out on her bed. They were Riley's spare pair, rough canvas worn soft with use, and the sight of them made her breath catch.

Ladies did not wear trousers. Ladies rode sidesaddle, modestly, with their skirts arranged just so. Her mother would be appalled. Would have been appalled—the correction came automatically now. Her mother was no longer a voice that mattered in her life.

But the old rules still had weight. Still pressed against her like stays too tightly laced.

"You can ride sidesaddle if you prefer," Riley had said that morning, matter-of-fact. "It's proper, and you'll need to learn it eventually anyway—especially if you're ever riding in public. But it's harder. Unbalanced. You're perched on the side of the horse with both legs on one side, and if you lose your seat, there's nothing to catch yourself with. Most women who learn to ride start astride—it's safer, more natural. But it requires trousers."

Sage picked up the trousers, felt the weight of the fabric. The weight of the choice.

She thought of Rebecca saying: *We do not traffic in pretense at Hartwell.* Thought of Jennifer's easy comfort in her own skin, Riley's absolute confidence in his unusual place in the world. Thought of Mr. Pemberton's hands on her body, taking what he wanted, society's rules giving him permission.

What did propriety matter, really? What had it ever protected her from?

She put on the trousers.

They felt strange—the fabric against her legs, the freedom of movement, the way they made her aware of her body in a completely different way than skirts did. She pulled on one of Rebecca's old linen shirts, too large in the shoulders but serviceable, and studied herself in the looking glass.

She looked like a woman pretending to be a boy. Or perhaps like a woman who had stopped pretending to be a lady.

The distinction felt important, though she couldn't articulate why.

She was still studying her reflection when a soft knock came at the door. Jennifer's voice: "Sage? Riley sent me up to check—ah."

Jennifer stopped in the doorway, taking in the trousers, the oversized shirt, the way Sage was standing with her arms held slightly away from her body as if unsure what to do with herself.

"Well," Jennifer said, and her smile was warm. "You look ready for something."

"I feel ridiculous."

"You feel different. That's not the same thing." Jennifer crossed to her, unhurried, her movements visible the way she always made them now—Sage had noticed that, the way Jennifer and Rebecca both moved around her so she could see them coming. The way they gave her time to prepare.

Jennifer reached up and tucked a strand of hair behind Sage's ear. A small gesture, quick and maternal, the sort of thing a mother might do without thinking.

Sage felt herself tense. Felt the familiar impulse to jerk away, to put distance between herself and unexpected hands.

But she didn't. She held still, let the touch happen, let it end.

"There," Jennifer said, as if she'd merely been tidying rather than testing. "You had a bit coming loose."

Underneath the flutter of her heartbeat was something else. Something like triumph. She had allowed that touch. Had felt her body's warning and chosen, consciously, not to flee.

"I'm nervous," she said. "About the riding."

"Of course you are. It's new and strange and you might fall off." Jennifer's eyes crinkled. "But you won't break. And Clover won't let you come to any real harm. She's been teaching frightened people for years."

"Like Promise teaches me patience?"

"Something like that." Jennifer moved to the door, then paused. "You're doing beautifully, you know. All of it. The horses see it. We see it too."

After she left, Sage stood for a moment longer, her hand drifting up to touch the place behind her ear where Jennifer's fingers had been. And she had let it happen.

***

When she emerged from the house, Riley was waiting by the paddock with Clover, already tacked. Riley's eyebrows rose slightly at the sight of her, but the expression was approving rather than shocked.

"Good. That'll make things easier." Then, reading something in Sage's face: "Your mother would have opinions about this, I expect."

"My mother has opinions about everything." Sage's voice came out sharper than intended. "She had very particular ideas about what a proper young lady should be. Should do. Should tolerate."

Riley was quiet for a moment, adjusting Clover's girth. "And you're done tolerating."

It wasn't a question. But Sage answered anyway. "Yes. I'm done."

"Then let's teach you to ride."

***

Sage stood next to Clover in the paddock, staring up at the mare's back as though it were a vast distance instead of merely tall. The trousers made her feel exposed in a way that had nothing to do with actual visibility and everything to do with old rules breaking. But they also made her feel free. The contradiction was dizzying.

The horse seemed enormous from this angle, all muscle and height and living, breathing presence.

"She won't hurt you," Riley said, checking the girth strap one more time. "Clover's taught more people to ride than I can count. She knows her job."

Riley moved to Clover's head and slid a finger along the bit where it rested against the mare's lip, running the pad of his thumb across the metal edge. "Before every ride," he said, matter-of-fact, "you check the tack. Every piece. Run your fingers along the bit—feel for rough spots, burrs, anything sharp. Check the leather for cracks, the stitching for fraying, the buckles for rust. Every time." He moved back along Clover's side, testing a buckle on the cheekpiece, tugging the reins where they attached. "The horse can't tell you his equipment is wrong. He can only tell you it hurts, and by then the damage is done."

Sage nodded, trying to absorb this along with everything else—the names of the saddle parts, the right way to approach, the left-side mounting, all of it pouring in faster than she could hold.

Clover turned her head to look at Sage with one large, dark eye. Her expression was placid, almost bored. Sage reached up to touch the mare's neck, feeling the warmth of her, the solid reality.

"I'm frightened," Sage admitted.

"I know. That's all right. Being frightened and doing it anyway—that's courage." Riley moved to the mounting block, a sturdy wooden step positioned next to Clover's left side. "Come here. Left foot in the stirrup, hands on the pommel and cantle—here and here—then push yourself up and swing your right leg over. I'll help."

Sage climbed onto the mounting block, her heart hammering. This close, Clover was even larger, the saddle an unfamiliar landscape of leather and metal. She put her left foot in the stirrup as Riley had shown her, gripped the saddle, and tried to haul herself up.

She got perhaps six inches off the block before her arms gave out and she thumped back down, graceless and embarrassed.

"Try again. Use your legs more—push off the block, don't just pull with your arms."

Second attempt: she managed to get herself higher, but her right leg caught on something and she ended up half-draped over the saddle like a sack of grain, neither up nor down, scrambling awkwardly while Clover stood with the patience of a saint.

"Hold on—" Riley's hands came to her waist, steadying her, helping guide her leg over. The touch was firm but impersonal, meant only to help, and Sage realized with a start that she wasn't flinching. Wasn't pulling away. Riley's hands were just... helping.

When had that changed?

Then she was sitting in the saddle, properly upright, and the world had transformed. Everything looked different from up here—the paddock spread wider, the fence lines seemed lower, and beneath her was Clover's broad back, warm and solid and moving slightly with each breath the mare took.

"Oh," Sage said, and couldn't articulate anything beyond that.

"Feet in the stirrups—there. Now sit back a bit, you're perched too far forward." Riley's hands came to her knee, adjusting the angle of her leg. "Here. Feel that? Your leg should hang long, heels down, following the line of the horse's side. Not gripping with your knees—that'll make you stiff. Just draped around her, like you belong there."

Belong there. Sage tried to let her legs relax into the position Riley was showing her, though every muscle wanted to tense up, to grip hard, to hold on for dear life even though Clover was standing perfectly still.

"Your back," Riley continued, moving around to adjust Sage's shoulders. "Straight but not rigid. Think of a string pulling up from the top of your head. Weight down through your seat bones—do you feel them? You're sitting on two bones at the bottom of your pelvis. That's your foundation."

Riley's hand came to her knee again, repositioning the angle for the third time. "Heels down. You keep letting them creep up."

Sage forced her heels down. Riley's hand moved to her lower back, pressing gently. "Straighten here. You're collapsing through the middle."

She straightened. Clover walked on. Riley walked alongside, watching, assessing, and his hand came to her knee again—

"I *know.*" The words came out sharp as a slap. Sage wrenched her leg away from Riley's hand, and Clover stopped, ears flicking back at the sudden tension. "Stop—just stop touching me. I know where my leg goes."

The paddock went very quiet.

Riley stepped back. One step, then two. His hand dropped to his side and he stood still, giving her the same careful distance he gave a horse that had just struck out. His expression didn't close—that was the worst part. It stayed open, patient, and Sage could see in his eyes that he understood this wasn't about the riding, which made her want to scream because she didn't want to be understood. She wanted to be angry and have it be simple.

"I'm going to walk away for a minute," Riley said, voice level. "Take some time with Clover. I'll be at the barn."

He turned and walked away. Didn't look back. Didn't ask questions.

Sage sat on Clover in the empty paddock, her hands shaking on the reins, and felt the anger drain out of her like water through sand, leaving behind nothing but the grit. Riley had been helping. Riley, who had never been anything but patient, who had fitted the tack room door with a hook without being asked, who spoke to her in the same steady voice he gave frightened horses—Riley, and she had drawn blood.

Clover shifted her weight, and Sage gasped as the saddle moved beneath her. Every muscle locked up.

"Easy. She's just adjusting her feet. You're going to feel everything she does up here—every shift, every breath, every step. That's normal. That's how it works." Riley moved to Clover's head, gathered the reins. "I'm going to lead her in a small circle, just walking. You don't have to do anything yet except sit there and feel what it's like. Ready?"

No. Not at all. But Sage nodded anyway.

Riley clucked softly and Clover began to walk.

The sensation was unlike anything Sage had expected. The mare's back rolled beneath her like a gentle wave, side to side, forward and back, a rhythm that was both foreign and somehow organic. She could feel the movement of each leg—the left hind pushing, the right hind following, the alternating roll that transferred through Clover's barrel into Sage's seat, her spine, her whole body.

"Breathe," Riley called. "You're holding your breath."

Sage forced air into her lungs. The movement continued, inexorable and steady, Clover's patient walk carrying them in a wide circle around the paddock. After a few rounds, Sage found her body beginning to follow the rhythm without thinking about it, her hips moving with the mare's stride, her shoulders loosening.

"There you go," Riley said. "You're finding the feel of it. That's her walk—four beats, one-two-three-four. You can count it if it helps. Feel how she's using her back? How each step comes up through her body into yours?"

Sage counted silently. One-two-three-four. One-two-three-four. The rhythm was soothing once she stopped fighting it, almost meditative. Clover's ears were relaxed, floppy, unconcerned. Just a horse doing a job she'd done a thousand times before, carrying yet another awkward human who didn't quite know how to sit properly.

"Good. Now we're going to try something," Riley said. "I'm going to let go of the lead. You're going to hold the reins—gently, like you're holding baby birds, not strangling them. And you're going to ask her to walk forward by squeezing very gently with your legs. Just a little pressure. She knows what it means."

Riley handed up the reins and stepped back.

Sage was alone on the horse.

Panic flickered, but Clover just stood there, patient and calm, waiting. Sage gathered the reins as Riley had shown her—one in each hand, passing through her closed fingers, not pulling, just holding. She squeezed her legs against Clover's sides, barely more than a suggestion.

Clover walked forward.

"Oh!" Sage gasped, and immediately pulled on the reins without meaning to. Clover stopped.

"That's all right. She's responding to you. You asked her to walk, she walked. You pulled back, she stopped. She's listening." Riley moved to walk alongside. "Try again. Squeeze to walk, soft hands on the reins, follow her mouth—don't pull unless you want her to stop."

Sage tried again. Squeeze. Clover walked. This time Sage forced her hands to stay soft, to move with the motion of the mare's head—which was bobbing slightly with each step, she realized, and if she didn't follow that motion the bit would pull on Clover's mouth.

They made it halfway around the paddock before Sage's arms started shaking from tension and she had to ask Clover to stop. But she'd done it. Had asked a horse to move and the horse had listened.

"That's enough for first lesson," Riley said, coming to help her dismount. "Don't want to overdo it or you'll be too sore to move tomorrow."

Getting down was somehow more terrifying than getting up had been. Sage had to swing her right leg back over, trusting that Riley would catch her, that she wouldn't just fall and break something. She managed it gracelessly, sliding down the last bit and landing hard on the ground, her legs immediately trembling.

"You did well," Riley said. "Better than most first-timers. You've got decent balance, and you weren't fighting her too much." Riley began loosening the girth. "We'll keep you astride for now—it's safer while you're learning the basics. But eventually you'll need to master sidesaddle too."

"For propriety?" Sage couldn't keep the edge from her voice.

"For practicality. If you ever ride in public—to town, to church, to a meet—you'll need to ride sidesaddle. Society expects it. And if you're serious about working with Promise someday..." Riley paused. "Well. A lady on a stallion attracts enough attention. A lady in trousers on a stallion would cause scandal that could hurt Rebecca. Hurt all of us."

Sage understood. The household existed on the margins of acceptability as it was. Rebecca and Jennifer's "companionship," Riley's presence, their methods with horses—all of it was tolerated only because they maintained certain proprieties in public.

"How much harder is it? Sidesaddle?"

"Harder. Your balance is completely different—both legs on one side, sitting sideways to the horse's motion. If you lose your seat, there's nothing between you and the ground. But it can be done well." Riley's expression was serious. "Ladies have been hunting and jumping sidesaddle for generations. It's a real skill. We'll get you confident astride first, then teach you both."

She dismounted without waiting for help. Untacked Clover with the mechanical precision Riley had drilled into her—girth first, then saddle, then bridle, each piece checked and hung properly. Led the mare back to her stall. Filled the water bucket. Measured the grain.

Then she found a pitchfork and went to the stall Riley was mucking.

He looked up when she appeared in the doorway. Said nothing. Went back to forking soiled straw into the wheelbarrow.

Sage took the other side of the stall and began working. Fork, lift, deposit. Fork, lift, deposit. The rhythm of it, the physical labor, the smell of horse and straw and honest dirt. They worked in silence for a long time—twenty minutes, perhaps. Long enough for the stall to be stripped to bare floor, fresh straw spread, the water bucket refilled.

"I'm sorry about earlier," Sage said, not looking up from the straw she was spreading.

"I know," Riley said. And they kept working.

***

That afternoon, Sage returned to Promise's paddock for her usual evening session, changed back into her dress but still feeling the ghost-sensation of the trousers, the freedom of movement they'd allowed. Her legs were so stiff she had to move carefully, and sitting down on the ground made every muscle protest. But she settled into her spot with her book, prepared for the familiar pattern.

Promise stood waiting at the fence.

Not right at it—still a good ten feet back. But closer than he'd ever been before. He was grazing, but his head came up when she sat down, and his ears pricked forward. Not pinned back in fear. Forward, with clear interest.

He was watching her.

Sage held very still, hardly breathing. This was different. This was attention without terror, curiosity without panic. Promise's head remained up, his gaze fixed on her, and he took a step closer. Then another.

Eight feet from the fence now. Close enough that she could see the scars on his shoulder clearly, see the way his coat had begun to take on a healthier sheen with good feeding. Close enough to see the intelligence in his eyes as he assessed her.

She didn't move. Didn't speak. Just sat there while he looked at her, while something shifted between them—some calculation being made, some decision being weighed.

After perhaps a minute, Promise lowered his head and returned to grazing. But he didn't move away. Stayed there, eight feet from the fence, eating grass while she sat on the other side.

Not together. Not yet anything close to trusting.

But not separate in the same way they had been before.

Sage opened her book but found she couldn't read. Couldn't focus on anything except the black horse grazing nearby, the sound of him pulling up grass, the occasional huff of breath through his nostrils. The simple fact of his proximity.

Her legs ached from the riding lesson—muscles she didn't know she had, complaining about uses they'd never been put to. But underneath the discomfort was something else. A sense of... ownership, perhaps. Her body had done something today. Had learned something. Had been more than just the thing that carried her around.

She thought about Clover's solid warmth beneath her. The strange, shifting balance of sitting on a moving creature. The moment Riley's hands had adjusted her position and she had not flinched—had simply accepted the touch as teaching, as help.

When had that become possible?

She remembered Mr. Pemberton's hands. On her arm, her waist, her hip. Positioning her the way he wanted. Moving her like a doll, like a thing without will.

Riley's hands had been so different. Impersonal but kind. Touching her body as a student's body—something to be taught, not something to be used. And her body had known the difference. Had recognized, somehow, that this was not that.

Promise took another step closer to the fence. Seven feet now.

*My body knew,* Sage thought. *Before my mind could sort it out, my body knew Riley's touch was safe.*

Maybe that was how it worked. Maybe the body could learn new truths even while the mind was still arguing with old ones. Maybe she could teach herself, the way Rebecca was teaching Promise—not by forcing anything, but by offering different experiences. Accumulating evidence. Building a new understanding, touch by touch.

She had ridden a horse today. Had felt Clover's power and responsiveness, had guided that thousand-pound creature with her legs and hands. Had discovered that her body could do things she'd never imagined.

Her body was not just the thing Mr. Pemberton had grabbed. Not just the frozen, failing flesh that had been unable to fight him off.

Her body was also the thing that could learn to ride. Could post the trot, could find the rhythm, could communicate with another creature through pressure and release. Could be strong and capable and *hers*.

Promise was watching her, those dark eyes assessing. She met his gaze steadily.

"I rode today," she told him softly. "A horse. For the first time. And I didn't fall off."

His ear flicked toward her voice.

He didn't answer—of course he didn't—but he didn't move away, either. Just stood there, seven feet from the fence, watching her with those dark, knowing eyes.

Progress. Real, measurable progress.

***

That evening, at supper, Sage found herself eating with genuine appetite for the first time since arriving. The work of learning to ride had left her physically exhausted in a way that felt clean, earned. Her muscles ached but it was good ache, the kind that meant she'd used her body for something purposeful.

"Riley says you rode today," Rebecca observed, passing the potatoes.

"Barely. I walked around the paddock a few times without falling off. I don't think that counts as riding."

"It counts," Jennifer said warmly. "Everyone starts somewhere. I'm told my first riding lesson involved more crying than actual horse."

"You were six years old," Rebecca pointed out. "And the pony bit you."

"All the same. Starting is the hardest part."

"Promise came closer today," she said. "After I rode. He was grazing right near the fence. Watching me."

A look passed between Rebecca and Riley.

"He's curious," Riley said. "He's been watching you work with the other horses all week. Watching you groom them, walk them out, today watching you ride. He's trying to understand what you're doing."

A look passed between Riley and Sage—brief, acknowledging something neither of them mentioned. The afternoon's friction. The silent repair. The work that had followed, side by side, without need for analysis.

"Same time tomorrow?" Riley asked.

"Yes," Sage said. "I want to learn this."

***

Promise stood at the far end of his paddock in the evening light, watching the small-human disappear into the big-house. She had been at the fence again today—twice now, morning and evening, the pattern he had learned was safe. Morning-human, evening-human, always sitting, always not-moving, always leaving-without-demanding.

But today was different. Today the small-human had done something new.

He had watched from his space as the tall-human-who-moves-easy—the one with the voice that had no sharp-edges, the one who fed the other horses without pain—had brought out the old-calm-mare. The calm-one who moved with the confidence of knowing she was safe, who had no fear-smell even when humans were close.

And then the small-human had climbed onto the old-calm-mare's back.

Promise had watched this with confusion that pulled at something in his chest. Not fear-confusion. Different confusion. Curiosity-confusion.

The small-human had been clumsy, yes. Had moved wrong, sat wrong, gripped-too-tight in ways that would have made him angry if a human had done that to him. But the old-calm-mare had not been angry. Had not tensed or fought or tried to escape. Had just walked in steady circles while the small-human learned to sit, learned to move with the walking-rhythm, learned to not-fall-off.

And the tall-human-who-moves-easy had touched the small-human. Hands on legs, on back, on shoulders—adjusting, teaching. The small-human had not pulled away. Had not fought. Had accepted the touching as if touching was safe.

Promise did not understand this.

In his before-time, touching meant pain. Hands meant hurt-coming-soon. The man-who-hits would touch to position his body wrong, to yank his head down with the metal-mouth-pain, to strike with the hard-stick until he stopped whatever he was doing that made the man angry.

But the tall-human's touching had been different. Gentle-touching. Teaching-touching. And the small-human had accepted it.

More confusing: when the riding-work was done, when the small-human had climbed down and the old-calm-mare had been led away, the small-human had come to his fence again. Had sat in her usual spot. And her scent was different than usual.

Not fear-scent. Not the frozen-prey-scent she had carried when she first arrived. This was something else. Something bright and warm, like the sun on grass. Satisfaction-scent, maybe. Achievement-scent.

She had been pleased with herself.

Promise had found himself moving closer to the fence than before. Closer than he intended. Closer than was safe.

But nothing bad had happened. She had just sat there, not-moving, not-demanding. Same as always. Boring, like the tall-human had said horses become when they learn you won't hurt them.

But not quite boring. Because Promise was starting to wonder.

Wonder what the small-human was learning. Wonder why the old-calm-mare accepted her so easily. Wonder what it would feel like to be touched the way the tall-human touched—gentle-teaching instead of pain-demanding.

Wonder if different might actually be possible.

He crushed the wondering immediately. Different was dangerous. Hope was the trap.

But the small-human kept coming back. Morning, evening, every day the same. And today she had ridden the old-calm-mare and the old-calm-mare had not been hurt by it.

And somewhere in his chest, despite everything he knew about humans and pain and the ways hope always led to worse-pain, Promise felt something shift.

Not trust. Not yet. Not even close.

But maybe the beginning of a question.

What if?
