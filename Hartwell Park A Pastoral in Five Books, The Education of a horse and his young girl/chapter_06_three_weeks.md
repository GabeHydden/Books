### Chapter 6: Three Weeks

**Day Ten**

The soreness was worse than Sage had anticipated. She woke the morning after her first riding lesson unable to sit up properly, her inner thighs screaming protest, her back muscles knotted in ways she hadn't known possible. Even breathing seemed to engage muscles that had decided they were finished cooperating.

"That's normal," Jennifer said, setting a cup of tea at Sage's elbow. Her hand brushed Sage's shoulder as she passed—deliberate this time, Sage thought. A small testing. A small gift.

Sage didn't flinch.

She noticed the absence of the flinch the way you might notice a missing tooth—by probing the space where something used to be. Three weeks ago, that touch would have sent her jerking sideways. Two weeks ago, she would have tensed, braced, forced herself to stay still. Now it was just... a hand on her shoulder. Contact and release. Nothing more.

"Your body's learned to use itself in entirely new ways," Jennifer continued, sitting down with her own tea. "Give it a few days."

Sage wrapped her hands around her cup, thinking about all the ways her body had learned new things. Riding. Grooming. Sitting still while a traumatized horse decided whether to trust her. Accepting touch without her heart slamming against her ribs.

"I didn't flinch," she said quietly. "Just now. When you touched me."

Jennifer's eyes crinkled with warmth. "I noticed."

"I'm keeping track. Of the changes. The way Promise is changing, and the way I am."

"Good." Jennifer reached across the table and squeezed Sage's hand—brief, warm, gone. "That's good. Sometimes we heal so slowly we don't notice it happening. The noticing matters."

But Sage didn't have a few days. She had a horse to sit with and a lesson to attend. So she gritted her teeth, changed into Riley's trousers—which somehow made the ache worse by reminding her legs exactly what they'd done yesterday—and made her way to the paddock.

Riley took one look at her careful gait and laughed. Not unkindly, but with the recognition of someone who'd seen this many times before. "Can you get on the mounting block?"

"I think so." Sage climbed up with enormous effort, every muscle complaining.

"Good. Now get on the horse."

This time the mounting was slightly less graceless, though Sage's body protested every moment of it. But once she was in the saddle, something strange happened. The ache didn't disappear, exactly, but it settled into something bearable. The warmth of Clover's back, the gentle rock of her walk—it was almost soothing.

"We're just walking today," Riley said. "Getting you comfortable with steering. Left rein to go left, right rein to go right, both reins to stop. Easy pressure. Think of it like suggesting rather than demanding."

By the end of the lesson, Sage could guide Clover around the paddock in reasonably accurate circles and figure-eights. Her body still ached, but the ache felt earned. Purposeful.

Promise watched from his paddock, head up, ears forward.

***

**Day Twelve**

"Mucking is the foundation of all horsemanship," Riley said, handing Sage a pitchfork. "If you can't manage the shit, you can't manage the horse."

Sage stared at the implement, then at the soiled straw in Clover's stall. The mare had been turned out for the day, and the evidence of her night's occupation was... substantial.

"You're joking."

"I am not. This is the work. Every day. Every horse." Riley demonstrated, lifting the soiled straw and depositing it in a wheelbarrow with practiced efficiency. "You learn what's normal for each horse—how much they eat, how much comes out, what color, what consistency. Changes in any of that can mean illness. So you pay attention."

It was disgusting work. Hot work, even in the relative cool of morning. Physical work that made Sage's already-sore muscles protest. But there was something meditative about it too. The rhythm of fork-lift-deposit. The satisfaction of seeing the stall transform from soiled to clean. The way Riley worked alongside her, not supervising but partnering.

"My mother would die if she could see me now," Sage said, pausing to wipe sweat from her forehead.

Riley glanced over. "Would she?"

Sage thought about it. Really thought. "No. She'd pretend not to see. That was always her way. If she didn't acknowledge something, it didn't exist."

"That why she didn't believe you? About what happened?"

The question was gentle but direct. Sage had told Riley some of it during their conversation about trauma, but not the specifics. Not the details that still made her throat close up.

"She believed me," Sage heard herself say. "She just decided it was my fault. Easier than admitting a gentleman—a respectable man, from a good family—could be a monster. Easier to blame the girl than challenge the system that gave him permission."

Riley was quiet for a moment, forking soiled straw. "The system that says a man owns a horse can do what he wants with it. That says a husband owns his wife, same principle. That says some people exist for other people's use." Another forkful. "We're trying to build something different here. Not perfect. But different."

Sage understood. The household, the methods, the quiet rebellion of it all. Women who loved women. People who existed outside gender's neat boxes. Horses treated as partners rather than property. All of it a small, stubborn insistence that the world could be other than what it was.

"I want to be part of this," Sage said. "Building something different."

Riley smiled. "You already are."

***

**Day Thirteen**

Riley needed fence wire from the village. "Come along," he said. "I could use a hand carrying."

Market day had drawn more people than Sage was accustomed to seeing on her Sunday walks to church. The street was crowded with carts and livestock and women with baskets, the air full of competing voices. Sage kept close to Riley as they made their way toward the ironmonger's, weaving between a pen of geese and a woman selling eggs from a handcart.

It was fine. She was fine. She had been coming to this village every Sunday for three weeks, sitting in the same pew, nodding to the same faces. This was familiar ground.

They reached the ironmonger's shop. Riley went inside. Sage waited with the horses, which was easier than navigating the cramped interior, and watched the market flow around her.

A man came out of the inn across the street. Nothing remarkable about him—middle-aged, well-dressed, a farmer or minor gentleman by his coat. He stopped in the inn doorway and stood there, blocking it, speaking to someone still inside. His voice carried across the street—a low, confident laugh, the easy authority of a man who had never once questioned his right to stand wherever he pleased and make others go around him.

The laugh. The doorway. The shape of a man filling a space she would need to pass through.

It wasn't him. She knew it wasn't him. Pemberton was in Bath, two counties away, and this man had never seen her, never spoken to her, never thought of her for a single moment of his life.

But her body didn't know that. Her body saw: man, doorway, no way past. Her body heard: that particular pitch of laugh, the one that said *I take what I want and who will stop me.* Her body decided, without consulting her mind, that she was in the library again and the door was blocked and there was nowhere to go.

She was gripping the hitching rail with both hands. Her vision had narrowed to a point. The market sounds—geese, voices, cart wheels on cobbles—receded to a distant roar, as though she were underwater and the world above was happening to someone else.

Riley was beside her. She didn't know when he'd come out of the shop. He didn't touch her. Just stood where she could see him, the coil of fence wire over one shoulder, and said, "Thunder's near hind felt warm on the ride in. Would you check it for me?"

A task. Her hands released the rail and moved to Thunder's leg. She ran her fingers down the cannon bone, around the fetlock, across the heel. The leg was fine—not warm, not swollen—and she suspected Riley knew that. But her hands were doing something now, something skilled and purposeful, and her breathing was following her hands back to steadiness.

She checked all four of Thunder's legs. Then all four of Clover's. By the time she straightened, the man had left the inn doorway and the street was just a street and her heart had slowed to something like its normal pace.

They rode home without speaking. At the gate, Riley said, "Market day's always crowded. Sundays are better."

Sage nodded. Couldn't manage more.

***

**Day Fifteen**

"Today we trot," Riley announced.

Sage's stomach dropped. She'd been walking on Clover for five days now, gaining confidence, learning to steer with increasing precision. Walking felt manageable. Trotting felt like inviting disaster.

"I don't think I'm ready."

"You're ready enough. And the only way to get better at trotting is to trot." Riley checked the girth one more time. Sage ran her fingers along the bit as Riley had taught her—feeling for rough edges, checking where the metal met the rings—then tested each buckle on the cheekpiece. Everything smooth, everything sound. She stepped back.

"It's going to feel bouncy. Uncomfortable. Your instinct will be to grip with your legs and tense up, but that'll make it worse. You need to learn to post—rise and fall with the horse's rhythm. I'll show you."

Riley demonstrated from the ground, miming the rising and falling motion. "The trot is a two-beat gait. Boom-boom, boom-boom. You rise on one beat, sit on the next. Rise, sit, rise, sit. It takes practice."

Sage mounted, her body no longer screaming quite so much at the effort. Small mercies. Riley led Clover in a slow walk to warm up, then: "I'm going to ask her to trot now. Just try to sit it first—feel the rhythm. Don't worry about posting yet."

Riley clucked, and Clover moved into a trot.

Sage immediately understood why posting was necessary. The trot was teeth-rattling, bone-jarring, nothing like the gentle rock of the walk. She bounced helplessly in the saddle, her body slamming down at irregular intervals, no rhythm to be found.

"Try to relax!" Riley called.

Relaxing seemed impossible. Sage gripped harder with her legs, which made the bouncing worse. Her hands pulled on the reins without meaning to, and Clover dropped back to a walk, patient and uncomplaining.

"Again," Riley said. "This time, try counting. Feel for the boom-boom. One-two, one-two."

They tried again. And again. And again. By the end of the lesson, Sage had managed perhaps three consecutive posting steps before losing the rhythm and collapsing back into chaotic bouncing. Her body ached in entirely new places. She was fairly certain she'd bitten her tongue at one point.

But she'd felt it. Just for those three steps. The rhythm, the rise and fall, the way posting let you move with the horse instead of against her.

"That's more than most people get on their first try," Riley said, helping her dismount. "Give it a few more lessons. Your body will learn."

Sage changed back into her dress and went to sit with Promise, moving with the careful stiffness of someone who'd discovered muscles she didn't know existed.

Promise had come to the fence. Not right to it, but close. Six feet back, maybe. Closer than he'd been yesterday.

He raised his head when she sat down, ears forward, watching her. She was limping slightly from the riding lesson, and something about her careful movements seemed to interest him. His nostrils flared, scenting.

"Rough day," she told him quietly. "Learning to trot is harder than it looks."

He didn't react to her voice, but he didn't move away either. Just stood there, grazing occasionally, watching her with that same focused attention.

***

**Day Eighteen**

Sage was grooming Thunder in the barn, working the curry comb in small circles over his shoulder, when the gelding suddenly swung his head around and nipped at her arm. Not hard—warning rather than attack—but sharp enough to make her yelp and jump back.

Riley appeared immediately. "What did you do?"

"Nothing! I was just brushing him."

"Show me where you were brushing."

Sage indicated the spot on Thunder's shoulder. Riley pressed gently with his fingers, and Thunder's ears went back, his body tensing.

"There. Feel that? He's sore. You were brushing over a sore spot and he told you to stop." Riley's voice was matter-of-fact. "This is what I mean about listening. He communicated. You didn't hear him, so he had to get louder."

Sage thought back. Thunder's ears had been back, now that she replayed the moment. His body had shifted away slightly. She'd ignored the signs, focused on her task rather than his response.

"I should have paid attention."

"Yes. But you learned. Now you know to watch for soreness, to read his body while you work. Thunder's not angry at you—see? He's already relaxed again. You just needed the reminder to listen." Riley demonstrated, working around the sore spot, and Thunder's eyes went half-closed with pleasure. "Horses are honest. If you hurt them, they tell you. If you help them, they tell you that too."

That afternoon, Sage groomed Whisper with exquisite attention, watching every twitch of ear, every weight shift, every signal the mare gave. Whisper leaned into the brush when Sage found an itchy spot, practically groaning with satisfaction. The feedback was immediate, clear, unmistakable.

"You liked that, didn't you?" Sage murmured, brushing the spot more thoroughly. Whisper's lower lip went loose and floppy, the ultimate sign of contentment.

Later, sitting by Promise's paddock with her book, Sage thought about communication. About how the horses told you everything if you paid attention. About how Mr. Pemberton had ignored every signal she'd given—the stiffening body, the pulling away, the verbal "no"—because he hadn't cared to listen.

She thought about the word *no*.

She had said it to Mr. Pemberton. Had said it clearly, had meant it completely, had believed—stupidly, childishly—that the word would matter. That her voice had power.

He had taught her otherwise. Had taught her that her voice was nothing when a man decided not to hear it.

For weeks afterward, she had felt mute. Not physically—she could speak, could make sounds—but mute in some deeper way. As though the words that came out of her mouth were just noise. As though she had learned, once and for all, that her voice changed nothing.

But here, at this fence, she spoke to Promise. And he listened.

Not the words—he couldn't understand the words. But her tone. Her presence. The consistent, patient offering of herself, day after day, asking nothing, demanding nothing.

He was listening to her in a way Mr. Pemberton never had. Was taking her seriously in a way her mother never had. Was responding to her presence as though it mattered—as though *she* mattered.

Promise was five and a half feet from the fence now. Half a foot closer than yesterday. Still watching. Still curious. Still careful.

"I'm learning to listen," she told him softly. "I'm learning to pay attention. And you're listening too, aren't you? You hear me. Not my words, but... something."

His ears swiveled toward her voice, then away. But he stayed close. That was answer enough.

She had a voice. It was small, it was quiet, but it was hers. And here, in this paddock, with this horse who had every reason to ignore humans entirely, she was being heard.

***

**Day Nineteen**

Sage was carrying water buckets to the paddock trough—a task she could now manage without slopping half the contents on her boots—when she noticed Whisper standing oddly at the far end of the field. The mare's weight was shifted forward, her left hind barely touching the ground, and her head was higher than usual, ears flicking with discomfort.

Sage set down the buckets and watched. Whisper took a step, then stopped. Took another. The gait was wrong—short and careful on that hind leg, as though the ground hurt.

She found Riley in the barn, cleaning tack. "Something's wrong with Whisper. She's favoring her left hind. Won't put full weight on it."

Riley looked up. "Since when?"

"I don't know. I just noticed. She's standing differently—her whole posture's changed. Head up, ears tight. She's in pain."

Riley set down the bridle and followed her out. They watched Whisper from the fence for a moment, then Riley climbed through and approached the mare quietly, running a hand down her shoulder before lifting the hind hoof.

"There." Riley's thumb pressed near the heel, and Whisper flinched, pulling the foot away. "Abscess. Feel here—the heat."

Sage reached through the fence as Riley brought Whisper closer. The hoof wall near the heel was hot to the touch, the tissue swollen beneath her fingers.

"How long has this been brewing?" Sage asked.

"Day or two at least. I checked her feet yesterday morning and missed it—must have been just starting." Riley set the hoof down gently. "Good catch. Another day and she'd have been properly lame."

They brought Whisper into the barn. Riley showed Sage how to prepare a poultice—bran mash and Epsom salts, wrapped in burlap and bound to the hoof with twine. Whisper stood patient through the process, occasionally nosing at Sage's shoulder as if to say *yes, this, thank you*.

"She'll need poulticing twice a day until it drains," Riley said. "I'll show you the wrapping tomorrow. You can take it on if you want."

"I want."

Rebecca appeared in the barn doorway as they were finishing. She looked at Whisper's wrapped hoof, then at Sage.

"Riley says you spotted the lameness from across the paddock."

"She was standing wrong. It was obvious."

"It wasn't obvious. I walked past her this morning and didn't see it." Rebecca's gaze was direct, assessing. "Good eye."

She left without further comment. That was Rebecca's way—observation delivered, no elaboration needed.

That evening, Jennifer set a plate in front of Sage at supper and said, "Rebecca told me about Whisper. You don't miss much, do you?"

It wasn't quite a compliment. It wasn't quite a question. Sage felt the truth of it settle somewhere uncomfortable—the reason she didn't miss much, the years of scanning every room and every face for the first sign of danger. The skill that had kept her alive in Bath and was now, improbably, keeping a horse sound.

"I suppose I don't," she said.

***

**Day Twenty-One**

Three weeks. Sage marked the anniversary of her arrival at breakfast, surprised by how different she felt. How much had changed in twenty-one days.

The bruises were completely gone now. She could see it in the morning mirror—no mark, no shadow. The visible evidence healed.

Her body had changed too. Stronger. The riding and farm work had put muscle on her arms, her legs, her back. She moved differently—more confidently, taking up space without apology. The trousers no longer felt scandalous when she put them on. They felt practical. Right.

And she could post the trot. Not perfectly—she still lost the rhythm sometimes, still bounced ungracefully when she got tired. But she could do it. Could rise and fall with Clover's movement, could feel the two-beat rhythm and match it, could trot full circuits of the paddock without falling apart.

"You're ready to try the canter soon," Riley said after that morning's lesson. "Another week or two of trotting, then we'll introduce it."

The canter. Sage had watched Rebecca ride one of the other horses at canter—the three-beat gait that looked like flying, smooth and rolling and fast. The thought was both terrifying and thrilling.

That evening, she sat by Promise's paddock in the golden late-April light. The air smelled of growing things, of grass and apple blossoms and the particular sweetness of spring in the Cotswolds. Three weeks of this work. Three weeks of showing up, sitting down, asking nothing.

Promise waited at the rails.

Not six feet back. Not five feet. Right at the fence, his dark head hanging over it slightly, ears forward, watching her with unmistakable interest.

Five feet. He'd moved five feet closer over the course of three weeks. Inch by inch, day by day, a distance that could be crossed in two human steps but had taken him twenty-one days to travel.

Sage's breath caught in her throat. She wanted to move toward him, to close the distance, to reach out. But she held perfectly still. This was his choice. His pace. She was just the constant, the boring fixture he'd learned was safe.

They looked at each other across the five feet of space.

Promise lowered his head and tore up a mouthful of grass. Chewed. Swallowed. Raised his head again to look at her.

Then he took one step forward. Another. A third.

He was at the fence. Right at it. Close enough that Sage could see the individual whiskers on his muzzle, the depth of his dark eyes, the small scars scattered across his face and neck.

Close enough to touch, if she dared. If he allowed it.

But she didn't reach out. Didn't move at all. Just sat there, holding his gaze, letting him make all the decisions.

After a long moment, Promise huffed through his nostrils—a soft sound, not quite friendly but not hostile either. Acknowledgment, maybe. Or just processing this new proximity.

Then he turned and walked away, back to the middle of his paddock, resuming his grazing as though nothing significant had happened.

But something had happened. Something enormous.

He'd crossed the last five feet. Had stood at the fence where she could touch him, had looked at her without panic, had chosen to be that close.

***

That night at supper, Sage told them. "Promise came to the fence today. All the way to it. He just stood there, looking at me."

Riley set down a fork. "Three weeks."

"Is that fast?"

"Could have taken three months. Could have taken three years." Riley glanced at Rebecca. "With some horses, it never happens."

Rebecca nodded once, the way she did when data confirmed a hypothesis. "You've had a useful week. The abscess catch, and now this."

It was, Sage thought, the most Rebecca thing anyone had ever said—equating a horse's trust with a competent hoof inspection. But she understood what Rebecca meant. Both were the result of the same thing: showing up, paying attention, and letting the watching become something more than watching.
