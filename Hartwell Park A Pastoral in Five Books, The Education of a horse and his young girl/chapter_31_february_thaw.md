# Chapter 31: February Thaw
**Book IV: Winter — Reflection**
**POV: Sage**
**Mid-February 1814**

The change came so gradually that Sage almost missed it. One morning she walked to the barn and realized the air didn't bite quite so hard. The next day she noticed water dripping from the stable eaves—ice melting, though ice still crusted the water troughs. By the end of the week, the first brave snowdrops had pushed through the frozen ground near the garden wall, their white heads nodding in a breeze that felt almost gentle.

"February thaw," Jennifer said, watching Sage examine the tiny flowers. "It happens most years. A few weeks of softness before winter remembers itself and comes back for one last bite."

"It feels like hope," Sage said.

"It is hope. Just don't trust it too far. March can be cruel."

But even Jennifer's caution couldn't dim the sense of awakening that had settled over Hartwell. The horses felt it too—Promise had been particularly energetic during their morning ride, breaking into an eager canter without being asked, his whole body saying *spring spring something is coming*.

Six weeks until the fair. The number had begun to feel real in a way it hadn't before.

---

Rebecca called a meeting in her office that afternoon, the four of them crowded into the small space among the ledgers and records. The fire in the tiny grate barely warmed the room, but no one complained. There was too much to discuss.

"Let's start with the sale horses," Rebecca said, consulting her notes — a sheet of paper covered in her small, precise handwriting, each horse's name followed by numbered points. She was in her element here, surrounded by her systems, the conversation structured around information she'd already organized in her mind. "Marcus?"

"Ready." Riley spoke with quiet confidence. "He's settled into himself over the winter. Good honest movement, responsive to the aids, no vices. He'll make someone an excellent hunter or hack."

"Oliver?"

"More than ready. He could have sold last autumn if we'd had a buyer. Solid, reliable, nothing flashy but nothing wrong either. The kind of horse that sells himself."

Rebecca nodded, making notes. "Belle?"

Sage felt eyes turn to her. Belle had been her project through the winter—patient work with the nervous filly, building confidence one small piece at a time.

"She's not ready," Sage admitted. "She's better—much better than she was. But she still spooks at unexpected things, and crowds would overwhelm her. Another season, maybe autumn, and she'll be there."

"Good assessment." Rebecca's approval warmed Sage more than the fire. "It's important to know when a horse isn't ready, not just when they are. Selling a horse before they're truly prepared damages our reputation more than waiting."

"That leaves Promise," Jennifer said quietly.

The room shifted, attention focusing. Promise wasn't a sale horse, but his showing at the fair mattered more than any sale—for Hartwell's reputation, for their financial future, for the statement it would make about Rebecca's methods.

"Two demonstrations," Rebecca said, consulting the numbered list she'd written out. "First, Sage rides him in the ladies' exhibition — sidesaddle, walk and trot, showing his manners and responsiveness. Gentle work, nothing flashy. Just showing that he can be trusted."

She paused, pen tapping once against the ledger edge before she placed it down parallel to the binding. "Second, I ride him over the fences. Sidesaddle — we know better than to put anyone on him astride after what happened with Riley." Her eyes moved briefly to Riley's shoulder, then back to her notes. "Show his movement, his scope, his athleticism. Prove he's more than a pretty walk."

The room was quiet for a moment. Then Riley shifted against the doorframe.

"He won't show for you."

Rebecca looked up. "I beg your pardon?"

"He'll tolerate you. He'll go where you point him. But he won't *show*." Riley's voice was careful but certain — the voice he used when he'd been thinking about something for a long time before speaking. "I've watched you school him over the lower fences this month. He jumps clean because you're a good enough rider to make him. But he's flat. Ears back, neck braced, doing the job because you're asking, not because he wants to. Anyone who knows horses will see the difference."

Rebecca's jaw tightened, but she didn't interrupt. She was listening the way she listened to a horse — completely, without the social impulse to defend.

"Now watch him with Sage," Riley continued. "Ears forward. Neck soft. He lengthens his stride before the fence because he *wants* to jump, not because she's driving him to it. That's the horse we need people to see. The horse who chooses the work."

"Sage has never jumped at speed," Rebecca said. "She's never ridden a course at anything above a hunting pace. The fair fences are higher than—"

"She's ready." Riley said it without hesitation. "Her seat is better than half the men who'll be riding that day. She's been over every fence on this estate at a canter, and Promise has never once thought about stopping with her. You've said yourself her balance is exceptional."

"Exceptional for her level of experience. That's not the same as—"

"Rebecca." Riley straightened from the doorframe. "You trained her. Are you telling me you didn't train her well enough?"

A beat of silence. Jennifer made a small sound that might have been a suppressed laugh, quickly hidden behind her teacup.

Rebecca's eyes narrowed, but something shifted in her expression — the particular look she got when a logical chain she hadn't considered rearranged itself in her mind. She turned to Sage.

"Can you do this? And I don't want courage — I want honesty. The fences at the fair are higher than anything you've jumped. The speed required to be impressive — the kind of speed that makes people think 'stud prospect' rather than 'pleasant hack' — is faster than anything you've ridden. If you fall in front of three hundred people, it's not just your pride that suffers. It's Hartwell's reputation."

Sage's heart was hammering. She hadn't expected this — hadn't expected Riley to argue for her, hadn't expected to be sitting in this small warm office deciding whether she was brave enough or foolish enough to say yes.

"I don't know if I can," she said. "But I know Promise will give me everything he has. And I don't think he'll give that to anyone else."

Rebecca studied her for a long moment — that too-direct gaze that no longer made Sage flinch. Then she looked at Riley, who met her eyes steadily, and at Jennifer, who gave a small nod.

"Six weeks," Rebecca said finally. She picked up her pen and added a new line to her numbered list, the handwriting small and precise. "We have six weeks to turn 'I don't know' into 'yes.' That means training every day the weather allows. Fences, speed work, course practice. I'll design the progression." She looked up. "If at any point I decide you're not ready, we revert to my riding him. No arguments. Agreed?"

"Agreed," Sage said.

"Good." Rebecca's pen was already moving, sketching a training schedule in the margin. "We start tomorrow."

---

After the meeting, Sage caught Riley in the corridor.

"Why did you do that?"

Riley turned, one hand on the banister. "Do what?"

"Argue for me. You could have said nothing. Rebecca would have ridden him herself and it would have been fine."

"It wouldn't have been fine. It would have been adequate." Riley came back down the two steps he'd climbed, standing level with her. "There's a difference between a horse doing as he's told and a horse showing what he is. Promise does as he's told for Rebecca. He shows what he is for you."

"But if I'm not good enough—"

"You're good enough." Riley said it the way he said most things — quietly, without ornament, as if stating something he'd observed rather than something he believed. "I've watched you ride every day for months. I've seen your seat over fences, the way you feel his stride and adjust without thinking. You're ready, Sage. You just don't know it yet."

Sage leaned against the wall, the plaster cool through her dress. "And if Rebecca decides I'm not? She said she'd pull me."

"Then she pulls you, and she rides him herself, and it's adequate instead of extraordinary." Riley's eyes were steady. "But she won't pull you. She knows what I know. She just needed someone to say it out loud."

---

Sage went to the barn. She needed to see Promise, to touch him, to feel the reality of his warm body and steady presence. The fair had been abstract for months — something distant, something to prepare for eventually. Now it was concrete. Six weeks. Forty-two days.

Promise was dozing in his stall, one hind leg cocked, his head drooping. He lifted it when she appeared, ears pricking forward, and gave his soft welcoming nicker.

"Hello, beautiful." She let herself into his stall, not bothering with halter or lead. Just being with him, the way she'd done in those early days when being with him was all she could offer.

He pushed his nose against her shoulder. *I see you. You see me.*

"Riley thinks we can do this," she told him, running her hand down the flat plane of his face. "Jump fences at the fair. In front of hundreds of people. Show them what you are."

Promise blew out a breath, warm against her palm. His body was soft, easy — present in the way he was only ever present with her. The horse who leaned into her hand. The horse who followed her voice. The horse who chose her, every single day, and would keep choosing her as long as she kept showing up.

"I'm terrified," she admitted. "But I think he's right. I think we're ready."

Promise lowered his head and rested it against her chest — the heavy, trusting weight of him, given freely. Sage wrapped her arms around his neck and breathed.

---

The days that followed were the most demanding of Sage's life.

Rebecca set up a training course in the lower field—fences that started low and grew higher as the weeks progressed. Sage rode Promise over them again and again, learning the feel of his jump, the moment of suspension, the landing that came faster at higher speeds. She learned to fold forward over the leaping head, to keep her weight balanced despite the asymmetry of sidesaddle, to grip with muscles she hadn't known she had.

She fell. Of course she fell. The first time was on the third day, when Promise took a fence bigger than she expected and she came off his shoulder, landing hard in the soft dirt of the arena. She lay there for a moment, wind knocked out of her, staring at the winter sky.

Riley was there before she could move, dropping to his knees beside her. "Sage—"

"I'm fine." She wasn't sure if it was true, but she said it anyway, pushing herself up. Her hip ached. Her pride ached more. "I'm fine. Help me up."

"You should rest—"

"I should get back on." Sage accepted Riley's hand, letting him pull her to her feet. Promise had stopped a few lengths away, looking back at her with worried ears. "He needs to see that falling doesn't mean stopping. That's what Rebecca taught him. That's what she taught me."

Riley's face was tight with concern, but he didn't argue. He helped Sage back into the saddle and stepped back, watching as she gathered her reins and turned Promise back toward the fence.

This time, she stayed on.

---

The evenings were harder than the days.

Sage would return to the house exhausted, muscles screaming, and sit in the parlor unable to think about anything except the next morning's training. Riley sat with her, bringing tea she barely tasted, offering quiet company that asked for nothing.

"I keep thinking about whether I was right," Riley said one night, when Sage was so tired she could barely keep her eyes open. "Pushing for this. Putting you in the ring instead of Rebecca."

"You were right." Sage stared into the fire, watching the flames dance. "Promise gave me everything. His trust, his partnership, his healing. The least I owe him is everything I have in return."

"I don't want you to get hurt because of something I said in a meeting."

"It's not because of something you said." Sage turned to look at Riley, at the worry etched into his face. "It's about... when I came here, I didn't believe I was worth anything. I was broken. Used up. Damaged beyond repair — just like everyone said about Promise." She reached for Riley's hand. "He proved them wrong about himself. Now I have to prove them wrong about me."

"You already have. You don't need a horse race to prove your worth."

"Maybe not to you. Maybe not to the people here." Sage squeezed Riley's fingers. "But I need to prove it to myself. I need to know that I can be brave when it matters. That I can rise to something instead of shrinking from it."

Riley was quiet for a moment. Then he lifted Sage's hand to his lips and pressed a kiss to her knuckles.

"Then I'll be there," he said. "At the fair. Cheering louder than anyone. And if you fall, I'll be the first one over the fence to help you up."

"I won't fall."

"I know. But if you do."

Sage leaned into Riley's shoulder, letting his warmth seep into her aching body. "You believed in me before I believed in myself. That counts for something."

"It counts for everything." Riley's arm came around her, pulling her close. "And if you fall, I'll be the first one over the fence to help you back on. That's what we do here, isn't it? We try again."

"That's what we do," Sage agreed. And closed her eyes, and let herself be held.

---

By the fourth week, Sage could take the full course at something approaching race speed. Not fast enough to win against experienced riders, Rebecca said, but fast enough to be impressive. Fast enough to make people notice.

"The goal isn't to beat anyone," Rebecca reminded her. "The goal is to show Promise's capabilities. If you complete the course cleanly—all fences cleared, no refusals, no falls—that's a success regardless of time."

"But faster is better."

"Faster catches eyes. But clean catches respect." Rebecca watched as Sage walked Promise through the course, cooling him after their latest practice run. "You're doing well. Better than I expected, honestly."

"That's not exactly a compliment."

"It is, actually. I expected you to be good. You're exceeding good." Rebecca's rare smile appeared. "Promise believes in you. I can see it in how he takes the fences—he's not hesitating, not second-guessing. He trusts that you'll be there with him."

Sage stroked Promise's neck, feeling the warmth of him beneath her hand. "He's always trusted me."

"No. He learned to trust you. There's a difference." Rebecca fell into step beside them. "Trust isn't something horses give automatically. It's something they offer when they've been given reason to. You gave him reasons, Sage. Day after day, month after month. You earned what he gives you now."

"And now I have to be worthy of it."

"You already are. The fair is just proving it to everyone else."

---

The night before the final week of training, Sage couldn't sleep.

She lay in her bed, staring at the ceiling, running through the course in her mind. First fence, second fence, the turn that came fast, the combination that required perfect timing. She could see it all, feel it all, the rhythm of Promise's stride and the moment of leaving the ground and the heart-stopping instant of suspension before landing.

What if she fell? What if she failed him? What if all this training wasn't enough, and she came apart at the crucial moment, and Promise's future was ruined because she wasn't good enough?

The fears circled like crows, black and insistent.

A soft knock came at her door.

"Sage?" Riley's voice, quiet. "I saw your light. Can I come in?"

Sage pushed herself up. "Yes."

Riley slipped through the door, closing it softly behind him. He was in his nightclothes, a shawl wrapped around his shoulders against the February chill.

"I couldn't sleep either," he admitted, settling on the edge of Sage's bed. "I keep thinking about the fair."

"Me too."

"Are you scared?"

Sage considered lying. Decided against it. "Terrified."

"Good." Riley's hand found hers in the darkness. "I'd be worried if you weren't. Fear means you understand what's at stake."

"What if I'm not good enough? What if I fail him?"

"Then you fail him." Riley's voice was gentle but honest. "And we figure out what comes next. But Sage—you're not going to fail him. I've watched you train. I've seen how you've pushed yourself. You're ready for this."

"You don't know that."

"No. But I believe it." Riley shifted closer, his warmth a comfort against the cold night. "I believe in you. Promise believes in you. Rebecca believes in you. At some point, you're going to have to believe in yourself."

Sage felt tears prick at her eyes. "What if I can't?"

"Then believe in us believing in you. Borrow our faith until you find your own." Riley's fingers intertwined with hers. "That's what you did with Promise, isn't it? You believed in him when he couldn't believe in himself. You held the hope for both of you until he learned to hope on his own."

"And now he's teaching me to do the same thing."

"Now you're teaching yourself. He's just reminding you it's possible."

They sat together in the darkness, hands clasped, breathing in sync. Outside, the February night was cold and still. Inside, something warm kindled in Sage's chest—not confidence, exactly, but something adjacent to it. The willingness to try. The courage to risk.

"Stay," she said quietly. "Not... just stay. I don't want to be alone tonight."

Riley didn't hesitate. He pulled back the covers and slid in beside her, their bodies fitting together like pieces of a puzzle. Sage felt the steady rhythm of Riley's heartbeat against her back, the warmth of his breath on her neck.

"Whatever happens," Riley whispered, "I'm proud of you."

Sage closed her eyes. The fears were still there, circling, but they seemed smaller now. Less absolute. 

Tomorrow, she would train again. In a few weeks, she would ride Promise over fences at speed in front of hundreds of people, with Hartwell's future resting on her shoulders. It was terrifying.

But she wasn't alone. And that made all the difference.

---

The thaw held through the end of February, the days mild enough to train outside, the evenings soft enough to ride out across the estate. Sage took every opportunity to school Promise over fences, to practice the course, to build the partnership they would need at the fair.

She was ready. Or as ready as six weeks could make her.

"My mother asked about you," Riley said one evening as they walked the horses back toward the barn. "About your intentions."

"My intentions?" Sage laughed, despite her exhaustion. "That sounds very formal."

"She's a formal person, under all the warmth." Riley's smile was fond. "She wanted to know if you planned to stay. At Hartwell, I mean. After..."

"After what?"

"After the fair. After whatever comes next." Riley's voice was careful, as if the words might shatter something fragile. "She wanted to know if Hartwell was temporary for you, or permanent."

Sage considered the question. A year ago, she couldn't have answered it. A year ago, she'd barely believed she had a future at all. Now...

"Permanent," she said. "If you'll have me."

Riley's breath caught. "Sage—"

"I mean it. This is my home. You're my... you're everything." The words felt inadequate, too small for the enormity of what she felt. "I don't know what happens at the fair. I don't know if I'll succeed or fail. But I know I want my life to be here. With you. With all of you."

Riley pulled Clover to a halt, and Sage stopped Promise beside her. For a moment they sat in silence, the horses breathing quietly, the evening light soft around them.

"I'll have you," Riley said finally. "For as long as you want to be had."

"That might be forever."

"I'm counting on it."

They rode the rest of the way home in comfortable silence, but their hands stayed linked across the space between horses, and when they reached the barn, neither was in any hurry to let go.

---

That night, lying in her bed with the mild February air drifting through her cracked window, Sage thought about everything that had changed since she came to Hartwell. The terrified girl who'd arrived in Rebecca's carriage seemed like a stranger now—someone she'd known once, felt compassion for, but no longer was.

She thought about Promise, who had made the same journey from brokenness to belonging. She thought about the fair, now only weeks away, where she would ride him over fences at speed—sidesaddle, because that was what he needed, because meeting him where he was had always been the point.

She thought about Riley, warm beside her in the darkness, dreaming whatever dreams awaited.

And she thought about the future—not with fear, not with the old paralyzing terror, but with something that felt remarkably like readiness. She might fail. She might fall. But she would try, and trying was more than she'd ever believed herself capable of.

Spring was coming. She could feel it in the air, see it in the snowdrops pushing through frozen ground, hear it in the birds beginning to return. Winter's long dark was ending, and what grew in its absence was something worth waiting for.

She closed her eyes and let sleep take her.
