# Chapter 29: The Long Dark
**Book IV: Winter — Reflection**
**POV: Sage**
**January 1814**

January pressed down on Hartwell like a weight. The days were short and grey, dawn arriving late and dusk falling early, the hours of true daylight barely enough to accomplish the necessary work. Sage found herself rising in darkness and returning to the house in darkness, the brief bright interval between feeling almost like a dream.

The cold had deepened too, moving past the crisp freshness of December into something harder and meaner. Ice formed on the water troughs overnight and had to be broken each morning. The horses grew shaggier, their winter coats thick and plush, and steam rose from their bodies in great clouds whenever they moved.

Promise seemed unbothered by any of it. He'd adapted to winter life with the same steady pragmatism he'd brought to everything else in his healing—accepting the cold, the darkness, the changed routines as simply the way things were now. When Sage arrived at his stall each morning, stamping feeling back into her frozen feet, he greeted her with the same soft nicker he always gave, nose pushing against her shoulder as if to say *you're here, finally, I've been waiting.*

"I know," she'd tell him, producing an apple from her pocket. "I always come."

And she always did. That was the thing about the work—it didn't stop for weather or mood or the dragging weight of winter darkness. The horses needed feeding and watering and exercising regardless of how anyone felt about the cold. There was something almost comforting in that constancy, Sage had found. When the days felt endless and grey, the work gave them shape.

---

The morning chores were finished by the time the sun finally crested the hills, weak and watery but welcome nonetheless. Sage stood in the barn aisle, warming her hands around a cup of tea Riley had brought from the house, watching the pale light creep across the yard.

"Marcus is coming along well," Riley said, joining her with his own cup. "Another few weeks and he'll be ready for anything the fair can throw at him."

They'd been working the nervous gelding steadily through the winter, building his confidence with varied experiences—different routes, different weather, different companions. He'd progressed from skittish to uncertain to cautiously willing, and lately had begun to show flashes of genuine enthusiasm for his work.

"He trusts you now," Sage said. "I can see it when you ride him. He looks to you for reassurance instead of looking for things to be afraid of."

"That's the goal, isn't it? Replace fear with trust." Riley's shoulder brushed against hers as they stood together. "Same as with Promise. Same as with..."

They didn't finish the sentence, but Sage heard the unspoken words anyway. *Same as with you.*

"Can I ask you something?" Sage said.

"Always."

"Your mother said you turned down three marriage proposals. Before I came here." Sage stared into her tea, not quite brave enough to meet Riley's eyes. "Why?"

Riley was quiet for a long moment. Down the aisle, Thunder shifted in his stall, his grey head appearing over the door to watch them.

"Because none of them saw me," Riley said finally. "They saw a young woman who was good with horses. They saw someone who could be useful, who could help run their farms or impress their friends. They saw a problem to be solved—a woman who lived unconventionally and needed to be brought back to proper society." A bitter note crept into his voice. "None of them saw *me*. What I wanted, who I was, what kind of life I was building."

"What kind of life were you building?"

"This one." Riley gestured with his cup, taking in the barn, the horses, the pale winter light. "A life where I could do work I loved with people who respected me. Where I didn't have to pretend to be something I wasn't. Where I could..."

"Could what?"

Riley turned to face her, dark eyes serious. "Where I could love who I loved without having to hide it or be ashamed of it."

The words hung in the cold air between them, visible almost as the clouds of their breath.

"Your mother and Rebecca," Sage said softly.

"They showed me it was possible. A life built on partnership and respect, on shared work and shared values. No one tells them who to be or how to live. They made their own rules." Riley's voice was fierce now, the passion that usually hid beneath his calm surface breaking through. "I wanted that. I still want that."

"With me?"

The question came out smaller than Sage intended, almost a whisper. Riley's expression softened.

"With you. If you want it. If that's something you can imagine wanting." He set down his tea and took both of Sage's hands in his. "I know you came here broken. I know you're still healing. I'm not asking you to be anything you're not ready to be. But I need you to know—what I feel for you isn't confusion or convenience. It isn't because you're here and available. I love you, Sage. I think I've loved you since I watched you sit by that paddock fence day after day, refusing to give up on a horse everyone else had abandoned."

Sage's breath caught. "Riley—"

"You don't have to say it back. You don't have to say anything. I just needed you to know."

But Sage found she did have something to say. It had been building in her for months, through the autumn's crisis and the winter's quiet, through snow rides and Christmas gifts and the slow accumulation of moments that added up to something she hadn't dared name.

"I'm afraid," she admitted. "I'm afraid of what I feel because it's so much bigger than anything I've felt before. I'm afraid that if I let myself love you completely, I'll lose myself in it. I'm afraid that I don't know how to be loved without being hurt."

Riley's hands tightened on hers. "I would never hurt you."

"I know. I know that here." Sage touched her temple. "But here—" She moved her hand to her heart. "Here still remembers what it was like before. What it was like to trust someone and have that trust destroyed."

"Promise trusted again," Riley said quietly. "After everything that was done to him. He learned that not everyone would hurt him."

"Promise had me. Someone patient enough to wait, to let him come to trust in his own time."

"You have me." Riley raised Sage's hands to his lips and pressed a kiss to her knuckles, gentle and deliberate. "I'm patient. I've been patient my whole life, waiting for something I wasn't sure existed. I can wait a little longer for you to feel safe."

The tears came before Sage could stop them—not sobs, just a quiet overflow of something too big to contain. Riley pulled her close, wrapping arms around her, and Sage buried her face in the rough wool of Riley's coat.

"I do love you," she whispered into that wool. "I'm just still learning what that means."

"We'll learn together." Riley's voice was rough with emotion. "Same as everything else around here."

They stood like that for a long time, wrapped together in the cold barn with the horses watching curiously and the pale winter light slowly strengthening around them. Down the aisle, Promise nickered softly—whether at the unusual stillness of his humans or simply requesting attention, Sage couldn't tell.

"Your horse is demanding acknowledgment," Riley murmured into her hair.

Sage laughed, a wet and shaky sound. "He always is."

"Can't blame him. He's got good instincts about when you need to be interrupted."

They separated reluctantly, Sage wiping her face with her sleeve. Riley's eyes were bright too, though he'd deny it if asked.

"Back to work?" Sage said.

"Back to work. The horses don't care about our emotional breakthroughs."

But Riley caught her hand as they walked toward Promise's stall, and didn't let go until they absolutely had to.

---

The afternoon brought the kind of work that Sage had grown to love—the patient, repetitive tasks of training that built trust one small piece at a time. She worked with Belle, the nervous filly who still needed another season before she'd be ready for sale, asking nothing more than calm acceptance of grooming and handling.

Belle had come to Hartwell as a yearling, sold by a farmer who found her "too flighty" to bother with. In truth, she was simply sensitive—alert to every sound and movement, easily overwhelmed by too much stimulus. With patient work, that sensitivity could become an asset rather than a liability. A horse that noticed everything could become a horse that responded to the lightest aids, if she learned that noticing didn't have to mean fearing.

"That's it," Sage murmured as Belle stood for the brush, only flinching slightly when the wind rattled the barn door. "Nothing to worry about. Just the wind being rude."

Promise watched from his stall, head over his door, as Sage worked with the filly. Sometimes Sage thought he was supervising—making sure she was doing it right, or perhaps remembering his own journey from fear to trust.

"You were worse than her, you know," she told him when she finished with Belle and came to his stall. "At least she lets people approach. You wouldn't let anyone within ten feet for weeks."

Promise snorted, as if offended by the comparison.

"It's true. You were a disaster when you got here." She scratched behind his ears, feeling him lean into the touch. "Now look at you. Distinguished gentleman stallion, soon to be famous stud horse. Very impressive."

He pushed his nose against her shoulder. *I see you. You see me. We're here together.*

"Always," she agreed. "Always."

---

Evening came early, as it did in January, and the household gathered in the parlor while the darkness pressed against the windows. Jennifer had made soup, thick and warming, and they ate in comfortable silence while the fire crackled.

"The farrier will come Tuesday," Rebecca said eventually. "Promise needs his feet trimmed before the fair preparations begin in earnest."

"Already?" Sage looked up. "It's only January."

"It's nearly February. The fair is the first week of April. That's barely two months to get everything ready—the horses polished, the tack cleaned, the logistics sorted. Time moves faster than you'd think."

Two months. It seemed both impossibly soon and infinitely far away. So much had happened since Sage arrived at Hartwell—could it really be almost a year? And yet in other ways, she felt like she'd always been here, like the life before had happened to someone else entirely.

"Are you nervous?" Jennifer asked gently. "About the fair?"

"Terrified," Sage admitted. "But also... excited? Is that strange?"

"Not at all." Jennifer smiled. "That's how all the best things feel. The things worth doing are always a little frightening."

"Love is like that," Riley said quietly, and their eyes met Sage's across the room. "Frightening and exciting and absolutely worth it."

Rebecca looked between them, a knowing expression crossing her face. "Well. It seems January hasn't been entirely about darkness after all."
