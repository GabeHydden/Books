# CHAPTER 34: SALES DAY

Morning brought watery sunshine and the smell of frying bacon drifting from the inn's kitchen. Sage had slept poorly—the unfamiliar bed, the noise from the yard below, the constant awareness that Promise was out there in a strange stall surrounded by strange horses. She'd checked on him twice in the night, both times finding him awake but calm, his eye reflecting the lantern light as he watched her approach.

Now, with dawn fully broken and the fair stirring to life around them, there was work to do.

Riley had already gone to Fair Hill by the time Sage came down. Rebecca sat in the inn's parlor with a cup of tea, reviewing a list of potential buyers she'd compiled from correspondence over the winter. Jennifer was arranging for their midday meal, her practical nature finding comfort in logistics.

"Marcus first," Rebecca said without preamble. "He's the one who needs the right match. Oliver will sell himself to anyone with eyes."

They walked together through the awakening fair—past traders setting up stalls, past horses being led to water, past the early crowds already gathering along the flashing lanes. The smell of horses and wood smoke and meat pies mingled in the cool morning air. Sage kept her eyes moving, watching for green and gold, but Thornton's colors were nowhere in sight.

At Fair Hill, Riley had Marcus and Oliver groomed and ready. Marcus stood with his head high, nostrils flaring at every new sight and sound, but his feet stayed planted. A year ago, he would have been dancing at the end of his lead rope, whites of his eyes showing. Now he was nervous but contained—uncertain to cautiously willing, as Rebecca had put it. Sage felt a swell of pride. She'd helped make him this way.

"He looks well," she said, running her hand down his neck.

"He's ready." Riley handed her Marcus's lead rope. "You should be the one to show him. You did most of the work."

The flashing lane was a broad stretch of packed earth running between two rows of spectators—buyers, dealers, curious onlookers, all watching as horses were trotted past to display their movement and temperament. It was, Sage realized, rather like being on stage. The horse's every step was scrutinized, every flick of the ear noted.

She led Marcus to the head of the lane, her heart beating faster than it should. This was just walking and trotting. She'd done this a hundred times at Hartwell.

But not with people watching. Not with Marcus's future depending on how he performed in the next few minutes.

"Easy, lad," she murmured. "Just like at home. Nothing to prove—just show them who you are."

She asked him to walk, and he walked. His stride was even, his head carriage natural, his eye soft despite the crowd. Halfway down the lane, she clucked him into a trot, and he responded without hesitation—forward but not rushing, balanced, light on his feet. A three-year-old gelding who had once spooked at his own shadow, now trotting calmly past dozens of strangers.

When she brought him back to the start, there were already two men waiting to speak with Rebecca.

The first was a farmer from Northleach, looking for a steady mount for his eldest son. He examined Marcus with rough but knowing hands, checking legs and teeth and hooves. His questions were practical: age, training, temperament around other horses, willingness to stand for the farrier.

The second was a younger man, a gentleman by his dress, who watched Marcus with a different kind of attention. "He was nervous when you started," he observed. "But he settled quickly. That's training, not just temperament."

"Both," Sage said. "He was born sensitive. We taught him that sensitivity didn't have to mean fear."

The gentleman—Mr. Ashby, he introduced himself—asked to see Marcus trot again. Then he asked to handle him himself, taking the lead rope and walking the gelding in circles, changing direction, asking for halt and walk transitions. Marcus responded to each request with the same calm willingness he'd shown Sage.

"How much?" Mr. Ashby asked Rebecca.

They negotiated while Sage stood with Marcus, her hand on his neck. The price they settled on was fair—more than fair, actually. Mr. Ashby clearly recognized quality when he saw it.

"I have a young wife," he said, as he counted out the notes. "She's learning to ride, but she's timid. I've been looking for a horse who won't take advantage of that—one who'll teach her rather than frighten her."

"He'll do that," Sage said, and her voice came out thick. She'd known this was coming. Had known since autumn that Marcus was being prepared for sale, that his whole purpose at Hartwell was to be trained and sold. But knowing and feeling were different things.

She handed the lead rope to Mr. Ashby, and Marcus went with him willingly, ears pricked forward with curiosity about this new human. He didn't look back. Horses rarely did—they lived in the present, not the past. It was only humans who carried their goodbyes with them.

"You did well," Riley said quietly, appearing at her shoulder. "He'll have a good life."

"I know." Sage wiped her eyes with the back of her hand. "It's just—I remember when he wouldn't even let me touch his face. And now he's going off with a stranger like it's nothing."

"That's not nothing. That's everything. That's exactly what we taught him." Riley's hand found hers, squeezed once. "Come on. Oliver's waiting."

---

Oliver's sale was easier, as Rebecca had predicted. He was a plain bay gelding with no flash and no drama—the kind of horse that would never turn heads but would never let you down either. Sage trotted him down the flashing lane, and he performed exactly as expected: steady, reliable, unbothered by the crowd or the noise or anything else the fair could throw at him.

An older farmer bought him within twenty minutes, barely haggling over price. "I've got a farm to run," the man said gruffly. "Don't need fancy. Need honest."

"He's the most honest horse I've ever known," Sage told him, and meant it.

Two sales before midday. Rebecca was pleased—Sage could see it in the set of her shoulders, the way she tucked the money carefully into her case. This was why they'd come. This was the business that kept Hartwell running.

It was Riley who mentioned Promise.

"You could flash him," they said, as the three of them walked back toward the Crown Inn. "Not to sell—just to show what he can do. Generate interest for the stud inquiries."

Sage considered it. Promise would be magnificent on the flashing lane, his black coat gleaming, his Arab-influenced head turning every eye. The trot that had taken months to unlock, floating and free, would draw gasps from the crowd. People would remember him.

But.

"No," she said. "Not yet."

Riley raised an eyebrow. "No?"

"We don't need to prove anything today. The exhibition tomorrow will show what he can do." Sage thought of Riley's fall, of Rebecca's words about pushing too fast, of all the lessons they'd learned about patience. "I'd rather save his energy and his trust for when it matters. The flashing lane is chaos—strange horses, crowds pressing close. He handled yesterday well, but every test costs something. I don't want to spend what we might need later."

Rebecca, walking ahead, turned back with something like approval in her expression. "Good. That's good thinking."

It was strange, Sage reflected, how restraint could feel harder than action. Everything in her wanted to show Promise off, to let the world see what they'd built together. But wisdom wasn't about what you could do. It was about what you chose not to.

---

The offer for Thunder came just before midday.

Riley had ridden him down to the river to water, and a gentleman had followed them back—a tall man in expensive clothes, with the bearing of someone accustomed to fine horses. He introduced himself as Lord Cavendish and asked, with the directness of the wealthy, whether the grey gelding was for sale.

"He wasn't meant to be," Rebecca said carefully. "He's our head groom's personal mount."

"I'll pay two hundred guineas."

The number hung in the air. Sage saw Jennifer's face go still. Two hundred guineas was more than Thunder was worth—more than almost any gelding was worth. It was enough to repair the barn roof, replace the aging carriage, put money aside for a difficult winter.

"That's very generous," Rebecca said, her voice giving nothing away. "May I ask what draws your interest?"

"I watched your groom ride him in yesterday." Lord Cavendish nodded toward Riley, who stood with Thunder's reins in hand, expression unreadable. "The horse is responsive without being dull, forward without being hot. And your groom sits him beautifully—which tells me the horse has been properly trained, not just broken to saddle."

Rebecca glanced at Jennifer. Something passed between them—a conversation without words, the kind that came from years of partnership.

"I'll need to discuss it with my household," Rebecca said. "Thunder has been with us for six years. He's not a decision to make lightly."

"Of course." Lord Cavendish inclined his head. "I'm staying at the Talbot. Send word by tomorrow evening if you're interested."

He walked away, and silence fell over their little group. Riley's hand had tightened on Thunder's reins, knuckles white.

"We don't have to," Jennifer said quietly. "The sales today were good. We'll manage."

"We always manage." Rebecca's voice was tired. "That's not the same as thriving."

Thunder stood between them, unconcerned with human economics, lipping at Riley's pocket in search of treats. He was twelve years old—past his prime but years of good work left in him. He'd taught Sage to be brave when she didn't know she could be. He'd carried Riley through countless miles of Cotswold lanes. He was part of Hartwell in a way that couldn't be measured in guineas.

But two hundred guineas was two hundred guineas.

"We'll talk tonight," Rebecca said finally. "After the exhibition planning. This isn't a decision to make in the middle of a fair."

---

Sage saw Thornton an hour later.

She'd gone to check on Promise, slipping into the cool dimness of the Crown's stable to find him dozing in his narrow stall. He woke at her approach, ears pricking forward, and she spent several minutes just standing with him—hand on his neck, breathing in his familiar smell, letting the tension of the morning drain away.

She was walking back across the inn yard when she heard the voice.

"Well. There she is. The girl who thinks she can train horses."

He was standing near the gate, blocking her path without quite blocking it—a large man in green coat and gold waistcoat, his face florid and his eyes cold. Sage knew him immediately, though they'd never met. The colors. The cruelty in the set of his mouth.

Marcus Thornton.

"Mr. Thornton." She kept her voice steady. "I don't believe we've been introduced."

"We haven't. But I know who you are. Rebecca Morrison's little project." His gaze moved over her with dismissive assessment. "I saw you flash that nervous gelding this morning. Decent enough work, I suppose. For a girl."

Sage said nothing. Her heart was pounding, but she'd learned from Promise—from all the horses—that showing fear only invited pursuit.

"I also saw what's in that stall." Thornton's voice dropped, and something ugly moved behind his eyes. "The black stallion. That's my horse."

"He belongs to Hartwell Park now. Mrs. Morrison purchased him legally."

"From a knacker's yard, I'm told. Because I had the good sense to send him there." Thornton stepped closer. He smelled of tobacco and something sour—spirits, perhaps, this early in the day. "That animal is dangerous. Vicious. He put three of my best riders in the surgeon's care before I gave up on him."

"He's never been vicious at Hartwell. Only frightened."

Thornton laughed—a harsh, barking sound. "Frightened. That's what your Mrs. Morrison tells herself, is it? That every horse is just misunderstood?" He leaned in, close enough that Sage could see the broken veins on his nose. "I've been breaking horses for thirty years, girl. I know what's in that animal's nature. You can gentle him down, make him look pretty for the ladies, but underneath? He's still the horse who nearly killed my men. And sooner or later, he'll show it."

"Then you won't mind watching the exhibition tomorrow." Sage was surprised by how calm her voice sounded, when everything inside her was screaming. "You can see for yourself what patience and kindness have built."

Something flickered in Thornton's face—surprise, perhaps, that she'd pushed back. Then his expression hardened.

"I'll be watching," he said. "And when that horse shows his true nature—when he throws you, or bolts, or does whatever it is that vicious animals do—I'll be there to remind everyone that I warned them." He straightened, adjusting his coat. "Enjoy your exhibition, Miss Morrison. I expect it will be... educational."

He walked away without waiting for a response, his heavy boots loud on the cobblestones.

Sage stood in the inn yard, trembling, until Riley found her there ten minutes later.

"What happened? You look—"

"Thornton." The name tasted like ash. "He knows about Promise. He says he'll be watching tomorrow."

Riley's expression darkened. "Let him watch. Let him see exactly what his methods couldn't accomplish and ours did."

"He said Promise is dangerous. That he'll show his true nature eventually."

"Promise's true nature," Riley said quietly, "is what you've spent a year uncovering. Thornton only ever saw what his cruelty created."

They stood together in the yard, the fair's noise washing around them. Somewhere out there, Lord Cavendish was waiting for an answer about Thunder. Somewhere, Marcus was settling into his new life with Mr. Ashby. And tomorrow, Promise would face a crowd that included the man who had broken him.

"We should tell Rebecca," Sage said.

"Yes." Riley took her hand. "Come on. Whatever comes next, we face it together."
