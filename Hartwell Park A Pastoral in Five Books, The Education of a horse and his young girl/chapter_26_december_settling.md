# Chapter 26: December Settling
**Book IV: Winter — Reflection**
**POV: Sage**
**Early December 1813**

Winter had wrapped itself around Hartwell Park like a familiar blanket, cold but not unkind. Sage stood at the kitchen window watching the first real frost of December silver the paddock fences, her hands curled around a cup of tea that steamed in the early morning chill.

Behind her, Jennifer moved through the familiar rhythm of breakfast preparation—porridge bubbling, bread slicing, the comfortable sounds of a household waking. From somewhere deeper in the house came the murmur of Rebecca's voice, probably already planning the day's work.

This was home now. Not just refuge, not just the place she'd landed after everything fell apart. Home, in the way that word was supposed to mean.

"You're up early," Jennifer observed, setting a bowl of porridge at Sage's usual place.

"Promise was calling." Sage smiled, turning from the window. "I could hear him from my room—he knows when the house starts moving."

It was true. The black stallion had developed an uncanny sense for the household's rhythms, nickering from his stall the moment the first footsteps sounded on the stairs. He'd moved inside for winter, and the closer quarters had only strengthened his awareness of the humans who cared for him.

"He's settled well," Jennifer said. "I remember when he first arrived—wouldn't eat if anyone was within sight. Now he nickers the moment he hears footsteps, wanting his breakfast and his company."

"He's not spoiled. He's..." Sage searched for the right word. "Certain. He knows what he wants now. And he knows he'll get it."

"That's healing, isn't it? Knowing what you want. Being able to ask for it. Trusting the answer will be yes."

Sage considered this as she ate her porridge. Eight months ago, Promise hadn't known how to want anything except escape from pain. Now he had opinions about everything—which corner of his paddock caught the best morning sun, which treats he preferred, which grooming spots made him lean into her hands with blissful abandon.

She had opinions now too, she realized. About training, about horses, about what kind of life she wanted to build. That felt like healing as much as anything else.

---

The morning routine had become as familiar as breathing. Sage pulled on her warmest coat—a practical wool thing Rebecca had found in storage, sized for a woman who worked rather than one who merely looked decorative—and crossed the frost-crunchy yard to the stables.

Inside, warmth enveloped her—the heat of horse bodies, hay, the small stove Riley kept burning in the tack room on the coldest days. The barn smelled of horses and grain and the particular sweetness of good hay, scents that had become as comforting as the kitchen's bread and woodsmoke.

From the main stable came the sounds of horses stirring—soft nickers, the rustle of hay, hooves shifting on straw. The three day-workers would arrive soon to muck stalls and haul water, but the core work of Hartwell happened through the hands of those who lived here.

Sage collected Promise's halter from its hook outside his stall. He'd moved inside for the winter two weeks ago—Rebecca's decision, once it became clear he was settled enough to handle the closer quarters. His stall was at the end of the row, with Thunder on one side and an empty stall on the other, giving him space while still making him part of the barn's community.

He was waiting at his door when she arrived, head over the half-gate, ears pricked forward. The nicker he gave her was soft and familiar, nothing like the terrified silence of his early days.

"Good morning, beautiful," she said, producing an apple from her pocket.

He took it with the careful gentleness he'd learned, then pushed his nose against her shoulder in that gesture that had become their private language. *I see you. You see me. We're here together.*

"I see you too," she murmured, rubbing behind his ears.

She let herself into his stall and buckled on his halter while he stood quiet, no trace of the terror that had once accompanied any equipment. When she led him out into the aisle, Riley was already there with Thunder, cross-tying the grey gelding for grooming.

"Morning," Riley said. "You want Thunder or Clover after Promise?"

"I'll take Clover. You did her yesterday."

This was how mornings worked now—not Riley doing the horses while Sage focused only on Promise, but the two of them dividing the work between them. Sage had earned her place in the rotation, knew each horse's quirks and preferences, could be trusted with Thunder's occasional testing or Whisper's sensitive mouth or Marcus's nervous moments.

They worked in comfortable rhythm, the barn filling with the sounds of curry combs and brushes, the soft conversations people have with horses. Sage groomed Promise, then moved to Clover's stall while Riley finished Thunder and started on Marcus. When the day-workers arrived to muck stalls and fill water buckets, Sage and Riley had already moved on to the young horses—Belle, Oliver, the others who needed handling to prepare them for their futures.

It was during this quiet work that Rebecca appeared, her practical boots ringing on the stone floor, a ledger tucked under her arm.

"Sage. When you're finished with Promise, I'd like you to join me in the office. We need to discuss the spring fair."

The spring fair. Sage had heard it mentioned in passing—the annual county event where Hartwell's horses went to find new homes—but the details had remained vague. Now, apparently, it was time to make them concrete.

---

Rebecca's office was a small room off the main stable, barely big enough for a desk, two chairs, and the towering shelves of records she'd accumulated over fifteen years of running Hartwell. The desk itself was immaculate — ledgers stacked by size, ink pot in its exact center, pen laid parallel to the book's edge. Everything in its place, the way Rebecca's physical world always was. Sage settled into the worn leather chair across from Rebecca's desk, trying not to feel like she was being called before a headmistress.

"The spring fair is the first week of April," Rebecca began without preamble, opening her ledger. She was more at ease here than Sage had ever seen her in the parlor — shoulders settled, hands placed with purpose, her whole body oriented toward the work in front of her the way it oriented toward horses in the field. "It's our primary opportunity to sell the horses we've trained and to establish our reputation for the year. A good fair means financial security. A poor one means a difficult twelve months."

Sage nodded, understanding settling in. She'd known Hartwell wasn't wealthy—the comfortable-but-worn furnishings, the practical rather than fashionable clothing, the careful attention to waste made that clear. But she hadn't fully grasped how the finances worked.

"This year we'll be bringing Marcus, Oliver, and possibly Belle, though she may need another season." Rebecca made a note in her ledger. "Marcus has come along beautifully—your work with him made a real difference. He'll fetch a good price from someone wanting a reliable young gelding."

*Your work with him.* The words warmed something in Sage's chest. She'd spent hours with the nervous bay horse, helping him learn that trying new things didn't have to mean pain. Knowing that work would translate into his finding a good home felt like purpose made tangible.

"What about Promise?" she asked.

Rebecca's pen paused. "Promise won't be sold. He's too valuable to sell."

"Valuable how?"

"His bloodlines, for one. Thoroughbred-Arabian cross with documented lineage—that's desirable stock for anyone breeding hunters or sport horses." Rebecca set down her pen and met Sage's eyes with that direct, unblinking focus that still caught Sage off guard — not unkind, just unmodulated, as though softening her gaze was a social skill she'd never quite acquired. "But more than that: Promise is proof that our methods work. Not just on broken-down cart horses or nervous youngsters, but on a genuinely damaged stallion. If we can rehabilitate him—not just to tolerance, but to genuine partnership—that proves something."

Sage thought about this. "You kept him as a stallion on purpose. It would have been easier to geld him."

"Much easier. Stallions are more difficult in every way—handling, housing, the way they react to mares. Gelding him would have simplified his rehabilitation significantly." Rebecca's expression held something like respect. "But his bloodlines were too valuable to waste. And if I could prove that even an intact male, even one as traumatized as Promise, could be brought back to trust... that's a statement about what's possible."

"So at the fair—"

"At the fair, we show him. You'll ride him sidesaddle in the ladies' exhibition, demonstrating his gentleness and responsiveness. Then Riley or I will show his speed and athleticism. We're not selling him; we're advertising him."

"Advertising?"

"Stud services." Rebecca smiled slightly at Sage's expression. "Mare owners will see a beautiful, well-bred stallion with impeccable manners and impressive movement. They'll want their mares bred to him. The stud fees from even a handful of mares would provide more reliable income than selling him outright ever could."

Sage sat back, absorbing this. Promise's future wasn't just about their partnership—it was about Hartwell's survival. His success meant security for everyone here.

"Thornton will likely be there," Rebecca added, her voice carefully neutral. "He usually attends the county fairs. If he sees Promise..."

She didn't finish the sentence. She didn't need to. Thornton had sent Promise to die, had declared him worthless, dangerous, broken beyond repair. Seeing him transformed would be... complicated.

"Will that be a problem?"

"It might be dramatic." Rebecca's smile was thin. "But I've never shied from drama when the truth was on my side. Let him see what patience and partnership can accomplish. Let him explain to anyone who asks why he gave up on such a horse."

---

The afternoon brought the kind of pale winter sunshine that made everything look like a watercolor painting—soft edges, muted colors, the world holding its breath against the cold. Sage saddled Promise with the sidesaddle and they rode out across the estate, following the familiar paths through bare-branched woods and frost-silvered meadows.

Riley rode alongside on Clover, the older mare steady and calm as always. They didn't speak much—the cold made conversation difficult, breath misting between words—but the silence was companionable rather than awkward.

Promise moved beautifully beneath Sage, his stride free and easy despite the unfamiliar terrain. He'd grown confident over the autumn weeks, no longer startling at unexpected sounds or shying from unfamiliar sights. The world had become interesting rather than threatening.

They paused at the crest of a hill that overlooked the whole of Hartwell's valley. The main house sent up curls of chimney smoke; the stable complex spread behind it, paddocks like a patchwork quilt of brown and white. In the distance, the village church spire marked where the rest of humanity continued their December routines.

"It's beautiful," Sage said.

"It is." Riley pulled Clover alongside Promise, close enough that their stirrups nearly touched. "I've lived here my whole life, and I still notice it sometimes. Especially in winter, when everything's stripped down to essentials."

"Stripped down to essentials," Sage repeated. "I like that."

"Winter does that. Makes you focus on what matters—warmth, food, companionship. All the complications of summer just... fall away."

Sage looked at Riley—really looked, taking in the pink flush the cold had brought to their cheeks, the way their breath caught in the winter light, the steady calm of their presence. They'd spent nearly nine months working side by side, and somewhere in there, friendship had become something deeper. Something that felt like possibility.

"What matters to you?" she asked. "When everything's stripped down?"

Riley considered the question seriously, the way they considered everything. "The horses. The work—not because I have to, but because it's good work, meaningful work. My mother, Rebecca, this place." A pause. "You."

The word hung in the cold air between them, small and enormous at the same time.

"Me," Sage said.

"You've become... essential. To Hartwell. To the work." Riley's cheeks were definitely pinker now, and not just from cold. "To me."

Below them, Promise shifted his weight, patient with this human pause in the ride. Clover dropped her head to investigate a tuft of frost-covered grass. The world went on, unhurried, while something important settled between two people on a winter hilltop.

"You've become essential to me too," Sage said quietly. "I didn't expect that. When I came here, I wasn't expecting to feel anything again."

"And now?"

"Now I feel everything." She laughed, a little shakily. "It's terrifying, actually. Feeling things. Wanting things. Having people I could lose."

"Having people worth losing." Riley reached across the space between their horses and took Sage's gloved hand. "That's the risk, isn't it? Caring about anything means it can hurt you."

"Promise taught me that. Or maybe I taught him. Or we taught each other."

They sat like that for a long moment, hands linked across the gap between horses, the valley spread below them and the winter sky vast above. Nothing was declared. Nothing needed to be. Some things grew better in quiet, rooting deep before they bloomed.

"We should head back," Riley said eventually. "The light's going, and there's evening feeds to do."

"Always practical."

"Someone has to be." But Riley was smiling, and they didn't let go of Sage's hand until the path narrowed and the horses had to walk single file.

---

Evening at Hartwell meant the parlor fire and the day's accounts and the comfortable presence of the household gathered together. Rebecca reviewed the ledger with Jennifer while Sage sat nearby, mending a torn stable blanket with the careful stitches Jennifer had taught her.

Riley was out checking the horses one last time—their ritual, walking the aisle before bed, making sure everyone was comfortable in their stalls for the night. Promise had adapted well to being stabled; he seemed to enjoy having neighbors now, often hanging his head over his door to watch the activity in the barn. Sage had offered to go along, but Riley had smiled and said, "Stay warm. I'll be back."

So she sat by the fire, needle moving through fabric, and thought about the day. About finances and spring fairs and Promise's future as a breeding stallion. About stripped-down essentials and hands linked across the space between horses. About the way Riley had said *you've become essential* like it was simple fact, like the sky being blue or grass being green.

"You're smiling," Jennifer observed from across the room.

"Am I?"

"You are. It's nice to see." Jennifer set aside her own mending. "I remember when you first came here, Sage. You barely spoke. You flinched if anyone moved too quickly near you. You watched everything like you were waiting for the other shoe to drop."

"I was."

"And now?"

Sage considered. "Now I'm still watching, but it's different. I'm watching because I'm interested, not because I'm afraid. Watching Promise learn something new. Watching Marcus gain confidence. Watching—" She stopped, feeling her cheeks warm.

"Watching Riley?" Jennifer's voice was gentle, knowing.

"Is it that obvious?"

"Only to anyone with eyes." Jennifer smiled. "I'm not prying, dear. Just... glad. Riley's been solitary for a long time. They needed someone who understood the work, who shared the values. Someone worth opening their heart to."

"I don't know if I'm worth—"

"You are." Jennifer's tone brooked no argument. "You've worked harder than anyone I've seen to rebuild yourself this year. You've helped rebuild Promise. You've become part of this household in ways that matter. Whatever grows between you and Riley, it's growing from solid ground."

The front door opened, bringing a gust of cold air and Riley's familiar footsteps. They appeared in the parlor doorway, cheeks ruddy from the cold, bits of hay still clinging to their coat.

"Everyone's settled," they reported. "Promise was restless at first—kept looking over his door like he was waiting for something. Then I gave him a few minutes of scratching and he calmed right down. I think he just wanted company."

"He's gotten used to attention," Sage said. "I think he likes knowing people are nearby now."

"He does. He watches everything that happens in that barn." Riley's eyes found Sage's across the room, warm and steady. "He's come a long way from the horse who couldn't stand anyone within sight."

"So have you," Rebecca said, glancing up from her ledger with an expression that might have been meant for Sage, or Riley, or both of them. The warmth in it was real but brief — there and gone, like her rare smiles, before she returned to her numbers.

Outside, the winter night pressed against the windows, cold and dark. Inside, the fire crackled and the household settled into their evening routines. Down in the barn, a black stallion dozed in his warm stall, occasionally lifting his head to listen to the sounds of the other horses breathing nearby—part of a herd now, in ways he'd never imagined possible.

Tomorrow would bring more work—there was always more work. Marcus needed to be ridden, Belle needed patient handling, Promise would be turned out in the morning for exercise and fresh air before the day's training began. The endless rhythms of a working stable demanded attention. The spring fair loomed on the horizon with its promises and pressures.

But tonight, there was firelight and companionship and the quiet certainty of belonging. Tonight, that was enough.

**End of Chapter 26**
