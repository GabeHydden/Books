# Chapter 27: Snow Rides
**Book IV: Winter — Reflection**
**POV: Promise (opening), then Sage**
**Mid-December 1813**

## Promise

The white-falling started in the night.

Promise stood at his stall door, watching through the gap where the barn's great doors didn't quite meet. White-specks drifting down from the dark-above, landing soft on the ground-outside, piling up into something strange and new.

He'd seen rain-falling before—that was ordinary, wet and cold and nothing interesting. But this was different. This was silent-falling, gentle-landing, and where it touched the ground it stayed. By the time the dark began to lighten toward morning, the world had changed color entirely.

White-everywhere. White on the fence-rails and the paddock-ground and the roof of the shelter where he used to sleep before the stall-inside became his place. White catching on bare tree-branches, white smoothing over the ruts and bumps of the yard until everything looked soft and new.

Promise snorted, watching his breath make its own small cloud in the cold air. The white-stuff was interesting. He wanted to investigate.

From down the row came the sounds of other-horses stirring—Thunder shaking his head, making his halter ring against the stall bars; Clover's patient sigh as she waited for breakfast; the young ones shuffling in their straw. Promise had learned their sounds over the weeks of living-inside. He knew Thunder's impatient stomp and Marcus's nervous shifting and the soft way Whisper breathed when she was content.

He belonged here now. Not just tolerated, not just kept-separate in the quarantine-paddock. He had a place in the row, neighbors who knew his voice, humans who came reliably with grain and hay and the touches that meant safety.

The barn door scraped open, letting in a gust of cold and a swirl of white-specks. Small-human appeared, bundled in more layers than usual, her breath making clouds like his.

Promise nickered. *Here. I'm here. I see you.*

She came to his door, brushing white-stuff from her shoulders. "Good morning, beautiful. Have you seen the snow?"

Snow. That was the word for the white-falling. Promise filed it away with all the other human-words he'd learned—halter and lead and good and steady and the most important one, the one that meant him: *Promise*.

Small-human produced an apple from somewhere in her layers. He took it carefully, then pushed his nose against her shoulder in their greeting-way. She smelled like woodsmoke and wool and the particular scent that was just her, familiar and safe.

"We're going out today," she said, rubbing behind his ears. "All of us, through the snow. Would you like that?"

He didn't understand all the words, but he understood her tone—the lift of excitement, the question in it. And he understood *out*, which meant leaving the barn-safe and going into the world-beyond.

Once, that would have meant fear. Now it meant interesting.

---

## Sage

The snow had transformed Hartwell into something from a fairy story. Every branch wore a white sleeve, every fence post a jaunty cap. The paddocks had become smooth white blankets, unmarked except where the horses had been turned out earlier and left their wandering trails of hoofprints.

Promise had been beside himself during turnout, snorting and prancing and investigating the strange cold stuff with an intensity that made Sage laugh. He'd pawed at it, sending up sprays of white. He'd dropped his nose to sniff it, then jerked back in comical surprise at the cold. He'd even rolled in it, coming up crusted with snow and looking enormously pleased with himself.

Now, brushed clean and tacked with the sidesaddle, he stood in the yard with his ears pricked so far forward they nearly touched. Every few moments he'd stamp a hoof, impatient to be moving, to explore this changed world.

"He's keen," Riley observed, bringing Thunder alongside. The grey gelding tossed his head, equally eager to be moving, his breath making great clouds in the cold air.

"He's never seen it before. Not like this." Sage gathered her reins, feeling Promise shift eagerly beneath her. "Easy. We're going."

Rebecca had decided they'd all ride out together—the whole household, a rare treat. Jennifer sat on Clover, the steady mare patient as always beneath her lighter rider. Rebecca had taken Marcus, using the outing as training for the young gelding who needed experience with varied conditions before the spring fair. Riley was on Thunder, and Sage on Promise.

The horses needed exercise regardless of weather, and the snow wasn't deep enough to be dangerous. More importantly, Promise needed to experience the world in all its variations if he was going to be shown at the spring fair.

They set out along the main track that wound through Hartwell's woods, the horses' hooves crunching through the fresh snow with a sound like biting into an apple. Promise's head was high, his ears swiveling constantly, taking in every snow-draped branch and white-capped stone.

"Steady," Sage murmured when he spooked slightly at a clump of snow sliding from a branch. But it was a small spook, more surprise than fear, and he settled immediately at her voice.

This was new for both of them—riding out in genuine winter conditions, the world so changed from its usual appearance. But Promise's trust in her held firm even when the landscape didn't look like anything he knew.

They emerged from the woods onto the high meadow, and Promise stopped dead, staring.

The valley spread below them, transformed into a white wonderland. Smoke rose from cottage chimneys in the village. The church spire wore a cap of snow. The stream was a dark ribbon cutting through all that white, the only thing that looked the same as always.

"It's beautiful," Jennifer said softly, Clover halting contentedly beside them.

"He thinks so too." Promise hadn't moved, his whole body oriented toward the view, ears pricked, nostrils flaring as he took in the cold scents of a world remade.

---

They rode on, the four of them spread across the meadow in a loose line. Rebecca led on Marcus, the young gelding stepping carefully but confidently through the snow—good practice for him. Jennifer flanked on Clover, and Riley brought Thunder alongside Sage and Promise.

The cold bit at Sage's cheeks, but inside her layers she was warm enough, and the beauty of the day made the chill worthwhile. Sunlight broke through the clouds occasionally, making the snow glitter like scattered diamonds.

Promise moved beautifully beneath her, his stride long and free despite the unfamiliar footing. He'd figured out the snow quickly—lifting his hooves higher, placing them more deliberately. Clever horse. He'd always been clever, once the fear stopped blocking his ability to think.

"He's doing well," Rebecca called back. "The footing doesn't bother him?"

"He adjusted fast. He's curious about it more than worried."

That was the thing about Promise now—curiosity had replaced so much of his fear. The same intelligence that had once been entirely devoted to detecting threats now spent itself on understanding his world. Every new experience was something to investigate rather than flee.

They paused at the stream to let the horses drink. The water ran dark and clear between banks of snow, and Promise dropped his head to it with the casual confidence of a horse who'd learned that water was just water, nothing to fear.

While the horses drank, Jennifer guided Clover closer to Promise. The mare and stallion stood side by side, their breath mingling in the cold air.

Promise's head came up. His nostrils flared, and something shifted in his posture—a subtle alertness, an awareness. Mare-scent, carried on the cold air, and his stallion nature responding.

But he didn't do anything. Didn't pin his ears or nip or try to drive Clover anywhere. Just acknowledged her presence with that flared-nostril awareness, then dropped his head to drink again.

"Good boy," Sage murmured, scratching his withers.

Rebecca had noticed too — of course she had, she noticed everything about horses even while managing Marcus through a small circle to keep the young gelding from getting antsy. Her eyes had tracked the interaction with that quality of attention she gave to behavior, cataloguing it the way she catalogued everything. "He's learning to manage himself," she observed. "That's important. A stallion who can be trusted around mares without making a scene—that's valuable."

"Is that part of why you brought him out with Clover today?"

"Partly. He needs to learn that mares exist without it being a crisis. By spring, he'll need to behave impeccably around other horses, including mares. The fair will be chaos—hundreds of horses in close proximity. If he can't control himself, we can't show him."

Promise lifted his head, water dripping from his muzzle, and turned to look at Sage as if he'd understood every word. Probably he'd just heard his name. But sometimes she wondered how much he really comprehended.

---

The ride back took them along a different route, through the eastern woods where the snow lay thicker under the denser canopy. Promise picked his way carefully here, trusting Sage's guidance when the footing grew uncertain.

Riley fell in beside them where the path widened, Thunder's grey shoulder nearly brushing Promise's flank. The two gelding and stallion had settled into companionable proximity, moving together like partners rather than rivals.

"He really has changed," Riley said quietly. "I remember when he first arrived—the way he looked at everything like it might kill him. Now look at him. He's enjoying this."

"He's enjoying his life." Sage felt the truth of it in the way Promise moved, the easy swing of his stride, the relaxed set of his ears. "I think maybe he didn't know that was possible. That life could be something you enjoyed instead of just survived."

"Did you know? When you first came here?"

The question caught Sage off guard. She considered it as they rode, the snow-muffled silence of the woods wrapping around them.

"No," she said finally. "I think I'd forgotten. Or maybe I'd never really learned. Before—" She didn't need to specify what before meant. Riley knew. "Before, life was something that happened to me. I was just trying to survive it. I didn't know you could..." She gestured vaguely at the snow, the horses, Riley's presence beside her. "Choose it. Build it. Make it yours."

"And now?"

"Now I'm learning." She met Riley's eyes, dark and warm despite the cold. "You're teaching me. Promise is teaching me. This whole place."

"You're teaching yourself," Riley corrected gently. "We're just providing the conditions. You're doing the growing."

They rode in silence for a while after that, but it wasn't an empty silence. It was full of things acknowledged but not yet spoken, feelings that needed more time to find their words.

The path narrowed again, and Riley dropped back to ride behind. But just before they separated, their gloved hands found each other—a brief squeeze, there and gone, like the touch of snow melting on warm skin.

---

They reached the stables as the light was beginning to fail, the short December day already surrendering to dusk. The horses were steaming slightly despite the cold, their breath great plumes of white as they stood in the yard.

Promise stood quiet while Sage dismounted and ran up her stirrup, his earlier excitement settled into tired contentment. He'd worked today—really worked, navigating unfamiliar footing and managing his instincts and taking in a hundred new experiences. A good tired, the kind that came from using body and mind together.

"I'll take him," Riley offered. "You should get warm. You're shivering."

Sage hadn't noticed, but now that Riley mentioned it, the cold had seeped through her layers. Her fingers were stiff inside her gloves.

"You're sure?"

"I'm sure. Go. I'll settle him and join you for tea."

Sage handed over Promise's reins, but before she left, she pressed her hand to his neck—a wordless thank-you for the day, for his courage and curiosity and the way he'd carried her through a world made strange and beautiful by snow.

Promise turned his head and bumped her shoulder with his nose. *I see you. You see me. We're here together.*

"Always," she murmured. "Always."

She walked toward the warmth of the house, leaving Riley to lead Promise into the barn. Behind her, she heard Riley's voice, soft and fond: "Come on, handsome. Let's get you warm too."

The snow was still falling lightly, adding fresh white to what had already accumulated. Tomorrow there would be more work—there was always more work. Marcus needed riding, Belle needed patience, the daily rhythm of the stable demanded attention.

But tonight there would be warmth and tea and the company of people who had become, impossibly and wonderfully, her family.

Promise nickered from inside the barn, a sound of contentment. Riley laughed at something—probably his demanding insistence on immediate grain.

Sage smiled and went inside.

**End of Chapter 27**
