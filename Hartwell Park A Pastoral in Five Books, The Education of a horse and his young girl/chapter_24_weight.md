# Chapter 24: Weight
**Book III: Autumn — Partnership**
**POV: Sage, with Promise sections**
**November 1813**

November had stripped the last leaves from the trees and laid frost across the paddocks each morning. Sage's breath plumed white as she walked to the round pen, the saddle heavy in her arms.

Three weeks since they'd returned to training. Three weeks of the new pace — one session per day, fifteen minutes at most, watching Promise more carefully than she'd ever watched anything in her life. The halter work had gone smoothly. The leading had resumed. The longe line, the long-lining, the bit — all relearned, all accepted, Promise moving through each step with the cautious willingness of a creature choosing to trust again despite everything.

The surcingle had been harder. The first time Sage brought it into the round pen, Promise had backed to the far rail and stood trembling, his eye showing white. She'd set it on the ground and sat beside it for twenty minutes until he approached on his own, nosing the thing with suspicious precision before blowing out and looking at her as if to say: *Fine. But I'm watching you.*

It had taken four days before he'd wear it again. But he wore it now without flinching.

"Ready?" Rebecca asked from the fence.

"No." Sage's arms tightened around the saddle. "But I don't think I'll ever feel ready. So we might as well try."

They'd introduced the saddle as an object days ago. Let Promise smell it, mouth the leather, watch it sit on the fence rail. He'd shown curiosity rather than fear — nosing at the stirrups, investigating the girth straps, treating it as just another strange human thing to learn about.

But wearing it was different. Wearing it meant weight. And weight meant what had happened to Riley.

Sage didn't let herself finish the thought. She climbed into the round pen where Promise waited, already haltered, already watching her with those dark curious eyes.

"Good morning," she said softly. "We're going to try something new today."

---

**PROMISE**

The small-human carries the big-leather-thing into the circle-place. He's seen this thing before — smelled it, touched it with his muzzle, learned its scent-signature of leather and oil and metal. Just another object-to-know, like the halter-thing and the bit-thing and the belly-band-thing.

But this object has a different-weight in his memory. This is the thing that was on his back when the world broke. When the wrong-weight-human sat above him and his body remembered every terrible thing and he threw-violent and the wrong-weight-human hit the ground with the sound that still echoes in his body-knowing.

The small-human sets the big-leather-thing on the ground where he can see it. Approaches him slow-careful, hands making the good-circles on his neck.

"We're going to put the saddle on you today," she says. Her voice is calm-surface but he can hear the underneath-trembling. "Just to see how it feels. If you hate it, we'll stop. I promise."

Promise. His name-sound. Also the word for certainty-kept.

She picks up the saddle, holding it low so he can smell-investigate. He does, though he's done this before. The leather-smell is familiar. But now it carries another smell-memory underneath — sweat-fear-falling, the sharp scent of the terrible-morning.

Then she lifts it higher. Toward his back.

Everything in him goes tight-frozen.

---

**SAGE**

She felt the change in him instantly. One moment he was relaxed under her hand, the next every muscle had turned to stone.

"Easy," she murmured, lowering the saddle immediately. "Easy, Promise. Just showing you. See? I'm putting it down."

She set the saddle on the ground and stepped back to his head, both hands on his neck, making slow circles until his breathing started to ease.

"That's okay. That's good information. You don't like it coming toward your back."

Of course he didn't. The last time the saddle had been on his back, a rider had sat above him and triggered every trauma his body carried. Weight on his back meant being ridden, and being ridden had only ever meant suffering — first from Thornton's men, and then, despite every good intention, from the morning Riley fell.

"We'll take it slow," she told him. "As slow as you need."

She spent the next twenty minutes just lifting the saddle and lowering it. Not toward him — just in his field of vision. Letting him watch it move up and down, up and down, until the motion became boring rather than threatening.

Then she started lifting it toward her own body. Holding it against her hip. Against her chest. Showing him that this weight-thing could rest on a body without causing harm.

Promise watched with his ears pricked, his attention unwavering. Learning, the way he always learned — through observation, through processing, through making his own conclusions.

"That's enough for today," Rebecca called from the fence. "End on calm."

Sage set the saddle down and gave Promise an apple. His eye was softer now, the terrible tension gone. But she'd seen that freeze. She knew what they were up against.

---

It took three more sessions before Promise could stand still while Sage lifted the saddle toward his back. And another two before he allowed her to rest it — just rest it, not cinch it — across his withers for a count of five.

Each session ended when he gave any sign of distress. Each session started with the same patient routine: smell the saddle, watch the saddle move, see it on Sage's body, let it approach. Building comfort through repetition. Trust through consistency.

On the eighth day, they got the saddle fully on his back for the first time since the fall.

---

**PROMISE**

The weight settles onto him and every instinct screams *wrong-wrong-flee-now*.

Weight means rider. Rider means pain. This is body-knowledge, learned in blood and fear over seasons of before-time. Reinforced-sharp by the terrible-morning when the wrong-weight-human sat above him and his body exploded and the human hit the ground. The leather-thing pressing down on his back triggers every survival-pattern he possesses.

But he doesn't flee.

He stands trembling, legs braced, breath coming too-fast. The small-human is beside him, her hands making those constant-circles on his neck, her voice a stream of calm-sounds that wash over him like water.

"You're doing so well. I know it feels strange. I know it's scary. But look — nothing's hurting. Nothing's pulling. It's just weight. Just the saddle sitting there."

Just-weight. Just-saddle.

He tries to believe this. Tries to hold onto the knowing-new instead of the knowing-old. Small-human put this on him. Small-human's hands, small-human's smell, small-human's care. Small-human who has never hurt.

The older-one-who-decides approaches slow-steady and does something with the belly-straps. A slight tightening around his middle. His skin twitches-automatic, trying to shake off the wrongness. But the saddle doesn't shift, doesn't dig, doesn't hurt.

"Walk on," the older-one says in her teaching-voice.

Walk? With this-thing on him?

Small-human guides him forward with the lead-rope. One step. Two. The saddle moves-with-him, shifting slightly with each stride. Strange-feeling. Heavy-different.

But not painful. Not hurting. Just... there.

They walk a small circle. The saddle stays settled. The humans stay calm. Nothing bad happens.

By the time they stop, his trembling has eased to occasional-shivers. The weight is still wrong-feeling but becoming less-terrifying. Becoming another thing-to-learn instead of thing-to-fear.

---

**SAGE**

The first time Promise walked a full circuit of the round pen with the saddle on, Sage had to blink back tears.

"Don't celebrate yet," Rebecca warned, but she was smiling. "We've got a long way to go."

A long way. But they were going there. Step by careful step, building on the foundation they'd re-laid, watching him instead of their own expectations.

The saddle work continued over the following days. Walk with the saddle. Trot with the saddle. Learn that the weight wasn't going anywhere, that it was part of him now when he was working. Promise adapted with the cautious deliberation of a horse who had learned that new things could be survived — not the rapid, eager progress of autumn, but something steadier. More tested.

Two weeks after the first re-saddling, Rebecca judged him ready for the next step.

"Weight over his back," she said. "Not sitting yet. Just leaning. Getting him used to the feel of a body up there."

Sage's stomach clenched. "Me?"

"You're the lightest of us. And you're the one he trusts." Rebecca paused. "You're the only one he trusts with this."

They started with Sage standing on a mounting block beside Promise while he wore the saddle. Just standing there. Letting him get used to her being above him, her presence at a different angle than he was used to.

He craned his neck around to look at her, clearly puzzled by this change in their usual positioning. But he didn't panic. Just... looked.

"Now lean," Rebecca instructed. "Put your weight across the saddle. Don't try to mount. Just lean."

Sage bent at the waist and let her upper body rest across Promise's back. She could feel him tense beneath her — every muscle going tight with that same freeze response. But she didn't pull away. Just lay there, breathing slowly, letting him feel her weight.

"Talk to him," Rebecca said.

"You're okay," Sage murmured against the saddle leather. "This is just me. Just Sage. I'm not going to hurt you. I'm just lying here. Feel how nothing bad is happening?"

Beneath her, Promise's breathing gradually slowed. The terrible tension began to ease, degree by degree.

"Good boy," she whispered. "Such a brave, good boy."

She stayed draped across him for a slow count of thirty. Then she straightened up, and Rebecca handed her an apple to give him.

"Again tomorrow," Rebecca said. "And the day after that. Until he's bored with it."

---

It took six days for Promise to stop tensing when Sage leaned across the saddle. By the seventh day, he barely reacted — just turned his head to see what she was doing, then went back to watching the barn cat stalk something in the grass.

"Next step," Rebecca said. "Weight in the stirrup."

This was different from leaning. Leaning spread her weight across his whole back. The stirrup concentrated it on one side — an unbalanced pressure that would shift and pull as she moved.

The first time Sage put her foot in the stirrup and pressed down, Promise sidled away from the strange sideways tug. They spent two days just getting him comfortable with her foot being there, with the slight pressure of her weight beginning to settle.

"More now," Rebecca instructed on the third day. "Really lean into it. Let him feel you starting to pull yourself up."

Sage gripped the front of the saddle and pressed down into the stirrup, lifting her other foot off the mounting block so her full weight hung from that one point. Promise's ears went back and his body tensed, but he didn't move. She held for a count of three, then stepped back down.

"Good boy. Again."

They repeated this dozens of times over the next several days. A little more weight each session. A little longer holding it. Promise learning that the strange sideways pull wasn't going to hurt him, that Sage hanging from his side was just another thing to accept.

By the fifth day, she was pulling herself high enough that her belly pressed against the saddle, her right leg dangling, her full weight suspended from the stirrup and her grip on the pommel. Promise stood solid beneath her, occasionally turning his head to watch this strange behavior with patient confusion.

"He's ready," Rebecca said.

Sage's heart hammered so hard she could feel it in her throat. "Are you sure?"

"No. I'm never sure. But he's as ready as he's going to get, and so are you." Rebecca moved to Promise's head, taking his lead rope. "I'll hold him. Riley will spot you."

Riley stood by the mounting block, arm still in the sling but the hand freed now, mobile enough to help if needed. They'd come to every session for the past week, watching from the fence, offering the occasional quiet observation. Now they looked at Sage with an expression that held everything — hope and fear and the particular understanding of someone who had been up there and been thrown.

"All you have to do is swing your leg over and sit," Rebecca said. "Don't ask him to move. Don't do anything. Just sit."

Just sit. As if it were simple. As if this moment wasn't the culmination of everything they'd worked for since March — and everything they'd rebuilt since the fall.

Sage climbed the mounting block. Promise stood quiet below her, his ear flicked back toward her presence. Listening. Waiting to see what would happen next.

"It's just me," she told him, her voice barely above a whisper. "Just Sage. We're going to try something new. If you hate it, I'll get off. I promise."

She leaned across the saddle first, letting him feel her weight in the familiar way. He didn't tense. Didn't even shift his stance.

"Good boy. Now I'm going to swing my leg over. Slow and careful. Here I come."

---

**PROMISE**

The small-human's weight settles across him like it has for many days now. This is known-pattern. Boring-familiar.

But then something changes.

Her body shifts. Her leg moves — up and over, crossing his back, settling on the other side. And suddenly the weight isn't just pressing-down. It's surrounding-him. Weight on both sides. Weight across his spine. Weight in the stirrup-things that press against his sides.

*Rider.*

The word-concept explodes through him like fire. Rider means pain. Rider means force. Rider means before-time-horror of spurs and whip and hands that pull until his mouth bleeds. Rider means the terrible-morning when the wrong-weight-human sat this way and his body exploded and the human fell and everything broke.

He wants to leap-sideways, buck-violent, throw this weight off before the hurting starts —

But.

But the weight isn't hurting. The hands aren't pulling. The legs aren't kicking.

And the weight is *hers*.

Small-human sits very-still above him. Her weight is there, present and undeniable, but it's just... sitting. Not demanding. Not forcing. Not causing pain. And underneath the leather-smell and saddle-smell, there is the smell he knows better than any other. The smell that has meant safe since the beginning-of-new-time. The smell of flat-palms and good-touch and apples and the particular salt-scent of her tears against his neck.

"Easy," she whispers, and he can hear those tears in her voice again. "Easy, Promise. I'm just sitting here. I'm not going to hurt you. I'm never going to hurt you."

The older-one holds his head-rope, steady and calm. The other-human — the one who fell, the one with the hurt-arm — stands nearby, watching.

Everyone is very-still. Very-quiet. Waiting for him to decide what happens next.

He could throw her. Could rear or buck or spin and send her crashing to the ground the way he sent the other-human. His body knows how. His body is *begging* him to, every survival-instinct firing at once, screaming *rider-means-pain, throw-the-rider, save-yourself*.

But.

But this is small-human. This is the one who sat by his fence for days-uncounted making herself boring. Who offered apples from her flat-palm. Who cried into his neck after the terrible-morning and sat at the rail for hours even when he wouldn't look at her. Who came back and came back and came back, patient-steady, until he could stand beside her again.

This is small-human who has never once caused him pain-deliberate. Who stops when he says stop. Who waits when he needs to wait. Who has been learning-beside-him all along, both of them students, both of them scared, both of them trying.

He takes a breath. Then another.

The weight is still there. The legs are still on both sides. His body still screams that this is wrong-dangerous-flee. But the scream is quieter now, held at distance by the knowing that runs deeper than fear: *This is her. This is small-human. This is safe.*

He doesn't relax.

He can't. The position is still wrong-echoing, still vibrating with every terrible thing that has ever happened with legs-on-both-sides. His mind knows it's her. His body doesn't care. His body only knows the shape of the thing — rider-astride, legs-pressing, weight-above — and his body says *danger*.

So he stands. Not fleeing, not accepting. Something in between. Holding himself together through will-alone while his mind and body argue.

He endures.

---

**SAGE**

She felt it when he didn't break — the moment when flight was possible and he chose not to take it. His muscles stayed locked, his breathing stayed fast, but he didn't explode.

He was letting her stay.

The tears she'd been fighting spilled over, running down her cheeks. She didn't try to wipe them away. Just sat there, on Promise's back, feeling the impossible reality of this moment.

But even through the tears, she could feel what was wrong.

He wasn't relaxing. His head hadn't lowered. His breathing hadn't settled. He stood beneath her like a horse made of locked iron — tolerating but not accepting, enduring but not at peace. Every muscle held in rigid control, as though one wrong movement from either of them would shatter whatever fragile bargain he'd struck between his mind and his body.

She'd felt this in other horses. The ones who obeyed because they'd learned that resistance brought worse consequences. The shut-down compliance of a creature who had given up fighting, not because the fight was won but because fighting cost too much.

This wasn't that — not exactly. Promise wasn't shut down. He was *choosing*. Choosing to stand still because he trusted her, even though everything else about this position terrified him. But the choice was costing him something she could feel in the rigid tremor beneath her seat.

"That's enough," Rebecca said quietly. "Dismount. End on success."

Sage swung her leg back over, careful and slow, and slid down Promise's side. Her knees nearly buckled when she hit the ground.

The moment her weight left him, Promise shook — a full-body shudder that started at his withers and rolled through to his tail. His head dropped and he blew out hard, once, twice, three times. Tension leaving him in visible waves.

She buried her face in his neck, one arm around him, breathing him in.

"You did it," she whispered. "You brave, impossible horse."

He stood quiet under her arm. His eye, when she pulled back to look, was soft again — the locked-iron horse replaced by the Promise she knew. But she'd felt what was beneath that softness. The war between trust and terror. The position that his body could not accept even when his mind said *safe*.

---

They tried again the next day. And the next. And every day for a week.

Each time, Sage mounted and Promise stood. Each time, he held himself in that rigid, trembling stillness — not fighting, not fleeing, but not settling either. His breathing stayed fast. His muscles stayed locked. He tolerated her weight the way he might tolerate a thunderstorm: enduring it, waiting for it to pass.

Rebecca had her sit for a little longer each session. One minute. Two. Three. On the fifth day, she asked Sage to shift her weight slightly — not steering, not asking for movement, just settling deeper in the saddle.

Promise's ears pinned flat and his whole body braced, and Sage immediately went still.

"He's managing," Rebecca said from the ground, but her voice was careful. Analytical. "He's not panicking. He's not throwing you. But he's not with you either."

That was it exactly. He was managing. Not present. Not the horse who leaned into her hand at the fence, who followed her voice in the round pen, who chose her company over solitude. Up here, astride, legs on both sides — she might as well have been anyone. Any rider. Any weight pressing down on him from above.

"It's the position," Sage said, voicing what had been growing in her mind for days. "It's not me he's afraid of. It's this." She gestured at herself, at her legs on either side of him, at the shape she made in the saddle. "Every rider who hurt him sat like this. And when he's down there and I'm up here, in this position, he can't tell the difference between me and them."

"Riley said the same thing," Rebecca murmured. "That morning. Said Promise couldn't distinguish between the position and the person in it."

Sage looked down at Promise — at the tension in his neck, the too-fast breathing, the rigid endurance of a horse holding himself together through sheer force of trust. He was trying so hard. Giving her everything he had. And it wasn't enough, because the thing he was fighting wasn't doubt about her — it was body-knowledge older and deeper than anything his mind could override.

"I can't ask him to live like this," Sage said. "I can't ask him to spend every ride just... surviving me."

She dismounted. Promise shook himself, blew out, and within seconds was nosing at her pockets, his whole body loose and easy now that the weight was gone. The transformation was immediate and total — ground-Sage was his person, his safe-human, his comfort. Saddle-Sage was a shape his body couldn't stop fearing.

Riley was at the fence, watching. Their arm was out of the sling now — had been for a few days — though they still held it carefully, the shoulder not yet ready for full work. They said nothing as Sage led Promise out of the pen. But their eyes followed the conversation that hadn't quite happened yet.

---

That evening, Sage sat on the kitchen floor beside Riley's chair — their chair now, the armchair by the fire that had become Riley's place during the long weeks of recovery. Supper was over. Jennifer had gone to check on the horses. Rebecca was somewhere in the house, her footsteps occasionally audible above them.

"He tolerates me," Sage said. "Astride, he tolerates me. But he's not there. He's somewhere else entirely — just holding on, waiting for it to be over."

"I know," Riley said. "I've been watching."

"What do I do? I can't force him to be comfortable. I can't just keep sitting on him until he gets used to it — that's what Thornton would do. That's exactly what Thornton would do."

"Then don't do what Thornton would do."

"But what's left? If he can't accept astride — if the position itself is the problem, not the rider — then how does anyone ever ride him?"

Riley was quiet for a long time. The fire cracked. Outside, wind pushed against the windows.

"I don't know," Riley said. "But you're asking the right question. It's not *how do I make him accept this*. It's *how do I ride him without asking him to accept the thing that frightens him*."

"Those might be the same question."

"They might. But they feel different." Riley shifted in the chair, wincing slightly as the ribs protested. "When you're on the ground with him, he's perfect. Calm, trusting, connected. The problem starts the moment you're up there in that specific configuration — legs on both sides, weight above. Take away the configuration and the horse is fine."

"But that's what riding *is*. Legs on both sides. Weight above."

"Is it?" Riley looked at her. "Always?"

Sage opened her mouth, then closed it.

The fire cracked again. Promise's whinny drifted in from the paddock — not distressed, just calling to Whisper, who answered from the barn.

The question sat between them, unanswered, while the November wind found every crack in the old house and pushed its way in.
