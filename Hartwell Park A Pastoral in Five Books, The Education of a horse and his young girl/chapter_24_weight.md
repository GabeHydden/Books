# Chapter 24: Weight
**Book III: Autumn — Partnership**
**POV: Sage, with Promise sections**
**November 1813**

November had stripped the last leaves from the trees and laid frost across the paddocks each morning. Sage's breath plumed white as she walked to the round pen, the saddle heavy in her arms.

Three weeks since they'd returned to training. Three weeks of the new pace—one session per day, fifteen minutes at most, watching Promise more carefully than she'd ever watched anything in her life. The longe line work had gone smoothly. The long-lining had resumed without incident. Promise had relearned the bit, the surcingle, the feel of equipment on his body, and through it all Sage had held her breath, waiting for something to go wrong.

Nothing had gone wrong. But she hadn't stopped being afraid.

"Ready?" Rebecca asked from the fence.

"No." Sage's arms tightened around the saddle. "But I don't think I'll ever feel ready. So we might as well try."

They'd introduced the saddle as an object days ago. Let Promise smell it, mouth the leather, watch it sit on the fence rail. He'd shown curiosity rather than fear—nosing at the stirrups, investigating the girth straps, treating it as just another strange human thing to learn about.

But wearing it was different. Wearing it meant weight. And weight meant...

Sage didn't let herself finish the thought. She climbed into the round pen where Promise waited, already haltered, already watching her with those dark curious eyes.

"Good morning," she said softly. "We're going to try something new today."

---

**PROMISE**

The small-human carries the big-leather-thing into the circle-place. He's seen this thing before—smelled it, touched it with his muzzle, learned its scent-signature of leather and oil and metal. Just another object-to-know, like the halter-thing and the bit-thing and the belly-band-thing.

But today feels different. Today there's tension-careful in the small-human's body. Her breathing is controlled-deliberate, the way it gets when she's managing fear.

This makes him wary-alert. If she's afraid, should he be?

She sets the big-leather-thing on the ground where he can see it. Approaches him slow-careful, hands making the good-circles on his neck.

"We're going to put the saddle on you today," she says. Her voice is calm-surface but he can hear the underneath-trembling. "Just to see how it feels. If you hate it, we'll stop. I promise."

Promise. His name-sound. Also the word for certainty-kept.

She picks up the saddle again, holding it low so he can smell-investigate. He does, though he's done this before. The leather-smell is familiar now. The metal-bits don't frighten him.

Then she lifts it higher. Toward his back.

Everything in him goes tight-frozen.

---

**SAGE**

She felt the change in him instantly. One moment he was relaxed under her hand, the next every muscle had turned to stone.

"Easy," she murmured, lowering the saddle immediately. "Easy, Promise. Just showing you. See? I'm putting it down."

She set the saddle on the ground and stepped back to his head, both hands on his neck, making slow circles until his breathing started to ease.

"That's okay. That's good information. You don't like it coming toward your back."

Of course he didn't. How many times had weight on his back meant pain? A rider with hard hands, a whip, spurs that dug without mercy. Weight meant being ridden, and being ridden had only ever meant suffering.

"We'll take it slow," she told him. "As slow as you need."

She spent the next twenty minutes just lifting the saddle and lowering it. Not toward him—just in his field of vision. Letting him watch it move up and down, up and down, until the motion became boring rather than threatening.

Then she started lifting it toward her own body. Holding it against her hip. Against her chest. Showing him that this weight-thing could rest on a body without causing harm.

Promise watched with his ears pricked, his attention unwavering. Learning, the way he always learned—through observation, through processing, through making his own conclusions.

"That's enough for today," Rebecca called from the fence. "End on calm."

Sage set the saddle down and gave Promise an apple. His eye was softer now, the terrible tension gone. But she'd seen that freeze. She knew what they were up against.

---

It took three more sessions before Promise could stand still while Sage lifted the saddle toward his back. And another two before he allowed her to rest it—just rest it, not cinch it—across his withers for a count of five.

Each session ended when he gave any sign of distress. Each session started with the same patient routine: smell the saddle, watch the saddle move, see it on Sage's body, let it approach. Building comfort through repetition. Trust through consistency.

On the eighth day, they got the saddle fully on his back for the first time.

---

**PROMISE**

The weight settles onto him and every instinct screams *wrong-wrong-flee-now*.

Weight means rider. Rider means pain. This is body-knowledge, learned in blood and fear over seasons of before-time. The leather-thing pressing down on his back triggers every survival-pattern he possesses.

But he doesn't flee.

He stands trembling, legs braced, breath coming too-fast. The small-human is beside him, her hands making those constant-circles on his neck, her voice a stream of calm-sounds that wash over him like water.

"You're doing so well. I know it feels strange. I know it's scary. But look—nothing's hurting. Nothing's pulling. It's just weight. Just the saddle sitting there."

Just-weight. Just-saddle.

He tries to believe this. Tries to hold onto the knowing-new instead of the knowing-old.

The tall-calm-human approaches slow-steady and does something with the belly-straps. A slight tightening around his middle. The belly-band-thing is attached now, keeping the leather-thing in place.

His skin twitches-automatic, trying to shake off the wrongness. But the saddle doesn't shift, doesn't dig, doesn't hurt.

"Walk on," tall-calm-human says in her teaching-voice.

Walk? With this-thing on him?

Small-human guides him forward with the lead-rope. One step. Two. The saddle moves-with-him, shifting slightly with each stride. Strange-feeling. Heavy-different.

But not painful. Not hurting. Just... there.

They walk a small circle. The saddle stays settled. The humans stay calm. Nothing bad happens.

By the time they stop, his trembling has eased to occasional-shivers. The weight is still wrong-feeling but becoming less-terrifying. Becoming another thing-to-learn instead of thing-to-fear.

---

**SAGE**

The first time Promise walked a full circuit of the round pen with the saddle on, Sage had to blink back tears.

"Don't celebrate yet," Rebecca warned, but she was smiling. "We've got a long way to go."

A long way. But they were going there. Step by careful step, building on the foundation they'd laid, watching him instead of their own expectations.

The saddle work continued over the following days. Walk with the saddle. Trot with the saddle. Learn that the weight wasn't going anywhere, that it was part of him now when he was working. Promise adapted faster than Sage expected—or maybe not faster, maybe just at exactly the pace he needed, which happened to be quicker than her fear had allowed her to imagine.

Two weeks after the first saddling, Rebecca judged him ready for the next step.

"Weight over his back," she said. "Not sitting yet. Just leaning. Getting him used to the feel of a body up there."

Sage's stomach clenched. "Me?"

"You're the lightest of us. And you're the one he trusts most."

They started with Sage standing on a mounting block beside Promise while he wore the saddle. Just standing there. Letting him get used to her being above him, her presence at a different angle than he was used to.

He craned his neck around to look at her, clearly puzzled by this change in their usual positioning. But he didn't panic. Just... looked.

"Now lean," Rebecca instructed. "Put your weight across the saddle. Don't try to mount. Just lean."

Sage bent at the waist and let her upper body rest across Promise's back. She could feel him tense beneath her—every muscle going tight with that same freeze response. But she didn't pull away. Just lay there, breathing slowly, letting him feel her weight.

"Talk to him," Rebecca said.

"You're okay," Sage murmured against the saddle leather. "This is just me. Just Sage. I'm not going to hurt you. I'm just lying here. Feel how nothing bad is happening?"

Beneath her, Promise's breathing gradually slowed. The terrible tension began to ease, degree by degree.

"Good boy," she whispered. "Such a brave, good boy."

She stayed draped across him for a slow count of thirty. Then she straightened up, and Rebecca handed her an apple to give him.

"Again tomorrow," Rebecca said. "And the day after that. Until he's bored with it."

---

It took six days for Promise to stop tensing when Sage leaned across the saddle. By the seventh day, he barely reacted—just turned his head to see what she was doing, then went back to watching the barn cat stalk something in the grass.

"Next step," Rebecca said. "Weight in the stirrup."

This was different from leaning. Leaning spread her weight across his whole back. The stirrup concentrated it on one side—an unbalanced pressure that would shift and pull as she moved.

The first time Sage put her foot in the stirrup and pressed down, Promise sidled away from the strange sideways tug. They spent two days just getting him comfortable with her foot being there, with the slight pressure of her weight beginning to settle.

"More now," Rebecca instructed on the third day. "Really lean into it. Let him feel you starting to pull yourself up."

Sage gripped the front of the saddle and pressed down into the stirrup, lifting her other foot off the mounting block so her full weight hung from that one point. Promise's ears went back and his body tensed, but he didn't move. She held for a count of three, then stepped back down.

"Good boy. Again."

They repeated this dozens of times over the next several days. A little more weight each session. A little longer holding it. Promise learning that the strange sideways pull wasn't going to hurt him, that Sage hanging from his side was just another thing-to-accept.

By the fifth day, she was pulling herself high enough that her belly pressed against the saddle, her right leg dangling, her full weight suspended from the stirrup and her grip on the pommel. Promise stood solid beneath her, occasionally turning his head to watch this strange behavior with patient confusion.

"He's ready," Rebecca said.

Sage's heart hammered so hard she could feel it in her throat. "Are you sure?"

"No. I'm never sure. But he's as ready as he's going to get, and so are you." Rebecca moved to Promise's head, taking his lead rope. "I'll hold him. Riley will spot you. All you have to do is swing your leg over and sit. Don't ask him to move. Don't do anything. Just sit."

Just sit. As if it were simple. As if this moment wasn't the culmination of everything they'd worked for since March.

Sage climbed the mounting block for what felt like the hundredth time. Promise stood quiet below her, his ear flicked back toward her presence. Listening. Waiting to see what would happen next.

"It's just me," she told him, her voice barely above a whisper. "Just Sage. We're going to try something new. If you hate it, I'll get off. I promise."

She leaned across the saddle first, letting him feel her weight in the familiar way. He didn't tense. Didn't even shift his stance.

"Good boy. Now I'm going to swing my leg over. Slow and careful. Here I come."

---

**PROMISE**

The small-human's weight settles across him like it has for many days now. This is known-pattern. Boring-familiar.

But then something changes.

Her body shifts. Her leg moves—up and over, crossing his back, settling on the other side. And suddenly the weight isn't just pressing-down. It's surrounding-him. Weight on both sides. Weight across his spine. Weight in the stirrup-things that press against his sides.

*Rider.*

The word-concept explodes through him like fire. Rider means pain. Rider means force. Rider means before-time-horror of spurs and whip and hands that pull until his mouth bleeds.

He wants to leap-sideways, buck-violent, throw this weight off before the hurting starts—

But.

But the weight isn't hurting. The hands aren't pulling. The legs aren't kicking.

Small-human sits very-still above him. Her weight is there, present and undeniable, but it's just... sitting. Not demanding. Not forcing. Not causing pain.

"Easy," she whispers, and he can hear tears in her voice. "Easy, Promise. I'm just sitting here. I'm not going to hurt you. I'm never going to hurt you."

The tall-calm-human holds his head-rope, steady and calm. The other-human (Riley) stands nearby with hands ready to catch-if-needed.

Everyone is very-still. Very-quiet. Waiting for him to decide what happens next.

He could throw her. Could rear or buck or spin and send her crashing to the ground. His body knows how. Has done it before, in the before-time, when the pain became too-much-to-bear.

But.

But this isn't before-time. This is now-time, here-time, Hartwell-time. This is small-human who sat by his fence for days-uncounted making herself boring. Who offered apples from her flat-palm. Who cried into his neck after the gut-pain-time and told him she almost-lost-him.

This is small-human who has never once caused him pain-deliberate. Who stops when he says stop. Who waits when he needs to wait. Who has been learning-beside-him all along, both of them students, both of them scared, both of them brave.

He takes a breath. Then another.

The weight is still there. Still strange-heavy-different. But it's not hurting. It's just... her. Just small-human, sitting on his back, trembling as much as he is.

Both of them terrified. Both of them trusting anyway.

He lets out a long breath and drops his head slightly. The signal for acceptance-willing. For I'm-okay-now. For we-can-continue.

---

**SAGE**

She felt it when he released—the terrible tension flowing out of his body like water, his head lowering, his breath coming out in a long sigh.

He was letting her stay.

The tears she'd been fighting spilled over, running down her cheeks. She didn't try to wipe them away. Just sat there, on Promise's back, feeling the impossible reality of this moment.

Eight months ago, this horse had been destined for the knacker's yard. Broken, traumatized, written off as too damaged to save. And now here he was, voluntarily allowing a human to sit on his back. Choosing trust when everything in his history told him trust would be betrayed.

"That's enough," Rebecca said quietly. "Dismount. End on success."

Sage didn't want to get off. Wanted to stay here forever, in this moment of triumph and connection and overwhelming love. But Rebecca was right. They'd done what they came to do. Anything more would be pushing.

She swung her leg back over, careful and slow, and slid down Promise's side. Her knees nearly buckled when she hit the ground—exhaustion and emotion and relief all hitting at once.

Promise turned his head to look at her. His eye was soft, curious, perhaps a little confused. *That's it? That's all you wanted?*

She buried her face in his neck, arms wrapped around him, sobbing with a joy so intense it hurt.

"You did it," she choked out. "You impossible, beautiful, brave horse. You did it."

---

That evening, after Promise was settled in his paddock with extra hay and the horse blanket they'd started using against the cold nights, Sage sat on the fence rail and watched him graze.

Rebecca came to stand beside her. For a long moment, neither of them spoke.

"How do you feel?" Rebecca finally asked.

"I don't know." Sage wiped her eyes, still raw from crying. "Happy. Terrified. Like something important just happened and I'm not sure I understand what it was."

"Something important did happen." Rebecca's voice was thoughtful. "He chose to let you stay. After everything—after Thornton, after the colic, after all the times his trust has been tested—he chose to let a human sit on his back. That's not nothing."

"I know."

"Do you?" Rebecca turned to look at her. "Because I want to make sure you understand what you've built here. This isn't just training. This isn't just a horse learning to accept a rider. This is a damaged creature choosing to be vulnerable with the person who's earned his trust. That's rare, Sage. And it's precious."

Sage looked at Promise, peacefully grazing in the fading light. The scars on his shoulder were visible even from here—reminders of everything he'd survived. But they didn't define him anymore. They were just part of his story, like the colic, like the knacker's yard, like all the pain that had come before.

He'd survived all of it. And now he was learning to do more than survive.

"I sat on his back," she said, still hardly believing it. "I actually sat on his back."

"You did. Tomorrow we'll try again. And the day after that. Building the same way we've built everything else—slowly, carefully, watching him instead of watching our hopes."

Sage nodded. Tomorrow would come with its own challenges. There would be setbacks, probably. Moments of fear, moments of doubt. The work wasn't finished just because she'd sat on him once.

But tonight, in the November cold with the stars coming out and Promise safe in his paddock, she let herself feel the weight of what they'd accomplished.

All those months of patience. All those small trust-pieces adding up. All the mistakes and recoveries and lessons learned. It had led to this: a moment of connection so profound it defied everything Promise's history had taught him to expect.

He'd let her sit on his back.

And tomorrow, she would get to do it again.

**End of Chapter 24**
