# CHAPTER 39: HOMECOMING

The morning of departure dawned clear and mild, the kind of spring day that made promises about summer. Sage woke stiff and sore, her back against the front boards of Promise's stall, straw in her hair and the blue ribbon still clutched in her hand. She'd slept sitting up, wedged into the narrow space at his feet, and he had stood watch over her all night—his great dark body careful and still, his hooves never once shifting toward where she lay curled beneath him.

Now his head was lowered, his muzzle inches from her face, his breath warm and steady.

"I'm up," she said, and he snorted softly, lifting his head to let her rise.

The inn yard was already bustling when she emerged—grooms leading horses, carriages being loaded, the chaos of a fair ending and its participants scattering back to their ordinary lives. Riley was waiting by the wagon with Thunder already hitched to the front.

"You slept in the stall again."

"He needed company." Sage stretched, feeling every muscle protest. "Or I did. I'm not sure which."

Riley's smile was soft. "Mother's been asking after you. She wants to make sure you eat before we leave."

"I'll eat on the road. I want to get him home."

Loading Promise was easier this time—he walked up the ramp without hesitation, his eye calm, his body relaxed. He knew where they were going now. He'd done what he came to do, and home was waiting.

The journey back to Hartwell felt shorter than the journey out, though the distance was the same. Perhaps it was the lightness in Sage's chest, or the way Promise dozed peacefully in the wagon instead of standing rigid with tension. Or perhaps it was Riley riding Thunder alongside the carriage, catching Sage's eye through the window every few minutes and smiling.

They stopped once, at a stream, to water the horses. Promise came down the ramp with easy confidence, drank his fill, and stood patiently while Sage checked his legs for any swelling from the journey. Around them, the Cotswold countryside spread in rolling green hills, stone walls cutting the landscape into patterns that had been old when her grandmother was born.

"It's beautiful," Sage said, more to herself than anyone.

"It is." Rebecca had come to stand beside her, watching Promise graze at the edge of the stream. "I've lived here thirty years, and I still notice it."

"Do you ever miss... other places? Other lives you might have had?"

Rebecca considered this. "Sometimes. When I was young, I thought I might travel—see Paris, or Vienna, or the mountains in Switzerland that travelers are always rhapsodizing about." She shrugged. "But I found something better. A place where I could do work that mattered. People who let me be who I am." Her gaze moved to Jennifer, who was stretching her legs by the carriage. "A life that fits."

Sage watched Promise tear at the grass, his black coat gleaming in the afternoon light. "I think I've found that too."

"I know you have."

They reached Hartwell as the sun was beginning its descent toward the western hills. The approach was familiar now—the lane curving between hedgerows, the first glimpse of the barn roof, the sweep of paddocks with their grazing horses. Clover raised her head as the carriage rattled past, whickering a greeting. Whisper came trotting to the fence, curious about the commotion.

Tom met them in the yard, his young face bright with excitement. "They're back! Miss Morrison, they're saying in the village that you won—that Promise beat Lady Catherine Thornton—"

"News travels fast," Riley observed, dismounting from Thunder.

"The miller's cousin was at the fair. He rode home yesterday and told everyone." Tom was practically vibrating. "Is it true? Did Promise really let that little girl ride him?"

Sage smiled, the first full smile she'd managed since the speed demonstration. "It's true. All of it."

Promise backed out of the wagon with the same calm he'd shown loading, his hooves finding familiar ground. His head came up, ears pricked, nostrils flaring—taking in the smells of home. The paddock where he'd learned to trust. The barn where he'd spent the winter. The fields where Sage had taught him to carry her over fences.

He was home.

---

They settled the horses together—all of them, working in comfortable silence. Promise went into his stall and immediately set to investigating his hay as if checking that everything was exactly as he'd left it. Thunder was turned out in the near paddock, rolling with obvious relief at being free of tack and harness. Clover and Whisper came to the fence to touch noses with him, the little herd reassembling itself.

"I'll start dinner," Jennifer said, heading toward the house. "Nothing elaborate—we're all too tired for elaborate. But hot food and our own beds."

Rebecca followed her, and Tom went to finish evening chores, leaving Sage and Riley alone in the stable aisle. The light was golden now, slanting through the open doors, catching the dust motes that drifted in the air.

"You said you wanted to talk," Riley said quietly. "About our future. What it might look like."

Sage turned from Promise's stall. In the golden light, Riley looked tired and beautiful—dust on their shirt, hay in their hair, eyes warm with something that made Sage's chest ache.

"I did. I do." She took a breath. "Everything happened so fast at the fair. The competition, and Thornton, and Emma, and then the celebrating—there wasn't time to think about anything except the next moment."

"And now?"

"Now I keep thinking about what comes after." Sage leaned against the stall door. Inside, Promise had settled into eating, his presence steady and warm at her back. "Rebecca said Promise's stud fees will secure Hartwell's future. Four hundred guineas or more, she thinks. And she talked about me teaching Emma—about me having a gift."

"You do have a gift."

"I have training. And patience. And a very good teacher." Sage smiled slightly. "But the point is—there's a future here. A real one. Work I can do, a place I can be, people I—" She stopped, started again. "When I came here, I was running away from something terrible. Hartwell was refuge, not destination. But now..."

"Now it's home."

"Yes." The word came out simple and sure. "This is where I want to be. Not because I have nowhere else to go, but because this is where I belong."

Riley moved closer, close enough that Sage could feel the warmth of them in the cooling air. "I've been here my whole life, near enough. My mother brought me when I was four years old, and Rebecca..." Riley paused, something soft crossing their expression. "Rebecca became a second mother to me, especially after my father died. She taught me everything about horses, but more than that—she showed me what it meant to live by your convictions. To build a life that fits who you actually are, not who the world says you should be."

"She did the same for me," Sage said. "In one year, what she did for you in sixteen."

"The foundation was already there. She just helped you find it." Riley's hand found Sage's. "For a long time, I thought Hartwell was enough—the horses, the work, the family I'd always known. Then you arrived. And suddenly everything I thought was enough... wasn't."

"Riley—"

"Let me finish." Riley's grip tightened. "I told you I love you. I told you I want forever. But I never asked what that means to you. What kind of life you're imagining when you imagine us together."

Sage looked down at their joined hands—her fingers still bearing the calluses of a year's work, Riley's strong and sure and familiar. She thought about the question seriously, the way it deserved.

"I imagine mornings," she said slowly. "Waking up knowing you're there. Doing chores together, the way we've been doing. Watching you ride, watching you teach, learning from you the same way I learn from Rebecca." She paused. "I imagine being partners. In the work and in everything else. I imagine growing old here, with the horses and the fields and the family we've found."

"That's what I imagine too." Riley's voice was rough. "Exactly that. Every morning. Every day. For as long as we're given."

"Then that's what we'll do." Sage stepped closer, until there was no distance left between them. "We'll build that life. Together. Starting now."

Riley kissed her then—soft and sure, tasting of dust and travel and home. Behind them, Promise snorted and went back to his hay, utterly unconcerned with human romance. Somewhere outside, a thrush was singing its evening song. And the light through the stable doors was turning from gold to rose, painting everything in colors that looked like hope.

---

Later, after dinner, after the stories had been told again and Jennifer had cried happy tears and Rebecca had allowed herself a second glass of wine, Sage slipped out to the paddock.

The stars were emerging, one by one, in a sky that was deepening from blue to black. Promise stood at the fence, watching her approach, his dark shape barely visible against the darkness of the hills beyond.

"Hello, you," she said, reaching out to scratch the spot behind his ear.

He leaned into her hand, his breath warm against her arm. A year ago, he wouldn't have let her within ten feet. A year ago, he'd been a broken creature who'd forgotten how to trust. Now he stood here in the spring darkness, content and calm, pressing his nose against her shoulder the way he did when he wanted to feel her close.

"We did it," she told him. "Everything we set out to prove—we proved it. You're not what Thornton said. You're not vicious or dangerous or beyond saving. You're just a horse who needed someone to be patient. Someone to be kind."

Promise's ear flicked toward her voice.

"And you taught me something too. You taught me that fear doesn't have to win. That broken things can heal. That trust—real trust—is worth every moment of doubt along the way." She pressed her forehead against his neck, breathing in the warm horse-smell of him. "Thank you for choosing to try. Thank you for letting me in."

He stood there with her, solid and quiet and present, while the stars wheeled overhead and the spring night wrapped around them. Not far away, the house glowed with lamplight—Jennifer clearing the dishes, Rebecca making notes in her breeding ledger, Riley banking the fire. Her family. Her home. Her life, chosen and earned and fiercely her own.

Tomorrow there would be work to do. Letters to write, inquiries to answer, a young girl named Emma to teach. There would be new horses arriving eventually—broken ones, frightened ones, ones who needed what Hartwell could offer. The work would never end, not really. But that was all right. The work was the point.

Sage straightened, gave Promise one last scratch, and turned toward the house. Halfway across the yard, she looked back. He was still watching her, his dark shape outlined against the star-scattered sky, and she could have sworn—though she knew it was fancy—that he looked content.

"Good night," she called softly. "Sleep well."

Then she went inside, to the warmth and the light and the people waiting for her, and closed the door on the spring darkness.

The year was over. The story was complete.

But the life—the life was just beginning.
