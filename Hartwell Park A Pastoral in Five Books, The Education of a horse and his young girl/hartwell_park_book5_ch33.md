# CHAPTER 33: THE JOURNEY

Dawn came grey and soft, the sky the color of Promise's dapples in certain light. Sage woke stiff, her back pressed against the stall door where she'd fallen asleep sometime after midnight, horse blanket rough against her cheek. Through the bars, Promise's breathing—slow, steady, peaceful. He'd slept better than she had.

She stood, joints protesting, and brushed straw from her skirt. Promise was already watching her, ears pricked forward, that soft questioning look he gave her every morning now. *You're here. Good. What happens next?*

"Today," she said quietly, reaching through the bars to touch his nose. "Today we go."

His breath was warm against her palm. He didn't know what those words meant—couldn't understand about the fair or Thornton or any of it. He only knew she was here, and that was enough. She envied him that simplicity.

The household was stirring when she crossed the yard to the house. Kitchen light spilled golden through the window; she could smell bread and bacon. Jennifer met her at the door with a cup of tea already poured.

"You look like you slept in the barn."

"I did."

Jennifer's expression softened. "Eat something. It's a long journey, and you'll need your strength."

Sage wasn't hungry—her stomach was a knot of nerves—but she forced down bread and butter while the sky lightened outside. Rebecca appeared in riding clothes, already consulting a folded sheet of paper — the travel schedule she'd drawn up the night before, each stage of the journey timed and noted in her small precise hand. Riley came in from the barn smelling of hay and horses.

"Thunder and Marcus are eating. Oliver's already finished—he's always been an early riser." Riley's hand found Sage's shoulder, squeezed once. "Promise?"

"Watching everything. Ready, I think. More ready than I am."

"You're ready." Riley said it like fact, not reassurance. "You've done the work."

By the time the sun cleared the hills, they were loading the wagon. Marcus went in first, nervous but trusting Riley's familiar hands on his lead rope. Oliver followed, placid as always—the kind of horse who accepted whatever came because he'd learned that humans generally meant him no harm.

Promise was last.

Sage led him from his stall into the yard, where the wagon waited with its three passengers already shifting and settling. The morning air was cool, carrying the green smell of spring and the distant bleating of sheep on the hills. Promise's head came up, nostrils flaring at the unfamiliar vehicle—different from the one that had brought him here a year ago, but a wagon nonetheless.

"Easy," Sage murmured. "I know. I know what you remember."

She gave him time to look, to smell, to process. His body was tense under her hand, muscles bunched and ready. A year ago, he would have reared, struck, fought his way free. A year ago, he had arrived in a wagon like this one, terrified and half-broken, expecting only pain.

Now he stood trembling but still, because she was here, and she had never lied to him about safety.

"Whenever you're ready," she said.

It took three attempts. The first time, he got his front hooves on the ramp and backed off, whites of his eyes showing. The second time, he made it halfway before a noise from inside—Marcus stamping nervously—sent him scrambling backward. Sage's arms ached from holding the lead rope steady without pulling, without forcing.

The third time, Rebecca came to stand at the wagon's far end, holding an apple. She positioned herself at the exact centre of the ramp's sightline — deliberate, measured, the way she positioned herself for everything — and waited. She didn't call him or coax him. Just stood there, presence steady and patient, the apple visible in her palm.

Promise's ears swiveled toward her. He knew Rebecca—her steady presence, her calm authority. He had never once been hurt by her hands. And he knew apples.

He walked up the ramp.

Sage's eyes burned as she followed him in, securing him in the stall beside Marcus. His sides were dark with nervous sweat, but he was in. He'd chosen to be in.

"Good lad," Rebecca said, and gave him the apple.

---

The journey to Stow-on-the-Wold took most of the morning. Fifteen miles of Cotswold lanes, the wagon rattling along behind Rebecca's carriage while Riley rode Thunder alongside. Jennifer sat with Sage and Rebecca in the carriage, her steady presence a comfort. Tom, the stable boy, had been left in charge at Hartwell—feeding Clover, Whisper, and Belle, tending the garden, keeping the household running until their return.

Sage rode in the carriage with Rebecca, though she spent half the journey craning to look back at the wagon, watching for any sign of distress. The horses were quiet, lulled by the rhythm of movement. Promise's black head was just visible through the wagon's slats, ears swiveling at every new sound.

"He's fine," Rebecca said. "Better than fine. A year ago that horse would have kicked the wagon apart before we reached the gate."

"I know." Sage turned back to face forward. The countryside rolled past, stone walls dividing green fields, sheep dotting the hillsides like scattered clouds. "I keep waiting for something to go wrong."

"Nothing has gone wrong yet. And if it does, we'll manage it." Rebecca's voice was matter-of-fact. "That's what we've spent a year learning to do."

They passed through villages—Bourton-on-the-Water, the Slaughters, Naunton—each one busier than the last as they neared the fair. Other travelers on the road now: farmers leading horses, wagons loaded with goods, gentry in carriages heading to the same destination. More than once, heads turned to watch their little caravan pass—a fine black stallion visible in the wagon, drawing eyes even in transit.

"People are looking," Sage said.

"Let them look. That's the whole point."

By midday, the road climbed toward Stow, and Sage could see the town crowning its hill ahead. Even from a distance, she could tell something was different. The usually quiet market town was transformed—color and movement everywhere, the sound of voices and horses carrying on the wind.

"Ready?" Rebecca asked.

Sage wasn't sure she'd ever be ready. But she nodded anyway.

---

The fair was chaos.

Sage had imagined something orderly—stalls in neat rows, designated areas for showing. What she found was a sprawling, teeming mass of humanity and horseflesh spread across every available space. The market square was packed with traders hawking everything from harnesses to horse liniments. Lanes leading out of town were lined with horses tied to posts, to wagons, to anything that would hold them. On the slopes of Fair Hill, she could see the encampments—canvas tents, painted wagons, smoke rising from cooking fires.

And horses. Horses everywhere. Tied on long picket lines strung between posts. Tethered to wheels and fence rails. Being led through crowds, trotted down lanes, washed in the stream that ran along the town's edge. The sound of neighing layered over human voices, harness jingling, cart wheels on cobblestones.

Promise's reaction was immediate. Even in the wagon, she could see his head come up, his body go rigid. So many horses—so many mares—so much to process all at once.

"Easy," Sage called to him, though she wasn't sure he could hear her over the noise. "Easy, love. I'm here."

Rebecca directed the carriage toward the Crown Inn, one of the few establishments with actual stabling. The inn yard was already crowded—grooms rushing past, horses being led in and out, a gentleman arguing with the ostler about the quality of hay.

"Wait here," Rebecca said, and disappeared into the chaos.

She emerged ten minutes later, looking satisfied — the particular satisfaction she showed when a logistical problem had been solved to her specifications. "One stall. Last one available — I had to pay double the usual rate. But Promise will have proper shelter, and separation from the mare lines."

"Marcus and Oliver?"

"Picket line on Fair Hill. Riley's already taken them up—Thunder's in a paddock at the inn; he'll need rest after the journey. Marcus and Oliver will be fine together, and there's a canvas windbreak if the weather turns."

They unloaded Promise in the inn yard. He came down the ramp stiff-legged and snorting, head high, every muscle quivering with tension. The scent of mares hit him like a wave—Sage could see it in the flare of his nostrils, the arch of his neck, the way his hindquarters bunched. Mares everywhere, some of them surely in season. Stallion instinct, ancient and deep, that had nothing to do with training or trust.

"Promise." Sage kept her voice steady even though her heart was pounding. "Look at me."

His eye rolled toward her. Wild. For a terrible moment, she saw the horse from a year ago—the one who had charged fences and struck at handlers, the one everyone had called vicious.

"I'm here," she said. "I'm right here. You're safe."

Slowly—so slowly—the tension began to ease. Not disappear entirely. He was still a stallion surrounded by mares, his blood still singing with ancient imperatives. But he was also a horse who had spent a year learning that humans could be trusted. That Sage could be trusted. That the pulling didn't have to control him.

He dropped his head and blew out a long breath.

"Good lad," Sage whispered, and led him toward the stable.

---

The stall was nothing like Hartwell.

Narrow—barely wide enough for Promise to turn around. Rough wooden partitions on either side, high enough to prevent contact but low enough that he could see the horses in neighboring stalls. A stone manger in the corner, iron ring for tying, straw that smelled like it had been used before and not quite properly cleaned.

On one side, a rangy bay gelding who pinned his ears when Promise approached. On the other, a grey mare—not in season, thankfully, but still a mare.

Promise balked at the entrance.

"I know," Sage said. "It's not home. But it's safe, and it's only for a few days." She walked into the stall ahead of him, standing in the cramped space, letting him see that she wasn't afraid. "Come on. I'll stay with you until you settle."

He came. Reluctantly, each step careful and suspicious, but he came. Sage tied him to the ring with enough slack to let him move, then stood with him while his breathing slowed and his eyes stopped rolling.

The bay gelding on one side snorted. Promise's ears flattened, and for a moment Sage tensed—but he only stretched his neck over the partition, sniffed once, and withdrew. Establishing himself. Acknowledging. Not fighting.

"You're a gentleman," she told him. "Even here. Even when everything's strange and scary."

She stayed until his head began to lower, until he lipped at the hay in his manger with something like interest. Riley appeared at the stall door, smelling of wind and grass from the ride up Fair Hill.

"Thunder and the others are settled. Marcus is nervous but eating. Oliver's already made friends with a cob on the next line." Riley's eyes moved over Promise, assessing. "How is he?"

"Better than I expected. Better than I am, honestly."

"You should eat. Rest if you can. Tomorrow's going to be long."

Sage nodded, but she didn't move. Through the narrow window at the end of the stable row, she could see the chaos of the inn yard—horses being led past, grooms shouting, a carriage trying to maneuver through the crowd. Somewhere out there, in the larger chaos of the fair, Thornton's green and gold colors were flying. She'd seen them from the carriage, across the sprawl of Fair Hill. His encampment, his horses, his presence.

"He's here," she said quietly. "Thornton."

"We knew he would be."

"Knowing and seeing are different things."

Riley moved into the stall, standing close enough that their shoulders touched. Promise watched them with mild curiosity—these two humans who were always together now, who smelled of each other and moved around each other like horses who had learned each other's space.

"Whatever he does," Riley said, "you won't face it alone."

Sage leaned into that shoulder, just for a moment. Let herself be held by the closeness, the certainty. Then she straightened, brushed straw from her skirt, and turned back to Promise.

"Get some rest," she told him. "Tomorrow, we show them what you've become."

He blinked at her slowly, then went back to his hay. Content, for now, because she was here and the hay was edible and the strange horses on either side had been appropriately acknowledged. He didn't know about tomorrow. He didn't need to.

Sage envied him that. But she was learning—slowly, over the course of a year—that trust meant not needing to know everything. It meant believing that whatever came, she wouldn't face it alone.

She left the stable with Riley's hand in hers, walking out into the chaos of the fair, into the spring evening, into whatever came next.
