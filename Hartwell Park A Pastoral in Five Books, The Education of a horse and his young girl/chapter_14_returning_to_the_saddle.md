# Chapter 14: Returning to the Saddle

The first three days were the worst. Sage couldn't take a full breath without pain lancing through her left side, couldn't bend or twist or lift anything heavier than a teacup. She moved through the house like an old woman, one careful step at a time, and at night she had to sleep propped up on pillows because lying flat made her ribs scream.

Jennifer checked her twice daily, fingers gentle but thorough, feeling for any sign that the bruising was worsening, that cracked ribs might be broken instead. But each examination brought the same verdict: badly bruised, painful, but healing. Time and rest would fix it.

Time. Rest. Two things Sage had never been good at accepting.

She stood at her window on the third morning, watching Riley work Promise in the round pen. Even from this distance she could see the stallion's easy movement, the way his inside ear stayed fixed on Riley, listening. He'd progressed so much in the past weeks. And she was stuck inside, useless, while her body mended from her own mistake.

"Healing is work too," Jennifer said from the doorway, reading Sage's frustration in the set of her shoulders. "The hardest kind, sometimes."

"I should be out there. I should be—"

"You should be letting your body recover so you can go back to work properly." Jennifer crossed the room, stood beside her at the window. "Promise will still be there when you're healed. He's not going anywhere. And Thunder needs time too—his mouth is still tender. Riley's been hand-walking him, working him on the longe line, giving him easy days. When you both come back to work, you'll both be ready."

Sage nodded, though the forced inactivity chafed worse than the pain did.

***

On the fourth day, Rebecca appeared after breakfast with a proposition.

"Your ribs are healing. Jennifer says you can start moving around more, as long as you're careful. So today, you're going to help me with Promise's round pen session. Not working him yourself—just being present, learning by watching. And this afternoon, you're going to groom Clover. Light work. Building back your strength without risking re-injury."

It wasn't riding, but it was something. Sage accepted gratefully.

Promise's session that morning was a study in patience. Rebecca worked him for just ten minutes—walk, halt, direction change, halt again. Nothing strenuous. Nothing that pushed him beyond his comfort. But every response was precise, every command followed without hesitation.

"Watch his ears," Rebecca said quietly. "See how he's listening? That's what you want with every horse eventually. That quality of attention. That willingness to tune in to what you're asking."

Sage watched Promise move in smooth circles, his black coat gleaming in the June sun. His stride was loose and relaxed, nothing like the tense, frightened creature who'd arrived three months ago. This horse was confident. Comfortable. Choosing to work because the work made sense and brought rewards.

"How long until he's ready for a halter?" Sage asked.

"Another week, maybe two. He needs to be completely solid in his understanding of pressure and release, of voice commands, of moving forward and stopping on request. Once that's second nature, we'll introduce the halter. Then leading. Then..." Rebecca smiled. "Then we'll see what he's ready for next."

"Months before anyone rides him."

"Months or more. And that's if everything goes smoothly. If he has setbacks, if something spooks him, if he needs more time—we give him that time. Always." Rebecca called Promise to halt and he stopped immediately, turning to face her. She walked to him with a carrot, scratched his favorite spot behind his ears. "Patience isn't passive, Sage. It's active work. It's choosing to move at the right pace instead of the pace you want. That's what you forgot with Thunder—you wanted to move forward when he was telling you he needed to slow down."

The words weren't unkind, but they landed heavily nonetheless. Sage nodded, hand pressed against her aching ribs.

"I remember now. My ribs won't let me forget."

"Good. Use that. Let the physical memory reinforce the lesson, so when you're healed and back in the saddle, you remember what it cost to lose control."

***

That afternoon, Sage stood in Clover's stall with a grooming kit and a horse who stood like a stone, patient as always. Just brushing her was work—reaching up to her withers pulled at Sage's ribs, bending to do her legs made her breathe carefully through the pain. But Clover seemed to understand, standing perfectly still, making it as easy as possible.

"Thank you," Sage murmured, running the brush down Clover's neck. "For being patient with me. For teaching me when I didn't know anything. For not holding it against me that I'm slow and clumsy right now."

Clover's ear swiveled back, acknowledging. Her eyes were half-closed, peaceful. She'd always been like this—steady, reliable, safe. The foundation Sage had learned on. The horse who'd never punished her mistakes, who'd carried her through learning to post the trot and sit the canter and understand what partnership felt like.

By the time Sage finished grooming, her arms trembled and her ribs throbbed, but it was the good kind of tired. The kind that meant she'd done something useful, even if small.

Riley found her sitting on the bench outside Clover's stall, resting.

"How do you feel?" Riley asked.

"Sore. Frustrated. But better than yesterday."

"Good. Tomorrow you can groom Thunder, if you're up for it. He could use the attention, and it'll be good for both of you to reconnect before you ride him again."

Sage's stomach clenched at the thought. "What if he won't forgive me?"

"He already has. Horses don't work like that—they don't nurse grudges or plot revenge. He was angry in the moment because you hurt him. Now that moment's passed. When you work with him tomorrow, he'll meet you fresh, no judgment about what happened last week. The question is whether you can meet him the same way."

"I don't know if I can."

"Then fake it until it's true. Put your shoulders back, breathe deep as your ribs allow, and act like you trust yourself to do better. Eventually, you will."

***

The next day, Sage made her slow way to Thunder's stall with grooming kit in hand and doubt in her heart. The grey gelding was dozing in a patch of sun, one hip cocked, completely relaxed. When he heard her approach, his head came up, ears forward.

Not angry. Not afraid. Just: *Oh, it's you.*

Sage let herself into the stall carefully, moving slowly so he could see her intentions. Thunder watched but didn't move away. When she reached out to touch his neck, he allowed it, his skin warm under her palm.

"I'm sorry," she told him quietly. "For hurting you. For losing control. For being the kind of rider you couldn't trust." She ran her hand down his shoulder, feeling the muscle beneath, the solid warmth of him. "I'm going to do better. I promise."

Thunder blew out softly through his nose—not quite acceptance, not quite forgiveness, but not rejection either. Just: *We'll see.*

Sage groomed him with meticulous care, every brush stroke deliberate and gentle. When she reached his head, he lowered it willingly, let her work around his ears and forelock. She checked his mouth carefully—the cut at the corner was healing well, just a faint pink line now. The inflammation was gone.

"You're better," she said, relief flooding through her. "You're healing."

Thunder lipped at her sleeve, the gesture casual, familiar. The wariness from the day after the rear was gone. He'd moved on, the way horses did, living in each moment rather than carrying the weight of past ones.

If only humans could do the same.

By the time she finished grooming him, Sage's ribs were screaming, but her heart felt lighter. Thunder had let her back in. Had given her the fresh start Riley promised. Now she just had to earn it properly.

***

On the seventh day after the fall, Jennifer examined Sage's ribs and pronounced her healed enough to try riding—but only at a walk, only on Clover, and only for fifteen minutes.

"And if it hurts too much, you stop immediately," Jennifer warned. "No pushing through pain. Your ribs are still mending. One wrong move and you'll set yourself back weeks."

Sage promised, though the thought of finally getting back in the saddle made her almost giddy with relief.

Riley met her at the barn that afternoon with Clover already tacked up. The old mare stood placid as ever, ready to be the safe harbor she'd always been.

"Just mounting and dismounting might hurt," Riley warned. "Go slow. Use the mounting block. Let Clover stand perfectly still until you're settled."

Sage approached the mounting block with her heart in her throat. Putting her left foot in the stirrup pulled at her ribs. Swinging her right leg over made her gasp. But then she was sitting in the saddle, gathering reins, feeling Clover's solid warmth beneath her, and despite the pain it felt like coming home.

"How is it?" Riley called.

"Hurts. But I can do this."

"Walk her around the ring. Nothing fancy. Just feel what it's like to be back in the saddle."

Sage asked Clover to walk and the mare moved forward in her characteristic steady rhythm—one-two-three-four, one-two-three-four. For the first few strides, every movement jolted Sage's ribs. But she breathed through it, let her body remember how to follow the horse's motion, and gradually the pain lessened to a dull ache.

Around the ring they went. Once. Twice. Three times. And with each circuit, Sage felt something loosening in her chest that had nothing to do with her ribs. Fear, maybe. Or doubt. The question of whether she could do this, whether she could come back after such a bad fall and be the rider she needed to be.

"That's enough for today," Riley called after ten minutes. "Dismount carefully."

Getting down hurt almost as much as getting on had, but Sage managed it. She stood beside Clover, one hand on the mare's neck, trembling slightly from the exertion and the emotion both.

"You did it," Riley said, approaching. "First ride after a bad fall. That's the hardest one. Tomorrow will be easier."

"When can I ride Thunder again?"

"Not yet. Another few days on Clover first, building your strength back. Then maybe Whisper—she's forgiving but forward, which will be good for you. Thunder comes last. When you're fully healed and confident again. When you can ride him at his best, not when you're still weak and uncertain."

Sage nodded, understanding the wisdom even if she didn't like the timeline. Thunder deserved her at her best. Deserved a rider who was strong and confident and in control. Not someone still mending from losing that control.

She cooled Clover out with careful attention, groomed her, thanked her for being the steady teacher she'd always been. By the time she was done, her whole body ached, but it was the good kind of ache. The kind that came from doing real work, from taking real steps forward.

That evening, she made her way to Promise's fence despite her exhaustion. He came to greet her immediately, taking his apple with the gentle precision she'd come to love.

"I rode today," she told him, scratching behind his ears. "Just Clover, just walking. But I got back on. After falling, after hurting Thunder, after learning that losing control has consequences—I got back on anyway."

Promise leaned into her hand, comfortable. The evening sun turned his black coat to blue-black, made him look almost otherworldly in the golden light.

"That's what this is, I think. Not just learning to ride, but learning to get back on when you fall. Learning to do better after you've done worse. Learning that mistakes don't have to define you—they can teach you instead, if you let them."

She thought about the past week: the pain, the shame, the forced rest. Thought about Thunder's cut mouth healing, about her bruised ribs mending. Thought about Riley's words: *Horses meet you fresh, no judgment about what happened last week.*

Maybe she could learn to do the same for herself. To acknowledge the mistake, learn from it, and then move forward without carrying it like a weight.

"I'll keep getting better," she promised Promise. "For you. For Thunder. For all of them. And for myself. Because that's what it means to do this work—to keep learning, keep growing, keep choosing to be better than you were yesterday."

Promise pushed his nose against her shoulder, then turned to graze, comfortable in her presence. The gesture of a horse who'd learned to trust, slowly and carefully, one day at a time.

Just like Sage was learning to trust herself again.

One breath. One ride. One moment of choosing patience over force, control over impulse, learning over shame.

The work continued.

Healing continued.

Both of them, in their own ways, learning to be brave enough to try again.
