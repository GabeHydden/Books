# CHAPTER 38: AFTERMATH

The crowd began to disperse slowly, reluctantly, as if they weren't quite ready to let go of what they'd witnessed. Sage heard snatches of conversation as people drifted past—*did you see that horse... the girl couldn't have been more than twelve... gentle as a lamb, he was... and Thornton just walked away...*

She stood with her hand on Promise's neck, unable to move, unable to speak. The blue ribbon was still clutched in her other hand, the silk crumpled and damp from her grip. She should be celebrating. She should be laughing, talking, accepting congratulations. Instead she felt hollowed out, emptied of everything except a strange, trembling exhaustion.

"Sage."

Riley's voice, soft and close. A hand on her shoulder, warm and steady.

"Sage, come here."

She turned, and Riley was there, and suddenly she was crying—great heaving sobs that seemed to come from somewhere deeper than her chest, somewhere she hadn't known existed. Riley's arms came around her, holding her up, holding her together, while Promise stood quiet beside them and the last of the crowd pretended not to notice.

"It's all right," Riley murmured into her hair. "It's over. You did it. He did it. It's over."

"I was so scared." The words came out broken, barely intelligible. "When Emma got on—when I let go—I thought—"

"I know. I know." Riley's hand moved in slow circles on her back. "But you trusted him. And he proved you right."

Sage pressed her face into Riley's shoulder and let herself fall apart. All the fear she'd been holding back, all the doubt, all the desperate hope—it poured out of her in waves, leaving her shaking and raw and somehow lighter than she'd been in months.

When she finally pulled back, wiping her face with her sleeve, Rebecca was standing nearby with Jennifer. Both of them were smiling — Rebecca's small and controlled, already retreating back behind her usual composure now that the crisis had passed, Jennifer's wide and watery.

"Well," Rebecca said. Her hands were at her sides, placed there with that deliberate stillness, as though she needed to hold herself very still to keep the emotion from spilling into her body. "I think that's quite enough excitement for one day."

Sage laughed, the sound catching on a sob. "Is it always like this? When it works?"

"No." Rebecca moved closer, her hand finding Promise's neck beside Sage's. "Sometimes it's quieter. Sometimes you don't know if it's worked until years later." She paused. "But sometimes—rarely—you get a moment like this. When everything you've believed is proven right in front of the world."

Promise turned his head, his muzzle brushing against Sage's arm. She could feel the warmth of his breath, the solid reality of him. Her horse. Her triumph. Her proof.

"Come," Jennifer said gently. "Let's get him settled. There will be time for celebrations later."

---

They made it perhaps twenty feet before the first inquiry found them.

"Mrs. Morrison! A moment, if you please!"

A gentleman in a well-cut coat was hurrying toward them, his face eager. Behind him, Sage could see others hovering—a cluster of well-dressed men and women, all watching Promise with appraising eyes.

"Lord Whitmore," Rebecca said, inclining her head. "How may I help you?"

"That stallion." Lord Whitmore gestured toward Promise. "I've never seen anything like him. The movement, the temperament—and after what we just witnessed..." He shook his head in wonder. "I have three mares I'd like to breed next spring. What are your stud fees?"

Before Rebecca could answer, another voice cut in. "I'd like to discuss the same matter, Mrs. Morrison. My wife's mare is exceptional—Dorian's dam, actually—and I believe your stallion would be a perfect match."

"And I," said a third voice. "I've been looking for a sire with that kind of disposition. Flashy movement is common enough, but a horse who'll take care of his rider? That's worth its weight in gold."

Within minutes, Rebecca was surrounded. Sage stood to the side with Promise, watching as her aunt fielded questions, quoted prices, and arranged for follow-up conversations. The numbers being discussed made her head spin—fifty guineas for a single covering, with interest from at least six different breeders.

"That's three hundred guineas," Riley said quietly, appearing at her elbow. "At minimum. Probably more by the time she's done negotiating."

"Three hundred guineas," Sage repeated. The number seemed impossible. That was more than Hartwell Park earned in a year from all their training and sales combined.

"And that's just this fair." Riley was grinning. "Word will spread. By next spring, she'll have breeders coming from three counties away."

Promise stood quietly through all of it, his head low, his eye calm. He seemed unfazed by the attention, the voices, the hands that kept reaching out to touch his neck and shoulder. Every now and then his ear would flick toward Sage, as if checking that she was still there, still watching over him.

*I'm here*, she thought at him. *I'll always be here.*

---

The Crown Inn's private dining room was warm with candlelight and the smell of roasted meat. Rebecca had arranged for dinner to be served there—just the household, no outsiders, no more inquiries or congratulations or questions about stud fees.

Sage sat between Riley and Jennifer, too tired to eat much but too keyed up to sit still. The day kept replaying in her mind—the jumping course, the prize ceremony, Thornton's challenge, Emma's ride. It felt like a week's worth of events compressed into a single afternoon.

"I spoke with Lord Cavendish," Rebecca said, setting down her wine glass.

The table went quiet. Riley's hand found Sage's under the tablecloth.

"He was... understanding," Rebecca continued. "More understanding than I expected, actually. He said he'd seen Thunder carrying Riley through the fair, and he'd seen what happened with Promise, and he thought—" She paused, a small smile crossing her face. "He thought any establishment that could produce a horse like Promise probably knew what they were doing with their own animals."

"So he withdrew the offer?" Jennifer asked.

"Not exactly." Rebecca looked at Riley. "He said the offer stands, if we ever change our minds. But he also said he respects our decision to keep him."

Riley let out a breath Sage hadn't realized she'd been holding. "We're keeping him?"

"We're keeping him." Rebecca's voice was firm. "Thunder has been with us for six years. He's taught Sage to be brave, he's carried you through more miles than I can count, and he's part of this family." She reached for her wine glass again. "Two hundred guineas is a great deal of money. But some things aren't for sale."

Jennifer reached across the table to squeeze Rebecca's hand. "We'll manage," she said quietly. "We always do."

"We'll more than manage." Rebecca's smile widened, and Sage recognised the particular brightness in her eyes — the same look she got when a training plan came together, when the numbers aligned, when the system she'd built proved sound. This was how Rebecca felt triumph: not as a rush of emotion but as confirmation that her logic had been correct. "I've had eight serious inquiries about Promise's stud services today alone. Conservative estimates put next spring's breeding fees at four hundred guineas — possibly more if interest continues to grow."

Sage stared at her. "Four hundred guineas?"

"At minimum. And that's not counting the attention this will bring to our training services." Rebecca lifted her glass. "Ladies, I believe we can say with some confidence that Hartwell Park's future is secure."

They drank to that—even Sage, though her wine was well watered. The warmth spread through her chest, mixing with the exhaustion and the residual emotion until she felt almost drunk on the combination.

"There's something else," Rebecca said, after the toast. Her gaze settled on Sage. "I had a conversation with Squire Fairfax while you were settling Promise."

"About Emma?"

"About you." Rebecca set down her glass. "He was impressed. Not just by Promise—by the way you handled him, the way you handled the whole situation. He said he'd never seen anyone so young with such command of a difficult horse."

Sage felt her cheeks flush. "I just did what you taught me."

"You did more than that. You trusted your own judgment when it mattered most." Rebecca paused — not for effect, but with the quality of someone assembling a thought to its precise completion before releasing it. "The Squire asked if we might be willing to take Emma on as a student. Not for training — for teaching. He wants her to learn what you know. How you work with horses."

The flush deepened. "Me? Teach Emma?"

"Why not? You've been teaching Promise all year. You've been learning alongside me, absorbing everything I know." Rebecca's expression softened. "Sage, you have a gift. The same gift I recognized in myself, forty years ago. The Squire sees it. The crowd saw it today. The only person who doesn't seem to see it is you."

Sage looked down at her plate, overwhelmed. A year ago, she'd been a grieving girl with no prospects, sent to live with an aunt she barely knew. Now she was sitting in an inn, celebrating a victory that would secure her family's future, being told she had a gift worth teaching.

"I don't know what to say."

"Say you'll think about it." Rebecca rose from her chair. "But not tonight. Tonight, we celebrate. Tomorrow, we rest. And the day after—" She smiled. "The day after, we go home."

---

Later, after the dinner had wound down and Jennifer had retired and Rebecca had gone to check on the stabling arrangements — walking the aisle the way she always did, same order, same careful inspection of each horse, the routine unchanged whether they were at home or at a strange inn, Sage found herself standing outside Promise's stall in the quiet of the evening.

He was dozing, one hip cocked, his head low. The straw was fresh—Riley had seen to that—and the water bucket was full. In the dim light of the stable lantern, his black coat gleamed like silk.

"We did it," Sage said softly. "We actually did it."

Promise's ear flicked toward her voice, but he didn't lift his head. He was tired too. It had been a long day for both of them.

"She let herself into the narrow stall, moving slowly, and settled onto the straw at his feet. The space was cramped—a straight stall, not a proper box—but Promise shifted his weight carefully, making room for her against the front boards, his great dark bulk rising above her like a shelter."

"I was so scared," she told him. "When Emma got on. When I let go of your bridle. I thought—I don't know what I thought. That everything would fall apart. That you'd prove Thornton right."

Promise shifted his weight, his tail swishing lazily.

"But you didn't. You took care of her. You chose to be gentle." Sage's voice caught. "I'm so proud of you. I'm so proud of who you've become."

He lifted his head then, turning to look at her. In the lantern light, his eye was dark and deep and utterly calm. He took a step toward her until his muzzle was resting against her shoulder—the same gesture he'd offered that first night in the paddock, when she'd told him she was afraid too.

"We're going home tomorrow," she whispered. "Back to Hartwell. Back to the paddock where you first let me touch you, and the barn where you first let me ride you, and all the places where we learned to trust each other."

His breath was warm against her neck. She reached up to scratch the spot behind his ear that always made him lean into her hand.

"Thank you," she said. "For trusting me. For letting me in. For being brave enough to try."

Promise didn't answer—he couldn't, not in words. But he stayed there, his head heavy on her shoulder, his breathing slow and steady. And Sage stayed too, curled in the front of his stall, feeling the warmth of him beside her.

Outside, the fair was winding down. Tomorrow they would pack up and leave, and the road would carry them back to everything familiar. But tonight, in the quiet of the stable, there was nothing but this: a girl and her horse, resting together after a battle well won.

It was enough. It was more than enough.

It was everything.
