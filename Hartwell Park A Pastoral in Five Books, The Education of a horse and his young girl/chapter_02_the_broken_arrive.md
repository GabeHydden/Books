### Chapter 2: The Broken Arrive

The wagon lurches and his body slams against wood. Again. Pain-sharp in the shoulder that is already pain-dull from before, from the time when the man-who-hits used the hard-stick on that exact place until something inside went wrong-feeling. The rope around his neck pulls tight when he tries to keep his feet under him, chokes air away, makes panic rise like water rising in his chest.

Dark. The space is dark and too-small and smells of fear-sweat and wood and his own waste because there is nowhere else for it to go. How long? Time doesn't make sense anymore. Before-time there was pattern: morning-food, pain-work, evening-stable. Predictable pain is different than chaos-pain. At least he knew when it was coming.

Now everything is chaos.

The wagon hits something in the road and he goes down hard, knees buckling, hindquarters slamming into the side. The rope catches him wrong and for a moment he cannot breathe at all, just the terrible pressure on his throat and the sound that comes from him is not a sound he has made before. High and broken and—

Air. He gasps it in, scrambles upright again, every muscle shaking with the effort of staying on his feet. The men up front are talking, their voices rough and sharp like the hard-stick, but he has stopped trying to understand human-sounds. Human-sounds mean nothing. Sometimes they mean pain-soon, sometimes they mean pain-now, sometimes they mean nothing and then pain anyway.

Better not to listen. Better to focus on keeping breath, keeping feet, staying alive until the next moment and then the next.

Movement-memory floods him: the before-before time, when grass was endless and other-horses were warm-presence and his dam's smell meant safe. That time is so far away it might be dream-memory, might be something he invented when the pain got very bad and his mind went to the empty-place where nothing could reach him.

He does not go to the empty-place anymore. Going there means not-fighting, and not-fighting means more pain comes. The man-who-hits learned that the empty-place made him stop responding, and then the hard-stick came harder, the metal-mouth-pain came sharper, the rope-that-twists-neck came tighter. So he learned to stay present, stay feeling, stay hurting. At least hurt-and-present means he can still run if running becomes possible.

Running has not been possible for—time. Much time.

The wagon slows. His ears flick forward before he can stop them, hope sparking in his chest before he remembers hope is dangerous. Hope is the moment before disappointment, and disappointment is when the pain gets worse because he dared to expect different.

But the wagon is definitely slowing. The rhythm of the wheels changes, the men's voices get louder, and then—stopping. Complete stopping. The absence of movement makes him sway, his legs having learned to compensate for constant motion and now unlearning, now adjusting, now shaking with exhaustion and relief and terror because stopped-wagon means something-happens-next.

Something-happens-next is always bad.

Light floods in and he throws his head up, ears flat, trying to see everything at once. Two men, not the man-who-hits, but new-men which might be worse because at least he knows what the man-who-hits will do. These men smell of sweat and leather and impatience. One of them is saying words, sharp and fast, and the other one is reaching for the rope.

No.

He lurches back but there is nowhere to go, the wagon-walls are too close, and the rope goes tight around his neck again. Choking. He rears up, smashes his head on the wagon-roof, comes down hard and wrong on his already-damaged shoulder. White-hot pain shoots through him and he screams, the sound tearing from his throat.

"Vicious bastard," one of the men says, and even through the panic he knows that word. Vicious. The man-who-hits used that word right before the very-worst pain came. Right before the decision to send him to the place-of-ending.

The place-of-ending. He does not understand it completely but he understands enough: the smell of old blood, the terror-scent of other horses, the men with dead-eyes who work with sharp-metal. He was going there. The man-who-hits took him there, sold him to the dead-eyes men, left him in a pen that reeked of final-fear.

But then—confusion. A different human came. A human with a voice that did not have sharp-edges, who looked at him with eyes that were not dead and not angry. Who put coins in the dead-eyes man's hand and took the rope and led him back out, away from the smell of ending.

And now here. Wherever here is.

The rope jerks and he has no choice but to move forward, down the ramp that drops from the wagon's back. His legs betray him—the damaged one especially—and he stumbles, nearly goes down, catches himself only because falling means being dragged and being dragged means the rope tightens and tightening means no-air.

Solid ground under his feet. Grass-smell. Not mud, not stone, not the killing-floor. Grass.

One of the men curses as he thrashes, trying to see, trying to understand. Open space all around, more space than he has seen since—since the before-before time. Fences but far away. Sky above, huge and blue and nothing is crushing him, nothing is pressing close.

"Get him in the bloody paddock before he kills someone," the other man says, and they are pulling him, leading him, and every part of him screams to fight but fighting makes everything worse so he moves with them, stumbling, trying to keep the rope from choking, trying to understand where-is-here.

A gate. An enclosure. They pull him through and then—blessedly, miraculously—the rope loosens. One of them is doing something at his head, fingers fumbling with the halter, and he throws his weight backward, certain this is the moment when pain comes, when the hard-stick appears, when—

The halter comes off.

The men step back, quickly, and slam the gate between them and him.

He stands frozen, every muscle locked, waiting for what comes next. But the men are walking away. Simply walking away. Leaving him here in this space that is bigger than the wagon, bigger than the man-who-hits's stable, bigger than anything he has known in all the time-since-before.

His neck hurts where the rope was. His shoulder is fire-pain. His legs shake so badly he thinks he might fall down just from standing still. But he is alone. The men are walking toward buildings in the distance, their voices getting quieter, and he is alone in this space with grass under his feet and no rope on his neck and—

He does not understand.

Slowly, so slowly, he lowers his head. The grass is spring-green and soft-looking and his stomach clenches with hunger he has learned to ignore. Is this a trick? Will reaching for the grass trigger the pain? Will men appear with hard-sticks the moment he dares to—

He tears up a mouthful. Chews. Waits for punishment.

Nothing happens.

Another mouthful. The sweetness of it almost painful after so long with only the dry-bitter hay thrown into his stall when the man-who-hits remembered. He keeps expecting the interruption—the shout, the blow, the rope jerking his head up. But there is only grass and the sound of wind and in the distance, the low call of another horse.

Other horses. He can smell them on the wind, many horses, their scent carrying across the space. Not fear-scent. Not pain-scent. Just... horse-scent. Alive and present and calm in a way that makes no sense to him.

Where is this place?

He takes three steps forward, testing. The fence is there but far away, much farther than he can remember having. The space makes him feel exposed and vulnerable but also—something else. Something he does not have a name for. The opposite of trapped.

Four more steps. Five. The grass continues, endless green spreading in every direction. He grazes as he moves, unable to stop himself, his body demanding fuel even as his mind stays hypervigilant, watching for the trap, the trick, the moment when this becomes pain again.

At the far end of the enclosure he stops, turns, looks back at the gate. The men are gone. The wagon is turning, starting to leave, the sound of its wheels fading into distance. Beyond the paddock he can see buildings—a house made of honey-colored stone, a barn with doors standing open, other fences marking other spaces.

And there, at a window in the big house. A figure.

Small-human. Female-human, he thinks, though human-gender is not something he has learned to reliably identify. She is very still, just standing there looking at him. Not moving toward him. Not calling out. Not doing anything except watching.

He watches back.

The stillness is wrong. Humans do not stay still like that. Humans move-fast, shout-loud, come-at-him with hands and ropes and metal things. This human is doing none of that. She is just... there. Separate from him by distance and fencing and the space between, but there.

Something about her stillness reminds him of the other-horses when they are deciding whether a thing is threat or not-threat. The freeze-and-assess that comes before the decision to flee or stay. She is doing that. Freezing. Assessing.

He understands that response. Lives that response. Has lived nothing but that response for all of time-since-before.

A sound escapes him—not quite a whinny, more like a question. The distance is too far for her to answer even if she had answer-sounds. But she shifts slightly, her hand moving to her throat in a gesture he does not understand, and then she is still again.

They stand like that for a time that might be long or might be short—time is still strange, still unreliable. Two creatures frozen in assessment, neither trusting enough to move, both trapped in the space between hope and certainty that hope is foolish.

Finally she turns away from the window, disappearing into the depths of the house.

He is alone again.

The aloneness should be relief—no human means no immediate danger. But something in his chest tightens anyway, some feeling he cannot name. She was there and now she is not-there and the absence feels... wrong. Different than the wrongness he is used to.

He shakes his head hard, making his mane fly, trying to dispel the confusion. Focuses instead on what is real: grass under feet, space around body, no rope on neck, pain in shoulder but pain he has had before and survived. These things he can understand. These things are facts.

The watching-human, the stillness, the strange tightness in his chest when she left—these things are not facts. These things are confusion-feelings, and confusion-feelings are dangerous because they distract from staying alive.

He returns to grazing, forcing himself to focus on the immediate. Grass. Water—he can smell it, a trough at the other end of the paddock. Fence lines to memorize. Possible escape routes if escape becomes necessary.

But his ears keep flicking toward the house. Toward the window where she stood.

And underneath the vigilance, underneath the pain and the exhaustion and the learned-certainty that kindness is a lie humans tell before hurting you worse—underneath all of that is something very small and very fragile.

A wondering.

What if this place is different?

He crushes the thought immediately. Different is not possible. Different is the trap. Different is what they want him to believe right before they show him it was all the same after all, that pain is the only constant, that trust is the fastest way to die.

But the grass is real. The space is real. The watching-human with her stillness—she was real too.

He does not know what to do with real things that do not hurt him.

So he grazes, and he waits, and he tries not to hope.

Hope is the most dangerous thing of all.
