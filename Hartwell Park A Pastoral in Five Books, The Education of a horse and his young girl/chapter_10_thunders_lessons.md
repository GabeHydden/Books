# Chapter 10: Thunder's Lessons

Thunder stood in cross-ties in the barn aisle, his dappled grey coat gleaming from the grooming Sage had just finished. Sixteen hands of muscle and opinion, watching her with one dark eye as she reached for the saddle. His ears swiveled forward—interested, assessing.

"Take your time," Riley said from where he leaned against the tack room door. "Let him see everything before it touches him."

Sage held the saddle where Thunder could investigate it, letting him lip at the leather, blow his breath across the pommel. Clover had never cared about any of this—patient Clover, who'd stood like a stone while Sage fumbled with buckles and straps. But Thunder cared. Thunder had opinions about everything.

She settled the saddle on his back, moving slowly, waiting for his acceptance before cinching the girth. He shifted his weight but didn't protest. When she reached under his belly for the girth strap, his skin twitched.

"Easy," she murmured, and felt rather than saw Riley's approving nod.

It took ten minutes to tack him up—twice as long as it took with Clover. By the time she led him to the exercise ring, her palms were already damp.

The morning sun beat down with summer's full weight now, three days into June. In the distance, she could see Rebecca working Promise in the round pen again, the stallion's black form moving in smooth circles. He'd done three sessions now, each one showing more understanding, less hesitation. Yesterday he'd responded to "whoa" on the first request.

She was proud of him. And nervous about disappointing him by proving incompetent on Thunder.

Riley held Thunder's head while Sage mounted. The ground seemed farther away than it had on Clover, and when she settled into the saddle she could feel the difference immediately—the breadth of his barrel between her legs, the coiled energy in his hindquarters, the way he shifted his weight with barely contained impatience.

"Walk him around the ring a few times," Riley said, stepping back. "Let him get used to you. Let you get used to him."

Sage gathered her reins, took a breath, and squeezed with her legs.

Thunder moved forward with a stride that made Clover's walk seem like shuffling. Sage had to adjust her seat immediately, following the bigger movement, posting even at the walk just to find her balance. He pulled at the bit, testing—*How much rein will you give me? How much can I take?*

"Half-halt," Riley called. "Close your fingers, sit deep, tell him to wait for you."

She closed her fingers on the rein. Thunder tossed his head but slowed marginally. Not a fight, just a question: *Are you sure?*

"Yes," she told him, sitting deeper. "I'm sure."

They made one circuit of the ring, then another. By the third, Sage had found something like rhythm with his walk, though it felt precarious compared to Clover's steady four-beat. Thunder flicked an ear back toward her—listening, but also waiting. Waiting to see what she'd ask for. Waiting to see if she could handle it when he gave it.

"Good," Riley said. "Now ask for trot. Posting trot, just like you learned. He'll test you, but you know how to ride it."

Did she? Sage thought of Clover's gentle trot, the one that had taken her weeks to learn to post. Thunder's trot would be nothing like that.

She squeezed with her legs, felt Thunder's immediate response—*Finally*—and then she was posting, rising and falling with a two-beat rhythm that was faster and bigger and more powerful than anything Clover had ever given her. For three strides she had it, moving with him, balanced and controlled. Then he surged forward, taking more rein than she'd offered, and she lost her rhythm, bouncing hard in the saddle for two beats before catching herself.

"Don't pull," Riley called. "Half-halt and sit it until you find the rhythm again."

Easier said than done. Thunder had opinions about pace, and his opinion was *faster*. Sage closed her fingers, sat as deep as she could, tried to slow her own breathing. After what felt like an eternity but was probably five strides, she found his rhythm again, started posting, felt the satisfaction of moving with him instead of against him.

"Good!" Riley's voice carried approval. "Two more circuits, then bring him back to walk."

The two circuits felt like running a gauntlet. Every time she found her rhythm, Thunder would test—surge forward, drift toward the rail, toss his head. Each time she had to adjust, regain her balance, prove she could handle him. By the time Riley called for the walk transition, Sage's legs were shaking and her hands ached from maintaining contact with his mouth.

But she'd done it. She'd ridden Thunder's trot without falling off.

"Well done," Riley said when she finally halted. "That's harder than it looks."

"That's *much* harder than Clover." Sage stroked Thunder's neck with a trembling hand. He was barely sweating, while she felt like she'd run a mile.

"Thunder makes you earn every step. But that's the point. Clover taught you the basics. Thunder's teaching you to ride through difficulty. To stay balanced when your horse is braver than you are, stronger than you are, more sure of himself than you are."

"What if I'm not ready?"

"You weren't ready." Riley's smile held a challenge. "You did it anyway. That's how you get ready—by doing the thing you're not ready for and learning from it."

***

The second ride, that afternoon after the worst of the heat had passed, went worse.

Sage had felt confident after the morning session. She'd ridden Thunder's trot. She'd managed his testing. She could do this.

But confidence without earned competence was dangerous, as she was about to learn.

Thunder must have sensed the shift in her—that edge of pride that crept into how she gathered her reins, how she asked for the trot. Because when she squeezed her legs this time, he gave her more than trot. He gave her something faster, something that felt like flying and falling simultaneously, and Sage barely had time to realize he'd shifted into canter before her seat bounced wrong and suddenly the ground was rushing up to meet her.

She landed hard on her right side, the impact driving the air from her lungs. For a moment she couldn't breathe, couldn't think, could only lie there in the packed earth of the ring while her vision went white around the edges.

Thunder had stopped immediately, standing ten feet away with his head turned toward her. Not fleeing. Not panicking. Just standing there with an expression that seemed to say: *Well. That happened.*

"Don't move yet," Riley said, suddenly beside her. "Take your time. Where does it hurt?"

"Everywhere," Sage managed, and it came out more as a wheeze than words.

"Can you breathe? Move your fingers? Your toes?"

She could. Everything worked. Nothing broken, just bruised—body and pride both. Riley helped her sit up slowly, then stand, testing each joint before letting her take her full weight.

"What happened?" Riley asked.

"I got cocky," Sage said, and felt tears of frustration burning behind her eyes. "I thought because I'd ridden his trot this morning, I was ready for anything. I wasn't paying attention to what he was giving me. I wasn't ready for the canter and I bounced wrong and—"

"And now you know why we don't rush," Riley said, not unkindly. "Thunder was testing you. You failed the test. But you're standing up, which means you can try again."

"Now?"

"Not this second. Walk him in hand for a few minutes so he knows the fall wasn't his fault. Groom him and put him away. Think about what you learned. Then tomorrow, you ride him again."

Sage looked at Thunder, who was watching her with those intelligent eyes. Not malicious. Not mean. Just honest about what she'd asked for and what she'd been capable of handling.

She walked over to him, took his reins, rubbed his forehead. "That was my fault," she told him. "Not yours."

His ear flicked. *Obviously.*

***

That evening, Sage sat on the fence of Promise's paddock, watching him graze in the golden light of late afternoon. Her right hip throbbed where she'd landed, and she knew tomorrow would bring spectacular bruises. But she'd walked Thunder afterward as Riley instructed, groomed him carefully, thanked him for the lesson even if it hurt to learn it.

Promise lifted his head, saw her, and walked over to the fence. She produced an apple from her pocket—she always had one now—and he took it with his usual delicate precision. While he crunched it, she scratched behind his ears.

"You should have seen Thunder today," she told him. His ear swiveled toward her voice, listening. "He's magnificent when he moves. So much power. Riley says when I can ride him properly—really ride him, not just hang on—it'll feel like dancing. Like the horse is thinking with you instead of under you."

Promise pushed his nose against her shoulder—the gesture he'd developed over the past weeks, the one that meant *here, with you, comfortable*.

"That's what I want," she continued, scratching the spot behind his ears that made him lean into her hand. "Not to make you do anything. But to move together, like Rebecca does with the other horses when she rides. Like they're having a conversation and both of them understand what's being said. Like partnership."

She thought of watching Rebecca ride Whisper over jumps last week—the way horse and rider seemed to become one creature, flowing over obstacles with perfect understanding. The way Whisper's ears had been forward with joy, not pinned with resentment.

"Thunder's teaching me what that takes. How precise you have to be with your body, your balance, your hands. How every shift in your weight means something to the horse. How riding isn't about controlling another creature—it's about learning to speak their language well enough to suggest things. And having them choose to say yes."

Promise blew out through his nose—that soft sound that had become familiar—and turned his head to lip at her sleeve. Not anxious. Just companionable. Just being together while the sun sank lower and the swallows cut patterns against the sky.

***

The next morning, Sage woke sore in places she didn't know could be sore. Jennifer appeared at breakfast with arnica salve and knowing sympathy.

"Riley told me about yesterday," Jennifer said, working the salve into Sage's bruised hip with gentle fingers. "Your first fall. How do you feel about it?"

"Embarrassed. Frustrated. Determined not to let it happen again."

"Good answers, all of them." Jennifer recapped the tin. "But the most important thing is that you're getting back on today. That's what separates riders from people who tried riding once."

"Riley said the same thing."

"Because it's true. Horses are honest about what you're ready for. Thunder told you that you weren't as competent as you thought. Now you know. Now you can work on getting there."

Sage dressed carefully, every movement pulling at tender muscles. When she reached the barn, Riley had Thunder already cross-tied, and Rebecca was there too, which was unusual for morning riding lessons.

"I hear yesterday was educational," Rebecca said without preamble.

"That's one word for it."

"Good. You needed humility. Clover taught you the basics, but she's so forgiving that you could make mistakes without consequence. Thunder doesn't work that way. He requires competence. And right now, you don't have enough of it."

The words stung, but Sage couldn't argue with them.

"So today," Rebecca continued, "we work on building that competence. No trotting. No cantering. Just walking—but walking with purpose. Steering precisely. Transitions that are smooth, not abrupt. Circles that are round, not wandering ovals. Teaching your body to communicate with his body without your brain having to think about every movement."

"That sounds harder than cantering," Sage said.

"It is," Riley agreed. "But it's the foundation. You skipped ahead yesterday and learned why that doesn't work. Today we go back to basics."

***

She rode Thunder for an hour, and it was the most exhausting hour she'd spent in a saddle yet. Walking. Just walking. But walking in perfect circles, in figure-eights, in serpentines. Halting from walk and walking from halt with invisible aids. Asking him to yield his shoulder, to move his hindquarters, to bend through his ribcage around her inside leg.

Every time her attention wandered, Thunder drifted. Every time her hands got hard, he threw his head. Every time her seat shifted, he stumbled or spooked at nothing—showing her, with brutal honesty, exactly where her riding fell apart.

By the end of the hour, Sage could barely feel her legs. But she could also feel something else: a connection with Thunder that hadn't existed before. A conversation happening through reins and seat and legs, growing more precise with each circuit.

"Better," Rebecca said when she finally called Sage to halt. "Much better. You're starting to understand what riding actually is."

"What is it?" Sage asked, though she thought she might know.

"Constant conversation. Constant adjustment. Constant awareness of what your body is doing and what his body is doing and how they fit together. Clover let you be lazy. Thunder won't. And someday, Promise will need you to have learned from Thunder what Clover couldn't teach you."

Sage looked across the property to where Promise's paddock lay in morning sun. She could just make out his black shape against the green grass.

"How many more falls before I'm ready?" she asked.

"However many it takes," Rebecca said. "The question isn't whether you'll fall. The question is whether you'll get back on. Whether you'll learn from each fall instead of letting fear win."

"I'll get back on."

"Good. Because you can't rehabilitate traumatized horses from the ground. Well—you can, but eventually someone has to ride them. Someone has to prove to them that weight on their back doesn't mean pain. That tack doesn't mean torture. That human direction can be partnership instead of domination. And that someone needs to know what they're doing."

Riley appeared with a lead rope. "Cool him out, groom him, think about what you learned. Then we'll watch Rebecca work Promise in the round pen."

***

Promise's session was brief—ten minutes of work, no more. Rebecca sent him around the pen at a walk, called him to halt, rewarded him with carrots and praise. Then she changed direction and did it again. The whole thing looked simple, almost boring.

But Sage, watching from outside the panels, saw what was really happening. Saw Promise's inside ear locked on Rebecca, listening. Saw the moment his stride evened out, understanding what was being asked. Saw him halt smoothly when Rebecca said "whoa," then turn to face her, waiting for the next request.

"He's learning to listen," Riley said quietly beside her. "Learning that human direction can be clear and consistent. That the right answer brings relief and reward. That work can be a conversation instead of a battle."

"When will he be ready for a halter?"

"Not yet. He needs to be completely confident in this first. Needs to understand the language of pressure and release, of voice commands, of what we're asking from him. Then we can add equipment. But not before."

Sage thought of yesterday's fall, of asking Thunder for more than she was ready to handle. "Patience," she said.

"Patience," Riley agreed. "For both of you."

Rebecca finished the session and made a fuss over Promise—scratching his favorite spot behind his ears, offering three carrots, praising him with voice and touch. He leaned into her hands, comfortable, trusting. 

Then Rebecca turned toward Sage and smiled. "He's doing well. Better than I expected, actually. He's curious about the work now, not just tolerating it. That's the difference between a horse who'll learn quickly and one who fights every step."

"Because he's choosing it," Sage said.

"Because he's choosing it," Rebecca confirmed. "Just like you're choosing to get back on Thunder tomorrow, even though you're sore and probably a little frightened. That's the work. Choosing it every day, even when it's hard. Especially when it's hard."

***

That afternoon, Sage watched Promise's second session from outside the round pen. Rebecca asked him to change direction—stepping to block his forward path, redirecting his energy. Promise balked, ears back, unsure. Rebecca asked again, patient but firm. On the third ask, he turned, found the new direction, and Rebecca released immediately.

"He's confused," Sage said to Riley. "He keeps looking for the catch. Like he's waiting for the punishment when he gets it wrong."

"He is waiting for that. Takes a long time to unlearn."

"Maybe if she didn't use the whip at all. Even held low like that—he sees it and his whole body changes. His neck goes rigid."

Riley glanced at her. "Rebecca knows what she's doing with the whip."

"I know she does. But look at him." Sage gestured toward the pen, where Promise had stopped again, body braced, eyes on the longe whip in Rebecca's left hand. "Every time she shifts it, he flinches. What if she tried a session without it entirely? Just her body and voice?"

Riley was quiet, watching. In the pen, Rebecca sent Promise forward again, and this time he moved willingly enough, but his inside ear stayed pinned back—not listening, Sage thought. Monitoring. Tracking the whip the way she'd once tracked every hand that came near her.

After the session, Sage approached Rebecca at the gate. "I think the whip is slowing him down."

Rebecca looked at her. The direct, assessing look that could feel like being pinned to a board. "The longe whip is a communication tool. It extends my reach. Without it, I can't effectively direct him from the center of the pen."

"But he's not hearing it as communication. He's hearing it as threat. His whole posture changes when you pick it up."

"I'm aware of his response to the whip." Rebecca's voice had gone precise, clipped—the register she used when her methods were questioned. "I've been working with traumatized horses for fifteen years, Sage. The whip is held low, never raised above waist height, never used to strike. He'll desensitize to its presence through repeated exposure to its harmless use."

"Or he'll just learn to tolerate it while his body stays terrified." The words came out sharper than Sage intended. "I can see it in him. You taught me to see it."

The silence that followed had edges. Riley had gone very still by the fence.

Rebecca closed the gate with careful precision. "You're not wrong about what you're seeing. But you're drawing the wrong conclusion from it. Removing the tool doesn't remove the fear—it just removes my ability to communicate clearly while we work through the fear." She met Sage's eyes. "I understand why you identify with his response to the whip. But identification isn't the same as expertise."

The words landed like a slap. Not cruel—accurate. But accuracy could cut as well as cruelty.

Sage said nothing. Turned and walked to the barn. Behind her, she heard Riley say something low to Rebecca, and Rebecca's short reply.

She spent the rest of the afternoon grooming horses, working through the sting of it. Rebecca was probably right. Rebecca was almost always right about horses. But Sage had seen Promise's body, had read the tension that Rebecca apparently considered acceptable, and she couldn't make herself believe that what she'd seen didn't matter.

The friction didn't resolve that day. At supper, Rebecca discussed the training schedule as though nothing had happened. Sage listened, contributed where asked, and didn't bring up the whip again. But something had shifted between them—not broken, but strained. The first real disagreement between student and teacher, and neither of them quite knew what to do with it.
