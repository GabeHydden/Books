# EPILOGUE: THREE SPRINGS LATER

*April 1817*

The chestnut mare arrived on a Tuesday, in a wagon that rattled up the lane just as the morning mist was burning off the fields.

Sage heard it before she saw it—the clatter of wheels, the nervous stamp of hooves, a high whinny that carried an edge of panic. She set down her tea cup and rose from the kitchen table, catching Riley's eye across the room.

"That'll be the Ashworth mare."

"The one who threw her rider into the fence?"

"The same." Sage was already reaching for her coat. "Lord Ashworth's daughter. Broke her collarbone. The mare's been locked in a stall for three months while they decided what to do with her."

Riley grimaced. "Three months. She'll be half-mad."

"Probably more than half." Sage paused at the door. "I'll need you on the gate. And send Tom for Rebecca—she'll want to see this."

The wagon had stopped in the yard by the time Sage reached it. The driver—a nervous young groom in Ashworth livery—was already climbing down, his face pale.

"Miss Morrison? I'm to deliver the mare. Lord Ashworth said you'd know what to do with her."

"I do." Sage moved to the back of the wagon, keeping her movements slow and deliberate. Inside, she could hear the mare shifting, her hooves scraping against the boards, her breathing harsh and rapid. "What's her name?"

"Ember, miss. On account of her color." The groom swallowed. "She was a good horse, before. Sweet-tempered. But after the accident—"

"I understand." Sage had heard the story already, in Lord Ashworth's letter. A hunt gone wrong, a fence that was higher than it looked, a fall that could have killed his daughter. The mare had panicked, and in her panic she'd struck out, and now she was labeled dangerous. The Ashworths wanted her gone but couldn't bring themselves to send her to the knacker.

So they'd sent her to Hartwell instead.

The ramp came down with a clatter, and Ember exploded backward out of the wagon—a blur of chestnut and white-rimmed eyes, her hooves striking sparks on the cobblestones. She hit the end of the lead rope and reared, screaming, and the groom yelped and dropped the rope entirely.

"Gate!" Sage called, and Riley was already there, swinging it open. Ember bolted through into the small paddock beyond—the same paddock where Promise had spent his first terrified weeks, three years ago.

She ran the fence line, looking for escape. Finding none, she wheeled and faced them, her sides heaving, her neck dark with sweat. A handsome mare, Sage could see—well-bred, well-made, probably lovely under saddle before fear had unmade her. Now she stood with her head high and her ears flat, every line of her body screaming *stay away*.

"Well," Rebecca said, appearing at Sage's elbow. She moved more slowly these days, her joints protesting the damp mornings, but her eyes were as sharp as ever. "That's a frightened horse."

"Three months in a stall after a bad fall. She doesn't know what's real anymore."

"No." Rebecca watched the mare pace. "But she will. Given time."

Sage nodded. She knew what came next—the patient waiting, the quiet presence, the slow rebuilding of trust. She'd done it before. She'd do it again.

"I'll sit with her this afternoon," she said. "Start the way we always start."

"Good." Rebecca's hand found her shoulder, squeezed once. "I'll leave you to it."

---

The morning passed in the rhythm that had become familiar over three years. Sage checked on the yearlings in the north field—two colts and a filly, all Promise's get, all showing his calm temperament and floating trot. She reviewed the breeding schedule with Riley, confirming which mares were due to arrive next month and which local breeders had inquired about covering fees. She answered three letters, wrote two more, and updated the training log she'd been keeping since her second year at Hartwell.

At midday, Emma Fairfax arrived on her bay gelding, her riding habit dusty from the road.

"I came as soon as I heard." Emma was sixteen now, tall and confident, her childhood softness sharpened into something more determined. She'd been coming to Hartwell twice a week for two years, learning everything Sage could teach her. "Is it true? The Ashworth mare?"

"She's in the east paddock. Scared half to death."

Emma's eyes lit with the particular brightness that meant she was already thinking about the work ahead. "Can I help?"

"Eventually. For now, she needs quiet. But you can watch, if you like. See how it starts."

They walked together to the paddock fence, where Ember still paced. The mare's panic had subsided into wariness—she no longer ran the fence line, but she stood as far from the humans as possible, her body angled for flight.

"She's beautiful," Emma said softly. "Under all that fear."

"They usually are." Sage leaned against the fence, her posture deliberately relaxed. "Fear doesn't make them ugly. It just makes them hard to see clearly."

"How long will it take? To reach her?"

Sage considered the question. Three years ago, she would have given a hopeful answer—weeks, maybe, or a few months. Now she knew better.

"As long as it takes. Could be weeks. Could be a year. Could be longer." She glanced at Emma. "That's the first lesson. You don't get to decide the timeline. They do."

Emma nodded slowly, absorbing this. She'd heard it before—Sage had told her a dozen times—but some lessons needed repeating until they settled into bone.

"I'll come back Thursday," Emma said. "To watch. And maybe—if she's ready—to sit with you."

"Thursday," Sage agreed. "Bring a book. Something long."

---

The afternoon sun was warm on Sage's back as she settled onto the grass outside Ember's paddock. Not inside—not yet. That would come later, when the mare had learned that human presence didn't always mean pain. For now, Sage sat with her back against the fence post, a book open in her lap, and simply existed in Ember's world.

The mare watched her from the far corner, ears swiveling. Suspicious. Uncertain. But not running.

That was something. That was the beginning.

Sage read for a while—a novel Jennifer had lent her, something about a woman navigating society's expectations—but mostly she just sat. Breathing. Being. Letting Ember grow accustomed to her presence the way water grows accustomed to a stone.

Footsteps in the grass behind her. She didn't turn—she knew the sound of Riley's walk as well as she knew her own heartbeat.

"She's stopped pacing," Riley observed, settling onto the grass beside her.

"An hour ago. She's been watching me ever since."

"Progress."

"The very beginning of it." Sage closed her book, letting her hand find Riley's. Three years of mornings and evenings, of shared work and shared silences, of building exactly the life they'd imagined in that golden stable light. Riley's hand was as familiar as her own now—the calluses, the strength, the warmth.

"Rebecca's resting," Riley said. "Mother made her. Said she'd been on her feet too much this week."

"She has been." Sage frowned slightly. Rebecca was fifty now, and the years were beginning to show in ways they hadn't before. She tired more easily. Her knees ached in cold weather. She'd started talking about the future in ways that made clear she was thinking about what would happen when she was gone.

"She's not ill," Riley said, reading her expression. "Just... slowing down."

"I know. It's just strange, seeing her need rest." Sage leaned into Riley's shoulder. "She always seemed invincible."

"No one's invincible. But she's got years left in her yet. And she's got us to carry on the work."

Sage nodded. That was the heart of it, really. Not just the training, not just the methods—but the continuation. The passing on of knowledge, of philosophy, of a way of being with horses that Thornton and his kind would never understand.

Across the paddock, Ember had lowered her head slightly. Still watchful, still wary, but something had shifted in her posture. She was thinking, perhaps. Processing the strange fact of a human who sat quietly and asked for nothing.

"She'll get there," Riley said.

"They always do. The ones who come here." Sage smiled slightly. "It's what Hartwell is for."

---

Evening brought the particular golden light that Sage had come to love—the way it slanted across the fields, turning everything to amber and rose. She made her rounds as the sun descended: checking water troughs, counting heads, making sure the gates were latched and the barn doors secured.

Promise's paddock was her last stop.

He stood at the fence waiting for her, as he always did. Ten years old now, in his prime, his black coat gleaming with the health of good care and good work. Three seasons of breeding had established him as exactly what Rebecca had predicted—a sire whose offspring inherited not just his movement and his conformation, but his temperament. His patience. His capacity for trust.

"Hello, old friend," Sage said, reaching out to scratch the spot behind his ear.

He leaned into her hand, his eyes half-closing with pleasure. In the next paddock over, a two-year-old filly lifted her head—Promise's daughter out of Lord Whitmore's mare, with her father's dark coat and her mother's refined head. She watched Sage with calm curiosity, no fear in her at all.

"Your daughter's doing well," Sage told him. "She'll be ready to start under saddle this autumn. Gentle as anything. Just like you."

Promise's ear flicked toward her voice. He didn't understand the words—couldn't—but he understood her presence, her tone, the way her hand moved on his neck. They had a language between them that went deeper than words. The language of trust, built painstakingly over years.

"I started a new one today," she said. "A mare. Chestnut. Terrified of everything." She leaned her forehead against his neck, breathing in the familiar smell of him. "She reminded me of you. That first spring, when you wouldn't let anyone near you. When I used to sit by your fence and read, just hoping you'd notice I wasn't there to hurt you."

Promise shifted, pressing his nose against her shoulder—the gesture he'd offered her that first time, years ago, and a thousand times since. *I'm here. You're here. That's enough.*

"You changed everything," Sage told him quietly. "Not just for me. For all of them—the ones who came after, the ones who'll come after that. Because you proved it was possible. You proved that broken things can heal."

Behind her, she heard the kitchen door open, heard Riley's voice calling her name. Dinner was ready. The day was ending. Tomorrow there would be more work—Ember to sit with, yearlings to train, letters to write, a life to continue living.

But for now, for this one moment, there was just this: a woman and her horse, standing together in the golden evening light, the fields of Hartwell spreading green around them and the future opening wide ahead.

Sage gave Promise one last scratch, pressed her cheek against his neck, and turned toward the house.

The work would never be finished. There would always be another frightened horse, another broken creature needing patience and time. That was the nature of the calling she'd found—or that had found her, that spring day four years ago when she'd arrived at Hartwell with nothing but her grief and her determination to survive.

But that was all right. The work was the point. The work, and the love, and the slow patient building of trust.

She walked across the yard toward the lit windows of the kitchen, toward Riley waiting in the doorway, toward the family she'd chosen and the life she'd built. Behind her, Promise watched her go, his dark shape outlined against the fading sky.

Spring had come again to Hartwell Park. And all was well.

*THE END*
