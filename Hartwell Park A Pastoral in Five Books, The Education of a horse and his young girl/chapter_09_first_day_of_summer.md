# Chapter 9: First Day of Summer

The first morning of June arrived with light so bright it seemed to have weight. Sage woke to it pooling across her floor, thick and honey-colored, and for a moment simply lay there, watching dust motes drift through the warmth. Outside, a wood pigeon called its lazy three-note song. The air that moved through her open window carried the scent of grass and roses and something deeper—earth holding heat, promising the long days ahead.

Summer had come to Hartwell Park.

She dressed in the blue cotton day dress Jennifer had altered for her, then changed her mind and reached for the work dress instead—the one that had once been Rebecca's, sturdy brown linen that could take stains without complaint. Her hands, tying the laces, were brown from sun and roughened from work. The bruises on her throat were long gone. When she caught her reflection in the small mirror above the washstand, she almost didn't recognize the woman looking back. Stronger. Clearer. Taking up space without apology.

The woman her mother would call ruined.

The woman she was learning to call herself.

***

Promise was already at the fence when she reached his paddock, which meant he'd been watching for her. The realization still caught her sometimes—that he waited for her now, that her approach meant something other than threat. His coat gleamed blue-black in the morning light, the worst of the winter damage grown out, replaced by healthy hair that caught the sun like water.

"Good morning," she said, and he pushed his nose over the rail toward her, making the soft huffing sound that meant *hello* and *hurry* and *where's my apple* all at once.

She laughed and produced one from her pocket. He took it with the delicate precision he'd developed over the past month, lips careful against her palm, then crunched it with obvious satisfaction while she scratched behind his ears. He leaned into the touch, eyes half-closing.

A year ago—no, even two months ago—she would have said this was impossible. That something this broken could not mend. That someone like her could not stand here, comfortable in her own skin, while a traumatized stallion leaned into her hand and asked for more.

"Rebecca wants to start round pen work today," she told him, smoothing his forelock. "Just walking in circles when she asks, with no halter, no line. Learning to listen to her body language and voice. No weight on your back. No demands except to pay attention."

His ear flicked toward her, the one signal she could read as clearly as words. *Listening. Uncertain. But willing to try.*

"We'll go slow," she promised. "We always do."

***

The household was already stirring when she returned to the main house. Jennifer stood at the kitchen table kneading bread dough, her sleeves rolled to her elbows, flour dusting her forearms. She looked up when Sage entered and smiled.

"You've been to see him already."

"He expects his morning apple now. I've created a very demanding monster."

"The worst kind." Jennifer turned the dough with practiced efficiency. "Breakfast is nearly ready. Rebecca's asked everyone to the exercise ring after—she wants to discuss summer plans. Riley's already out in Promise's paddock setting up the round pen panels."

Summer plans. The phrase settled in Sage's chest with a weight she hadn't expected. Spring had been about arriving, about surviving, about learning to trust the smallest things—a fence between her and a frightened horse, the rhythm of Clover's walk, the calluses forming on her hands. Summer would be about building on that foundation. About doing the harder work.

She was ready. She thought she was ready.

She poured herself tea from the pot on the stove and leaned against the counter, watching Jennifer work. The kitchen smelled of rising bread and the lavender that hung drying from the ceiling beams. Morning light slanted through the windows, catching on copper pots and making them glow.

"Rebecca mentioned I'd be moving to Thunder soon," Sage said. "For riding."

Jennifer glanced at her, reading something in her voice. "Riley says you're ready for a bigger challenge. That Clover's taught you everything she can at this stage."

"Clover's perfect." The words came out more defensive than she'd intended. "She's patient and kind and—"

"And you've mastered her," Jennifer said gently. "Which is wonderful. But Thunder will teach you different things. How to sit a spook. How to ride a horse with opinions. How to be brave when your partner's braver than you are."

"I'm not brave."

"Perhaps not yet. But you're braver than you were when you arrived." Jennifer shaped the dough into a round and set it aside to rise, then wiped her hands on her apron. "Do you know what Rebecca told me once? She said courage isn't the absence of fear. It's deciding the work matters more than the fear does."

Sage thought of Promise, of that first morning when he'd touched her shoulder. How her whole body had gone still with wonder and terror both, certain that if she moved she'd shatter the moment. How she'd chosen to stay still anyway.

"The work always matters more," she said quietly.

Jennifer's smile held something that might have been pride. "Then you'll be fine with Thunder."

***

The exercise ring shimmered with heat by the time they gathered—Sage, Rebecca, and Riley, who'd been setting up the longe equipment. The longe line lay coiled on the ground next to a long whip, and the sight of it made Sage's stomach clench even though she knew Rebecca would never use it to cause pain. Promise knew whips as weapons. They would have to teach him something different.

"Summer," Rebecca said without preamble. She stood at the centre of the ring with her hands flat against her thighs, feet planted, body squared to them as though she were addressing a training session rather than a conversation. "Summer is when we build the foundation that makes autumn possible. Sage, you've learned to ride at walk, trot, and canter on Clover. You've developed an excellent seat and good hands. Now we need to develop your confidence and your ability to ride through difficulty."

"Thunder," Sage said.

"Thunder." Rebecca's eyes were steady. "He's not dangerous. But he's not Clover either. He'll test you—not maliciously, but because that's who he is. He needs to know you can handle him. And you need to know you can handle him, because eventually Promise will need the same from you."

Eventually. The word hung in the bright air like a promise and a threat both.

Riley spoke up, their voice matter-of-fact. "I'll be with you every ride until you're confident. Thunder's a good teacher if you listen to him. He'll tell you exactly what you're doing wrong."

"How reassuring," Sage said, but she was smiling despite herself.

"Additionally," Rebecca continued, "we'll begin your sidesaddle training. I know you prefer riding astride, and for most of your work here you can. But if you're to ride out in company, to represent Hartwell Park in any public capacity, you'll need to be competent sidesaddle."

Sage had known this was coming. Had seen the sidesaddle in the tack room, had understood that the freedom to ride astride on the estate came with the price of competence in society's preferred method. Still, the thought of it—legs hooked around pommels, all her weight on one side, trying to balance and communicate with a horse while twisted sideways—made her want to protest.

She didn't. "Yes, ma'am."

"Don't look so grim. Riley learned it, and they wear trousers every day." Rebecca's expression softened slightly. "You don't have to love it. You just have to be able to do it when necessary. Most of your riding will still be astride."

Small mercies.

"As for Promise," Rebecca said, turning her attention to the paddock where the stallion grazed in morning light, "he's ready to begin round pen work. Teaching him to respond to body language and voice commands, to understand that moving when asked and stopping when asked doesn't mean pain. It's the foundation we need before we can introduce any equipment—halters, lines, eventually tack. Everything else builds from this basic communication."

"How long before someone can ride him?" The question came out before Sage could stop it.

Rebecca's look was patient but firm. "Months. Perhaps many months. He's only just begun to truly trust us, Sage. The physical work of training is the easy part. The psychological work—convincing him that weight on his back won't mean pain, that tack won't mean torture—that takes time."

"I know." And she did know. She'd learned patience at Promise's fence, learned that healing happened in increments too small to measure until suddenly you looked up and realized you'd traveled miles. But there was still some part of her that wanted to rush, wanted to prove something—to Promise, to herself, to the world that said girls like her had no future.

Rebecca must have read it in her face. "The time will come. But first, we build the foundation. First, we earn what we're given."

First, we wait.

***

Promise's training session was scheduled for mid-morning, after the worst of the heat had built but before it became oppressive. Sage followed Rebecca and Riley to Promise's paddock, where Riley had already set up a temporary round pen—six portable panels arranged in a circle about forty feet across, positioned in the corner where Promise spent most of his time. The grass inside was already trampled from his pacing, familiar ground.

"We're not ready for halters or lines yet," Rebecca explained, checking each panel connection with her hands — testing the latch, the hinge, the gap between rails — before moving to the next. Systematic. Thorough. The same way she checked everything. "Before we ask him to accept equipment, he needs to understand that he can work with us, that responding to our asks doesn't mean pain. Round pen work teaches that. It's just him and me, no physical connection, learning to communicate through body language and voice."

"How do you make him move without touching him?" Sage asked.

"Pressure and release. I use my position, my energy, to create gentle pressure that asks him to move. When he responds correctly, I release the pressure—step back, soften my body. He learns that the right answer makes the pressure go away, that cooperation brings relief." Rebecca's eyes were on Promise, who stood at the far end of his paddock watching them with wary interest. Her gaze had that quality Sage had come to recognise — utterly focused, reading the horse's body the way other people read faces, seeing things Sage was only beginning to learn to notice. "It's the foundation of everything else. If he won't respond to this, he'll never accept a halter, a line, a saddle."

Riley opened the gate of the round pen and stepped out, then closed it behind them. Rebecca stood in the center, waiting. For a long moment, nothing happened. Promise stayed where he was, sixty feet away, every muscle ready to flee.

"We'll give him time," Rebecca said quietly. "Let him decide to investigate."

Sage and Riley positioned themselves outside the panels, well back, not looking directly at Promise. The morning sun beat down. A bee droned past. Somewhere in the distance, Thunder whinnied and another horse answered. And slowly, so slowly, Promise took a step toward the round pen.

Then another.

His neck was low, his approach careful, but he was coming. Sage held her breath. When he reached the panels he stopped, nostrils flaring as he investigated this new thing in his territory. His ears flicked forward and back, assessing. Then, with a decision that seemed to surprise even him, he stepped through the open gate into the round pen.

Rebecca had positioned herself so that closing the gate wouldn't require sudden movement. Riley reached over slowly and latched it. Promise's head came up, but he didn't bolt. Just stood there, eight feet from Rebecca, watching.

"Good," Rebecca said, her voice calm and even. "That's very good, Promise."

She took a step toward his hindquarters—not directly at him, but angled, creating a subtle pressure. Promise's ear swiveled toward her. She took another step, raising her arms slightly, making herself bigger. His weight shifted.

"Walk on," she said firmly, and stepped forward again.

Promise moved. Three steps along the rail of the round pen, then stopped, turning to face her. His expression was uncertain—*What is this? What do you want?*

Rebecca dropped her arms, turned her shoulder away, releasing the pressure. "Good lad."

She waited, then created pressure again. This time it took less—just her body angling toward his hip, her energy directed. He moved again, following the rail for half the circle before stopping. Each time he stopped, she released. Each time he stood still too long, she asked again.

By the fifth request, he was beginning to understand. The pressure meant *move*. The release meant *yes, that's right*. Moving forward made the pressure stop.

"Walk on," Rebecca said, and this time he went a full circuit without stopping, his stride lengthening as he found a rhythm. His inside ear stayed trained on her, listening, waiting for the next signal.

Sage watched, transfixed. There was something almost like a dance in it—Rebecca turning in the center, matching his movement, her body language clear and consistent. Promise responding, tentative at first but growing more confident. Learning that the work didn't hurt. That the right answer brought relief.

After five circuits, Rebecca stepped back completely, dropping all pressure, turning her body away from him. "Whoa."

Promise stopped immediately, then turned to face her. His sides heaved slightly from the exertion—not much work, but more than he'd done in months. His eyes were wide but not panicked. *Did I do right? Is this the end of it?*

Rebecca approached slowly, praise in every line of her body. When she reached him he held steady, and she scratched his forehead, then produced a carrot from her pocket. He took it carefully, crunching while she praised him.

"Well done," she told him. "We'll do this every day now. Just a few minutes. Just enough to teach without overwhelming. You're learning to listen, and I'm learning how to ask you things. That's what this is—both of us learning to speak the same language."

Promise leaned into her hand, and Sage felt something in her chest ease and tighten simultaneously. Progress. Healing. The slow work of building trust.

Not equipment yet. Not demands for collection or speed or precision. Just: *Can you move when I ask? Can you stop when I release? Can we communicate?*

The foundation beneath the foundation.

"Tomorrow," Rebecca said, catching Sage's eye over Promise's back, "you'll begin with Thunder."

***

That afternoon, Sage spent an hour in Thunder's stall, just being present while she groomed him. He was bigger than Clover—sixteen hands to her fourteen—and where Clover had been calm patience itself, Thunder radiated barely contained energy. He shifted his weight while she brushed him, not quite fidgeting but not quite still either. When she reached his shoulder he turned his head to look at her, one dark eye considering.

*You're the new one*, that look said. *Let's see what you're made of.*

"I'm probably not made of much," she told him, running the brush down his neck. "But I'm here anyway."

His ear flicked. *We'll see.*

She'd ridden him once, briefly, at a walk with Riley leading. She'd tacked him herself that day—taking twice as long as she would have with Clover, partly because his saddle was heavier and the girth needed a longer reach, but mostly because she'd been careful. She'd run her fingers along every inch of the bit, feeling the weight of it, the thicker mouthpiece so different from Clover's lighter snaffle. Had checked each buckle on the bridle, tested the stitching on the reins where they joined the bit rings. Riley had watched from the tack room doorway and given a small nod when she finished. No comment. Just the nod.

She'd felt the difference in the saddle immediately—the power coiled in his hindquarters, the way he moved with a bigger stride than Clover's patient plod. Had felt, too, the way he tested her seat with every step, asking: *Can you sit this? Can you handle me?*

Tomorrow she'd find out if her answer was yes.

Riley appeared at the stall door as she was finishing the grooming. "Nervous?"

"Terrified."

"Good." Riley leaned against the door frame, studying her. "Confident riders get complacent. Nervous riders pay attention. You need to pay attention with Thunder."

"I'm quite good at paying attention these days." Sage hung up the brush and gave Thunder a final pat. His skin was warm under her hand, his coat gleaming from the grooming. "What if I'm not ready?"

"Then you'll learn. That's what riding is—learning to be ready by doing the thing you're not ready for." Riley's smile was wry. "Though you won't hear Rebecca say it that way. She prefers to pretend we're all prepared for every challenge that comes."

"Are we?"

"No one is. That's the whole point."

Sage followed Riley out of the barn, Thunder's considering gaze following them. The afternoon sun was brutal now, beating down on the packed earth of the yard. In the distance, Promise grazed in his paddock, his black coat gleaming like polished stone. Clover dozed under the oak tree in her pasture. The swallows that nested in the barn eaves cut patterns against the blue sky, tireless and joyful.

Summer at Hartwell Park. The season of growth. Of heat and long days and the patient accumulation of skill. Of learning to ride through fear and building foundations that would carry them through autumn and beyond.

Sage thought: *I chose this. I'm choosing this.*

And that, somehow, made all the difference.

***

That evening, Sage sat at her window as had become her habit, watching Promise in the silver wash of moonlight. He stood quiet, head low, at peace in a way that still seemed like a small miracle. Beyond his paddock, the fields rolled away into darkness, dotted with the pale shapes of grazing horses. The night air carried the scent of honeysuckle and cut grass and the promise of heat to come.

Jennifer appeared in the doorway behind her, carrying two glasses of cold cider. She handed one to Sage and settled on the bench by the wall.

"Rebecca mentioned you looked nervous this afternoon," Jennifer said after a moment. "About Thunder." The phrasing was careful — *mentioned*, not *said she was worried about*. Rebecca would have stated it as fact, not concern. Jennifer translated.

"I am nervous. He's..." Sage searched for the right words. "He's not mean. I know that. But he's so much more than Clover. What if I can't—"

"You can," Jennifer said simply. "Maybe not perfectly. Maybe not immediately. But you can. You've already proven that by learning to ride at all. By being here. By choosing this work when you could have chosen safety."

"Sometimes I think safety would have been easier."

"Undoubtedly. But you didn't choose easy. You chose honest." Jennifer sipped her cider. "Thunder will teach you to trust yourself when your horse is braver than you are. And someday, Promise will need you to remember that lesson."

Someday. That word again, full of weight and possibility.

"Do you think I'll ever ride him?" Sage asked quietly. "Really ride him?"

"I don't know. Perhaps. Perhaps not. Perhaps someone else will ride him and you'll work with him from the ground, and that will be enough." Jennifer's voice was gentle but unflinching. "What matters is not whether you ride him. What matters is whether you give him the partnership he deserves, whatever form that takes."

In the paddock, Promise shifted his weight, lifted his head to test the wind. His mane stirred slightly in the breeze. He looked, Sage thought, like a creature who had decided that existing didn't have to hurt. Who had learned that nighttime could mean rest instead of hypervigilance. Who had begun to trust that morning would come and with it, a woman who kept her promises.

"I think," Sage said slowly, "I'm less afraid of falling than I am of failing him."

"Good," Jennifer said. "That means you understand what the work is really about."

They sat in comfortable silence, watching the night settle over Hartwell Park. Tomorrow would bring Thunder and all the challenges he represented. Would bring sidesaddle lessons and the strange twisted balance they required. Would bring more round pen sessions, teaching a traumatized stallion to understand that moving when asked could be a conversation instead of a demand, that human direction didn't have to mean pain.

Tomorrow would bring summer in all its bright intensity.

But tonight, there was just this: two women drinking cider in the gathering dark, a black horse at peace in his paddock, and the quiet certainty that healing—however slow, however uncertain—was possible.

Sage raised her glass toward Promise's distant shape.

"To summer," she said softly.

"To summer," Jennifer agreed. "And to the work it brings."

Outside, a nightjar called its strange churring song. The stars wheeled overhead, indifferent and beautiful. And in the paddock at the edge of the property, a stallion named Promise lowered his head to graze, trusting—just a little, just enough—that tomorrow would not bring pain.

That tomorrow might even bring something worth hoping for.
