# CHAPTER 36: THE SPEED DEMONSTRATION

The jumping course had been laid out in a field just north of town, where the ground rose gently toward a copse of oak trees. Eight fences in total—brush, rails, a stone wall replica, a water tray—with two combinations that required careful striding and a sharp turn at the far end that would test any horse's balance.

Sage stood at the edge of the course, her hands cold despite the May sunshine, and tried to remember how to breathe.

"You've jumped higher at home," Riley said, standing close enough that their shoulders touched. "That brush fence is nothing compared to the hedge behind the north paddock."

"At home, no one's watching."

"At home, Thornton isn't watching." Riley's voice was quiet. "That's what this is really about, isn't it?"

Sage didn't answer. Around them, the crowd was gathering—perhaps two hundred people, more than had attended the ladies' exhibition. Word had spread about the black stallion from Hartwell, the one who'd shown so beautifully in the ring. Now they wanted to see if he could move.

Rebecca appeared at her elbow, a folded programme in her hand with notes pencilled in the margins. "The order's been set. You're fifth of eight. That gives you time to warm up properly."

"Who else is riding?"

"Lord Ashford's hunter is first. A few I don't recognize in the middle places. Lady Catherine Thornton is seventh." Rebecca laid it out in order, each competitor accounted for, the way she laid out everything — sequential, complete, the full picture before the assessment. She paused. "And her father's been talking to people. I've heard him in the crowd."

Sage's stomach tightened. "What's he saying?"

"The usual. That Promise is dangerous. That we're fools for trusting him." Rebecca's hand found Sage's shoulder — placed there deliberately, the way she placed everything, a gesture that cost her visible effort but was offered anyway. "Let him talk. The best answer is what Promise does in that ring."

---

The first rider entered the course—Lord Ashford on a solid bay hunter, sixteen hands of sensible breeding and careful training. They approached the first fence at a measured canter, cleared it competently, and continued around the course with workmanlike precision. One rail down at the second combination—the horse had gotten too close and clipped it with a hind hoof—but otherwise clean. The crowd applauded politely as Lord Ashford trotted out, his expression satisfied despite the fault.

The second rider was a young woman on a flashy chestnut, all high head carriage and dancing hooves. Beautiful to look at, but nervous—Sage could see it in the way the mare's ears flicked constantly, in the tension along her neck. They made it over the first three fences, then the mare refused the water tray, planting her hooves and sending her rider lurching onto her neck. The second attempt succeeded, but the hesitation had rattled them both. They finished with the water refusal and two rails down, the chestnut blowing hard and the rider's face flushed with embarrassment.

Third was a grey gelding with a heavyset man aboard—good horse, mediocre rider. The gelding did his best to compensate, adjusting his stride when the man asked for takeoff too early or too late, but they still knocked two rails and nearly came to grief at the sharp turn. Clean enough to finish, but nothing remarkable.

Fourth, a neat bay mare with a competent rider. Clean round, no faults, but cautious—they'd taken every fence at minimum height, never asking for more than was required. Safe. Respectable. Forgettable. The timekeeper called out two minutes and twelve seconds.

"Your turn," Rebecca said.

Sage mounted from the block Riley had arranged, settling into the sidesaddle with practiced ease. Promise stood steady beneath her, his ears pricked toward the course, his body humming with contained energy. He knew what was coming. He'd felt her nerves all morning and had responded by being calmer than she'd ever seen him—as if he understood that she needed him to be strong.

"I see you," she whispered, leaning forward to touch his neck. "You see me."

His ear flicked back toward her voice. Understanding. Trust.

They entered the course at a collected canter, Promise's stride measured and balanced. The first fence—a simple brush—rose ahead. Sage felt him gather beneath her, felt the coiled power in his haunches, felt the moment of suspension as they lifted. They landed soft, already moving, already adjusting for the second fence.

The rails came next, two strides apart. Promise measured the distance with an instinct that couldn't be taught, his hooves barely skimming the top pole. The crowd made a sound—appreciation, surprise—but Sage barely heard it. She was inside the rhythm now, inside Promise's movement, feeling every stride through her seat and hands.

The stone wall. Higher than the others, solid-looking, the kind of fence that made horses hesitate. Promise's ears went forward, assessing, and then he launched himself over with power to spare. For a moment they were flying—truly flying—and Sage felt something crack open in her chest. Joy. Pure, uncomplicated joy.

The water tray. Blue-painted boards with a shallow splash of water meant to test a horse's courage. Some horses balked at the strange sight, the way the light caught the surface. Promise barely hesitated—one stride to check, ears forward with curiosity rather than fear, then over, his tail streaming behind him like a banner.

Past the halfway point now, the sharp turn approaching. This was where horses lost their balance, where sidesaddles made everything harder. Sage sat deep, asked for the turn with her weight and her inside rein, felt Promise respond with perfect precision. His hindquarters came under him, his forehand lifted, and they emerged from the turn with barely a stride lost. Better than barely—they'd gained ground, Promise's natural athleticism turning a challenge into an advantage.

The first combination. Two fences, one stride between. Promise met the first at exactly the right spot, landed light, and immediately launched again. Clean. Perfect. Sage heard herself laugh—couldn't help it—the sound lost in the wind of their passage.

The second combination, tighter than the first. Promise's ears flattened briefly with concentration, then pricked forward again as he sorted out the striding. One-two, one-two, and they were through, not a rail touched, not a moment's hesitation.

The final fence—a spread of brush and rails that required both scope and bravery. Promise pricked his ears, gathered himself, and flew. They cleared it by inches to spare, landing soft on the far side, and Sage brought him down through trot to walk, her heart pounding so hard she could feel it in her temples.

Clean round. Every fence, every combination, every turn. Sidesaddle, on a stallion, in front of two hundred people.

The applause that broke out was more than polite. Real appreciation, real surprise. The timekeeper's voice cut through the noise: "One minute, fifty-nine seconds!"

Sage brought Promise to a halt, her hand finding his neck, feeling the warmth of him beneath her glove. His breathing was elevated but steady, his eye bright with something that looked almost like pride.

"You magnificent creature," she told him, not caring who heard. "You absolute wonder."

---

The sixth rider was another clean round—a dark bay mare with a young man aboard, both of them competent but unremarkable. Two minutes and eight seconds. They'd taken no risks, made no mistakes, and generated no excitement. The crowd applauded with the same polite appreciation they'd shown Lord Ashford.

Seventh was Lady Catherine Thornton.

Her horse was a tall black mare, impeccably turned out, moving toward the start with the high-stepping gait of a horse who'd been trained to precision. Lady Catherine's face was set in a mask of concentration, her hands firm on the reins, her posture rigid in the sidesaddle.

They approached the first fence at a driving canter—faster than anyone else had taken it. Lady Catherine's whip was already in her hand, tapping against the mare's flank in a constant rhythm. Over the brush, a good jump, and immediately the whip again. Tap-tap-tap. The mare's ears were pinned flat against her head.

The rails. Lady Catherine drove the mare forward with her heels, the whip striking now rather than tapping. The mare cleared both fences, but her stride was choppy, forced—she was running from the whip rather than jumping to it. Clean, but joyless.

At the stone wall, Lady Catherine used her spurs. Sage saw the mare's sides ripple, saw her haunches bunch not from collection but from pain. They cleared it with room to spare—the mare was talented, there was no denying it—but Sage winced at the strike of the crop that followed.

"She's hurting her," Sage breathed. Riley's hand found hers and squeezed.

The water tray. The mare's ears flicked back—not hesitation, exactly, but awareness of what would happen if she slowed. Lady Catherine's heels were already driving, the whip already falling. Over clean, landing hard, the crop striking twice before they'd gone three strides.

Around the turn—too fast, but the mare was athletic enough to hold her balance while Lady Catherine's hands stayed hard on her mouth. Through the first combination, clean, the whip falling as reward. Through the second combination, clean again, more whip.

The final fence. The mare was sweating now, her neck dark with it, but her form was impeccable—she'd been trained too thoroughly to fail, no matter the cost. Lady Catherine drove her forward with heels and crop and hands, demanding the jump. The mare gathered, launched, and cleared it perfectly.

Two minutes and four seconds. Clean round.

Lady Catherine trotted out to genuine applause—she'd gone fast, she'd gone clean, and the crowd appreciated skill regardless of how it was achieved. The mare's sides were heaving, marked with sweat in the pattern of crop strikes. Her ears stayed flat against her head.

Sage felt sick.

"That's how Thornton trains," Rebecca said quietly, appearing at her elbow. Her voice had gone flat and precise — the register it shifted into when she was observing something that offended her understanding of horses, all feeling pressed down beneath accuracy. "That's what Promise came from. And that's what you saved him from."

The eighth and final rider was almost an afterthought—a young gentleman on a rangy chestnut who knocked three rails and refused the water tray twice before getting over it. The crowd had already begun to murmur among themselves, and Sage heard snatches of conversation: *the black stallion... did you see him jump?... who's the girl?... faster than Lady Catherine...*

She hadn't been thinking about winning. She'd been thinking about proving that Promise could do it, that he could be trusted, that everything they'd worked for was real. The competition itself had been secondary—a framework for the demonstration, not the point.

So when the steward stepped forward and called for the competitors to gather for the prize ceremony, she felt genuinely confused.

"Go on," Riley said, grinning. "They're calling you."

"But I didn't—we weren't trying to—"

"You went clean. One of three clean rounds, and you did it five seconds faster than Lady Catherine—sidesaddle, on a stallion." Riley's grin widened. "Go accept your ribbon, Sage."

---

The eight riders gathered in a loose line at the edge of the course, horses fidgeting and blowing while the crowd pressed closer. Sage found herself positioned near the center, Promise standing quiet beneath her, his attention on the activity around them with calm curiosity.

The steward—a portly man in a brown coat—consulted his notes and cleared his throat. "Ladies and gentlemen, our results for the speed demonstration. In third place, with a clean round and a time of two minutes eight seconds, Mr. David Hartley on Dorian."

The young gentleman with the dark bay mare rode forward to accept his ribbon. Polite applause.

"In second place, with a clean round and a time of two minutes four seconds, Lady Catherine Thornton on Midnight Star."

Lady Catherine's face went rigid. For a moment she didn't move, and Sage saw something flash across her expression—not just disappointment, but something darker. Fear, perhaps. Then she rode forward, the black mare moving stiffly beneath her, and accepted the white ribbon without a word. When she returned to the line, the ribbon was crumpled in her fist.

"And in first place—" The steward paused, and Sage felt the crowd's attention sharpen. "With a clean round and a time of one minute fifty-nine seconds, the fastest round of the day—Miss Sage Morrison on Promise, representing Hartwell Park."

For a moment, Sage couldn't move. Then Riley's voice cut through her shock—"Go!"—and she was riding Promise forward, the crowd's applause washing over her like a wave.

The blue ribbon was pressed into her hand. Someone was saying something about the exceptional round, about the difficulty of sidesaddle at such speed, about the stallion's remarkable form. Sage heard herself thanking them, felt herself smiling, but it all seemed distant, dreamlike.

She'd won. They'd won. The vicious stallion from Thornton's yard, the one who'd been sent to the knacker, had just beaten every horse at the fair—including Thornton's own daughter on her expensive, impeccably trained mare.

She rode back to the line, the blue ribbon clutched in her hand, and that was when she saw him.

Marcus Thornton was pushing through the crowd, his face dark with fury. He didn't stop at the edge—he came right into the assembly area, his bulk clearing a path, his eyes fixed on Sage and Promise with an intensity that made her horse shift uneasily beneath her.

"Mr. Thornton," the steward began, "the ceremony isn't quite—"

"Very pretty." Thornton's voice was loud enough to carry, pitched for the crowd. "Very pretty indeed. A trained circus trick."

The murmurs around them shifted, taking on an edge. Lady Catherine had gone pale, the white ribbon still crumpled in her hand.

"Mr. Thornton," Rebecca said, stepping forward from where she'd been standing with Jennifer. "This is neither the time nor the place—"

"When is the time, Mrs. Morrison? When that animal hurts someone? When he shows his true nature and some poor fool pays the price?" Thornton's gaze swept the crowd, playing to his audience. "You've all seen a lovely display today. A black stallion doing tricks for a young lady. Faster than my daughter's mare, yes—faster than everyone." His voice dripped with contempt. "But I owned that horse for two years. I know what he is."

He stepped closer, close enough that Promise's nostrils flared at his scent. The horse remembered. Sage could feel it in the sudden tension beneath her—the recognition, the old fear trying to surface.

"That animal," Thornton said, jabbing a finger toward Promise, "put three of my best riders in the surgeon's care. Broke one man's arm. Kicked another in the chest so hard he couldn't breathe for a week. The third—" He paused for effect. "The third nearly died. Would have, if the surgeon hadn't been close."

The crowd had gone silent. Sage could feel their attention shifting, doubt creeping in where admiration had been moments before.

"This young woman rides him," Thornton continued, his voice rising. "Her aunt rides him. Both of them experienced horsewomen who've spent months learning exactly how to handle him—how to keep him calm, how to avoid setting off his temper. But what happens when someone else tries?" He turned in a slow circle, addressing the entire crowd. "What happens when a groom leads him to water, or a farrier tries to shoe him, or God forbid—a child wanders too close?"

Promise's head had come up during the exchange, his body rigid with tension. Sage kept her hand on his neck, her voice low and steady, but she could feel him trembling. Not fear this time—something else. The memory of pain, perhaps. The smell of the man who had caused it.

"I'll tell you what happens." Thornton's voice dropped to something almost conversational, which somehow made it worse. "The same thing that happened to my men. That horse will turn. He'll strike, or kick, or bite—whatever it takes. Because underneath all the pretty training, he's still the same vicious animal I sent to the knacker's yard. The only difference is he's learned to hide it."

He turned back to Sage, and his smile was terrible.

"So here's my challenge, Miss Morrison. Right here, in front of all these good people." He spread his arms wide. "Let someone else ride that horse. Someone who hasn't spent months learning his tricks. Someone who doesn't know his special handling." His voice rose to carry across the crowd. "Find me one person here—any person—willing to get on that animal's back. And if they can walk him around this ring and come back safely, I'll admit I was wrong. I'll tell everyone here that I misjudged him."

He paused, letting the silence stretch.

"But if you can't find anyone willing to try—or if something happens when they do—then everyone here will know the truth. That you've been parading a dangerous animal through this fair, putting lives at risk, all so you could win a blue ribbon and prove a point."

The crowd was utterly still. Sage could feel hundreds of eyes on her—waiting, watching, wondering what she would say. Beside her, Lady Catherine sat frozen on her exhausted mare. The other competitors had edged their horses away, as if violence might erupt at any moment.

Promise stood rigid beneath her, his breath coming fast, his eye showing white at the edges. He knew this man. He remembered what this man had done to him. And he was waiting—waiting to see if the fear would win, or if the trust they'd built would hold.

Thornton crossed his arms over his chest, his expression triumphant.

"Well, Miss Morrison? Mrs. Morrison?" He looked from Sage to Rebecca and back again. "Surely you can find someone willing to prove me wrong?"

The silence stretched on. And on. And on.
