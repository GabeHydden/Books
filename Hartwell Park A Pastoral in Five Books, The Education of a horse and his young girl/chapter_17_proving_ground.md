# Chapter 17: Proving Ground

Thunder's trot bounced Sage sideways like a rag doll.

She grabbed at the pommels, trying to find the rhythm, trying to remember everything Riley had taught her about posting from this twisted position. Push down on the left stirrup, rise with Thunder's outside shoulder, sit for one beat, rise again. Except sitting meant landing on one seat bone instead of two, and rising meant all her weight balanced on one leg, and the whole thing felt like trying to dance on a boat in a storm.

"You're fighting it," Riley called from the center of the ring. "Stop thinking and just feel the rhythm. Same as when you learned to post astride—your body knows what to do."

Sage gritted her teeth and tried to stop thinking. Up-down, up-down, following Thunder's outside shoulder, feeling for the moment to rise, the moment to sit. Her right thigh burned against the upper pommel, taking all the strain of rising and sitting. Her left leg hooked around the lower pommel provided no help, just anchor. Everything was wrong, asymmetrical, backward.

And then—just for three strides—it clicked.

Her body found the rhythm without her mind's interference, rose and sat with Thunder's motion instead of against it, and suddenly they were moving together instead of fighting each other. Thunder's trot smoothed out under her, his head lowered slightly, and for those three glorious strides Sage felt what Riley had promised: this wasn't a different kind of riding, it was the same language spoken with a different accent.

Then she thought about it and lost it completely, bouncing again.

"There," Riley said, grinning. "You felt it. Do it again."

***

By the end of that session—the fifth day of sidesaddle training—Sage could post Thunder's trot for a full circuit of the ring without losing the rhythm. Her muscles screamed and her balance remained precarious, but she could do it. Could communicate with Thunder through this sideways, twisted position. Could ride, not just perch.

"Tomorrow," Riley said as Sage dismounted on shaking legs, "we canter."

Sage should have been terrified. A week ago she would have been. But something had shifted in these five days of sidesaddle work. She'd learned that her body could adapt faster than she thought possible, that foundation accelerated new learning, that being temporarily incompetent didn't mean she'd lost everything she'd built.

"All right," she said, and meant it.

***

The canter came easier than the trot had.

Perhaps because Sage had learned to stop fighting, to trust her body's ability to translate what it already knew. Or perhaps because Thunder's three-beat rhythm was smoother than his trot, easier to follow even from a sideways seat. Whatever the reason, when she asked Thunder to canter on the seventh day of sidesaddle training, he picked it up smoothly and Sage went with him.

Not gracefully. Not yet. Her right leg still burned from holding position, her left hip still ached from the asymmetry. But she sat deep and quiet, following Thunder's rocking motion, and they cantered around the ring twice before Riley called them back to walk.

"Good," Riley said, and there was satisfaction in her voice. "Really good. Your foundation is showing, Sage. Most women take weeks to canter sidesaddle. You're doing it in a week because you already understand how to ride—you're just learning a new position."

Sage walked Thunder on a long rein, catching her breath, feeling the tremor in her muscles. A week ago this had seemed impossible. Now it was difficult, uncomfortable, but possible. The difference felt enormous.

"How long until we jump?" she asked.

Riley's eyebrows rose. "You want to jump sidesaddle?"

"You said it was real riding. I need to know how real."

"Two more days of canter work. Then we'll start with poles on the ground." Riley studied her with something like approval. "You're not afraid anymore. Of being incompetent, I mean. That's the biggest change I've seen in you this summer."

Sage considered this while Thunder stretched his neck down, pulling at the reins to graze a tuft of grass near the rail. Riley was right. Spring Sage would have been devastated by five days of feeling clumsy and wrong. Would have fought the learning, resented the difficulty, feared being seen as inadequate.

But Promise had taught her something about patience. About progress measured in increments too small to see until you looked back at where you'd started. About trusting the process even when it felt impossible.

"I'm learning," she said simply.

***

The poles came on day nine.

Riley set up three in a line, easy distances for Thunder's canter stride, and positioned Sage at the far end of the ring.

"Same as you've done astride," Riley said. "See the distance, count the strides, stay balanced over the poles. The only difference is you're sitting sideways."

The only difference. Sage almost laughed. But she gathered her reins, asked Thunder to canter, and aimed him at the first pole.

His ears pricked forward—*finally, something interesting*—and he locked onto the line of poles with the enthusiasm he always showed for jumping work. Three strides out, two, one, and they were over the first pole. Sage felt Thunder lift beneath her, felt the moment of suspension, landed and immediately had to rebalance for the next one. Three strides between, then over the second pole, rebalance, three strides, over the third.

They finished the line and Sage was grinning despite the awkwardness, despite how her position had shifted and her balance had been precarious. Thunder had carried her through it, had done his job with patience and precision, and she'd stayed with him.

"Again," Riley called. "Better this time. Keep your weight more left—you're leaning right on landing."

Again. And again. And again. Each time through the line, Sage's body learned a little more. Where to hold her weight over the poles, how to follow Thunder's lifting motion even from a sidesaddle position, how to rebalance between elements without hauling on the reins. By the sixth time through, she felt it—that moment of connection where she and Thunder were working together, where the poles were just a conversation between them instead of an obstacle to survive.

"That's enough for today," Riley said, though there was pride evident in her voice. "You're jumping sidesaddle. Nine days from never doing it before. That's remarkable, Sage."

It felt remarkable. Felt like proof that the foundation she'd built all summer—the falls and the getting back on, the patient work with multiple horses, the slow understanding of how to communicate through seat and weight and barely-there aids—was real. Was solid enough to support learning new, difficult things.

***

Two weeks into sidesaddle training, Rebecca appeared in the stable yard while Sage was grooming Thunder after another jumping session—raised poles now, small crossrails, nothing dramatic but enough to require real riding.

"The Ashworth hunt meets next Saturday," Rebecca said without preamble. "It's the August meet—informal, just a few hours in the morning before the heat gets too bad. I'm riding Whisper. You're riding Thunder. We're going to show them that Hartwell Park's methods produce horses and riders who can hold their own."

Sage's hands stilled on Thunder's neck. "The hunt?"

"You're ready. You can jump sidesaddle, you can control Thunder in company, and you know how to conduct yourself. I've already sent word we'll attend." Rebecca's expression was serious. "This is important, Sage. These are the people who dismiss what we do here as soft, as indulgent. Who believe force is the only way to train horses properly. We're going to show them otherwise."

"What if I fall off?"

"Then you get back on. But you won't fall off. Thunder knows his job, and you know yours." Rebecca moved closer, voice gentler. "I wouldn't ask this if I didn't believe you were ready. You've learned more this summer than most riders learn in years. You can do this."

***

The days between that conversation and Saturday moved too fast and too slow simultaneously. Sage practiced jumping sidesaddle daily, worked through increasingly complex exercises, rode Thunder out on the estate's trails to accustom him to uneven ground and unexpected obstacles. Each session built her confidence incrementally, but the knowledge of what Saturday would demand—riding in company, jumping in front of people who would judge every moment, representing Hartwell Park's reputation—sat heavy in her chest.

Friday evening, Jennifer appeared in Sage's room with a dark blue riding habit draped over her arm.

"Rebecca's," she explained, laying it carefully across the bed. "From when she used to ride in company, before... well. Before she stopped caring what society thought. It should fit you—you're nearly the same height, and I've taken in the waist."

Sage touched the fabric—fine wool, beautifully tailored, cut to fit perfectly over the sidesaddle position. The jacket was fitted, military in style, with small brass buttons. The skirt was full, designed to drape properly over both legs when hooked sidesaddle. A small matching hat with a veil completed the ensemble.

"I've never worn anything like this," Sage said quietly.

"You've never ridden in a hunt either. Firsts everywhere." Jennifer's smile was kind. "Try it on. Let's see if adjustments are needed."

The habit transformed her. Sage barely recognized the woman in the mirror—straight-backed, properly dressed, every inch a lady who rode with confidence. The jacket emphasized her shoulders, now strong from months of riding. The skirt fell in perfect lines. The hat sat at precisely the correct angle.

She looked like someone who belonged in society. Like someone who could ride out with the local hunt and not embarrass herself or Hartwell Park.

"You'll do beautifully," Jennifer said softly. "Rebecca is proud of you, even if she doesn't say it often. What you've learned in five months—most people don't accomplish in years."

***

Saturday morning arrived cool and misty, summer just beginning to hint at autumn's approach. Sage dressed in the riding habit with hands that only trembled slightly, pinned the hat carefully into her hair, and made her way to the stable yard where Riley had Thunder and Whisper already tacked in sidesaddle.

Rebecca was there too, dressed in her own riding habit—charcoal grey, severe, perfectly proper. She looked every inch the estate owner, the accomplished horsewoman, the woman who had trained under Thornton himself before rejecting his methods entirely.

"Ready?" Rebecca asked.

Sage looked at Thunder, who stood calmly despite the early hour and the unusual activity. Looked at Whisper, who danced slightly in place, eager for the work ahead. Thought about everything she'd learned—from Clover's patience, from Thunder's testing, from Whisper's joy, from Promise's slow trust-building that had taught her more about patience than any riding lesson ever could.

"Ready," she said.

Before mounting, Sage ran her fingers along Thunder's bit — the automatic check that had become as much a part of her routine as gathering the reins. Smooth. Sound. She nodded to herself, and Riley helped her mount. The sidesaddle position that had felt impossible two weeks ago now felt merely difficult. Manageable. She arranged her skirt properly, gathered her reins, settled her weight. Thunder shifted beneath her, rebalancing, then stood quiet.

Rebecca mounted Whisper with practiced ease, and they set off together down the lane toward Ashworth estate.

***

The meet was everything Sage had feared and nothing she'd expected.

Thirty riders gathered in Ashworth's front courtyard, mounted on horses that ranged from heavy hunters to elegant ladies' mounts. The men wore red coats, the women habits in various shades of blue and grey and brown. Hounds milled around the horses' legs, tails wagging, noses working. The whole scene looked like something from a painting—aristocratic, traditional, utterly foreign to Sage's months at Hartwell Park where work was done in simple clothes and evaluated by results, not appearances.

But then they noticed Rebecca.

Conversations paused. Eyes tracked their approach. Lord Ashworth himself—an older man with grey side-whiskers and a florid face—rode forward to greet them.

"Miss Morrison," he said, and there was something complicated in his tone. Not quite welcome, not quite dismissal. "I'm surprised to see you. It's been years since you rode out with us."

"Lord Ashworth." Rebecca's voice was perfectly polite, perfectly correct. "May I introduce my niece, Miss Hartwell. She's been training with me this season."

Lord Ashworth's eyes moved to Sage, assessed her position, her seat, Thunder's condition. "Miss Hartwell. That's a fine animal you're riding."

"Thank you, my lord. His name is Thunder. He's been patient with my learning."

Something flickered in Lord Ashworth's expression—surprise at her honesty, perhaps, or approval of her giving credit to the horse. "Well. The master's about to call hounds. Do try to keep up."

He rode off, and Sage glanced at Rebecca. "That could have been worse."

"Wait until we're actually riding. They'll be watching for mistakes, for evidence that our methods produce inferior horses." Rebecca's jaw was set. "We'll give them no such evidence."

***

The hunt moved off at a trot, hounds ranging ahead, the field following. Thunder settled into his working rhythm immediately, ears forward, paying attention to Sage's aids despite the chaos of thirty horses moving together. Sage focused on staying balanced, keeping proper position, remaining aware of the horses around them.

When the pace increased to a canter, Thunder accelerated smoothly, and Sage went with him. The sidesaddle position that had seemed so precarious two weeks ago now felt secure enough. She could post Thunder's trot in company, could sit his canter, could half-halt when he got too forward without pulling on his mouth.

The first fence came up suddenly—a low stone wall, nothing dramatic, but the field approached it with the casual confidence of people who'd been jumping since childhood. Sage saw the distance, counted Thunder's strides, and stayed balanced as he popped over it with room to spare. Landing, rebalancing, cantering on without fuss.

She'd done it. Jumped in sidesaddle, in company, and stayed on.

The second fence was a hedge with a ditch on the far side. Thunder locked onto it, measured his approach, and sailed over with more enthusiasm than strictly necessary. Sage grabbed at the pommels briefly on landing but recovered her balance and kept going.

Beside her, Rebecca and Whisper moved like water—effortless, elegant, making everything look easy. But Rebecca caught Sage's eye and nodded approval.

They jumped six more fences that morning—walls and hedges and a small coop—and each time Thunder carried Sage over safely while she did her best to stay balanced and not interfere with his work. By the time the master called the field to a halt near a small stream, Sage's legs trembled and her right hip screamed, but she'd stayed on, jumped clean, kept up with riders who'd been doing this their entire lives.

Lord Ashworth rode up beside them, face still florid from exertion but expression thoughtful.

"That's well-schooled horse, Miss Hartwell. Honest over fences, good manners in company. Miss Morrison's training, I assume?"

"Yes, my lord. Though Thunder deserves most of the credit. He's been extraordinarily patient with my mistakes."

"Hmm." Lord Ashworth studied Thunder with professional interest. "Morrison, I assume you used your... gentler methods with him?"

"I did," Rebecca said evenly.

"Interesting. He jumps as boldly as any horse trained traditionally." Lord Ashworth's tone suggested this surprised him. "Perhaps there's something to your approach after all."

It wasn't acceptance. Wasn't approval. But it was acknowledgment that Hartwell Park's methods could produce results. That horses trained with patience and partnership could perform just as well as horses trained with force.

Rebecca inclined her head. "Perhaps there is."

***

They rode back to Hartwell Park in comfortable silence, both women exhausted, both aware of what they'd accomplished. The sun was higher now, burning off the morning mist, promising another hot afternoon. Thunder and Whisper walked on loose reins, relaxed after their work.

"You did well," Rebecca said finally. "Very well. You represented Hartwell Park exactly as I'd hoped."

"Thunder did most of the work."

"You've learned to give credit to the horse. That's good. But you earned your place out there today. Five months ago you couldn't post a trot on Clover. Today you jumped a hunt course sidesaddle on Thunder in front of the local gentry. That's extraordinary progress."

Sage thought about that progress—the falls and the getting back on, the patient building of skills, the slow understanding of how to communicate with horses. Thought about Promise, still working in the round pen, still months away from accepting a halter, still learning to trust that humans could be kind.

"I had good teachers," she said. "Thunder and Clover and Whisper. Riley and you. Promise, most of all, teaching me about patience."

"Promise has months of work ahead still. Years, possibly, before he's ready to be ridden. You understand that?"

"I do. And I'll be ready when he is." Sage paused, then added quietly, "That's what this summer was for, wasn't it? Not just learning to ride, but learning to be the kind of rider he'll need. Patient. Competent. Willing to let him move at his own pace."

Rebecca smiled—rare and genuine. "You're ready. Not for riding him yet. But ready to understand what the work requires. Ready to be his partner when the time comes."

They rode through Hartwell Park's gates as the church bells in the village struck noon. In his paddock, Promise raised his head from grazing, watching them approach. His coat gleamed in the sunlight, dapples emerging as health returned. His eyes were bright, curious, no longer showing constant fear.

He'd come so far. They both had.

Sage dismounted in the stable yard, her legs barely holding her weight. She helped Riley untack Thunder, groomed him with meticulous care, thanked him for carrying her safely through her first hunt. When she finally made her way to Promise's fence, still wearing the riding habit though she'd removed the hat, he came immediately.

"I rode in a hunt today," she told him, scratching behind his ears while he leaned into her hand. "Jumped fences in front of people who think Rebecca's methods are too soft. And Thunder was perfect. Patient and brave and honest. Everything a good horse should be."

Promise blew out softly, comfortable with her presence, choosing proximity.

"Your time is coming," Sage continued. "Not yet. Not for months still. But it's coming. And when it does, I'll be ready. I'll know how to listen to you, how to move at your pace, how to be the partner you deserve."

The stallion pushed his nose against her shoulder—his favorite gesture, the one that meant *here-with-you-safe*. And Sage, exhausted and aching and transformed from who she'd been five months ago, leaned her forehead against his neck and breathed in the scent of him: grass and sun and the promise of healing.

Summer was ending. Autumn would bring new challenges, new work, new progress.

But today she'd proven she was ready.

Ready to be competent instead of merely willing.

Ready to be the rider Promise would someday need.

Ready to continue the patient work of building trust between two creatures who'd both learned that humans could hurt them—and were learning, slowly, carefully, that humans could also heal.

The work continued.

And both of them—horse and woman, student and student—were ready for what came next.
