# Chapter 32: Winter's End
**Book IV: Winter — Reflection**
**POV: Sage**
**Late February/Early March 1814**

March arrived like a held breath finally released.

The last of the snow had melted a week ago, leaving the fields muddy and the air carrying that particular smell of earth warming after long cold. Crocuses had joined the snowdrops in the garden, purple and yellow against the brown, and the bare branches of the apple trees showed the first swelling of buds.

Two weeks until the fair. Fourteen days. Sage counted them obsessively, the number both too large and too small.

Promise felt the change in the air as keenly as she did. He'd grown restless in his stall, eager to be out and moving, his winter coat beginning to shed in great handfuls when she groomed him. Beneath the shaggy remnants, his summer coat was emerging—sleek and black and gleaming, the dapples on his hindquarters more visible than they'd been in months.

"You're going to be beautiful," she told him, working the shedding blade across his shoulder. "The most beautiful horse at the fair."

He turned his head to look at her, one dark eye soft with what she'd come to recognize as affection. Then he returned to his hay, unconcerned with beauty or fairs or anything beyond the immediate pleasures of food and companionship.

She envied him that simplicity sometimes.

---

The final preparations had taken on an almost military precision. Rebecca had drawn up schedules, checklists, contingency plans. The horses to be sold—Marcus and Oliver—had been groomed and polished until they gleamed. Their tack had been cleaned and oiled, mended where needed, made presentable.

Promise's tack was another matter entirely.

"We need something that makes a statement," Rebecca said, examining the sidesaddle Sage had been using for training. It was serviceable, well-made, but plain. "Something that catches the eye before he even moves."

"I don't need fancy tack to show what he can do."

"No. But fancy tack doesn't hurt." Rebecca's smile was wry. "Half of selling horses is selling the idea of horses. People want to believe they're buying something special. Presentation matters."

So a new saddle had been commissioned from the saddler in the village—still sidesaddle, but with tooled leather and silver fittings that caught the light. A new bridle too, with a bit that Promise accepted easily, having long since learned that bits didn't have to mean pain.

"He looks like a prince's horse," Riley said when they tried out the new tack for the first time.

"He is a prince's horse." Sage stroked Promise's neck, feeling him stand proud beneath all his finery. "He just doesn't know it yet."

---

The training continued, but lighter now—maintaining fitness rather than building it, keeping Promise sharp without exhausting him. Sage took him over the practice fences every other day, varying the heights and combinations, keeping him thinking without making him sour.

He jumped beautifully. That had been the surprise of the past weeks—discovering that Promise wasn't just willing to jump, he was talented at it. His Thoroughbred blood showed in his scope and his Arabian blood in his cleverness, the way he assessed each fence and adjusted his stride accordingly. When they were in sync, when everything flowed, it felt like flying.

"You're ready," Rebecca said after watching one of their final practice sessions. "Both of you. Whatever happens at the fair, you've done the work."

"What if it's not enough?"

"It will be enough." Rebecca's voice was certain in a way Sage couldn't quite match. "You've trained harder than anyone I've ever worked with. Promise has come further than any horse I've ever rehabilitated. Even if everything goes wrong—if he refuses a fence, if you fall, if Thornton makes trouble—you've already proven what's possible. The fair is just showing it to a wider audience."

"And if Thornton does make trouble?"

Rebecca's expression hardened. "Then we handle it. Marcus Thornton is a bully and a coward. He hurts things weaker than himself because it makes him feel powerful. But Promise isn't weak anymore, and neither are you."

"He might try to stop us somehow. Claim Promise is dangerous, that he shouldn't be shown—"

"Let him try. Promise's record at Hartwell speaks for itself. A year of gentle handling, not a single incident of aggression. Dozens of witnesses who've seen him ridden, groomed, handled by multiple people." Rebecca put a hand on Sage's shoulder. "Thornton can say whatever he likes. The truth is on our side."

Sage wanted to believe that was enough. She wanted to believe that truth mattered more than reputation, that evidence mattered more than accusations. But she'd learned, in her life before Hartwell, that the powerful could make their version of events into the accepted one simply by speaking louder.

"I'm afraid of him," she admitted. "Not for myself. For Promise. For what Thornton might do if he sees how far Promise has come."

"Good. Being afraid means you'll be careful." Rebecca squeezed her shoulder. "But don't let the fear make you small. Promise needs you brave, Sage. He needs you to believe in him loud enough for everyone to hear."

---

The night before they were to leave for the fair, Sage couldn't sleep.

She'd tried—had gone to bed at a reasonable hour, had closed her eyes and breathed deep and done all the things that were supposed to bring rest. But her mind wouldn't quiet. It ran through the course, the fences, the crowd that would be watching. It conjured images of failure—Promise refusing, Sage falling, Thornton's triumphant sneer.

Finally she gave up and went to the barn.

The night was clear and cold, the stars brilliant overhead, the kind of sky that seemed to promise something. Sage let herself into the stable quietly, not wanting to wake the horses, but Promise was already awake. His head appeared over his stall door the moment she entered, ears pricked forward.

"You can't sleep either?" she asked, coming to stand beside him.

He nickered softly, pushing his nose against her shoulder.

"I'm scared," she told him. "I keep thinking about everything that could go wrong. All the ways I could fail you."

Promise blew out a breath, warm against her neck.

"You're not scared, are you? You don't know what tomorrow means. You don't understand about Thornton or the fair or any of it." She stroked his face, tracing the familiar lines of him. "You just trust me to take care of you, the way I always have."

He leaned into her touch, eyes half-closing.

"I wish I could be that simple. I wish I could just... trust. The way you do." Sage felt tears prick at her eyes. "But I'm human, and humans are complicated. We imagine disasters that haven't happened yet. We carry fear like a weight we can't set down."

Promise was quiet, patient, letting her talk. Letting her pour out her fears into the darkness while he stood steady beside her.

"I'm going to do my best," she said finally. "Whatever happens tomorrow, I'm going to do my best for you. I'm going to ride as well as I can, and jump as clean as I can, and show everyone what you've become. And if it's not enough—" Her voice caught. "If it's not enough, at least we'll know we tried."

She stayed with him for a long time, leaning against his warm shoulder, listening to the quiet sounds of the sleeping barn. The other horses breathed softly in their stalls—Thunder, Marcus, Oliver, Belle, Clover, Whisper. The herd that had become Promise's family. The home he'd never expected to find.

Eventually Sage's eyelids grew heavy, her fear settling into something more like resolve. Tomorrow would come regardless of whether she slept. Might as well face it ready.

"We've come so far," she murmured against his neck. "Both of us. From broken things that no one wanted, to... this. Whatever this is. Whatever we've become."

Promise shifted his weight, settling more comfortably, his body warm against hers.

"A year ago, I didn't think I'd ever feel safe again. I didn't think I'd ever belong anywhere, or love anyone, or want a future." She smiled into the darkness. "Now I have all of that. Riley, Jennifer, Rebecca. Hartwell. You."

He turned his head to look at her, one dark eye gleaming in the faint starlight that filtered through the barn windows.

"Tomorrow we show them," she said. "We show Thornton and everyone else what patience and kindness can build. We show them that the old ways aren't the only ways. That breaking isn't the same as training. That trust is stronger than fear."

Promise nickered softly, as if in agreement.

"And whatever happens—whatever Thornton tries, whatever the crowd thinks, whether we win or lose or fall or fly—we'll have each other. We'll come home to Hartwell together, and we'll keep living this life we've built. Because that's what matters. Not the fair, not the stud fees, not proving anything to anyone." She pressed her forehead against his. "Just this. Just us. Just being alive and together and whole."

The barn was quiet around them, the night deep and still. Somewhere outside, an owl called. The horses dozed in their stalls, safe and warm and cared for.

Sage didn't go back to the house. She pulled a horse blanket from the tack room and spread it on the straw outside Promise's stall, curling up with her back against his door. Through the bars, she could hear him breathing—slow, steady, peaceful.

Tomorrow, everything would change. Tomorrow, they would face the fair and the crowds and whatever trouble Thornton might bring. Tomorrow, a year of healing would be put to the test.

But tonight, there was just this: the quiet barn, the sleeping horses, and a girl who had learned to trust again, keeping watch over the horse who had taught her how.

Spring was coming. She could feel it in the softening air, smell it in the earth beginning to warm. The long winter was ending, and something new was ready to begin.

Whatever came next, they would face it together.

That was enough. That had always been enough.
