# HARTWELL PARK — Cleanup Pass

## Project Overview

This is a completed ~118,000-word historical fiction manuscript set in 1813-1814 England. Five books (39 chapters + epilogue), organized seasonally. The manuscript has been through five major revision passes (Rebecca characterization, chapter endings, colic-to-Riley-fall restructuring, Thunder tack chain, Riley pronouns). This cleanup pass addresses issues identified in a post-revision review.

## General Principles

- **Surgical edits only.** These are trims, corrections, and phrase reductions — not rewrites.
- **Cut, don't rephrase.** When a passage restates what the scene already showed, delete it. Don't rewrite it in different words.
- **Trust the scene.** The action, dialogue, and concrete images are doing the work. Remove the narration that explains them.
- **Preserve voice.** The prose style — contemplative, sensory, period-appropriate — is strong. We're removing excess, not changing tone.
- **When in doubt, cut.** A slightly abrupt ending is better than a redundant one.

# PASS C: Reduce Sage's Throat-Touching Gesture

The gesture currently appears 14+ times in Book I alone (5 times in Ch 1, 4 times in Ch 4). This is a meaningful character detail that's been overused to the point of tic. Reduce across the entire manuscript to these surviving instances:

**KEEP these (7 total):**
- Ch 1, line 6: "She kept her hand there" — establishing the gesture
- Ch 1, line 26: "She pressed her hand more firmly against her throat, that gesture that had become habit, armor, prayer" — the defining description
- Ch 2, line 91: Promise notices "her hand moving to her throat in a gesture he does not understand" — important for his POV
- Ch 4, line 121: "Sage touched her throat, that habit she couldn't break" — shows it continuing
- Ch 5, line 23: "Sage's hand went to her throat, that habitual gesture, but she caught herself halfway. Let her hand drop." — the turning point, the first time she resists
- Ch 8, line 247: "Her throat bore no marks" — the bruises are gone, final mention in Book I
- Ch 10, line 209: "he put his hands around my throat" — in the letter to her mother, referencing the assault directly

**CUT all other instances**, including:
- Ch 1, lines 36, 72, 110
- Ch 3, lines 73, 211
- Ch 4, lines 145, 147, 175, 215
- Ch 5, line 243
- Ch 6, line 151
- Ch 7, line 200
- Ch 8, line 173

**Method:** For each cut, delete the sentence or clause containing the gesture. In most cases the surrounding text flows naturally without it. Where cutting leaves an awkward gap, bridge with a period or merge surrounding sentences.

**Note:** References to the assault itself (what Pemberton did) that mention the throat can sometimes stay if they serve narrative purpose beyond the repetitive gesture. Use judgment — the goal is to stop the character from touching her throat every few pages, not to erase the assault from her memory.
