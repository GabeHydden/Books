# Chapter 13: Summer Heat and Tempers

The heat arrived on the third week of June like a physical weight, pressing down on Hartwell Park with breathless intensity. By mid-morning the air shimmered over the paddock fences, and the horses stood drowsy in whatever shade they could find, tails swishing at flies.

Sage woke sticky with sweat despite her window being open all night. The light streaming through the curtains had a thick, golden quality that promised the temperature would climb even higher. She dressed in her lightest work dress, already dreading the thought of riding in this heat, and made her way downstairs.

The kitchen was marginally cooler than her room, but not by much. Jennifer stood at the counter cutting fruit, her hair pinned up off her neck, face flushed.

"We're eating cold this morning," she said without preamble. "Too hot for the stove. Rebecca's already restructured the schedule—riding at six and again at seven in the evening. Nothing between ten and four. She left a list on the table."

Sage glanced at the paper by her plate. Rebecca's handwriting was small and precise, each item numbered, each horse's adjusted routine laid out with the kind of systematic clarity that left no room for interpretation. *1. Thunder: light work only, 20 min max. 2. Clover: walk/trot, no jumping. 3. Promise: round pen, 10 min, voice commands only.* Even the water schedule was specified—top up all troughs by nine, again at two, again at six.

"Everyone gets short-tempered in heat like this," Jennifer added, wiping her forehead with the back of her hand. "Horses included. You'll need to be extra patient today."

Sage accepted a plate of bread, cheese, and sliced apples gratefully. The warning seemed prescient an hour later when she went to fetch Thunder from his paddock. The grey gelding was already irritable, pinning his ears when she approached with his halter. She had to wait five minutes for him to relent and lower his head, and even then he tossed it twice, testing her patience.

"I know," she muttered, leading him toward the barn. "I don't want to work in this heat either."

In the barn aisle she tacked him with quick efficiency, wanting to get the work over with before the heat got worse. Saddle pad, saddle, girth cinched. She pulled the bridle off its hook and slid the bit into his mouth, buckling the noseband and throatlatch with practiced hands, her fingers moving through the motions without the careful inspection Riley had drilled into her in those first weeks. Thunder mouthed the bit, working it with his tongue, but Sage was already reaching for her gloves.

***

The exercise ring was already hot despite the early hour, the packed earth radiating yesterday's stored warmth. Riley was setting up poles for a simple gymnastic line—just three small jumps in a row, distances set for an easy canter stride between.

"We're keeping it light today," Riley said. "Just enough work to keep them from getting bored and stiff, not enough to stress them in the heat. Walk him around the ring a few times to warm up, then we'll try the line twice through. That's it."

Thunder's warm-up felt off from the first stride. He was heavy in Sage's hands, leaning on the bit, not listening to her half-halts. Once he tilted his head slightly to the left, as though trying to shift the bit's weight to one side. Sage corrected him with her right rein, annoyed. When she asked for trot, he gave her something between a jog and a stomp—too slow to post properly, too fast to sit comfortably.

"More leg," Riley called. "Send him forward. He's being lazy."

Sage squeezed with her legs. Thunder surged forward—too much, too fast—and she had to check him back with the reins. They repeated this twice more: ask, over-response, check, ask again. Sage felt frustration building in her chest, hot and uncomfortable like the weather.

"All right," Riley said. "Bring him to canter and take the line. Keep him steady between the jumps."

Sage asked for canter. Thunder gave her something that felt more like a four-beat lope, unbalanced and choppy. She asked again, sharper. He pinned his ears but picked up a proper three-beat.

They approached the first jump. Thunder saw it, locked onto it, and accelerated—taking the distance out of Sage's hands completely. They sailed over the first fence, landed, and Thunder surged toward the second before Sage could rebalance. Her hands got hard on the reins, trying to slow him, but he fought through it, jumped the second fence from too close, nearly stumbled on landing, and charged at the third in a flat, strung-out canter.

By the time they were through the line, Sage's jaw was clenched so tight it hurt. Thunder had fought her at every stride, and she'd fought back, and the whole thing had been a battle instead of a conversation.

"Circle," Riley called. "Bring him back to trot."

But Thunder didn't want to trot. He wanted to canter, and when Sage pulled on the reins to insist, he tossed his head and yanked the bit. Something in Sage's chest snapped—frustration, heat, exhaustion all combining—and she pulled harder. Much harder.

Thunder's head came up sharply, his mouth open against the sudden bit pain. For one heartbeat everything hung suspended. Then he reared.

Not a small rear. Not a protest. A full vertical rise, front hooves pawing air, and Sage's hands locked on the reins—not pulling now, just clinging, the leather her only anchor as gravity hauled her backward. For two terrible seconds her full weight hung from his mouth, her arms rigid, her body sliding while her fists refused to let go. Then the reins tore through her fingers and she hit the ground hard on her left side, the impact driving the air from her lungs. Thunder landed on all fours and bolted to the far side of the ring, head high, eyes showing white.

For several seconds Sage couldn't breathe. Couldn't move. Could only lie there in the dirt while her body screamed and her lungs refused to work. Then air rushed back in with a painful gasp.

"Don't move." Rebecca's voice, suddenly beside her. Hands checking her head, her neck, her spine with quick efficiency—the same methodical sequence she used when checking a horse for injury, the same systematic pressure-and-release. "Can you breathe? Can you move your fingers and toes?"

Sage demonstrated both, though breathing hurt. Everything hurt.

"Good. Anything broken?"

"Don't... think so." Speaking hurt too. Her left ribs felt like they'd been kicked.

"Sit up slowly." Rebecca helped her upright, and Sage managed to get there, cradling her left side. But Rebecca's attention had already shifted. Her eyes were fixed on Thunder across the ring—reading him, Sage realized, the way she always read horses: his posture, his breathing, the set of his ears, the way he held his head to one side. She was cataloguing his pain from forty feet away with the same precision she'd bring to reading a page of text.

"His mouth," Rebecca said, almost to herself. Then she turned back to Sage, and something in her expression locked into place—not hardening exactly, but narrowing. The way she looked when she was working through a problem, following the chain of cause and effect to its conclusion.

"What did you just do?"

The question landed like a physical blow. Sage looked at the ground, shame burning hotter than her bruised ribs.

"I pulled too hard on the reins," she whispered. "I hurt his mouth."

"You did more than that." Rebecca's voice had gone flat and precise, the way it did when she was explaining a training principle—laying out facts in sequence, building toward the conclusion that was already clear in her mind. "You took your frustration out on a horse. You punished him for your inability to ride him correctly, and when he reacted to that pain, you got hurt. Thunder wasn't being vicious. He was defending himself. That rear was his way of saying *stop hurting me* when you wouldn't listen to anything else."

Sage felt tears burning behind her eyes, but she blinked them back. Her ribs throbbed with each breath.

"Do you understand what happened here?" Rebecca continued, not pausing, not registering the tears or the way Sage's hands were shaking. She was following the logic of the thing, and the logic wasn't finished. "Thunder was testing you, yes. He was being difficult. But that's what Thunder does. That's his job—to make you prove you can handle him. And when you couldn't, when you got frustrated and angry, you hurt him. And then he hurt you. Not because he's a bad horse, but because you became dangerous to him first."

"I didn't mean to—"

"Intent doesn't matter. Outcome matters." The words came with the certainty of something she'd said many times before, a principle worn smooth from use. "Thunder doesn't know you didn't mean it. He only knows that when he questioned your riding, you hurt his mouth to make him comply. And he did what any sensible creature does when something hurts them—he tried to get away from it." Her voice shifted slightly—not softer, but the rhythm changed, slowed. "You're lucky, Sage. You're bruised and sore but you'll heal. Some riders who make this mistake end up with broken bones or worse. Thunder could have kicked you when you fell. He could have trampled you. He didn't, because he's not a mean horse. But you put yourself in that danger by losing control."

Riley had Thunder now, was stroking his neck, speaking quietly. The gelding was calming but his head was still high, body still tense.

Rebecca stood, her hand going briefly to the fence rail—gripping it, Sage noticed, the knuckles white for just a moment before she released it. She looked down at Sage still sitting in the dirt.

"Here's what's going to happen now. You're going to get up. You're going to help Riley cool Thunder out. You're going to check his mouth for damage from the bit—even though your ribs hurt, even though you're shaken. Because you hurt him first. His welfare comes before your comfort. Do you understand?"

Sage nodded, throat tight.

"And while you're caring for him, you're going to think very carefully about what happened and why. Because if you're going to work with traumatized horses—horses who've been hurt by humans using pain to force compliance—you cannot, *cannot*, be the human who does that to them. Not even for a second. Not even when you're tired and hot and frustrated."

Rebecca held out a hand to help Sage up. Every movement sent pain shooting through her left side, but Sage gritted her teeth and stood.

"Horses don't lie about how they feel," Rebecca said, and something in her voice shifted—quieter now, almost private, as if she were speaking to herself as much as to Sage. "They tell you exactly what's happening inside them, every moment, with their whole bodies. Thunder was telling you from the first stride that he was uncomfortable, hot, not wanting to work hard. And instead of listening and adjusting, you tried to force him to work the way you wanted. He escalated his communication until you couldn't ignore it anymore. That rear was him screaming at you. And you should never, ever push a horse to the point where they have to scream."

Sage limped toward where Riley stood with Thunder. The gelding's eyes tracked her approach, still wary. She wanted to sit down, wanted to go inside and lie down and let someone else deal with this. But Rebecca was right. Thunder came first.

"I'm sorry," she told him, though her voice came out strained. "I wasn't listening. I hurt you, and that was wrong."

Thunder's ears flicked toward her voice but he didn't relax. Not yet.

***

Sage spent the rest of the morning caring for Thunder with meticulous attention, though every movement pulled at her bruised ribs. She walked him in hand, forcing herself to keep moving even when she wanted to stop and catch her breath. Her left side throbbed with each step, and she could feel the bruising spreading, tightening.

But Thunder had to be cooled out properly. Had to be walked until his breathing was normal, until his agitation settled. So she walked him, one careful step at a time, murmuring apologies he couldn't understand.

When he was finally cool, she hosed his legs with hands that shook slightly—from pain or shame or both, she wasn't sure. She picked his feet carefully, checking each hoof even though bending over made her ribs scream. Then, dreading it, she tied him in the cross-ties to check his mouth.

She unbuckled the cheekpieces to remove the bridle, easing the bit from Thunder's mouth as gently as she could. He tensed as she touched his face—he remembered what had happened, remembered the pain—and she had to move slowly, carefully, earning his tolerance. When the bit was free she held it in her hands, meaning to set it aside.

Her thumb caught on something.

She turned the bit over, ran the pad of her finger along the left side of the mouthpiece. There—a rough edge, a burr of metal where the surface had worn or been damaged, sharp enough to catch her fingertip. Sharp enough to cut.

For a moment Sage didn't breathe. She stood there holding the bit, feeling the burr against her thumb, and the morning reassembled itself with horrible clarity. Thunder heavy in her hands from the first stride—not lazy, not testing her, but trying to shift his mouth away from the thing that was cutting him. The head-tilt she'd corrected with her rein. The tossing, the yanking, the refusal to settle. Every signal she'd read as *difficult* had been *something is wrong with my bit* and she hadn't—

She hadn't checked. Riley had taught her. Run your fingers along the bit before every ride. Feel for rough edges. The horse can't tell you his equipment is wrong. And she'd bridled him that morning without touching the bit at all.

The burr could have been there for days. Every ride, pressing against the soft tissue of his mouth. And she'd never felt it because she'd stopped looking.

When she got a clear look at his mouth, her stomach lurched. There was a cut at the left corner where the burr had sat—small but deep, the tissue around it raw and inflamed. The bars of his mouth were red and swollen on both sides from her weight on the reins during the fall.

"Oh, Thunder," she whispered, tears finally spilling over. "I'm so sorry."

She set the bit on the ledge of the nearest stall—carefully, as though it were evidence—and fetched salve from the medical kit, the one Rebecca kept for treating mouth injuries. Her ribs protested every movement, but she ignored them. Carefully, gently, she applied the salve to the cut and the inflamed areas, using her fingers to work it in with the lightest possible touch. Thunder tolerated it, though his ears stayed back, uncertain.

Riley appeared with carrots and helped hold Thunder steady while Sage finished treating his mouth. When she was done, Sage offered Thunder the carrots, one at a time. He took them carefully, chewing slowly, favoring the right side of his mouth—the side that hurt less.

"The bit," Sage said, her voice barely working. She pointed to where she'd set it on the stall ledge. "There's a burr on the left side. I didn't check it this morning. I didn't—" Her throat closed.

Riley picked up the bit and ran a finger along the mouthpiece. Found the burr. Went very still.

"How long has this been here?" Riley's voice was quiet. Not angry—something worse than angry.

"I don't know. I haven't checked the bit in—" Sage couldn't finish the sentence. Couldn't say *weeks, maybe*. Couldn't admit that the lesson Riley had taught her in those first days, the one she'd practiced so carefully on Clover and then on Thunder himself, had become something she skipped because it was boring and nothing had ever been wrong and she'd wanted to get to the riding.

Riley set the bit down and looked at Sage for a long moment. "I should have checked it myself before the lesson. I was in the ring. I should have looked." Then, quieter: "But I taught you to check, Sage. I taught you, and you stopped doing it."

Thunder crunched his carrot, watching her with one dark eye. Not forgiving, not yet. Just watching. Waiting to see what she'd do next.

Sage put him in his stall with extra hay and a salt lick, made sure his water was fresh and cool. Only then, when Thunder was settled and cared for, did she allow herself to sit down on the bench outside his stall.

The movement made her gasp. Her entire left side was on fire now, the bruising setting in hard. She touched her ribs gingerly through her dress and winced—definitely bruised, possibly cracked, though she didn't think anything was broken. Her hip where she'd landed throbbed. Her shoulder ached.

*Good*, she thought viciously. She deserved it. Thunder's mouth would hurt for days because of what she'd done—her temper and her negligence both—so her ribs could hurt too.

She heard Rebecca before she saw her. Footsteps in the barn aisle, unhurried, deliberate. Rebecca appeared at Thunder's stall, checked him over the door with that focused attention she gave injured horses—the quick systematic sweep of eyes cataloguing posture, breathing, weight distribution. Then her gaze found the bit on the stall ledge.

She picked it up. Turned it over in her hands. Ran her thumb along the mouthpiece with methodical precision, the way she might check a horse's tendon for heat—systematically, every surface. Her thumb found the burr and stopped.

"When did you last check this bit?" she asked, not looking up. Her voice was flat. Careful. "Really check it—fingers along every edge, the way Riley showed you?"

Sage couldn't speak.

"This burr has been here for some time. Days, perhaps." Rebecca turned the bit in the light, examining the rough metal. "Every ride he's done with this in his mouth, he's been feeling it. And every ride, you didn't notice." She set the bit down—precisely, aligned with the edge of the ledge—and turned to look at Sage. "The emotional control matters. Of course it matters. But the failure didn't start in the ring, Sage. It started in the tack room. It started every time you decided the preparation was less important than the performance."

The words landed on top of everything Rebecca had already said at the ring, and the combined weight of it was almost more than Sage could bear. She'd thought the lesson was about losing her temper. That was bad enough. But this was worse—this was a pattern, a slow erosion of the care Riley had taught her, and Thunder had paid for it.

"He was in pain from the moment I put the bit in," Sage said. The words scraped out of her. "Everything he did—the heaviness, the head-tossing, the fighting—he was trying to tell me his mouth hurt. And I thought he was being difficult."

"Yes," Rebecca said. There was no softness in it. Just confirmation.

She checked Thunder once more through the stall door, satisfying herself that he was settled, then walked away without another word.

Riley found Sage on the bench a few minutes later, still sitting, still staring at the bit Rebecca had left on the ledge.

"You need to let Jennifer look at those ribs," Riley said.

"I don't deserve—"

"Sage." Riley's voice was firm. "Thunder is fine. You treated his injury. Now you need to treat yours. You can't care for horses if you're hurting too much to move properly. That's not noble, it's just stupid."

Sage let Riley help her up, biting back a gasp at the movement. Every step toward the house was agony, but she made herself take them. Thunder's pain was her fault. Her own pain was just... consequence.

In the kitchen, Jennifer took one look at her and gestured to a chair.

"Shirt off," she said, no-nonsense. "Or at least loosened enough that I can see your side."

With Riley's help, Sage managed to get her dress unlaced enough for Jennifer to examine her ribs. The touch made her hiss.

"Deep breath," Jennifer instructed, hands gentle but thorough. "Does this hurt? Here? What about here?"

After several minutes of careful probing, Jennifer sat back. "You're lucky. Badly bruised but I don't feel any breaks. You'll be sore for at least a week, maybe two. Breathing will hurt. Bending will hurt. Riding will definitely hurt." She went to fetch a jar of arnica salve. "You need to rest today. No more work."

"I need to check on Thunder—"

"Riley will check on Thunder," Jennifer said firmly, working the salve into Sage's ribs with careful fingers. "You've done your part caring for him. Now you need to care for yourself so you can get back to work when you've healed. Understood?"

Sage nodded, biting her lip against both physical and emotional pain.

When Jennifer was done, Riley helped Sage back into her dress and walked her to the bench in the shade outside. They sat in silence for a moment before Riley spoke.

"Rebecca's hard on you because she sees what you could become," Riley said quietly. "And because—" Riley paused, seeming to choose words carefully. "When an animal gets hurt, everything else goes away for her. She can't think about anything else until she's certain the animal is safe and whoever caused the harm understands exactly what went wrong. She's been that way since I've known her. It's not that she doesn't care about you. It's that she couldn't get to caring about you until she'd finished caring about Thunder. Does that make sense?"

Sage thought about the way Rebecca had checked her injuries with brisk efficiency, then immediately turned her attention to reading Thunder's body across the ring. The way her analysis had been complete and unsparing, every point delivered in sequence without pausing to gauge its impact. The way she'd gripped the fence rail—the only crack in her composure, and one she'd hidden almost immediately.

"It makes sense," Sage said slowly.

"Most riders never learn to control their emotions," Riley continued. "They go their whole lives getting frustrated and yanking on reins and thinking that's just part of riding. Sometimes they hurt horses. Sometimes horses hurt them back, like Thunder did. But you can be better than that. You have to be better than that, if you're going to do this work."

"How do you control it?" Sage asked, her voice small. "The frustration, the anger when things aren't going right?"

"Practice. Same as everything else. You recognize the feeling when it starts—that tightness in your chest, that clenching in your jaw. And you breathe through it. You make yourself softer instead of harder. You ask yourself: what is my horse trying to tell me? What am I not hearing?" Riley's voice was patient, understanding. "It's not about never feeling frustrated. It's about never letting that frustration control your hands."

"I don't know if I can do that."

"You can. You already did it with Promise, when you were sitting at his fence for days and he wouldn't come close. You must have felt frustrated then, wanted to rush him, wanted to make progress happen faster. But you didn't. You stayed patient. You let him decide." Riley gestured toward Thunder's stall. "That's the skill you need with all of them. That patience you found with Promise—you need it with Thunder too. With every horse you ride."

"What if I can't?" The question came out barely above a whisper. "What if today wasn't just a mistake? What if I'm not good enough to do this work?"

"Then you keep trying until you are. Today you learned what happens when you lose control. Your ribs will remind you of that lesson for the next two weeks." Riley's voice was firm but kind. "Use that. Remember how this feels—physically and emotionally—and choose differently next time. That's all any of us can do."

***

That afternoon was too hot for any work at all. The horses stood drowsing in their paddocks, and the humans retreated to the shade. Sage made her slow, painful way to Promise's fence, moving carefully to avoid jostling her ribs. Every breath reminded her of what had happened.

Promise was standing under the single tree in his paddock, his head low, completely relaxed. She sat at her usual spot by the fence—sitting down hurt almost as much as standing—not expecting him to approach in this heat. But after a few minutes he ambled over anyway, drawn by habit or curiosity or the hope of an apple.

She produced one from her pocket with a wince, and he took it with his careful precision.

"I made a mistake today," she told him, one hand pressed against her aching ribs. "I got frustrated with Thunder and I hurt him. He reared and threw me off. My ribs are bruised and Thunder's mouth is cut. We both got hurt because I lost control."

Promise leaned into her scratching, comfortable. His presence was solid and uncomplicated—he didn't care about her mistakes with other horses. He only cared about whether she was safe and predictable with him.

"Rebecca says I have to be able to control my own emotions first. Always. Because if I can't..." She trailed off, looking at Promise's scars, the ones on his shoulder and neck that spoke of someone who'd lost control. Who'd hurt him with crop and bit and standing martingale. "Because if I can't control myself, I become dangerous. Like the humans who hurt you."

The realization settled cold and heavy despite the heat. She could become that person. The one who caused the scars. The one Promise had learned to fear.

"I'm learning," Sage continued, more to herself than to Promise. "Learning that it's not enough to know how to ride. I have to know how to control myself too. My emotions, my frustrations, my impatience. Because if I can't control those things, I'll hurt the horses I'm trying to help. I already did hurt one."

She thought about Thunder's cut mouth, the way he'd favored one side while eating. Thought about his wariness after, the way he'd tensed when she touched his face. Trust damaged in a single moment of lost control.

"It's hard," she admitted to Promise, scratching behind his ears despite the pull on her ribs. "Harder than learning to post the trot or sit a spook or count strides to a jump. Because those are physical skills that can be practiced and perfected. But this—managing my own internal state, keeping myself calm and patient even when everything is difficult—this requires a different kind of strength."

Promise pushed his nose against her shoulder, the gesture that meant *here, comfortable, present*. Even that gentle pressure made her ribs ache, but she didn't pull away.

"I'll keep learning," Sage promised him. "I'll get better. Not just at riding, but at being the kind of person who can ride you. When you're ready. If you're ready. Because you deserve someone who won't lose control. Who won't hurt you, even when things are hard."

She sat with him until the heat drove her back to the shade of the house, each step a reminder of the morning's lesson. Eventually Promise wandered back to the tree, and Sage made her slow way inside.

***

That evening, when the worst of the heat had broken, Sage was sitting carefully on the front steps, her posture guarded against the pain in her ribs, when the kitchen door opened behind her. Jennifer's voice, low: "She's on the steps. Go talk to her." And then, quieter still: "Gently, Rebecca."

A pause. Then the door opened wider and Rebecca came out, settling beside her with the careful deliberation of someone who had been told to sit and was now executing the instruction. She left a precise gap between them—not too close, not too far. The same distance she maintained with a nervous horse in the early days.

"How are you?" she asked, and the question had the slight formality of someone who knows this is the expected opening but isn't entirely sure what to do with the answer.

"Sore. Ashamed. But understanding why you were so angry."

Rebecca was quiet for a moment, looking out over the property. Her hands rested on her knees, fingers spread, very still. "Jennifer says your ribs are badly bruised but not broken. You'll be out of the saddle for at least a week, maybe more."

Sage nodded, the movement pulling at her side. "How is Thunder?"

Something in Rebecca's posture eased at the question—as if horse welfare were solid ground, a place she knew how to stand. "His mouth will hurt for several days. Riley's checking it twice daily and applying salve. He's eating, though he's favoring the less damaged side. He'll heal." She turned to look at Sage directly—that gaze of hers that was always a degree too steady, too searching. "The question is whether you'll heal—not just physically, but whether you'll let this destroy your confidence or teach you something essential."

"I don't know how to ride him again after this. How to face him knowing what I did."

"The same way you faced him this morning after it happened. You apologized—not with words he could understand, but with your actions. You cared for him despite your own pain. You put his welfare above your comfort. That matters." Rebecca's voice was firm. "When you're healed enough to ride again, you'll go back to basics with Thunder. Every piece of tack inspected before every ride. Walking only. Listening to what he tells you. Proving to him that you learned from your mistake. It won't be easy. He'll test whether you've changed. But you can earn back his trust if you're willing to do the work."

"What if I make the same mistake again?"

"Then you'll face the same consequences again, and hopefully learn the lesson more deeply. But I don't think you will." Rebecca paused, and what came next had the halting quality of something she'd been thinking about for hours—composing it carefully, the way she composed her training plans, getting each piece in the right order. "I was harsh with you today. Harsher than I should have been, perhaps. I saw Thunder's mouth and I couldn't—" She stopped. Started again, more carefully. "When an animal is hurt, I become very focused on the animal. On understanding what happened and making certain it doesn't happen again. I don't always... attend to the rest of it. The human part. Jennifer tells me this is a failing."

It was the most vulnerable thing Sage had ever heard Rebecca say. Not an excuse—Rebecca didn't make excuses—but an explanation. An acknowledgment that her response this morning, while correct in substance, had been delivered without any consideration for Sage's emotional state.

"You weren't wrong, though," Sage said. "About any of it."

"No. But being right and being kind aren't always the same thing, and I'm not always good at managing both." Rebecca's hands pressed flat against her knees, that careful stillness again. "I've seen riders who never learn this lesson. Who go their whole careers thinking that force is acceptable when patience fails. Who break horses' spirits with their tempers and never even realize they're doing it. Some of them get hurt badly—broken backs, shattered legs. Some of them never get hurt at all, and so they keep doing it, keep hurting horses, because there's no consequence to teach them." She gestured to Sage's injured side. "Your ribs will heal in two weeks. Thunder's mouth will heal in three days. But the lesson—that you learned in your body that losing control is dangerous—that will stay with you forever. You'll remember this before you let frustration take over your hands again."

"I hope so," Sage said quietly.

"I know so. Because you spent the morning caring for Thunder before Jennifer even looked at you. Because you're sitting here asking about his welfare before your own. Because you understand what you did wrong and why it mattered." Rebecca stood, brushed off her skirt. "Rest tomorrow. Let your ribs start healing. The day after, you'll work with Clover—just walking, nothing more—to keep your skills up. When you're ready, we'll put you back on Thunder. And you'll prove to him that you're better than your worst moment."

"What if he doesn't forgive me?"

"He already has. Horses don't hold grudges the way humans do. They respond to who you are in each moment, not who you were yesterday. Thunder will give you another chance." Rebecca's eyes were steady. "The question is whether you'll forgive yourself enough to take it."

She paused at the door, one hand on the frame, and looked back. "Sage. You are not Thornton. What happened today was a mistake, not a pattern. There is a difference." Then she went inside, and Sage heard Jennifer's voice again, low and warm, though she couldn't make out the words. Only the tone—approving, gentle, the particular voice Jennifer used when Rebecca had done something difficult and done it well.

After Rebecca went inside, Sage sat on the steps a while longer despite the discomfort, watching the sun sink lower and the shadows stretch long across the fields. In his paddock, Promise grazed peacefully, the heat finally releasing its grip as evening came on.

She thought about the lesson she'd learned today. About how controlling horses required first controlling yourself. About how the work of rehabilitation wasn't just teaching horses to trust humans again—it was teaching humans to be worthy of that trust. About how consequences—both physical and emotional—were sometimes the only way to learn that lesson deeply enough to change.

But she thought, too, about Rebecca. About the way she'd gripped the fence rail, the only sign that the morning had cost her anything. About the way she'd delivered her devastating analysis without once pausing to acknowledge Sage's pain—not from cruelty, but from a mind that worked in sequences, that followed the logic of cause and effect to its end before it could attend to anything else. About Jennifer's voice at the door: *Gently, Rebecca.* The quiet guidance of a woman who had spent fifteen years learning to translate her partner to the world.

Rebecca understood horses the way most people understood conversation—instinctively, completely, in their own language. With people, she had to work harder. Had to think through what came naturally to others. Had to be reminded, sometimes, that being right wasn't enough.

But she tried. She came out to the steps and said *I was harsh* and explained what she could of why, and that trying—that willingness to step outside her area of fluency—was its own kind of courage.

Her ribs throbbed with each breath. Thunder's mouth hurt from her mistake. But both would heal.

And she would be better. Would have to be better. Would remember this pain—hers and his—and choose differently next time. Would run her fingers along every inch of every bit before every ride, the way Riley had taught her, the way she'd stopped doing because it was tedious and nothing had ever been wrong.

The heat would return. Frustrations would return. The temptation to skip the unglamorous work would return.

But Sage would be ready. Would choose patience over force. Would breathe through the tightness in her chest instead of letting it control her hands. Would remember that the care was the work—all of it, every time—and that the shortcuts she took when no one was watching were the ones that hurt someone eventually.

One breath, one choice, one moment at a time.

Even when—especially when—it hurt to learn it.
