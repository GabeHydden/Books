# Chapter 22: The Fall
**Book III: Autumn — Partnership**
**POV: Promise, then Sage**
**Mid-October 1813**

---

**PROMISE**

Something is different-today.

He knows this the way he knows rain-coming or storm-approaching — not through thought-understanding but through body-knowing, the subtle shifts in the humans-around-him that his survival-senses read without trying.

Small-human comes early. Earlier than usual-pattern. Her hands shake slight-trembling when she clips the lead rope, but her voice is steady-warm, the sounds she makes full of the feeling he's learned means *good-thing-coming*.

She grooms him in the barn aisle, taking more time than usual, working the brush in the long slow strokes he likes best. He leans into the attention, comfortable. This is known-good. Small-human-touch is always known-good.

The saddle comes next. He watches her lift it from the rail — the shape-familiar now, the weight-expected. She settles it onto his back and he shifts to accommodate, the way he's learned. Her fingers work the girth, testing tension, adjusting. She runs a hand along the bit, the same way she always does — checking, careful. He mouths the metal and stands.

Then she leads him to the round pen, and the difference-today becomes clear.

The older-one-who-decides is already there, standing in the center with the rope-long in her hands. That is normal-expected. But the tall-one-with-quiet-eyes is there too, standing near the rail, wearing different-clothes. Heavier boots. Closer-fitting things. The kind of clothes that mean serious-work.

Promise's ears flick between the three humans, reading-assessing. Small-human. Older-one. Tall-one. All familiar. All proven-safe in their different ways.

Small-human clips the longe line and steps back to the rail. The older-one clucks and he walks, circling in the pattern-known. Walk. Halt. Turn. Walk again. The saddle sits solid-comfortable on his back. He's learned that the saddle means work and work means rewards and none of it means hurt.

Everything is normal-routine. Except for the tall-one, who has come away from the rail and stands near the older-one in the center, watching him with an expression his survival-senses can't quite read.

---

The older-one calls halt. Promise stops, turning to face her the way he's been taught. Small-human comes from the rail, takes his lead rope, stands at his head with her hand on his halter. Her fingers find the spot behind his ear and scratch, and he leans into her palm.

Known-good. Small-human at his head means nothing-bad-will-happen.

The tall-one approaches his left side. Promise watches, ears forward-alert. The tall-one has been near him many times — in the barn aisle, at the paddock fence, on the far end of the longe line. But not like this. Not at his shoulder with purpose in every step.

The tall-one's hand touches his neck. Flat palm, firm pressure. Different from small-human's touch — the hand is larger, heavier, the scent underneath the leather-and-hay smell is different-human. Not wrong. Just not-the-same.

The hand moves down his shoulder to the saddle. Rests there. Promise feels the faint vibration of another heartbeat through the leather.

Then the tall-one leans.

He knows this pattern. Small-human has done it — forearms across the saddle, weight pressing down, the creak of leather. He braces, rebalances. The tall-one's weight is different from small-human's weight. Heavier. The pressure sits in a different place across his spine.

But it is the same pattern-known. Weight-on-saddle. He has done this. He can do this.

The tall-one leans more. Promise shifts his feet, spreading slightly for balance. His ears flick back toward the weight, then forward again. Small-human is at his head, her voice making the soft sounds that mean *good, steady, I'm-here*.

The weight lifts away. His back lightens. Then the weight returns — heavier this time, the tall-one's feet barely on the ground, most of the tall-one's body suspended from the saddle.

Promise's skin twitches. A muscle in his shoulder jumps. But he holds.

He holds because small-human is at his head and the pattern is known and this is how the work goes — weight comes, weight goes, nothing hurts, and afterward there are carrots.

The tall-one's weight lifts away again. Returns. Lifts. Returns. Four times. Five. Each time Promise's body settles faster, learning this new variation — taller-heavier-different but still just weight-on-saddle.

Small-human's voice says the sound-pattern that means *good boy* and her fingers scratch behind his ear and he is calm. He is managing. He is being the horse they've taught him to be.

Then the tall-one puts a foot in the stirrup.

---

**SAGE**

Everything was going perfectly.

Promise stood calm under Riley's leaning weight, his ears relaxed, his breathing steady. Sage kept one hand on his halter and the other behind his ear, talking to him in the low murmur that had become their private language. Rebecca held the longe line from the center of the pen, watching everything with her usual intensity.

Riley had gone through the progression exactly as planned. Hand on neck. Hand on saddle. Leaning. Bouncing gently. Leaning with full weight. Each step patient, each step waiting for Promise to settle before moving forward. And Promise had settled every time, his trust in the routine — in Sage's presence — carrying him through.

"He's doing beautifully," Sage said, and meant it.

Riley straightened from the last lean, brushed dust from the front of his shirt. He looked at Rebecca.

"I'm going to lie across him now," Riley said. "Same as Sage did in practice. Belly down, legs hanging. If he tolerates that, we can try the mount."

Rebecca nodded. "Sage, stay exactly where you are. Promise, easy."

Riley put his left foot in the stirrup and pushed up smoothly, swinging his upper body across the saddle so he lay on his stomach, legs hanging on the near side, arms draped on the far side. More weight than the leaning — all of Riley's weight now, centered on the saddle, pressing down through the stirrup.

Promise tensed. Every muscle went tight, and Sage felt it through the halter — the sudden rigidity, the coiled energy of a horse deciding between trust and flight. His head came up, nostrils flaring.

"Easy," Sage said, her fingers steady on his halter. "Easy, boy. It's Riley. You know Riley."

His ear swiveled to her voice. His breathing was rapid but controlled. His eye, when it rolled back to look at the weight across his back, showed a rim of white — but just a rim. He was processing. Working through it.

Five seconds. Ten. Sage counted them, her own heart beating too fast. Promise's head lowered half an inch. His breathing slowed.

"He's settling," Rebecca said from the center. Quiet. Watchful.

Riley lay still, letting Promise feel the full weight, making no sudden movements. After twenty seconds, Promise blew out through his nose — the long exhale that meant tension releasing. His muscles softened under the saddle. His head dropped another inch.

"Good boy," Sage breathed. "Good, brave boy."

Riley slid off carefully. Promise shook himself once, all over, then turned to look at Riley with his ears forward — *what was that about?* — before nosing at Sage's pocket for a carrot.

"Again," Rebecca said.

Riley went up again. Belly-down, full weight. Promise tensed, settled, blew out — faster this time. The learning was visible, each repetition compressing the fear-cycle shorter. By the fourth time, he barely reacted at all. Just stood, weight braced, waiting for the pattern to complete.

"Good," Rebecca said, and something in her voice relaxed the smallest degree. "He's accepting it. The belly-down weight isn't triggering him."

Riley slid off and stood at Promise's shoulder, one hand resting on his neck. He looked at Sage, then at Rebecca.

"I'm going to sit up," Riley said. "Swing my leg over. Sit in the saddle properly."

Sage nodded, her hand tightening on Promise's halter. This was it. The moment they'd been building toward all autumn. The moment when Promise would carry a rider for the first time since Thornton's yard — a kind rider, a gentle rider, a rider who would ask instead of demand.

"I'll be right here," Sage told Promise, rubbing behind his ear. "Right here the whole time."

Rebecca shortened the longe line slightly. "Whenever you're ready, Riley."

Riley took a breath. Put his left foot in the stirrup. Pushed up, belly across the saddle — and this time, instead of staying there, swung his right leg over.

The motion was smooth-practiced, the kind of movement a body made when it had done a thing many times before. Riley settled into the saddle, weight shifting, finding balance, legs coming down naturally on both sides of Promise's body.

Both sides.

For one second — maybe two — Promise stood still. Sage felt it through the halter, the absolute stillness of a horse whose mind and body were at war. His ears were forward, his jaw clenched on the bit, every muscle locked in the rigid stillness that precedes either acceptance or explosion.

*Come on,* Sage thought. *You know Riley. You know this is safe. Come on.*

Then Promise went sideways.

Not a spook — a detonation. Every muscle fired at once, his hindquarters swinging left while his shoulders threw right, and the force of it nearly ripped the lead rope from Sage's hands. She staggered, held on, but Promise was already going up — front legs off the ground, body vertical, and Riley grabbed mane with both hands but the saddle was tilting and the physics were impossible —

Promise came down and spun. A full rotation, hindquarters whipping around, and the centrifugal force broke Riley's grip on the mane like fingers snapped from a ledge. Riley went sideways out of the saddle, one foot catching the stirrup for a terrible half-second before it came free, and then Riley was in the air and then Riley was on the ground.

The sound was wrong. Not the thud of a body hitting soft footing — something harder, sharper, a grunt forced out by impact and then silence.

Promise bolted to the far side of the round pen. The longe line tore through Rebecca's hands — Sage heard her hiss of pain — and then he was at the rail, pressed against it, trembling so hard she could see it from twenty feet away. His head was high, nostrils showing red, eyes rolling white all the way around. Sweat darkened his neck in patches.

He looked like the horse who'd arrived seven months ago. The one who wouldn't let a human within ten feet.

But Sage wasn't looking at Promise.

"Riley!" She was on her knees in the dirt before she knew she'd moved, her hands hovering over Riley's crumpled shape. Riley lay on his left side, one arm trapped beneath his body, face pressed into the round pen footing. Not moving.

*Not moving.*

"Riley, can you hear me?"

A sound. Not words — a groan, low and wrong, and then Riley moved. Tried to push up with the right arm, collapsed, and the sound that came out was sharp and bitten off. Pain.

"Don't move. Don't try to move." Sage's hands found Riley's face, turning it gently from the dirt. Riley's eyes were open, glassy with shock, unfocused.

"Promise—" Riley managed.

"Don't worry about Promise."

"He wasn't — " Riley's breath hitched, and the next words came through clenched teeth. "He wasn't being mean. He was somewhere else. I felt it — the second I sat up, he was gone. Somewhere else entirely."

"Please stop talking. Please just stay still."

Behind her, she could hear Rebecca — quick footsteps moving toward Promise's corner, the low commanding voice working to manage a terrified horse. Rebecca would handle that. Rebecca always handled that.

"Where does it hurt?" Sage asked, her voice steadier than anything inside her.

"Shoulder. Left. And ribs." Riley's face was grey-white. "Caught the stirrup — felt it wrench when I came free. Can't —" A breath that turned into a gasp. "Can't breathe properly."

Sage's hand moved to Riley's left shoulder and found it wrong — the angle of it, the way it sat lower than the right, the unnatural hollow above the joint. She didn't know enough to diagnose, but she knew enough to recognize that a shoulder shouldn't look like that.

"Jennifer!" she shouted toward the barn. Then, louder, abandoning any pretense of calm: "*Jennifer!*"

Riley's right hand found Sage's wrist and gripped it. The strength of the grip was reassuring — Riley was conscious, coherent, holding on.

"Promise?" Riley asked again.

"Rebecca has him. He's in the corner. He's — " Sage glanced across the pen. Promise was pressed against the far rail, head high, still trembling. Rebecca stood ten feet away, not approaching, just talking in the low steady voice she used with frightened animals. "He's scared. But he's safe."

"He wasn't mean," Riley said again. "Promise. Remember that. He wasn't being mean."

"I know. I know he wasn't."

Jennifer appeared at a run, medical bag in hand, and from that point the morning fractured into sharp-edged pieces that Sage would later have to assemble into order.

Jennifer's hands, quick and certain, moving over Riley's body. The quiet clinical words — *dislocation, yes; ribs, at least two cracked; breathing compromised, watch for pneumothorax*. Riley's face going grey-white when Jennifer tested the range of the left arm and the shoulder ground against itself.

"We need to get you inside," Jennifer said. "Sage, other side. We're going to help Riley stand. Riley, right arm over Sage's shoulders. Don't try to use the left at all."

Getting Riley upright took three attempts. Each one wrung sounds from Riley that Sage would have given anything not to hear — not screams, nothing so dramatic, just small involuntary noises that escaped through clenched teeth and spoke of pain beyond what willpower could fully contain. By the time they were standing — Riley swaying, right arm clamped over Sage's shoulders, left arm hanging useless against the sling Jennifer had improvised from her own shawl — sweat stood out on Riley's forehead despite the October chill.

"Can you walk?" Jennifer asked.

"Yes." Riley's jaw was set so tight the word barely escaped. He took a step. Then another. Sage matched every one, bearing as much of Riley's weight as she could, her arm locked around Riley's waist.

They were halfway across the stable yard when Riley stumbled on a cobblestone. Sage caught him — both arms now, Riley's full weight against her chest, and she could feel Riley shaking, the fine constant tremor of a body in more pain than it could easily process.

"I've got you," she said. "I've got you. Keep going."

Riley's breath was hot against her neck. "I'm sorry."

"Don't you dare apologize."

"He wasn't ready. I knew he wasn't ready and I—"

"Not now. We'll talk about everything later. Right now you're walking to the house and Jennifer is going to fix your shoulder and that is the only thing that matters."

They reached the kitchen door. Jennifer had gone ahead to prepare — Sage could hear her clearing the long table, issuing instructions to the cook for hot water and clean cloths. Sage helped Riley through the door, guided him to the chair Jennifer indicated, and only then stepped back.

Riley sat hunched forward, right hand cradling the left arm, face the color of ash. His shirt was torn at the shoulder seam and dirt streaked one side of his face. He looked diminished in a way Sage had never seen — Riley, who was always so capable, so steady, so certain in the saddle.

"Stay with Riley," Jennifer told Sage. "I need to reduce this shoulder before the swelling gets worse. It will hurt. Hold Riley's right hand and don't let him move."

Sage took Riley's right hand. Their eyes met, and in Riley's gaze Sage saw pain, yes — but also something else. Something that looked like the thing she herself had felt lying in the dirt after Thunder reared. The particular shame of a horseperson who has come off.

"It wasn't your fault," Sage said.

Jennifer positioned herself, hands on Riley's shoulder, and said, "On three. One—"

She didn't wait for three. The shoulder went back with a sound like a muffled pop, and Riley's grip on Sage's hand crushed hard enough to grind the bones together. Riley made no sound at all. Just went rigid, then slowly, carefully, let out a breath that shuddered all the way through.

"Done," Jennifer said. "The shoulder's back. I'm going to strap it. You'll be in a sling for at least a fortnight, probably longer. The ribs—" She pressed gently along Riley's left side, and Riley flinched. "Cracked, two of them at least. Same prescription as Sage got in summer. Rest. Time. No riding."

"How long?" Riley asked.

"Weeks. Perhaps a month."

Riley closed his eyes.

Sage still held Riley's hand. She realized she should let go. She didn't.

---

When Riley was settled — shoulder strapped, ribs wrapped, installed in the armchair by the kitchen fire with a cup of whatever Jennifer had mixed that would dull the pain — Sage made herself go back outside.

She didn't want to. Wanted to stay in the kitchen where Riley was, where the crisis was contained and manageable, where holding someone's hand was useful work.

But Promise was still in the round pen.

She found Rebecca leaning against the rail, watching. Inside the pen, Promise stood in the far corner — exactly where he'd been when Sage left. He hadn't moved. His head hung low now, the adrenaline spent, but his body still dark with drying sweat. Even from the rail Sage could see the rapid flutter of his nostrils.

"Is Riley—" Rebecca began.

"Dislocated shoulder. Jennifer put it back. Two cracked ribs, at least. Out of riding for weeks." The words came out flat, clinical, borrowed from Jennifer's vocabulary because Sage didn't trust her own.

Rebecca nodded once. She hadn't taken her eyes off Promise — hands flat on the rail, body angled toward the horse, processing what she could see with that focused intensity that blocked out everything else. The human part of the crisis — Riley's injury, Sage's guilt — would come later, once the animal was assessed.

"How is he?" Sage asked, though she could see the answer.

"He's gone." Rebecca's voice was quiet, stripped of its usual authority. "Not physically — he's sound, I checked him while you were inside. But everything we built, everything he learned about trusting humans on his back..." She paused. "Look at him, Sage. He's back in Thornton's yard."

Promise stood with every muscle locked, his weight pushed back onto his haunches as though ready to bolt. His ears were pinned flat. His eyes showed white. He looked like the horse who'd arrived seven months ago — the one who wouldn't let a human within ten feet.

Sage gripped the rail until her knuckles ached.

"I told you he was ready," she said. "I said he could handle it. I pushed for this."

"Yes."

"Riley said Promise didn't know him. Said Promise's trust was in me, not — and I told Riley it would be fine. That Promise knew Riley. That he'd be all right."

"Yes."

The single word, repeated, was worse than any lecture. Rebecca wasn't arguing. Wasn't softening it. Just confirming what Sage already knew.

"Riley tried to tell me," Sage whispered. "And I was so certain."

Rebecca was quiet for a long moment. Then: "We were all part of this decision. I chose Riley as the first rider. My logic was sound — Riley *is* the most experienced. What I didn't account for—" She paused, her jaw working. "What I didn't account for is that logic isn't always what matters with a horse like Promise. His trust isn't logical. It's specific. Earned by one person, through one particular relationship, and it doesn't transfer on a schedule we decide."

They stood together watching Promise tremble in the corner. The morning sun had risen above the tree line, warm and golden, completely at odds with everything that had happened beneath it.

"What do we do now?" Sage asked.

"Now we give him space. We don't touch him today. Don't approach. We let him come down from this on his own terms." Rebecca straightened from the rail. "And tomorrow, we start again."

"From the beginning?"

"From wherever he tells us he is."

Rebecca walked toward the house. Sage stayed at the rail.

In the round pen, Promise stood alone. The saddle had been removed — Rebecca must have managed it while Sage was inside with Riley, somehow getting close enough despite his terror, because the woman could do things with horses that defied explanation. But the marks of the girth were visible in his sweat-darkened coat. His whole body shook in small, rhythmic tremors, like a horse standing in freezing rain.

He didn't look at Sage. Didn't turn toward her voice when she said his name. Didn't acknowledge her at all.

She stood there anyway. For an hour. For two. Until Jennifer came to tell her that Riley was sleeping and she should eat something.

She ate without tasting. Went back to the rail. Promise hadn't moved.

The afternoon light changed. Shadows lengthened across the round pen. Somewhere on the estate, the other horses called to each other — Clover's low nicker, Thunder's imperious whinny, Whisper's answering call. The sounds of ordinary life continuing around a catastrophe.

Near dusk, Promise moved. Not toward Sage — toward his water bucket at the far side of the pen. He drank in short, nervous gulps, his head snapping up between each one to scan for threats. Then he retreated to his corner.

It was the first voluntary movement he'd made in hours. Sage took it as the only kind of hope available.

She stayed until full dark, until she couldn't see him anymore, until the cold drove her inside to the kitchen where Jennifer had left soup on the stove and Riley was still sleeping in the armchair by the fire with one arm strapped tight against his chest.

Sage sat on the floor beside Riley's chair, ate her soup, and listened to Riley breathe.
