# Chapter 22: Something Wrong
**Book III: Autumn — Partnership**
**POV: Promise, then Sage**
**Mid-October 1813**

---

**PROMISE**

The wrongness starts in the dark-time.

Not pain-sharp like before-time hurts. Not outside-pain from hitting or rope-burning or bit-cutting. This is inside-pain. Deep-gut-pain that twists and cramps and makes his belly feel full of stones-grinding.

He shifts his weight in the darkness. Moves from standing-position to different-standing-position. The wrongness doesn't ease.

His body knows what to do with outside-pain. Flee or fight or freeze. Those are the options-known, the survival-patterns burned deep. But inside-pain has no enemy to flee from, no threat to fight. The wrongness is *him*. Inside-him. Part-of-him-gone-wrong.

He paws at the ground. Once. Twice. The movement does nothing-helpful but his body insists-on-trying.

The night-air is cold-sharp against his coat. Stars wheel overhead, same stars that watched his before-time suffering. But this suffering is different-strange. No human caused this. No handler's cruelty, no trainer's force. Just his own body betraying-itself.

He lies down.

This is wrong-behavior and he knows it. Horses don't lie down in the dark-vulnerable-time unless something is very-wrong or very-safe. He's learned to feel safe-enough here to sleep-lying sometimes. But this isn't sleep-lying. This is pain-lying. This is body-saying *down-now-must-rest*.

The ground is cold-hard beneath him. He rolls slightly, trying to find position-that-eases. The stones-grinding-feeling in his gut shifts but doesn't release.

After long-time he struggles back to standing. The effort costs-more than it should.

He thinks about making noise. The scared-sounds or pain-sounds that might bring humans-running. In before-time, making noise brought punishment-swift. Silence was survival. Hide-weakness or weakness-gets-exploited.

But these-humans are different. He knows this now. Knows they come when called, help when needed, don't punish honest-communication.

Still. The old-knowing runs deeper than new-learning. Hide-weakness. Don't-show-pain. Survive-by-silence.

He stands in his paddock as the stars fade toward morning-grey, head lower than usual, gut twisting-wrong, and doesn't make a sound.

---

Dawn-light comes slow-gradual. The wrongness has grown from stones-grinding to fire-churning. His belly feels huge-tight, full of something that shouldn't be there.

He hears the barn-doors open. Hears human-voices starting morning-routines. Knows small-human will come soon with sweet-fruit and good-touch and expectations-of-work.

He should go to the fence. Should wait in the usual-spot, show the pattern-established. But moving hurts-more now. Every step jars the fire-churning-wrongness.

He stays where he is. Middle of paddock. Head hanging.

Maybe if he's very-still the wrongness will pass. Maybe his body will fix-itself like it fixed the rope-burns and the bit-cuts and all the other hurts of before-time.

He paws again. Looks at his flank—an automatic-gesture his body makes without thinking-conscious. As if he could see the wrongness, find it, fight it.

There's nothing to see. Just his own side, ribs showing less than before (he's healthy-weight now, filled-out-proper), coat gleaming even in the grey morning light.

But inside. Inside is wrong-wrong-wrong.

He lies down again. Rolls partway onto his back, legs folding awkward. The position-change brings brief relief-slight, then the fire-churning returns worse than before.

"Promise?"

Small-human's voice. Coming closer. Concern already there in the sound-pattern, even before she can see him properly.

He should get up. Should pretend-normal. Should hide-weakness like survival demands.

But his body won't obey. The wrongness has stolen his strength, his will to pretend. He lies on his side in the cold morning grass, breathing too-fast, gut churning-fire, and waits for whatever comes next.

---

**SAGE**

Something was wrong with Promise.

The knowledge hit Sage like cold water the moment she reached his paddock and found him lying down. Not the peaceful lying-down of a relaxed horse enjoying morning sun—this was wrong. His legs were curled awkwardly, his neck extended, his breathing visible even from twenty feet away.

"Promise?" She was already climbing the fence, her heart hammering. "Promise, what's wrong?"

He lifted his head at her voice but didn't rise. His eye, when it met hers, was dull with pain. Nothing like the bright curiosity she'd grown to expect.

Oh God. Oh God, no.

She dropped to her knees beside him, hands going automatically to his neck. His coat was damp with sweat despite the cold morning. His pulse, when she found it under his jaw, was racing fast.

"Can you get up? Promise, can you stand?"

He made an effort—she could see his muscles bunch, his legs shift—but fell back with a grunt that sounded wrong, pained, nothing like any sound she'd heard him make before.

"REBECCA!" The scream tore from her throat. "REBECCA, COME QUICK!"

She didn't wait to see if Rebecca heard. Her hands were moving over Promise's body, searching for injury, for obvious cause. Nothing external. Nothing visible. But his belly, when she pressed gently—

He flinched hard, trying to move away from her touch.

His belly. He'd been sensitive there yesterday. She'd noticed, mentioned it, and they'd decided not to give him a day off because they didn't want to break his momentum.

*Oh God. Oh God, we should have listened.*

Running footsteps. Rebecca appeared at the fence, took one look at Promise on the ground, and her face went tight with something Sage had never seen there before. Fear.

"How long has he been down?"

"I don't know. I just found him like this. Rebecca, what's wrong with him?"

Rebecca was already in the paddock, her hands moving over Promise with the same searching Sage had done but faster, more certain. She pressed her ear to his flank, listened for a long moment, then straightened with her mouth set in a grim line.

"Colic. His gut's gone quiet—no sounds at all. We need to get him up."

Colic. The word hit Sage like a physical blow. She knew about colic. Every horseperson knew about colic. Horses died from colic. Healthy horses, beloved horses, horses who'd been fine the day before—they died because something twisted wrong in their guts and there was nothing anyone could do.

"Help me," Rebecca said, already moving to Promise's head. "We need to get him on his feet. If he keeps rolling he could twist his intestine worse."

Together they urged, pulled, coaxed. Promise struggled, his legs scrambling in the dewy grass. On the third attempt he heaved himself upright, swaying, his head hanging so low his muzzle nearly touched the ground.

"Good boy," Rebecca said, her voice steady despite the fear Sage could see in her eyes. "Good boy. Now we walk."

"Walk?"

"We walk him. For as long as it takes. Walking keeps the gut moving, keeps him from lying down and rolling." Rebecca was already clipping a lead rope to his halter. "Sage, go wake Jennifer. Tell her we need warm water, bran mash, and the oil. She'll know which one. Then find Riley—we'll need to take shifts."

Sage didn't want to leave. Every fiber of her being screamed to stay with Promise, to fix this somehow. But Rebecca's voice held command-steel and she obeyed, running for the house with her heart pounding terror.

---

The morning became a blur of walking.

Round and round the paddock. Sage on one side of Promise's head, Rebecca on the other, keeping him moving even when he tried to stop, tried to go down, tried to give in to whatever was churning fire through his gut.

"Keep him walking," Rebecca said for the dozenth time. "If he lies down and rolls, he could twist his intestine. That's death. Walking is life."

Jennifer came with the oil—a long-necked bottle filled with thick linseed oil that they had to coax down Promise's throat while he fought and struggled. Half of it ended up on Sage's shirt. The rest, she prayed, went where it needed to go.

"It'll help lubricate his gut," Jennifer explained, already heading back to prepare the bran mash. "Help things move through if they're blocked."

Riley appeared to take over walking duty, their face set in the same grim lines everyone wore. "How long has he been like this?"

"Don't know. I found him down at dawn." Sage's voice cracked. "He was off yesterday. Sensitive in his belly. We noticed. We *noticed*, and we didn't—"

"Not now," Rebecca cut in, not unkindly. "Blame later. Work now."

So they worked. Walking, always walking. Promise stumbled and swayed and tried constantly to lie down, and they kept him moving. Jennifer brought warm bran mash that he refused to touch. They tried to get more oil into him, with limited success. They massaged his belly in slow circles, trying to stimulate whatever had stopped moving inside him.

The sun climbed. The morning turned to afternoon. Promise sweated and trembled and rolled his eye back with pain every time his gut cramped.

"Is he going to die?" Sage finally asked during one of her brief rest periods, watching Riley walk Promise in endless circles.

Rebecca was silent for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was tired and honest. "I don't know. Some horses come through colic fine. Some don't. It depends what's wrong inside, and we can't see that."

"If we'd given him yesterday off—"

"Maybe. Or maybe this was coming regardless and we just didn't see the signs." Rebecca turned to face Sage fully. "We can ask those questions later. Right now, we need to focus on keeping him alive."

"I should have listened. You told me I was pushing too fast. Riley asked if we were doing too many sessions. And I just—I was so excited about how well he was doing that I didn't—"

"Sage." Rebecca's hands gripped her shoulders, firm and steadying. "Yes. You should have listened. We all should have been more careful. But guilt won't save him. Only work will. Feel what you feel later. Right now, walk."

So Sage walked. Through the afternoon, into the evening, through the long cold night. They worked in shifts—two walking while two rested—but Sage couldn't sleep during her rest periods. Just sat at the edge of the paddock, watching Promise's miserable circuits, praying to whatever might be listening.

Sometime after midnight, Jennifer pressed a cup of hot broth into her hands. "You need to eat something."

"I'm not hungry."

"I know. Drink it anyway."

The broth was warm and salty and Sage barely tasted it, but she drank because Jennifer was right and because her body needed fuel for the walking still to come.

"He's strong," Jennifer said, sitting beside her. "He survived Thornton. He survived the knacker's yard. He's a fighter."

"He was just starting to trust us." Sage's voice broke on the words. "He was finally starting to believe that partnership was possible, that we wouldn't hurt him. And now his body is hurting him anyway, and there's nothing I can do to fix it."

Jennifer's arm came around her shoulders. Sage leaned into the comfort, too exhausted for pride.

"You're doing everything that can be done," Jennifer said. "Walking him. Keeping him moving. Giving him the best chance. That's all any of us can do."

"It's not enough."

"No. Sometimes it isn't." Jennifer's voice was gentle but unflinching. "Sometimes we do everything right and it still isn't enough. That's the truth of loving horses. Of loving anyone."

Dawn came grey and cold. Sage was walking Promise again—had lost track of how many miles they'd covered in their endless paddock circles—when she felt something shift.

Promise stopped. Not the fought-to-stop of trying to go down, but a different kind of stopping. His head came up slightly. His ears, which had been flat with pain for hours, flickered forward.

And then, with a noise that was equal parts ridiculous and beautiful, he passed gas.

Sage laughed—a desperate, exhausted, tearful sound. "Rebecca! Rebecca, he's—"

Rebecca was already there, pressing her ear to his flank. She listened for what felt like forever. Then her face broke into something that might have been a smile.

"I hear gut sounds. Faint, but there. His intestines are moving again."

An hour later, Promise passed manure for the first time since the colic started. It wasn't much—a few small, hard balls—but it was movement. It was his body working again.

Two hours after that, he showed interest in the bran mash Jennifer had kept warm through the night.

"Small amounts," Rebecca cautioned as Sage held the bucket. "Just a handful at a time. We don't want to overwhelm his system."

Promise ate. Slowly, carefully, but he ate. And when he'd had enough, he raised his head and looked at Sage with something approaching his normal expression. Still tired. Still pained. But present again. *There* again.

Sage buried her face in his neck and cried.

**End of Chapter 22**
