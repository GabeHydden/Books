### Chapter 4: The Sitting Begins

The morning after Promise's charge, Sage woke with the memory of his scream still echoing in her ears. She'd dreamed of it—that sound of pure terror translated into violence, the wood shuddering under his weight, his eyes rolling white. But underneath the dream-fear was something else. Understanding, perhaps. Or recognition.

She had made sounds like that, she realized. Not out loud—her mother would never have tolerated such a scene—but internally. Silent screaming that no one heard or chose to hear.

Riley found her after breakfast, standing at the kitchen window with a cup of tea gone cold in her hands, staring toward the quarantine paddock.

"Thinking about yesterday?" Riley asked, pulling on work gloves.

"I frightened him."

"You were there. That's all it took." Riley moved toward the door, paused. "If you're wanting to try again, do it differently. Don't approach. Just... exist nearby. Let him get used to the fact of you."

"How long does that take?"

"Depends on the horse. Could be days. Could be weeks." Riley's expression was matter-of-fact. "Most people don't have the patience for it. They want results. Want to see progress they can measure. But trauma doesn't heal on a schedule."

After Riley left, Sage stood at the window a moment longer. Then she went upstairs, selected a book from the small collection Jennifer had placed in her room—_The Odyssey_, appropriately enough—and walked out to Promise's paddock.

The morning was raw, the kind of early spring cold that promised warmth by afternoon but held frost in its bones at dawn. Yesterday's rain had left the ground soft, and her boots made sucking sounds in the mud as she approached. She could see Promise immediately, standing at the far corner exactly where he'd been after their last encounter. His head came up the moment she appeared, his body going rigid.

Sage stopped a good twenty feet from the fence. Far enough that she'd barely been able to reach yesterday before he'd charged. Far enough that the space between them felt vast and unbridgeable.

She sat down in the wet grass.

The action felt absurd even as she did it. Sitting in mud, in the morning cold, staring at a horse who wanted nothing to do with her. But Riley's words echoed: _Let him get used to the fact of you._

Promise watched her from his corner, every muscle taut. She could see it even at this distance, the readiness to explode into motion. His ears were flat back, his head high, his weight shifted onto his haunches. All the signs Riley had taught her. He was a wire pulled to breaking.

She opened her book. Began to read.

The words washed over her without quite registering—something about wine-dark seas and cunning Odysseus, about journeys and homecoming. But the act of reading gave her something to do with her hands, her eyes, her attention. Made her less of a direct threat. Just a figure sitting in the grass, turning pages, existing.

Nothing happened.

That was the point, she realized. Nothing happened was the most important thing that could happen.

After perhaps twenty minutes, her back aching from the damp ground, she stood slowly. Promise's head shot up higher, his body coiling. She didn't move toward him, just turned and walked back to the house, her skirts heavy with mud.

The kitchen door was propped open despite the cold, airing the room after the morning's baking. Jennifer was at the long table, rolling out pastry for a pie, flour dusting her forearms. She looked up when Sage came in, took in the muddy skirts and damp shawl, and nodded toward the stove.

"Kettle's hot. You look like you could use warming."

Sage poured tea and stood at the window for a moment, watching the distant shape of Promise in his paddock. He was still in his corner, still watching the spot where she'd been sitting.

Then, instead of retreating to her room as she would have a week ago, she found herself drawn to the table. She set down her cup and took a seat across from Jennifer. The kitchen smelled of butter and flour and the low warmth of the stove—comfortable smells, safe smells.

"Could I help?" she asked. "With the pastry?"

Jennifer looked up, surprised but pleased. "You could cut rounds, if you'd like. There's a cutter in the drawer there."

Sage found the cutter and joined Jennifer at the table. The work was simple—press, twist, lift, place on the baking sheet. The dough was cool and smooth beneath her fingers. Beside her, Jennifer worked in companionable silence, their elbows occasionally brushing as they reached for the same section of dough.

Sage noticed that she didn't flinch at the contact. Noticed, and said nothing.

"I sat with Promise," she said instead. "In the grass. For twenty minutes."

"I saw you from the window." Jennifer's hands didn't pause in their work. "That took courage."

"It didn't feel like courage. It felt like doing nothing."

"Doing nothing is harder than it sounds. Most people can't bear it—the waiting, the uncertainty. They want to fix things, make things happen." Jennifer glanced up. "Sitting still and letting someone else decide whether to trust you? That's not nothing. That's everything."

Sage pressed another round from the dough, considering. She thought about the space she was occupying. A week ago, she would have taken her tea to her room. Would have avoided being in the same room as anyone for longer than necessary, the presence of other people feeling like pressure against already-bruised skin.

Now here she was, working alongside Jennifer, their bodies near enough to touch, and she felt... not comfortable, exactly. But not unsafe.

"Can I ask you something?"

"Of course."

"When you came here, after your husband died—how did you know you were getting better? How did you measure it?"

Jennifer considered the question, her hands still working the dough. "Small things," she said finally. "The first time I laughed without feeling guilty. The first morning I woke and didn't immediately remember he was gone. The first time I let Rebecca touch my hand without wanting to weep." She looked up. "Why do you ask?"

Sage placed another round on the baking sheet. "I'm trying to notice. The small things. So I'll know."

Jennifer nodded slowly. "That's wise. The noticing itself is part of it, I think. Paying attention to what's changing, instead of only what hasn't."

They worked in silence for a while longer, the simple rhythm of cutting and placing strangely soothing. When the tray was full, Jennifer slid it into the oven and wiped her hands on her apron.

"You're doing well, you know," she said. "Better than you think."

Sage wrapped her hands around her now-lukewarm tea. "I don't feel like I'm doing anything. I just sit in the mud."

"You get up each morning. You eat breakfast with us now—that's new. You're learning the horses, learning the work. You're sitting with a frightened creature and offering him patience." Jennifer's eyes were kind. "That's not nothing, Sage. That's living. That's starting to build something."

Later, walking out to her room to change out of her muddy dress, Sage catalogued: She had stayed in the kitchen. Had worked beside Jennifer without tensing. Had let their elbows brush and felt nothing but the simple fact of contact.

Promise was still in his corner, watching the fence where she had been. They were both of them keeping track, she realized. Both measuring the distance left to cross.

***

"You're going to catch your death sitting in wet grass," Jennifer noted that evening, when Sage appeared for supper with her second gown of the day, having changed out of the mud-soaked one.

"Perhaps." Sage took her seat at the table—she'd managed to come down for meals now, though conversation still felt like moving through water. "But it seemed important."

Rebecca looked up from serving the stew. She ladled Sage's portion with the same precise economy she brought to everything — no drips, no excess, the bowl set down centered on its plate. "You sat with Promise today."

It wasn't a question. Rebecca rarely asked questions when she already had the answer. She stated what she'd observed and waited to see what you'd add.

"Twenty minutes. He watched me the entire time. Didn't move."

"That's good. That's very good, actually." Rebecca passed her a bowl. "He's watching instead of reacting. That's the first step."

"The first step to what?"

"To deciding you're boring." Riley spoke around a mouthful of bread. "Right now you're a threat. Eventually, if you just sit there enough, you become furniture. Part of the landscape. Not interesting enough to waste energy on."

She turned that over in her mind. Becoming boring. Being so consistently unthreatening that even terror learned to ignore her.

"How long did it take?" she asked. "With the other horses from Thornton?"

Rebecca glanced at Riley, then answered — giving the full account, the way she always did, each detail laid out in sequence without editing for what her audience might want to hear. "The fastest was three weeks before they'd tolerate proximity. The longest..." She paused. "The longest never did. There was a mare, seven years ago. She'd take food from us, eventually, but she never stopped flinching. Never stopped waiting for the blow. She lived here for four years, grazing and existing, but she never trusted. Not really."

"What happened to her?"

"Colic. We lost her two winters ago." Rebecca's voice was steady but her eyes showed the grief of it. "She was safe here. That had to be enough."

Sage touched her throat, that habit she couldn't break. "Safe isn't the same as healed."

"No," Rebecca agreed quietly. "But it's not nothing."

***

The next morning Sage returned to the paddock with her book and a shawl against the chill. Promise was in the same corner, watching her approach with the same rigid tension. She sat in the same spot—the grass still damp from yesterday, her skirts immediately soaking through—and opened to where she'd left off.

She tried to read, but her mind wouldn't settle into the words. Instead it kept returning to yesterday—Jennifer's hand brushing her shoulder in the kitchen, the way her body had jerked before her mind could intervene. The shame of it. But also Jennifer's calm, the way she hadn't made it into something Sage needed to apologize for.

Promise was watching her from the far fence. They were both of them watchful creatures now, she realized. Both waiting to see what would happen next. Both expecting, despite evidence, that the next thing might hurt.

She hadn't been a watchful creature before. Before Bath, before Mr. Pemberton, she had moved through the world assuming it was mostly safe. That people meant roughly what they said. That the rules of society—the ones about courtesy and propriety and what gentlemen did and didn't do—would protect her.

What a child she had been.

*He had seemed so civilized.* The thought surfaced unbidden. *At dinner, discussing poetry. Asking her opinion on the music. Her mother had been so pleased that a man of his standing was paying attention.*

Sage had thought his attention was about her mind. Her conversation. Had been flattered, even, that someone so established would find her interesting.

And then the library. The door closing. The way his face had changed when they were alone—not into something monstrous, but into something *honest*. As though the dinner-table courtesy had been the mask, and this was the real face beneath. Entitled. Certain. Amused by her confusion.

*You didn't misunderstand anything,* she thought, the old anger flaring. *You understood exactly what he was doing. You said no. You pushed at his chest. You tried to slip past him to the door.*

And he had put his hands on her throat and squeezed until the world went grey at the edges, and she had learned that *no* was just a word. That her will, her voice, her body's resistance—none of it mattered when a man decided otherwise.

Promise stamped his foot at something—a bird, perhaps, or a shadow—and Sage startled, her hand flying to her throat before she could stop it.

*We're the same,* she thought, watching him settle back into his vigilance. *Both learning that the rules we trusted were lies. Both waiting for the next betrayal.*

But Promise was here, in this paddock, being offered a different kind of truth. And so was she.

She looked down at her book, forced herself to find her place. Odysseus was clever, she thought. Clever enough to know when to fight and when to hide. When to speak and when to remain silent.

She wondered if he'd learned that the hard way too.

After thirty minutes she stood, walked away. Promise's eyes followed her the entire way back to the house.

The third morning brought the first real hint of spring warmth. The sun broke through the clouds by mid-morning, and Sage shed her shawl after the first fifteen minutes of sitting. Promise was still in his corner, still watching, but something had shifted minutely. His ears, while not forward, were no longer pinned flat. They swiveled occasionally toward other sounds—a bird calling, one of the other horses whinnying from a distant paddock—before returning to her.

She was becoming less than the entirety of his focus. The thought made her absurdly pleased.

On the fourth day, it rained. Sage considered staying inside, but something made her pull on her oldest dress and Rebecca's spare mackintosh and trudge out to her spot anyway. The ground had become truly sodden now, a muddy morass that squelched under her weight. She sat anyway, hunched under the coat, holding the book inside to keep it dry, not really reading.

Promise stood in the rain, his coat darkening with water, watching her sit in the weather with what might have been confusion. Or might have been nothing. She was learning how much she wanted to assign meaning to his expressions, and how little she actually knew about what went on behind those dark eyes.

But he was grazing now, she noticed. Not far from his corner, still with his body angled toward her, ready to flee. But grazing. Eating while she sat there. That felt significant.

The rain fell steadily, soaking through the mackintosh at the seams. Sage pulled her knees up under the coat and thought about her mother.

*You must have misunderstood his intentions.*

She had replayed those words a hundred times since Bath. Had examined them from every angle, looking for some interpretation that didn't mean what she knew it meant.

Her mother had looked at the bruises on her throat—livid, unmistakable, the size and shape of a man's hands—and had chosen to disbelieve what she saw.

*Surely you did something to encourage—*

What would have been sufficient evidence? Sage wondered. If Mr. Pemberton had killed her, would her mother have found some way to blame her corpse? *She shouldn't have been alone with him. She should have screamed louder. She must have given him reason to believe—*

There was no evidence that would have been sufficient. Sage understood that now. Her mother had needed to disbelieve her because the alternative was unthinkable: that a man of good family, good reputation, good standing, had attacked her daughter in their own home, and that the rules of society offered no protection. No recourse. No justice.

Easier to blame the girl than to see the world clearly.

Promise raised his head, ears swiveling toward something in the distance. Sage watched him assess the threat—a farmhand crossing a far field, perhaps, or the movement of another horse—and decide it was nothing. His head lowered. He returned to grazing.

She thought about what Rebecca had said: that Thornton had beaten Promise, and when Promise fought back, they called *him* vicious. Labeled the victim as the problem, rather than the violence that created him.

*We're both of us inconvenient,* Sage thought. *Both refusing to be what they wanted us to be. Both punished for our resistance.*

But Promise was here. Alive. Being offered a different way to live.

And so was she. That was something. That was, perhaps, everything.

By the fifth morning, Sage had established a rhythm. Wake at dawn, dress in her oldest gown—she'd given up on preserving her good clothes—take tea in the kitchen while Riley did morning feed, then walk out to the paddock with her book. Sit for half an hour. Watch Promise watch her. Then return to help with whatever work Riley had planned for the day.

She was learning about the other horses now, spending her afternoons with Riley in the barn or the paddocks. Clover let her stroke her neck now, the mare's head lowering in contentment when Sage found the right spot behind her ears. Thunder had learned she sometimes carried carrot ends in her pockets and would lip at her hands with hopeful insistence. Whisper remained timid but had started approaching when Sage sat quietly, curious in her careful way.

But Promise was different. Promise was the one she thought about during the night, when sleep wouldn't come and she lay staring at the ceiling, touching the fading bruises on her throat. Promise was the one who mirrored back something she recognized but couldn't quite name.

"You're thinking about him," Riley observed one afternoon, while teaching her how to read hoof condition. They were in the barn with Clover, the mare standing patiently while Riley demonstrated how to pick out a hoof.

"Is it that obvious?"

"You get a particular look. Like you're somewhere else." Riley set down Clover's foot gently. "Want to tell me what you're seeing?"

Sage thought about how to explain it. "When I sit out there, I keep thinking about what Rebecca said. About terror and viciousness looking the same from the outside. About how Promise is just trying to survive."

"Aye. That's what he's doing."

"The thing is..." Sage stopped, tried again. "I understand that. What it's like to have people mistake your fear for something else. To be told you're being dramatic, or difficult, or—" She cut herself off.

Riley set down the hoof pick, gave Sage his full attention. "Or that you brought it on yourself?"

The accuracy of it was like a blow. Sage's hand went to her throat.

"I knew a bit about what happened to you in Bath," Riley said quietly. "Rebecca told me some. Not details, just enough to understand why you're here. I hope you don't mind that."

"No." Sage's voice came out barely above a whisper. "It's... no, I don't mind."

"What happened to you wasn't your fault. Just like what happened to Promise wasn't his fault. He was young and spirited and that bastard Thornton decided to crush that out of him." Riley's jaw tightened. "Sometimes I think the worst thing about trauma is how it makes you believe you deserved it. Makes you carry shame that belongs to someone else."

Sage found she couldn't speak. The words lodged in her throat alongside all the other things she couldn't say.

"You're doing good work with him," Riley continued, turning back to Clover. "The sitting. The patience. He's noticing. It might not look like much, but he's learning that you just... exist. That you don't demand anything. Don't hurt anything. That's new information for him. Important information."

"Do you think..." Sage had to force the words out. "Do you think he'll ever not be frightened?"

Riley was quiet for a long moment, stroking Clover's neck. "I think he'll always remember. Trauma does that—leaves marks you can't see but can't forget either. But remembering and being controlled by it aren't the same thing. Maybe he learns that humans can be safe. Maybe he learns that the world has more in it than pain. That'd be something."

That'd be something. Sage turned the phrase over in her mind as she walked back out to Promise's paddock that evening, after the afternoon's work was done. The sun was lowering toward the horizon, turning everything gold and amber, and the air had finally warmed enough to feel genuinely spring-like.

Promise was grazing in the middle of the paddock now. Not in his corner. Still watchful, still ready to flee, but grazing in the open space with late afternoon sun warming his dark coat.

Sage sat in her usual spot—the grass finally beginning to dry, primroses starting to push up through the mud around her. She opened her book but didn't read. Just sat there, watching the horse graze and feeling the warmth on her own face.

After ten minutes, Promise raised his head. Looked at her. His ears swiveled forward—just slightly, just for a moment—before returning to their wary back position.

But it was forward. Interest. Attention without immediate threat assessment.

Sage felt something crack open in her chest. Hope, maybe. Or just the recognition that change, when it came, came in increments so small you could miss them if you weren't paying attention.

She stayed for forty-five minutes that evening. Longer than usual. The sun sank lower, painting the Cotswold stone of the house honey-gold, and the evening birds began their calls. Promise grazed in widening circles, never coming closer but no longer pressed against the far fence. Just existing in the space, learning that her presence didn't always mean pain.

When she finally stood to leave, he raised his head and watched her go. But he didn't startle. Didn't tense into readiness. Just watched.

Sage walked back across the muddy field, her boots ruined beyond saving now, her oldest dress more mud than fabric. She probably looked like a madwoman, spending hours sitting in wet grass to stare at a traumatized horse.

But Promise's ears had swiveled forward. Just for a moment.

That was something.

That was enough.

***

By the end of the week, the pattern was established. Every morning, every evening when weather and work allowed. Sage with her book and her patience, Promise with his vigilance and his slowly unfurling curiosity. She'd made it through most of _The Odyssey_ now, though she couldn't have summarized it if asked. The words were just something to do with her eyes while she taught a terrified horse that presence didn't equal threat.

Riley had been right about one thing: most people wouldn't have had the patience for it. Sitting in the cold and mud, watching an animal who wouldn't approach, who showed barely perceptible changes day by day. It would have seemed pointless.

But Sage recognized pointless. She knew the value of small things that looked like nothing from the outside. Knew that healing—if healing was even possible—came in increments too tiny to measure by any standard scale.

She recognized the gift because someone was doing the same for her. Rebecca with her matter-of-fact kindness. Jennifer with her gentle presence that never pressed too close. Riley with his patient teaching. None of them demanded more than she could give. None of them asked her to explain or justify or hurry her recovery.

They just existed around her, consistently, until she began to believe that maybe safety was possible. That maybe the world held more than what Mr. Pemberton had taught her.

It was exactly what she was offering Promise. Exactly what he needed.

And perhaps, in offering it to him, she was learning to offer it to herself as well.

On the seventh evening, as the sun set and the first stars appeared, Sage sat in her usual spot and felt the shift in the air that meant rain was coming. Promise felt it too—his nostrils flared, scenting the change, and he moved across the paddock with purpose, heading for the shelter of the barn overhang at the paddock's edge.

His path took him past where Sage sat. Not close—still ten feet away at least—but closer than he'd been since that first terrible charge.

He paused as he passed. Stood there for perhaps three heartbeats, looking at her.

She didn't move. Didn't speak. Just looked back at him, this broken creature who was learning, maybe, that the world might offer something other than pain.

Then he moved on, ducking under the overhang as the first drops began to fall.

Sage sat in the rain for a few minutes longer, letting it soak into her hair, her dress, her skin. The cold shock of it was almost pleasant. Real. Immediate. A reminder that she was still here, still present, still capable of feeling something other than numbness or fear.

When she finally stood and walked back to the house, she was smiling. Just slightly. Just enough.

Rebecca was at the window when Sage came in — standing in what Sage was beginning to recognize as her watching position, hands flat on the sill, body angled toward the paddocks rather than the room. Something in her aunt's expression suggested she'd been there a while.

"He passed close to you," Rebecca said.

"Yes."

"That's remarkable progress. Seven days."

Sage peeled off Rebecca's sodden mackintosh, hung it by the door. "It doesn't feel remarkable. It feels like nothing. Like I'm just sitting in the mud."

"That's because you're doing it," Rebecca said. "But I promise you, from where he's standing, it's everything."
