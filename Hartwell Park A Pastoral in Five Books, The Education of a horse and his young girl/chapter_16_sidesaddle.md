# Chapter 16: A Lady's Seat

"Today," Rebecca announced at breakfast three days after Sage's successful Thunder ride, "you begin sidesaddle."

Sage's teacup paused halfway to her lips. She'd known this was coming—had been mentioned since early summer as one of the skills she'd need—but knowing and facing were different things.

"Thunder?" she asked.

"Thunder. He's the best for it—steady enough to manage your initial imbalance, forward enough to keep going when you're struggling with your aids. Clover would let you sit there like a sack of grain. Thunder will make you work." Rebecca spread jam on her toast with the same calm she might use to sentence someone to hard labor. "I expect it will be uncomfortable, frustrating, and unlike anything you've done so far. But you'll manage."

Jennifer looked up from her medical journal, sympathy evident. "The first few sessions are always the worst. Your body has to relearn everything it knows about balance."

"How long until I'm competent?"

"Weeks, probably. Maybe months to be truly comfortable." Rebecca met her eyes. "But you need this skill, Sage. Not because society demands it—though it does—but because if you're to represent Hartwell Park, to work with other estates, to show these methods can produce results that compete with traditional training, you'll need to ride in company. And that means sidesaddle."

Sage nodded, though the thought of unlearning everything her body had fought to master made her want to protest. But Rebecca was right. The freedom to ride astride on the estate came with the responsibility of competence in society's preferred method.

***

The sidesaddle sat on its rack in the tack room like an instrument of torture. Sage had seen it dozens of times, had understood theoretically what it was for, but examining it now with the knowledge that she'd be sitting in it within the hour made her stomach clench.

Two pommels curved up from the left side—the lower one for her left leg to hook around, the upper one to brace her right thigh against. All her weight on one hip. No way to grip with her legs the way she'd learned. No independent seat where she could adjust her balance naturally. Everything would be sideways, twisted, asymmetrical.

"It looks medieval," she said.

Riley, who was already pulling Thunder's regular saddle off its rack, grinned. "It feels medieval too, at first. But women have been riding this way for centuries. You'll learn. Your body's already strong—you know how to follow a horse's motion, you understand balance and rhythm. This just... changes all the variables."

"That's not reassuring."

"It's not meant to be. Come on—let's get Thunder tacked up. The sooner you start, the sooner you'll stop hating it."

***

Thunder stood patiently in the barn aisle while Riley fitted the sidesaddle to his back, adjusting the girth with meticulous care. The saddle sat further back than a regular saddle, distributed weight differently. While Riley worked the girth, Sage went to Thunder's head and ran her fingers along the bit — both sides, every edge. Habit now, not thought. Thunder's ear flicked back, noting both the different saddle and the familiar touch of her fingers at his mouth, but he showed no concern. Just another day, another variation of the work.

Sage wished she could borrow some of his calm.

"The trick," Riley said, running a hand down the saddle to check its position, "is remembering that you still need to ride with your seat and weight, not just your hands. Most women learning sidesaddle grab the reins and try to steer that way because they can't use their legs properly. But that makes for terrible riding and a horse who won't respect you."

"How do I use my seat when I'm sitting sideways?"

"Very carefully." Riley stepped back, satisfied with the tack. "Come on. Let's get you on him before you think about it too much."

***

The mounting block had never seemed so high. Sage approached it with Thunder following patiently, and Riley positioned him perfectly alongside.

"Left foot in the stirrup, same as always," Riley instructed. "But when you swing up, you're bringing your right leg over the pommels, not over his back. Hook your left leg around the lower pommel, brace your right thigh against the upper one, and for God's sake don't fall off the other side."

Sage put her left foot in the stirrup, gripped the saddle with both hands, and tried to remember how to breathe. Thunder stood like stone, waiting. She pushed up, swung her right leg over, and nearly did exactly what Riley had warned against—her balance went wrong immediately, weight too far right, and she had to grab desperately at the pommels to keep from sliding off the right side of the saddle.

Riley caught her hip, steadying her. "There you go. Now arrange yourself. Left leg around the lower pommel, right thigh braced against the upper one."

Sage fumbled into position, feeling like every line of her body was wrong. Her left leg hooked around the pommel at mid-thigh, leaving her lower leg dangling. Her right leg pressed against the upper pommel, thigh muscle straining to maintain the pressure. Both legs on the left side of the horse. All her weight on her left seat bone. Her torso had to twist slightly right to stay square with Thunder's shoulders.

Everything hurt already and they hadn't even walked.

"How do I look?" she asked, knowing the answer.

"Like a question mark." Riley circled them, assessing. "But that's normal. Everyone looks wrong at first. Now gather your reins—they're longer than you're used to, to account for your position—and ask him to walk."

Sage gathered the reins, found them loose and foreign in her hands, and tried to figure out how to ask for walk when she couldn't use her legs. She squeezed with her right thigh against the pommel, tried to shift her weight forward, and said, "Walk on."

Thunder, bless him, walked.

And immediately Sage's entire world tilted wrong. His four-beat rhythm—one-two-three-four—that she'd learned to follow so naturally when sitting astride became a sideways rocking motion that made her tip forward, then back, then forward again. She grabbed at the pommels, trying not to panic, and Thunder slowed, confused by her sudden tension.

"Breathe," Riley called. "You're not going to fall off. Just breathe and follow his motion."

Sage forced herself to breathe, to relax her death grip on the pommels, to try to find the rhythm again. Around the ring they went—one circuit, two—and gradually, painfully, she began to feel it. Not comfortable. Not right. But less wrong than before. Her left leg learned to brace, taking some of the work off her right. Her seat found Thunder's back beneath her, even though it was only half a seat, only one bone instead of two.

"Better," Riley said after the third circuit. "You're starting to follow him instead of fighting him. Now try steering."

Steering. Right. Sage tried to remember everything she'd learned about using reins and weight and leg, then had to discard most of it because leg didn't work the same and weight was all on one side. She turned Thunder left using more right rein and shifting her weight slightly left—he obliged, though his ear flicked back in what seemed like polite confusion. Turning right was harder. She had to use more left rein and lean right, which felt exactly backward from what her body wanted to do.

By the time Riley called them to halt, Sage's right thigh was screaming, her left hip ached fiercely, and her back felt twisted like a wrung cloth.

"Good," Riley said, approaching to help her dismount. "That's enough for today."

"That was terrible."

"That was exactly what first sidesaddle always looks like. Tomorrow will be marginally less terrible. The day after, even better." Riley steadied Thunder while Sage tried to figure out how to dismount without falling on her face. "The important thing is you stayed on and you kept asking properly with your aids. That's more than most manage their first time."

Sage's right leg buckled when she finally got both feet on the ground. Riley caught her elbow.

"Your legs are going to feel strange for a few hours. Different muscles working. Come on—walk Thunder out while you cool down. Movement will help."

***

Leading Thunder back to the barn, Sage became aware of just how much her body hurt. Not the familiar ache of riding muscles worked hard, but something stranger—her right hip flexor, her left obliques, muscles she hadn't known existed until they started screaming. Thunder walked beside her peacefully, occasionally nosing at her shoulder as if to check she was all right.

"I know that was awful," she told him. "You were very patient with me. Thank you."

His ears swiveled toward her voice, accepting. Just another day's work for him, though probably a confusing one. Why did the small-human suddenly not know how to ride?

After untacking Thunder and grooming him with meticulous care—her way of apologizing for her graceless riding—Sage limped to the bench outside his stall and sat, resting her head against the wall. Her legs trembled, her back ached, and she had another session scheduled for tomorrow.

Riley appeared with two cups of water, handed her one, and sat beside her.

"Every woman who learns to ride sidesaddle goes through this," Riley said. "The frustration of being good at something and then suddenly being terrible at it again. But you have an advantage most don't."

"What's that?"

"You already know how to ride. You understand balance, rhythm, how a horse moves, how to communicate with your seat. Learning sidesaddle isn't learning from scratch—it's translating what you know into a different language. It'll come faster than you think."

Sage drank her water, considering this. "How long did it take you?"

"Three weeks to feel competent. Six weeks to feel comfortable. But I'd been riding astride since I was six. Had to learn sidesaddle when I was fourteen because my grandmother insisted." Riley's expression suggested what he'd thought of that insistence. "Hated every minute of it until suddenly I didn't. Until it just... clicked. And then I had two ways to ride instead of one."

"Do you ever ride sidesaddle now?"

"Only when I absolutely have to. When we're visiting other estates, when we need to show that Hartwell Park's methods work even within society's constraints. Otherwise?" Riley shrugged. "I prefer astride. More control, better balance, feels honest. But sidesaddle has its place. And you need to master it."

***

That afternoon, despite her aching muscles, Sage made her way to Promise's paddock. He came to the fence immediately, taking his apple with the gentle precision that still made her chest tighten with gratitude. While he crunched, she scratched behind his ears.

"I learned something new today," she told him. "Or rather, I learned how terrible I am at something new. Sidesaddle riding. It's this awful twisted thing where both your legs are on one side and you can barely use your aids and everything feels wrong."

Promise leaned into her hand, comfortable.

"But I need to learn it. Because if I'm going to help other horses like you—horses who've been hurt and need patience and time—I need to be able to show that our methods work. That Rebecca's way of training can produce horses who are just as skilled, just as capable, as horses trained with force. And that means riding in company, riding where people can see, riding in the way society expects."

She thought about this morning's frustration, the feeling of being incompetent again after weeks of building confidence. But she thought too about Riley's words: she already knew how to ride. This was just translation.

"The thing is," she continued, watching Promise graze near the fence, staying close, "learning this will make me better. Even though it's hard and awkward and makes me feel like I'm starting over. Because the more ways I know how to ride, the more horses I can help. The more situations I can handle. The more I can adapt to what each horse needs."

Promise lifted his head, looked at her with those dark intelligent eyes. His ears were forward, relaxed. He'd gained more weight in the past weeks, his coat gleaming with health, the hollows behind his ribs filling in. He was still working in the round pen with Rebecca—voice commands, direction changes, learning that human communication could be clear and kind. No halter yet. Still too soon. But progress, steady and patient.

"You're teaching me too, you know," Sage said quietly. "About patience. About doing things at the right pace, not the pace I want. About trusting the process even when it's slow and awkward and makes me feel incompetent. You're still learning to trust humans after they hurt you so badly. I can learn to ride sidesaddle. That's nothing compared to what you're doing."

The stallion blew out softly through his nose, then returned to grazing. Comfortable with her presence. Choosing to stay close. The simple trust of it—earned slowly over months of patient work—felt like a promise that difficult things could be learned if you were willing to feel incompetent along the way.

***

The next morning, Sage's body announced exactly how different sidesaddle was from regular riding. Her right hip flexor felt like someone had driven a spike through it. Her left obliques screamed when she tried to sit up. Even her shoulders ached from compensating for the twisted position.

Jennifer examined her at breakfast with professional interest.

"The asymmetry challenges everything," she said, fingers probing Sage's right hip gently. "You're using muscles that never had to work when you rode astride. It'll get better as they strengthen, but for now—" She crossed to the cabinet and returned with a jar of liniment. "This after riding, and hot baths when you can manage them."

"How many sessions until I don't want to die?" Sage asked.

"Honestly? About five. After that, the muscles adapt." Jennifer smiled sympathetically. "The first week is always the worst. But Riley says you stayed on and rode properly, which is more than most manage first try. Your foundation shows."

That foundation was the only thing that got Sage back on Thunder in sidesaddle that morning. Her body wanted to refuse, wanted to declare this impossible. But she'd fallen off Thunder at a canter and gotten back on. She'd bruised her ribs badly and gotten back on. She could manage sidesaddle for twenty minutes without falling off.

Thunder stood patiently at the mounting block again, and this time Sage knew what to expect. The wrongness of the position, the twist in her torso, the strange asymmetry. She mounted with less flailing, arranged herself with more efficiency, and when Riley told her to walk, she was ready for the sideways rocking motion.

Not comfortable. Not natural. But familiar from yesterday.

"Better already," Riley observed. "Your body remembers. Now let's work on your position. You're collapsing your right side—see how your right shoulder is lower than your left? That's compensating for feeling unstable. But it makes you less balanced, not more. Draw yourself up straight. Both shoulders level."

Sage tried to straighten, feeling every muscle protest. But when she did, when she found that upright position with both shoulders level despite her twisted seat, something shifted. The rocking motion became clearer. Thunder's rhythm became easier to follow.

"There," Riley said. "That's the position. Everything else builds from that. Now walk him around the ring and maintain it."

Around they went—one circuit, three, five. Each time around, Sage's body understood a little more. The brace of her right thigh against the upper pommel, taking weight but not clinging. The hook of her left leg around the lower pommel, providing anchor but not tension. Her seat following Thunder's back even though only half of it could touch. Her hands quiet despite the longer reins, communicating through seat and weight instead of pulling.

By the end of fifteen minutes, she was drenched in sweat and her muscles shook, but she'd turned Thunder left and right, halted him, asked him to walk again. All the basics. All from this twisted, sideways position.

"Excellent," Riley said when they finally stopped. "Tomorrow we'll try trotting."

Sage nearly fell off Thunder just from surprise.

"Trotting? Already?"

"You're ready. Your foundation is showing—you're not learning to ride sidesaddle from scratch, you're adapting what you already know. That accelerates everything." Riley grinned at her expression. "Besides, the sooner you can trot competently, the sooner you can canter. And the sooner you can canter, the sooner we can put you over some fences and really see what you're made of."

"In sidesaddle? Over fences?"

"How else do you think ladies follow the hunt?" Riley's grin widened. "This isn't just about looking proper, Sage. Sidesaddle done right is real riding. You'll see."
