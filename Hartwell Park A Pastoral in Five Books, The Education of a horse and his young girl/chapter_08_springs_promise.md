### Chapter 8: Spring's Promise

**Late April into Early May**

The apple came into Promise's life gradually, then all at once.

For three days after the first touch, Sage brought an apple to the fence and set it on the post. For three days, Promise watched it from the middle of his paddock, understanding it was meant for him but unable to quite believe the offering was without strings attached. Food from humans had always meant something. A bargaining chip. A trick. The thing withheld when he didn't perform correctly, used to force him into the wagon when the man-who-hits wanted him moved.

But on the fourth day, curiosity won.

Sage was reading her book, the apple sitting untouched on the fence post, and Promise found himself walking toward it. Not toward her—toward the apple. That was the distinction he made in his mind. He wasn't trusting her. He was investigating an object.

The apple was red, sun-warmed, and smelled sweet in a way that made his mouth water. He stretched his neck out, teeth closing around it carefully, and pulled it from the post.

The crunch was extraordinary. The sweetness flooded his mouth, juice running down his chin, and for a moment he forgot everything except the taste. When had he last had an apple? The before-before time, maybe. A lifetime ago.

Sage didn't move. Didn't reach for him. Didn't demand anything in exchange. Just sat there with her book, a small smile on her face, and let him eat.

The next day he came for the apple sooner. The day after that, she held it in her flat palm, and after a moment's hesitation, he took it from her hand. Her palm was warm and slightly damp, and he could feel her pulse through her skin—quick, excited—but she kept her hand still and let him take the apple on his terms.

Within a week, he was coming to the fence as soon as she arrived, looking for the apple. Sometimes she made him wait while she read a few pages. Sometimes she gave it to him immediately. The unpredictability was frustrating at first—he wanted the pattern, the certainty. But gradually he understood. She was teaching him that waiting didn't mean the good thing wouldn't come. Just that it would come in its own time.

Trust-learning. Patience-learning. Different-learning.

***

Sage's body knew she was cantering before her mind quite caught up with the fact.

Riley had asked Clover to canter—that three-beat gait that looked like flying from the ground—and suddenly Sage was moving faster than she'd ever moved on horseback, the mare's stride eating up the ground, the rhythm completely different from walk or trot. One-two-three, one-two-three, like a rocking horse, like a dance, like something between earth and air.

"Sit deep!" Riley called. "Follow her motion with your hips!"

Sage tried to relax into it, tried to let her body move with Clover's powerful stride. It felt enormous, this speed, this sense of controlled power beneath her. Her hands gripped the reins too tight and she forced them to soften, remembering Riley's lesson about bird-bones, about asking rather than demanding.

Clover was being patient with her, as always. The mare's canter was steady and rhythmic, not rushing, just carrying Sage around the paddock in smooth, rolling strides. After one circuit Sage found the rhythm, felt her hips begin to follow the motion naturally, and suddenly she understood.

This was flying. This was partnership. This was what it felt like to move with a horse instead of on one.

"There!" Riley's voice was triumphant. "You've got it! Keep that feeling!"

They cantered twice more around the paddock before Riley called for a walk, and when Sage brought Clover down to a gentle amble her whole body was shaking—not from fear but from exhilaration. Pure, uncomplicated joy.

"That's the canter," Riley said, grinning. "That's what you've been working toward."

Sage dismounted on trembling legs, threw her arms around Clover's neck, and laughed into the mare's mane. The horse tolerated this display of emotion with her usual dignity, just turning her head slightly to see what the strange human was doing.

"Thank you," Sage whispered to her. "Thank you for teaching me."

When she looked over at Promise's paddock, he was at the fence, watching. His ears were forward, his expression alert and interested. She wondered if he remembered cantering. Remembered what it felt like to move like that, before the man-who-hits had made movement mean pain.

Maybe someday he would canter again. Maybe someday it would mean joy instead of fear.

Maybe.

***

**First Week of May**

"I'd like to try grooming him," Sage said at breakfast one morning.

Rebecca looked up from her correspondence — letters arranged in a neat row by date, pen laid parallel to the paper's edge, ink pot precisely centered on its cloth. "What makes you think he's ready?"

"He takes the apples from my hand now. Every day. Sometimes he stays after eating, just standing there. Yesterday he let me touch his nose—just for a moment, but he didn't pull away." Sage set down her tea cup. "I think he's ready for more. Or ready to find out if he's ready."

Riley, buttering bread at the counter, spoke without turning around. "Start with the neck. Just the flat of your hand, no brush yet. If he tolerates that, you can try the shoulder. Watch his signals the whole time. If he asks you to stop, stop."

"I will."

"And Sage?" Rebecca's voice was serious. "If he's not ready, that's information too. Not failure. Just information."

Sage nodded, understanding. Healing wasn't linear. She'd learned that lesson in her bones. Some days Promise came to the fence immediately. Some days he stayed in the middle of his paddock, processing. Both were okay. Both were part of the work.

That morning she brought an apple as usual, but also brought a grooming cloth—soft, worn linen that wouldn't be too strange or scratchy. Promise took his apple, crunched it with evident satisfaction, and then stood there, watching her.

Sage held up the cloth, let him smell it. His nostrils flared, investigating. She moved very slowly, telegraphing every motion, and reached through the fence rails toward his neck.

He didn't move away.

She laid her hand—just her hand, no cloth yet—against his neck. His coat was warm from the morning sun, silky now that it had fully shed its winter dullness. She could feel his pulse beneath her palm, quick and strong. Could feel the slight tension in his muscles—not panic, just alertness. Processing.

"Good boy," she murmured. "Such a good, brave boy."

His ear flicked toward her voice, but he stayed still. Stayed present. Allowed the touch.

She stroked down his neck once, twice, three times. Gentle pressure, the kind she'd use on Clover or Whisper. The kind that said I'm here, I'm not hurting you, you're safe.

On the fourth stroke, Promise lowered his head slightly. Not pulling away. Not leaving. Simply softening. The tension in his neck eased a fraction, and his eye went half-closed.

He was tolerating this. Maybe more than tolerating. Maybe almost enjoying it.

Sage's eyes burned with tears but she blinked them back, not wanting to startle him with any sudden movement. She stroked his neck for perhaps two minutes, then stepped back, giving him space.

"That's enough for today," she said softly. "You did so well, Promise. So well."

He raised his head, looked at her with those dark, intelligent eyes, and then walked away. Not fleeing. Just done. Just ending the interaction on his terms.

Riley appeared from somewhere behind her—they'd been watching, Sage realized, staying back to give them space but there in case something went wrong.

"That was beautiful," Riley said quietly. "That was perfect. You read him exactly right—knew when to stop, gave him the choice to leave. That's how you build trust."

Sage wiped at her eyes, laughing a little at herself. "Why does everything with him make me cry?"

"Because you understand what it means. What it costs him to stay present. What it costs you to be that patient." Riley's hand on her shoulder was warm and steady. "You're both doing the hardest work there is. Relearning the world."

***

**Second Week of May**

The days grew longer and warmer. Apple blossoms gave way to tiny fruit that would ripen by August. The grass in the paddocks went from pale spring-green to deep emerald. Swallows returned to nest in the barn eaves, their swooping flight and cheerful calls marking the passage from spring to almost-summer.

Sage's routine was fully established now. Morning feed with Riley, then sitting with Promise (who now came for his apple immediately and tolerated grooming on his neck and the top of his shoulder), then riding lessons with Clover—cantering regularly now, working on circles and transitions and beginning to learn the aids for side-passing. Afternoons were for farm work: mucking, grooming the other horses, helping with whatever needed doing. Evenings were for sitting with Promise again, sometimes just being present, sometimes working on desensitization—showing him the grooming brush from a distance, letting him investigate the halter through the fence rails, teaching him that objects weren't threats.

She fell into bed exhausted each night and woke each morning eager to begin again.

"You're happy," Jennifer said one evening at supper. It wasn't a question.

Sage considered it. Was she happy? The word felt too simple for what she felt. Too neat. She wasn't the girl who'd arrived three weeks ago, numb and certain the world was only pain. But she wasn't entirely sure who she was now. Someone different. Someone still becoming.

"I'm purposeful," she said finally. "I'm working toward something. Learning something. That feels good."

"It looks good on you," Rebecca said. "You've come remarkably far, Sage. Both with Promise and with yourself."

"What happens next?" Sage asked. "I mean—we're almost to summer now. What does summer training look like?"

Riley caught Rebecca's eye before speaking. "For you? More riding. We'll start you on Thunder—he's got more spirit than Clover, will challenge you more. Eventually we'll introduce you to riding multiple horses, different temperaments. And we'll start teaching you sidesaddle properly."

Sage made a face. "I suppose I must."

"You must," Rebecca confirmed, but her eyes were kind. "If you're ever to ride in public, if you're ever to take Promise to a show or a meet—if that even becomes possible—you'll need to be competent sidesaddle. It's harder than astride. Takes different muscles, different balance. But you're strong enough now to begin learning it."

"And Promise?" Sage's voice was quieter. "What happens with him in summer?"

"More of what you're doing. Building trust, building tolerance. We'll start working him with the longe line eventually—teaching him that he can move around you in a circle without threat. That he can respond to voice commands. That work doesn't have to mean pain." Rebecca paused. "But Sage, you need to understand. We're talking months, possibly a year or more, before he'd be ready for any kind of riding. And even then, it might not be you who rides him first."

The honesty stung, but she couldn't argue. "Because I'm not experienced enough."

"Because he needs someone who can read the slightest signs of panic and respond appropriately. Someone who won't get hurt if he bolts or bucks or rears. Someone who can be calm and confident even when he's terrified." Rebecca laid this out with the same methodical completeness she brought to training plans — every consideration named, nothing softened, nothing held back. "You're learning those skills. You're learning them remarkably fast. But they take time."

"And he might never be rideable at all," Riley added. "Some horses who've been through what he has—they're never comfortable with a rider. They can live here, be handled, be groomed. But the weight on their back always means trauma. We won't know until we try, and we won't try until he's ready."

Sage nodded, absorbing this. She'd known, intellectually, that her fantasy of riding Promise—of showing Thornton and every man like him that gentle methods worked—might never happen. But hearing it stated so plainly made it real.

"I understand," she said. "I want what's best for him. Not what proves something for me."

"That," Rebecca said, "is exactly why you're the right person to be working with him."

***

**Third Week of May**

Promise's neck had become familiar territory. Sage could groom it thoroughly now, working the soft cloth over his poll, down behind his ears (he loved that spot, would lean into her hand when she found it), along the crest, down to where his neck met his shoulder. She'd introduced the soft brush, and he'd investigated it, seemed to find it acceptable. His coat gleamed under the attention, showing its blue-black highlights in the sun.

The shoulder was trickier. He was willing to let her touch it with her hand, but when she tried to move the brush there, his whole body went tense. His ears flicked back, not flat but uncertain. His weight shifted onto his hindquarters—ready to move if needed.

Sage stopped immediately. Stepped back. "Okay. Not today. That's okay."

She returned to his neck, to the safe territory, and felt him relax again. After a few minutes she tried once more, just her hand this time, no brush. Palm flat against his shoulder, moving in slow circles.

He tolerated it for perhaps ten seconds, then stepped away.

"Progress," Riley said from where they were working nearby. "Every time he lets you try, even if he says no, that's him making a choice. That's him communicating. Honor that and he'll trust you more."

Sage had been at Hartwell for a month now. A month that felt like both forever and no time at all. She could hardly remember the girl who'd arrived in Rebecca's carriage, bruised and certain her life was over. That girl felt like someone else. Someone she'd been but no longer was.

This person—the one who woke eager for the day's work, who could canter a horse and muck a stall and read equine body language like a second language, who could sit with her own fear and Promise's fear and not run from either—this person was new. Still forming. Still becoming.

"Rebecca," Sage asked one evening, "what if I stayed? Not just through summer. What if I... stayed?"

Rebecca looked up from her book — held close, read with the focused attention she gave everything, as though each page were a horse to be studied. They were in the study, Rebecca in the chair she always chose, the one angled toward the window with sightlines to the paddocks. Jennifer dozed in the chair by the fire, Riley out doing evening check on the horses. The lamplight made everything warm and golden.

"This was always meant to be your home for as long as you needed it," Rebecca said carefully. "But staying isn't just about having nowhere else to go. It's about choosing to be here. Choosing this work. This life. Are you choosing it?"

Sage thought about the question. Thought about Bath, about drawing rooms and calling cards and the endless performance of being a proper young lady. Thought about her mother's parlor and the way women spoke in careful circles around everything that mattered. Thought about the world that had given Mr. Pemberton permission to put his hands on her throat and then blamed her for his violence.

Then she thought about Promise taking his apple from her hand. About Clover's patient teaching. About Riley's quiet acceptance and Jennifer's warmth and Rebecca's wisdom. About the ache in her muscles from honest work. About the way she fell asleep each night without nightmares, without fear.

"I'm choosing it," she said. "I want to learn this. All of it. I want to become the kind of person who can help horses like Promise. Who can offer them something different than what the world has shown them."

Rebecca smiled. "Then you'll stay. We'll write to your mother, make it official. You'll continue your training—with the horses and in managing the property. Eventually, if this is truly your calling, we can discuss you taking on more responsibility. Perhaps even taking over some of the rehabilitation work."

"Really?"

"Really. You have a gift for this, Sage. Not everyone does. Some people learn the mechanics but never truly understand the psychology. You understand it." Rebecca paused. "Of course, staying means the neighbors will have opinions. A young woman living here indefinitely, unmarried, working with horses like a stable hand—they'll talk."

"Let them talk," Sage said, and was surprised by her own certainty. "I'm done living for other people's opinions."

Jennifer stirred in her chair, not quite awake but aware of the conversation. "Good girl," she murmured. Then, more fully awake: "You're happy."

Sage considered the word. Happy. It seemed too simple, too clean for what she felt. But underneath all the complexity—the grief that still surfaced sometimes, the anger at her mother, the memories that ambushed her in quiet moments—underneath all of that, there was something that might be called happiness.

"I think I am," she said slowly. "Or—not happy, exactly. Not the way I used to understand the word. But something close to it." She searched for better language. "I feel like I'm becoming someone. Someone I might actually want to be."

Jennifer reached across and squeezed her hand—brief, warm, the gesture that had become familiar over the past weeks. Sage squeezed back.

"When I came here," Sage said quietly, "I couldn't have done that. Couldn't have let anyone touch me without—" She stopped, not sure how to finish.

"I know." Jennifer's eyes were kind. "I watched you learn. Day by day, not even aware you were doing it. It was like watching a flower decide to open."

***

**Last Day of May**

The morning was warm and perfect, the kind of late-spring day that promised summer without quite delivering it yet. Sage brought Promise his apple and found him already at the fence, ears forward, clearly anticipating her arrival.

He took the apple, crunched it with his usual satisfaction, and then—before she could prepare the grooming cloth—he lowered his head and pushed his nose against her chest. Just pressed there, breathing her in, letting her scent wash over him.

It was deliberate. Intentional. Not investigating. Not assessing threat. Nothing more than closeness. Choosing contact.

Sage froze, hardly daring to breathe, and let him stay there as long as he wanted. His breath was warm against her collarbone. His whiskers tickled through the fabric of her dress. His dark forelock fell forward, almost brushing her chin.

"Hi," she whispered. "Hi, Promise."

His ears flicked toward her voice. He huffed softly, warm air against her skin, and then raised his head. Looked at her with those eyes that had seen too much, learned too much pain, and were somehow still capable of this. Of trust. Of choosing to be close.

"You're so brave," she told him. "So much braver than you know."

She groomed him then—neck, shoulder (which he tolerated today, a good day), the top of his withers. He stood quietly, processing the sensation, learning that touch could mean comfort instead of pain. That human hands could offer something other than violence.

When she was done, she stepped back, and he didn't immediately walk away. Just stood there, at the fence, existing in her presence. Comfortable. Safe.

A month ago he'd been pressed against the far fence, screaming at her to stay away. Today he was choosing to stay close even after the apple was gone, even after the grooming was done. Staying. Being present with her.

Riley appeared at her elbow. "Look at that," Riley said softly. "Look at what you've built."

"We've built," Sage corrected. "All of us. Rebecca, you, Jennifer. He trusts the place, not just me."

"He trusts you," Riley said firmly. "The place gave him safety, but you gave him choice. There's a difference."

That afternoon, Sage rode Clover out to the far pasture with Riley on Thunder. They cantered across the open ground, the horses moving in smooth parallel, the wind in Sage's face, the sun warm on her back. She felt competent. Confident. Strong.

When they pulled up by the old stone wall that marked the property's eastern boundary, Riley turned to her with a considering expression.

"Summer's coming," Riley said. "The real work begins. You ready?"

Sage looked back at Hartwell Park spread below them. The honey-colored stone house. The neat paddocks. The horses grazing peacefully. Promise in his enclosure, a dark shape against the green grass.

A month ago she'd been broken. Today she was... not healed, exactly. But healing. Learning what she was made of when she wasn't afraid. Learning that gentleness was its own kind of strength. Learning that patience and consistency could build something real.

"I'm ready," she said.

And she was.

***

That night, in her room, Sage stood at the window looking out over the paddocks one last time before bed. The moon was nearly full, silvering everything it touched. She could just make out Promise in his enclosure, a darker shadow against the night grass.

A month. Four weeks. Twenty-eight days.

It didn't seem possible that so much could change in such a short time. That a girl could arrive certain her life was over and leave that certainty behind like an ill-fitting dress. That a terrified horse could learn to take apples from a human hand. That broken things could begin to heal.

But here was the evidence: her own reflection in the window glass. Stronger. Clearer. More herself than she'd ever been. Her throat bore no marks. Her hands were calloused from work. Her body was tired in the good way, the way that meant she'd used it for something purposeful.

She touched her throat one more time, lightly. Remembered the bruises that had been there. Remembered Mr. Pemberton's hands, the way the world had gone grey at the edges, her mother's face turned away.

Those things had happened. They were real. They had changed her in ways she was still discovering.

But they were not the whole story. Not the last chapter.

She thought about Rebecca's words: *We work with them until they become who they were always meant to be.* Maybe that was true for horses and for people alike. Maybe the point wasn't to erase what had happened, but to grow large enough to contain it. To hold the wound alongside the healing, the memory alongside the hope.

She thought about Jennifer's hand brushing her shoulder that first week, the way she had flinched—and then stayed in her chair. About Riley's hands adjusting her riding position, and the way her body had known that touch was safe before her mind could catch up. About Promise's nose against her palm this morning, the warmth of his breath, the choice he had made to reach for her.

All those moments, accumulating. Building something.

And out in the paddock, a horse who'd been scheduled for slaughter was grazing peacefully, learning day by day that the world held more than pain.

Tomorrow would bring more of the same. More apples, more grooming, more careful building of trust. More riding lessons, more farm work, more integration into this strange, wonderful household that operated by rules she was still learning but already loved. More of Riley's patient teaching, Jennifer's warm presence, Rebecca's quiet wisdom.

More of Promise learning that humans could be safe. More of Sage learning that she could be trusted with precious, broken things—including herself.

Spring had brought them both here. Summer would build on that foundation. And beyond that—autumn, winter, another spring—stretched a future that was no longer terrifying. Just unknown. And unknown, Sage was learning, was not the same as dangerous.

She wasn't healed. That was too simple, too final a word for something that would never quite finish.

But she was healing. Actively, presently, ongoingly *healing*. Becoming someone who could be touched without fear. Someone who could touch without flinching. Someone whose body was, slowly and carefully, becoming hers again.

Promise raised his head in the moonlight, looked toward the house. Toward her window. She didn't know if he could see her there, silhouetted against the lamp light. Didn't know if he was looking for her or just surveying his territory, as horses do.

But she liked to think he knew she was there. Liked to think that his presence in the night and hers in the window were a kind of conversation. A promise being kept on both sides.

*I will show up tomorrow,* she thought. *And the day after. And the day after. For as long as you need me to.*

And maybe, in his own way, he was thinking the same thing.

Maybe they were both learning to keep promises to each other. And to themselves.

Spring was ending. Summer was coming. And they would face it together, two creatures learning to trust, learning to heal, learning that maybe—maybe—broken things could be mended after all.

Not fixed. Not returned to what they were. But mended. Made into something new. Something that bore its scars and its history and its hard-won wisdom, and chose to keep going anyway.

Chose to believe that tomorrow could be different than yesterday.

Chose hope.

Sage smiled at her reflection, at the stranger she was becoming, and closed the curtains.

Tomorrow would come soon enough.

And she was ready for it.
