# HARTWELL PARK: A Pastoral Novel
## Comprehensive Planning Document & Series Bible

### PROJECT OVERVIEW

**Title:** Hartwell Park: A Pastoral in Five Books
**Subtitle:** Or, The Education of a Horse and His Girl
**Genre:** Historical Literary Fiction / Pastoral / Animal Story / Coming-of-Age
**Setting:** Gloucestershire, England, 1813-1814
**Length Target:** 90,000-100,000 words (approximately 360-400 pages)
**POV:** Third person limited, alternating between Sage and Promise perspectives (70/30 split)
**Narrative Style:** Prose narrative with rich dialogue, internal monologue, and detailed physical description—NOT eclogue/dialogue form
**Tone:** Contemplative, naturalistic, visceral (includes physical fear, injury, setbacks)
**Central Theme:** The bond between traumatized beings who must both overcome fear—the horse learning to trust humans again, the girl learning to trust herself as a rider and partner

**Chapter 22: The Fall** (Promise POV, then Sage)
- **RILEY'S FALL — the backing attempt**
- Saturday morning: weight progression with Riley — leaning, belly-down across saddle
- Promise processes each step: heavier weight, different smell, but same pattern-known
- Riley swings leg over, settles astride — Promise's body-memory triggers

**Chapter 23: After** (Sage POV)
- Weeks of aftermath. Promise regressed — won't allow saddle, flinches from surcingle
- Sage caring for injured Riley — enforced intimacy, first extended close time
- Double guilt: hurt both Promise and Riley through enthusiasm
- Riley's insight: "He threw me because I wasn't you." Trust is specific, not transferable.
- Rebecca's analysis: "It was about the position. Astride. Legs on both sides."

- Day six: Promise pushes nose against Sage's shoulder — trust not destroyed
- Romantic beat: "I was more afraid when you fell than anything since Bath"

**Chapter 24: Weight** (Sage POV with Promise sections)
- Three weeks of rebuilding after the fall
- Surcingle reintroduction takes days (associated with what followed)
- Saddle re-introduced: methodical progression — showing, resting, full saddling
- Walking/trotting with saddle; then leaning across; then stirrup weight
- **THE MOMENT:** Sage swings leg over — Promise chooses not to flee
- But he doesn't relax: rigid, locked, "tolerating not accepting"
- Multiple sessions confirm: astride position is irreducible trigger
- Riley's question plants the seed: "Is it? Always?"
- Chapter ends on unsolved problem: how to ride without astride?

**Chapter 25: A Different Way** (Sage POV)
- Sage wakes with Riley's question — "Is it? Always?" — arrives at sidesaddle idea
- Finds Rebecca in tack room independently examining the sidesaddle
- Logic: astride = every rider who hurt him, including Riley's fall. Sidesaddle = no match in his trauma catalogue.
- **Solution: sidesaddle** — completely different weight/position/feel
- Sidesaddle works — Promise present, relaxed, moving *with* her. "He's here."
- Riley's generous response from fence: "He's not rejecting me. He's choosing the position that doesn't frighten him."
- Rebecca: never thought of sidesaddle as trauma solution in thirty years
- First snow of winter. Sage and Riley in kitchen doorway. "We've got work to do."

### SEASONAL DESCRIPTIONS

**Spring (March-May):**
- Lambing season for neighbors
- Mud season - fields soft, boots always dirty
- Wildflowers emerging: primrose, violets, bluebells
- Trees budding, then leafing
- Birds returning: swallows, larks
- Foals born (not at Hartwell, but visiting studs nearby)
- Days lengthening, more daylight for work
- Temperature: 45-60°F, rain frequent
- Work: turning horses out more, beginning training season

**Summer (June-August):**
- Long days, work from dawn to dusk
- Heat can be oppressive (70-80°F)
- Hay harvest in July (all hands needed)
- Thunderstorms frequent
- Roses blooming around house
- Swallows nesting in stable eaves
- Flies becoming problem for horses
- Stream runs low by August
- Work: intensive training, maintenance, preparing for autumn

**Autumn (September-November):**
- Harvest season
- County fairs and competitions begin
- Leaves turning: oaks last to turn
- Temperature dropping (50-65°F)
- First frosts in November
- Apples harvested
- Horses growing winter coats
- Days shortening rapidly
- Work: preparing horses for winter, final training push

**Winter (December-February):**

## HORSE TRAINING DETAILS

### Training Philosophy (Hartwell Method)

**Core Principles:**
1. **Consent-Based:** Horse must agree to each new step
2. **Patience First:** No timelines except horse's readiness
3. **Respect for "No":** Refusal is communication, not disobedience
4. **Force Never:** No whips, spurs, harsh bits, or pain-based methods
5. **Observation:** Reading horse's signals constantly
6. **Desensitization:** Gradual exposure to scary things
7. **Positive Reinforcement:** Treats, praise, rest as rewards
8. **Trust Building:** Every interaction builds or erodes trust

**vs. Thornton Method (Traditional):**
1. **Force-Based:** Horse must submit to human will
2. **Timeline Driven:** Must be ready by X date
3. **"No" Punished:** Refusal met with whip/spur
4. **Force Primary:** Pain teaches compliance
5. **Domination:** Breaking spirit to ensure obedience
6. **Flooding:** Force exposure to overcome fear
7. **Negative Reinforcement:** Relief from pain as reward
8. **Fear Building:** Horse obeys to avoid punishment

### Technical Training Elements

**Haltering:**
- 21 days average for traumatized horse
- Steps: object introduction, approach with halter, touch nose, place noseband, crown piece over ears, buckle
- Common fears: head shyness, restraint panic, past associations
- Success markers: relaxed acceptance, willingness to lower head

**Leading:**
- Teach following, not dragging
- Light pressure on rope, immediate release when correct
- Build on natural herd following behavior
- Success: walks beside handler with loose lead

**Groundwork:**
- Move hindquarters: shifts weight, enables turn on forehand
- Move forehand: shifts weight, enables turn on haunches
- Back up: strengthens core, teaches yielding
- Come forward: overcomes fear of approach
- Each has purpose for riding and horse's development

**Riding Preparation:**
- Desensitization to saddle: 14+ days
- Weight on back: 7-10 days of progressive leaning
- First mount: should be non-event if prep done right
- First ride: just sitting, no movement requested

**Jump Training:**
- Start ground poles (literally on ground)
- Raised poles (6 inches)
- Tiny crossrail (12 inches)
- Progressive height increase (2-4 inches at a time)
- Never force, must be willing
- Make it a puzzle/game
- Celebrate every success

**Competition Elements:**
- Course walking: memorizing path
- Combinations: multiple jumps close together (tests adjustability)
- Water jump: tests bravery, unique obstacle
- Technical scoring: faults for rails down, refusals, time
- Quality scoring: judge subjective assessment of performance

## STYLISTIC GUIDELINES

### Voice and Tone
- **Third person limited**, alternating between Sage and Promise POV
- **Prose narrative** with rich dialogue, internal thought, physical description
- **NOT eclogue/dialogue form** - full narrative prose
- **Contemplative and visceral** - both thought and physical sensation
- **Nature descriptions** rich and sensory
- **Philosophy emerges** from experience, not stated directly
- **Emotional honesty** including fear, pain, setbacks
- **Period-appropriate** language without being archaic

### POV Structure
**Sage Chapters (70%):**
- Third person limited, her perspective
- Access to her thoughts, fears, physical sensations
- Her observations of Promise and others
- Internal conflict and growth visible

**Promise Chapters (30%):**
- Third person limited, his perspective
- Non-verbal "thoughts" - sensory, emotional, instinctual
- How he perceives humans, understands situations
- His fear, confusion, gradual trust shown from inside
- Not anthropomorphized but emotionally honest
- Usually 1-2 per book, placed strategically

### Prose Style
- **Sentences:** Varied length, rhythm changes with emotion/action
- **Paragraphs:** 4-8 sentences typically, shorter for action/intensity
- **Dialogue:** Natural but period-appropriate, embedded in action
- **Description:** Sensory details, especially:
  - Physical sensation (pain, cold, exhaustion, fear)
  - Smell and sound (stable smells, horse breath, leather)
  - Touch (horse hair, reins, ground impact)
  - Visual details of horses, countryside, weather
- **Pacing:** 
  - Slower for training details and philosophy
  - Faster for riding scenes, falls, action
  - Contemplative for nature/seasonal passages
- **Metaphors:** From nature, horsemanship, seasons

### Writing the Riding Scenes
**Physical Detail Essential:**
- How it feels to sit on a horse moving under you
- The moment of losing balance
- Impact of falling, breath knocked out, pain
- Exhaustion from riding work
- Fear in the body - shaking hands, tight chest
- Connection when it works - moving as one

**Promise's Movement:**
- His power, his size, his responsiveness
- How his gaits feel different from other horses
- His sensitivity to rider's aids
- His carefulness after Sage's fall
- His enthusiasm when jumping

### Dialogue Rules
- **Embedded in narrative:** Not just exchanges, but showing:
  - How things are said
  - Physical context while speaking
  - Internal reaction to what's said
  - Actions during conversation

- **Characters speak distinctly:**
  - Rebecca: Precise, philosophical, can be stern, expects much
  - Riley: Direct, teasing, practical, good teacher
  - Sage: Questions, grows more confident, precise about feelings
  - Jennifer: Gentle, medical knowledge, observant
  - Thornton: Condescending, dismissive, formal

- **Period appropriate:**
  - More formal than modern, but natural
  - Some contractions in casual speech
  - Technical terms used correctly
  - No modern slang or psychology terms

### Scene Structure (Narrative, not Eclogue)
- **Opening:** Time/place/weather, character's state of mind
- **Action:** Training, riding, farm work - physical and detailed
- **Internal thought:** What character is thinking/feeling
- **Dialogue:** Natural to action, reveals character
- **Physical detail:** Sensory experience richly described
- **Observation:** What character notices about others/horses
- **Closing:** Image or thought that carries weight forward

### Chapter Structure
- **Length:** 2,000-2,500 words each
- **Opening:** Hook - question, image, or moment of tension
- **Rising action:** Training sequence or riding scene
- **Physical reality:** Include exhaustion, pain, fear, joy
- **Character development:** Internal shift or realization
- **Promise's state:** How he's progressing, what he shows
- **Closing:** Resonant image, often natural/seasonal

### Balance in Each Chapter
- **Physical description:** 30% (what's happening in body/world)
- **Dialogue:** 25% (conversations during work)
- **Internal thought:** 25% (character processing experience)
- **Action:** 20% (riding, training, farm work sequences)

### Promise's POV Guidelines
**When to Use:**
- Key moments of his fear/breakthrough
- After important events (Sage's fall)
- To show his growing understanding
- When his choice matters to plot
- Usually chapter 12, 17, 19, 29, 35 (strategic placement)

**How to Write:**
- Not anthropomorphic - he doesn't "think in words"
- Sensory experience primary
- Emotional truth without human psychology
- Instinctual decision-making
- Memory as body-feeling, not narrative
- His intelligence shown through problem-solving
- Connection to herd, to Sage, to place
