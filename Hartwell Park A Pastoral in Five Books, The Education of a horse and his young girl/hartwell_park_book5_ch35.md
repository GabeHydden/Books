# CHAPTER 35: THE RIVER AND THE RING

The river ran shallow and clear along the eastern edge of the fairgrounds, its banks worn smooth by generations of horses being led down to wash before showing. When Sage arrived at dawn, a dozen animals were already standing in the shallows—grooms scrubbing mud from legs, handlers sluicing water over dusty coats, the air filled with the sounds of splashing and soft whickering.

Promise balked at the top of the bank.

Below, the chaos was overwhelming. Horses everywhere—bays and greys and chestnuts, geldings and mares, some standing patiently while others danced and fussed at the ends of their lead ropes. The water ran between them like a ribbon of silver, catching the early light. And the smell—horse and river water and something else, something that made Promise's neck arch and his nostrils flare wide.

Mares. Several of them, at least two in season.

"Easy," Sage murmured, her hand firm on his lead rope. "I know. I know what you're feeling. But we're going down there, and you're going to be a gentleman about it."

She'd discussed this with Rebecca the night before, after reporting Thornton's threats. Rebecca had been calm, unsurprised. "He'll do what he'll do. We can't control that. What we can control is how Promise behaves—and every public moment where he's calm and mannerly is another piece of evidence against Thornton's claims."

The river washing wasn't just about getting Promise clean before the exhibition. It was about being seen. About letting the fair's buyers and breeders watch a stallion stand quietly among mares, handled by a young woman, behaving like the gentleman he'd learned to be.

Promise snorted, pawing at the ground. His whole body was tense, muscles bunched beneath his gleaming coat. Sage could feel the pull in him—the ancient instinct that said *chase, claim, mine*. A year ago, he would have been uncontrollable. Six months ago, he would have needed Rebecca's help to manage him.

Now, she waited.

"I'm not going to force you," she said quietly. "But I am going to ask you to trust me. The way you've trusted me all year. The way I've trusted you."

His ear flicked toward her voice. The tension didn't disappear—it couldn't, not with those mares so close—but something shifted in his posture. A decision being made.

He took a step forward. Then another.

They made their way down the bank together, Promise's hooves careful on the slippery earth. Other handlers glanced their way—a black stallion was always notable, and word had spread about the Hartwell horse. Sage kept her focus on Promise, on the rhythm of his breathing, on the way he responded to her voice and her hand.

At the water's edge, he stopped again. The stream was perhaps knee-deep here, running cool and clear over a bed of smooth stones. To their left, a chestnut mare stood with her handler, water dripping from her newly washed tail. She turned to look at Promise, ears pricked with mild curiosity.

Promise's body went rigid.

"Sage!"

The voice came from upstream. Sage turned to see Emma Fairfax picking her way along the bank, her riding habit slightly muddy at the hem, her face bright with excitement. Behind her, at a more dignified pace, came Squire Fairfax and his wife Margaret.

"I hoped we'd find you here," Emma said, slightly breathless. "Father said the Hartwell horses would likely be washing this morning, and I so wanted to see Promise again."

"Miss Fairfax." Sage managed a smile despite the tension humming through her lead rope. "Your timing is... interesting."

Emma's gaze moved to Promise, and her expression softened into something like wonder. "Oh, he's beautiful. Even more beautiful than at Christmas. His coat is like—like ink, or velvet, or—"

"Emma," her mother said gently. "Perhaps Miss Morrison is occupied."

"She is," Sage admitted. "He's a bit unsettled. The mares—"

"Of course." Emma nodded seriously, as if she understood completely. Perhaps she did; she'd grown up around horses, and her love for them was genuine, not just fashionable. "May I help? Sometimes it's easier with two people. I could hold him while you wash, or—"

Sage hesitated. The offer was kindly meant, but Promise was a stallion surrounded by mares, his blood running hot with instinct. Putting a twelve-year-old girl in charge of his lead rope seemed like madness.

But.

Emma had met Promise at Christmas. Had touched him gently, spoken to him softly, and he had accepted her with the same patience he showed Sage. And if Sage wanted to prove that Promise was safe—truly safe, for anyone—then this was exactly the kind of test that mattered.

"If your father permits," Sage said carefully. "You could hold his rope while I wash his legs. But you must tell me immediately if he becomes difficult. Any pinned ears, any tension—"

"I know what to watch for," Emma said. "I've been around horses my whole life."

Squire Fairfax looked uncertain, but his daughter's pleading expression and Sage's calm confidence seemed to sway him. "Very well. But I'll be right here, Emma. Any sign of trouble—"

"There won't be," Emma said, with the absolute certainty of youth.

Sage handed over the lead rope. Emma's hands were small but sure, her grip correct, her stance balanced. Promise's head swung toward her, nostrils working as he catalogued this new person holding his rope.

"Hello again," Emma said softly. "Do you remember me? I'm Emma. I gave you an apple at Christmas, outside the church."

Promise's ears swiveled. He lowered his head, sniffing at Emma's hand, her sleeve, her shoulder. Whatever he found there seemed to satisfy him. His posture eased, not entirely—the mares were still too close for that—but enough.

"Good lad," Sage murmured, and stepped into the water.

---

The washing took perhaps twenty minutes. Sage worked methodically, sluicing water over Promise's legs and belly, scrubbing at the inevitable stable stains, checking his hooves for stones. Throughout it all, Emma held the lead rope with quiet competence, talking to Promise in a low, steady voice.

Around them, the river washing continued—horses coming and going, handlers calling to each other, the occasional splash and snort. A bay mare was led past them close enough that Promise could have reached out and touched her. His neck arched, his body tensed, but Emma's hand stayed steady on the rope.

"Easy," she said, unconsciously echoing Sage's cadence. "She's just walking by. Nothing to worry about."

And Promise—remarkably, impossibly—listened. He watched the mare pass, his nostrils wide and his eye showing white at the edges, but he didn't pull, didn't rear, didn't do any of the things that Thornton had claimed made him vicious. He stood in the shallows with a twelve-year-old girl holding his rope, and he chose to be still.

"He's wonderful," Emma breathed, when the mare had passed. "So brave. You can tell he wanted to—but he didn't."

"That's the whole point," Sage said, wringing water from her cloth. "Anyone can train a horse to obey when there's nothing to distract them. The real test is whether they can choose to behave when every instinct is telling them otherwise."

Squire Fairfax had moved closer during the washing, his expression shifting from wariness to something like respect. "I confess I had my doubts," he said quietly, watching Promise accept Emma's pat on his neck. "When Mrs. Morrison first mentioned she'd taken on Thornton's problem stallion, I thought she'd lost her senses. That horse had a reputation."

"He still does," Sage said. "But reputations can be wrong."

"So I see." The Squire glanced toward the exhibition grounds, where crowds were already gathering. "We'll be watching this afternoon. My wife is particularly interested in seeing him under saddle."

"I hope we won't disappoint."

Emma handed back the lead rope with obvious reluctance. "Will you show him on the flashing lane? I'd love to see him trot—he must be magnificent."

"Not the lane," Sage said. "Just the exhibition ring. But I promise you'll see him move."

She led Promise up the bank, his coat dark and gleaming with water, his mane dripping rivulets down his neck. He walked beside her calmly, as if the last twenty minutes hadn't been a constant battle between instinct and training. As if standing in a river full of mares while a child held his rope was simply what horses did.

At the top of the bank, she paused to look back. The Fairfax family was still watching, Emma waving enthusiastically. Beyond them, the river continued its patient work—horses being washed, handlers calling out, the ancient ritual of preparing animals for show.

And there, at the far end of the washing area, standing apart from the crowd with his arms crossed over his green and gold waistcoat: Marcus Thornton. Watching.

Sage met his gaze across the distance. She didn't wave, didn't nod, didn't acknowledge him in any way except to stand straight and tall beside her gleaming black stallion. Then she turned and walked toward the inn, Promise following at her shoulder like the gentleman he'd become.

---

The ladies' exhibition was held in a roped-off area of the market square, with wooden benches arranged for spectators and a packed-earth surface that had been watered down to keep the dust from rising. By early afternoon, when Sage arrived with Promise, the benches were already full.

Riley was waiting at the entrance, holding Thunder's bridle. The grey gelding was tacked and ready, his coat brushed to silver silk.

"For warming up," Riley explained. "Rebecca thought Promise might settle better if he had a familiar face nearby."

"She was right." Sage reached out to stroke Thunder's nose. "How are you feeling? About—everything?"

Riley's expression flickered. The Thunder decision still hung over them, unresolved. Lord Cavendish's deadline was tonight, and Rebecca and Jennifer had been closeted in their room at the inn for an hour this morning, their voices too low to hear through the door.

"I'm trying not to think about it," Riley said. "Today is about Promise. About you. That's what matters right now."

They warmed up in a quiet corner behind the exhibition area—walking, trotting, a few careful canter transitions. Promise was alert but not anxious, his ears swiveling to track the crowd noise but his feet staying steady beneath her. The sidesaddle felt secure, the reins soft in her hands.

"You're ready," Riley said, watching them circle.

"Am I?" Sage brought Promise to a halt. "Every time I think I'm ready, something reminds me how much could go wrong."

"That's true of everything. That's true of life." Riley moved closer, one hand finding Sage's knee where it hooked over the pommel. "But you've done the work. Both of you. Whatever happens in that ring, you've already proven what matters."

Rebecca appeared at the entrance to the warm-up area. "They're ready for you. Four horses before you, then your turn." Her gaze swept over Promise, assessing. "He looks well. Calm." Her gaze tracked over Promise with that focused, cataloguing attention — checking his eye, his ear set, the tension in his neck, reading the horse the way she read every horse, completely and automatically, before she registered anything else about the scene.

"He's trying," Sage said. "We both are."

She watched the other exhibitors from the edge of the ring. Ladies on well-bred hunters, sitting their sidesaddles with practiced elegance. The horses were beautiful—a flashy chestnut, a refined grey, a solid bay—but Sage found herself noticing how they moved. Obedient but not present. Doing what was asked because they'd been trained to, not because they'd chosen to.

Lady Catherine Thornton rode third. Her horse was a tall black mare, impeccably turned out, moving through the required patterns with mechanical precision. Lady Catherine's face was set in a mask of concentration, her hands firm on the reins, her heels pressed hard against the mare's sides.

The crowd applauded politely when she finished.

Then it was Sage's turn.

She rode Promise into the ring, and the murmur that went through the crowd was audible. They'd heard about him—the Thornton stallion, the one who'd been sent to the knacker's yard and rescued by that Morrison woman with her unconventional methods. Now here he was, black as midnight and gleaming like silk, carrying a young woman sidesaddle as if he'd been born to it.

Sage took a breath. *I see you. You see me. We're here together.*

She asked for walk, and Promise walked—forward and free, his stride reaching, his head carriage natural. She guided him through a serpentine, changing direction smoothly, and he followed her aids as if he could read her thoughts. At the far end of the ring, she caught a glimpse of green and gold among the spectators. Thornton, watching with narrowed eyes.

Trot. Promise's floating, effortless trot, the one that had taken months to unlock. The crowd's murmur shifted to something appreciative. This wasn't just competence—this was beauty. This was a horse who moved like water, like music, like something wild made willing.

Canter. Right lead, left lead, a simple change through trot. Promise's ears were pricked forward now, his neck arched with what Sage could only describe as pride. He knew he was being watched. He knew he was beautiful. And unlike the horses before him, he wasn't just performing—he was *enjoying* himself.

She brought him down to walk, then halt, in the center of the ring. He stood square and still, his eye bright, his whole being radiating calm confidence. The applause that broke out was genuine—not polite acknowledgment, but real appreciation.

"Beautiful," someone called. "Absolutely beautiful."

Sage allowed herself a small smile. She reached down to pat Promise's neck, feeling the warmth of him beneath her gloved hand. They'd done it. The first test, passed.

As she rode out of the ring, she passed close to where Rebecca stood with Jennifer and Riley. Rebecca's face was carefully neutral — held there with effort, as though the emotion underneath was something she didn't quite know what to do with in public. Her eyes were bright. Jennifer was dabbing at her cheeks with a handkerchief. Riley simply reached up and squeezed Sage's hand as she passed.

"Tomorrow's the real test," Rebecca said quietly. "The speed demonstration. But today—today was exactly what we needed."

Sage nodded. Tomorrow would bring the jumping course, the crowds, and whatever Thornton planned to do. But for now, for this one afternoon, Promise had shown them all exactly what he'd become.

She looked back at the ring, where the next exhibitor was entering. Thornton still stood at the edge of the crowd, his face dark with something that might have been fury.

Let him watch, she thought. Let him see what patience and kindness have built.

Tomorrow, they would show him even more.
