# HARTWELL PARK
## Book I: Spring — Arrival

### Chapter 1: The Rescue

The carriage swayed over ruts in the lane, and each jolt sent a spike of pain through Sage's throat. She kept her hand there, fingers light against the bruises she knew had faded from purple to that ugly greenish-yellow that marked healing. Or what passed for healing. The bruises would fade completely in another week, perhaps. But she understood now—as she had not before that night in Bath—that some violations left no visible mark at all, and those were often the ones that lingered longest.

She pressed herself further into the corner of the seat, away from even the possibility of accidental contact. The carriage's interior felt too small, the air too close. Every enclosed space did now.

Through the window, Gloucestershire unfurled in shades of early spring green—the desperate, hopeful green of things that had survived winter and now reached toward warmth with something like faith. Sage watched it pass with the numb fascination of someone observing a world she no longer belonged to. Somewhere behind them, leagues of road away, Bath continued its elegant routines. Somewhere in that city, her mother was explaining her daughter's sudden departure to their acquaintances. *Hysteria*, she would say, in that particular tone that conveyed both regret and resignation. *The girl has always been prone to dramatic exaggerations.*

"We're nearly there."

Sage turned from the window to find her aunt studying her with those sharp grey eyes that seemed to parse truth from falsehood with surgical precision. Rebecca Hartwell sat with the upright posture of someone who had never learned to apologize for taking up space, her dark traveling dress severe against the carriage's worn upholstery. Her hands rested on her knees, fingers spread and very still—not fidgeting, not clasping, just placed there with a kind of deliberate calm, as if she'd long ago decided where her hands should go and saw no reason to reconsider. At seven-and-forty, she remained handsome in a way that owed nothing to delicacy and everything to the kind of character that carved itself into bone structure over time.

"You needn't speak if you don't wish to," Rebecca continued, her voice carrying that same quality it had possessed when she'd stood in the Morrison drawing room and announced Sage would be leaving immediately. Not harsh, precisely, but containing no room whatsoever for argument. "But I want you to understand something before we arrive. Hartwell Park is not—" She paused, seeming to weigh her words—not for delicacy, Sage would later realize, but for precision. "It is not like other places you have known."

Sage managed a nod, though she wasn't certain what response her aunt expected. Nothing in her life resembled any place she had known. The world had revealed itself to be constructed of trapdoors and false floors, and she had already fallen through the worst of them.

"We keep," Rebecca said carefully, "an unusual household. The neighbors consider us eccentric at best, dangerous at worst. I employ a woman as my head groom—Riley, you'll meet her—and she is..." Another pause. "She is not what society expects of someone in her position."

Despite everything, Sage felt a flicker of curiosity. "Because she's a woman?"

"That, yes. But also because Riley was born—" Rebecca stopped entirely now, her eyes searching Sage's face with an unblinking directness that most people would have softened or looked away from. She did not look away. "You will find that we do not traffic in pretense at Hartwell. Riley was born in a female body, but she is not a woman in the way most understand the term. She binds her chest and wears men's clothing and asks to be addressed with masculine terms. I employ her because she is the finest horseman I have ever met, and because she understands better than anyone what it means to be forced into a shape that does not fit one's soul."

The carriage rounded a curve, and through the window Sage glimpsed a stone gatehouse, its weathered pillars marking the entrance to a long drive lined with ancient oaks. She pressed her hand more firmly against her throat, that gesture that had become habit, armor, prayer.

"There is also Jennifer," Rebecca continued, and here her voice shifted—not warmer exactly, but the words came more easily, as though this particular subject required less careful construction. "Mrs. Blackwood, though she insists everyone call her Jennifer. She was a physician's wife, widowed eight years ago. We share the house." She held Sage's gaze with an intensity that demanded understanding. "We share it as companions. Partners. The neighbors believe we pool resources for economy's sake, and we allow them to believe this because it is safer than the truth. But you deserve the truth, Sage. You have been fed enough lies."

The carriage slowed, turning through the gates, and Sage found she could not look at her aunt directly. Her face burned—not with shame, precisely, but with the sudden overwhelming sense of her own naiveté. She had been so certain she understood the world. Had been so sure of its rules, its hierarchies, its unspoken boundaries.

And then Mr. Pemberton had cornered her in the library after dinner. The leering glances across the dining table had been uncomfortable but endurable. The comments about her figure, disguised as compliments, had made her skin crawl. When he'd followed her from the drawing room, positioned himself between her and the door, she'd felt the first flutter of real fear. His hand on her arm, too tight. Then her waist. Then—

She had said no. Had pushed at his chest. Had tried to slip past him.

And then his hands had been on her throat, and she'd understood with perfect, crystalline clarity that her voice, her will, her body meant nothing at all. That "no" was just a word men like him had learned to ignore.

*You misunderstood his intentions,* her mother had said, when Sage finally managed to speak of it two days later, bruises still livid on her throat. *Mr. Pemberton is a gentleman. You must have led him to believe—*

"I don't want to be anywhere," Sage said now, and was appalled to hear her voice crack. "But I suppose if I must be somewhere, I am grateful it is with you."

Rebecca's expression shifted—a softening that seemed to cost her effort, as if kindness were a language she'd learned rather than one she'd been born to. "You are safe here," she said. Then, after a pause: "I cannot promise more than that. But it is something."

The drive opened onto a vista that made Sage catch her breath despite her numbness. Hartwell Park spread before them—not a grand estate in the manner of the aristocracy, but something else entirely. The house itself was handsome but unpretentious: Cotswold stone gone honey-colored in the afternoon light, tall windows reflecting the sky, chimneys rising against a bank of clouds that promised rain by evening. But it was the land itself that arrested her attention.

Paddocks stretched in every direction, their fencing neat but clearly built for function rather than show. In the nearest field, a small herd of horses grazed, their coats dull with winter remnants but their postures relaxed in a way that spoke of routine comfort. Beyond them, she could see more enclosures, a weathered barn with doors standing open, what looked like an exercise ring, and further still, the green roll of pastureland dotted with sheep.

"Three hundred acres," Rebecca said, and something in her shifted now—her spine straightened further, her chin lifted, her voice took on the precision of someone reciting beloved facts. "Most of it pasture. We keep thirty horses at present, though that number varies. Some come to us for training, but most—" She gestured toward the grazing herd. "Most come to us broken in ways that cannot be undone with time. We give them a place to simply exist."

The carriage pulled up before the house, and a figure emerged from the direction of the barn—a person in rough working clothes, moving with the economical grace of someone comfortable in their body. They wore a battered hat pulled low, and as they approached to take the horses' heads, Sage saw the broad planes of their face, the line of their jaw, the way they moved with absolute confidence in space.

"Riley," Rebecca called, stepping down from the carriage with the same efficient economy of movement she brought to everything. No wasted gesture, no hesitation. "We're home. Would you see to the bags?"

"Welcome back, Miss Hartwell." Riley's voice was low, textured like weather-worn wood. They turned to Sage, and their eyes—a startling blue in a sun-weathered face—held neither pity nor excessive curiosity. Just a straightforward acknowledgment. "Miss Morrison. Glad you've come."

Sage climbed down on unsteady legs, her muscles protesting after hours of travel. She managed a nod, found she could not quite produce words. Riley seemed to understand, already turning to the luggage with practiced efficiency.

Rebecca had moved immediately toward the paddock fence, her attention caught by something in the nearest field. She stood watching the horses with an expression Sage couldn't read from behind—something intent and focused, her body angling toward the animals the way a compass needle finds north. Whatever she saw seemed to satisfy her; she gave a small nod and turned back to the house.

The front door opened, and a woman perhaps a decade younger than Rebecca appeared—handsome in a softer way, with red-gold hair pinned up haphazardly and a warm, mobile face that belonged to someone accustomed to smiling. She wore a simple day dress of practical wool, and there was ink on her fingers.

"There you are!" Jennifer Blackwood descended the steps with the kind of easy movement that suggested she'd long ago ceased worrying about what was proper. She took Rebecca's hands in greeting—held them perhaps a moment longer than convention dictated—and something passed between them. A look Sage couldn't interpret, Jennifer's eyebrows lifting slightly, Rebecca giving the barest shake of her head. Some private language, encoded by years. Then Jennifer turned to Sage, and whatever she'd read from Rebecca shaped the way she approached—slower than her natural pace, leaving more space between them than her personality seemed to want.

"You must be absolutely exhausted. Come inside, we've tea laid and I've opened the blue room for you. It overlooks the eastern paddocks, catches the morning sun beautifully."

Sage allowed herself to be shepherded inside, into a hallway that smelled of beeswax and the peculiar mineral scent of old stone. The house was furnished with comfortable eclecticism—good pieces worn by use, nothing ostentatious, books stacked on every available surface. Through an open door she glimpsed a study with a massive oak desk covered in correspondence arranged in what looked like careful stacks, and beyond that a sitting room with deep chairs pulled close to a fireplace.

The blue room proved to be exactly that—walls painted the color of robin's eggs, white linens on a substantial bed, a escritoire by the window overlooking fields where horses moved like pieces of a living painting. A fire had been laid but not yet lit, and Jennifer busied herself with the tinderbox while Sage stood in the center of the room, her traveling bag at her feet, uncertain what came next.

"I imagine," Jennifer said, coaxing flame from the kindling, "that you've had quite enough of people making assumptions about what you need or feel. So I shan't assume. But I will say that supper is at seven, we keep informal hours, and you are welcome to join us or take a tray up here if you prefer. There's hot water for washing, and I believe Riley is bringing up your trunk. Is there anything you require immediately?"

The kindness was almost harder to bear than her mother's dismissal had been. Sage pressed her fingers against her throat again, feeling the ghost of pressure that had nothing to do with healing bruises.

"I don't—" She stopped, tried again. "I don't know what I'm meant to do here."

Jennifer straightened from the now-crackling fire, and took a step toward her. Sage flinched back before she could stop herself—an involuntary recoil that left her burning with humiliation even as her body insisted on maintaining the distance.

Jennifer froze mid-step, and her expression shifted to something Sage couldn't quite name. Not pity. Understanding, perhaps. Recognition. She didn't apologize for moving, didn't make a show of it, just stayed where she was and let the space between them remain.

"Rest," she said simply. "Everything else can wait."

***

Sage did not go down to supper. She told herself it was exhaustion—which was true—but knew it was more accurately the paralysis that had gripped her since that night in Bath. The thought of sitting at a table, of making conversation, of presenting a self that held together under observation, felt impossible. Instead she sat by the window as evening deepened into dusk, watching the horses in their paddocks settle for the night.

There was a knock, and Rebecca entered with a tray. She set it on the writing desk without comment—placing it, Sage noticed, in the exact center of the desk, aligned with its edges—then joined Sage at the window.

"Riley will have fed them by now," she said, following Sage's gaze. "The routine here is quite regimented, though it might not appear so. Morning feed at six, mucking and grooming from seven until nine, turnout until evening feed at five. Between is training work, medical care, whatever each horse requires." She recited the schedule with the ease of someone who found comfort in predictable structures, in days that unfolded according to plan.

"How many people work here?" Sage heard herself ask.

"Riley and a young lad named Tom who comes up from the village—he's steady with a pitchfork if not much else. Jennifer and I help with the daily care, of course. We cannot afford a large staff, nor would I want one. Too many strangers unsettles the horses."

"The horses that come here broken," Sage said slowly. "What breaks them?"

Rebecca was quiet for a moment. Outside, the last light caught on a grey horse's coat, turning it briefly silver. "Many things. Brutality masquerading as training, most commonly. There are men—and it is always men, in my experience—who believe a horse must be *dominated*. Beaten into submission. They call it breaking for a reason, and they are proud of the speed with which they accomplish it." Her voice had gone hard as flint. "I don't break them. Never have. I work with them until they choose to work with me. It takes longer." She paused. "But it lasts."

Sage thought of hands on her throat. But not just there—hands everywhere she hadn't wanted them, taking what they had no right to take. Of her mother's voice: *You must have misunderstood his intentions. Surely you did something to encourage—* She knew broken things, she realized. Knew them in her bones. Knew what it meant to fight back and be punished for it. To have your resistance labeled as the problem, rather than the violation that provoked it.

"There's a horse coming tomorrow," Rebecca said. "A six-year-old stallion who was intended for racing. His owner—a gentleman named Thornton—employed the usual methods. Beat him with crops, used draw reins to force his head down, applied a standing martingale so tight he could scarcely breathe. When the horse fought back, as any creature would when tortured, Thornton deemed him vicious. Sold him for slaughter." She paused. "I bought him from the knacker's yard three days ago. He arrives tomorrow morning."

She delivered this the way she delivered all information—completely, precisely, each fact building on the last. There was no hesitation about whether the details were too much for a girl Sage's age, no softening of draw reins and standing martingales into vaguer euphemisms. Rebecca dealt in specifics. Later, Sage would learn that Jennifer sometimes touched Rebecca's arm during conversations like these—a gentle signal that meant *enough, she's heard enough*. But Jennifer was not here now, and Rebecca's account of Promise's suffering was thorough.

"Will you be able to—" Sage wasn't sure how to phrase it. "Can he be helped?"

"I don't know. Sometimes the damage is too deep." She turned to look at Sage directly. "But I've learned not to decide that for them."

Rebecca stood, moved toward the door, then stopped. "His name is Promise. I thought you should know that, in case you see him tomorrow. He is terrified of people, violent in his fear, and you must not approach him." She paused, seeming to weigh something. "But you may watch from a distance, if you wish. Sometimes that is where understanding begins."

After Rebecca left, Sage remained at the window until full dark. Somewhere in the night, a horse whinnied—a sound both lonely and oddly comforting, proof of other living things moving through the darkness. She touched her throat one more time, feeling the tender places. Then, hesitantly, she wrapped her arms around herself—something she hadn't been able to do since that night. Her own touch felt foreign, as though this body belonged to someone else now. As though she were borrowing it, inhabiting it without permission.

A horse named Promise. A household that operated by different rules. A place where broken things were not discarded but given space to exist.

*Two souls who have learned that the world is not safe*, Sage thought. The parallel was almost too neat to bear examining. But perhaps—perhaps Promise would learn that safety might still be possible. And if he could learn it, then perhaps...

She left the thought unfinished. It was too much to consider. Too much to hope for.

It was not hope, precisely. But it was not quite despair either. It was something else, something she had no name for yet.

Perhaps that was enough, for now.

Perhaps it was enough to simply exist, and see what came next.
