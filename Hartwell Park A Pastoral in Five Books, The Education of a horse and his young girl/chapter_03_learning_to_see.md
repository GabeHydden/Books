### Chapter 3: Learning to See

Sage woke to birdsong and the low sound of horses calling to each other across the paddocks. For a moment she didn't know where she was—the ceiling was wrong, the light came from the wrong direction—and panic seized her chest before memory returned. Hartwell Park. Rebecca. Safety, or something approximating it.

She rose and went to the window, drawn by the sounds outside. Dawn was just breaking, the sky pale gold at the horizon, and in the nearest paddock she could see Riley moving among the horses with a bucket. Even from this distance, the ease of movement was evident. Riley walked between the animals like water flowing around stones, no hesitation, no wariness, just simple presence.

One of the horses—a chestnut mare with a flaxen mane—lifted her head from grazing and walked over to Riley without being called. Put her nose in the bucket. Riley's hand came up to scratch behind her ears while the mare ate, and something about the casualness of the gesture made Sage's throat tighten. 

When had she last touched or been touched without flinching?

She dressed quickly in the simplest gown she'd brought, one she could manage without assistance, and made her way downstairs. The house was quiet but showed signs of morning life—bread and butter on the kitchen table, the kettle warm on the hob. Through the window she could see the paddocks spreading out in the growing light.

"You're up early."

Sage turned to find Jennifer in the doorway, already dressed for the day in practical wool, her hair in a loose braid.

"I couldn't sleep," Sage admitted. "Or rather, I did sleep, but then I woke and—" She gestured vaguely toward the window. "The horses."

Jennifer's smile was warm but not effusive. "They do make their presence known at dawn. There's tea, and bread if you're hungry. Riley's doing morning feed, but if you'd like to watch, there's no rule against it."

Sage found she did want to watch. Wanted to understand the thing she'd seen from her window—that ease, that lack of fear on both sides. So she took her tea and a shawl against the morning chill and made her way out to the paddocks.

Riley looked up as she approached, no surprise in the expression. "Miss Morrison. You're braver than most first morning here."

"Braver?" Sage stopped a few paces back from the fence, uncertain.

"Most people stay in bed till a decent hour. Or at least till the sun's properly up." Riley moved to the next horse, a bay gelding who lipped at the bucket with evident pleasure. "You can come closer. They won't bother you unless you've got grain in your pockets."

Sage took a step forward, then another. The chestnut mare was watching her now with dark, liquid eyes that seemed to take in everything. Sage froze.

"She's wondering what you are," Riley said, matter-of-fact. "Clover's never met you. Strange humans are worth examining."

"How do you know what she's wondering?"

"Look at her ears. See how they're forward, pointed at you? That's attention. Interest. If she was worried, they'd be back or flat. If she didn't care, they'd be loose, floppy." Riley demonstrated with hands, mimicking the positions. "Horses talk with their whole bodies, if you learn to listen."

Sage studied the mare more carefully. Clover was indeed focused on her, but the body—Sage was realizing she could read it now that Riley had given her the words—was loose. Relaxed. The weight shifted easily from foot to foot. Not tense.

"She's not afraid of me," Sage said slowly.

"No reason to be. You're not doing anything frightening." Riley moved to the fence, leaned against it. "Come here. I'll show you something."

Sage approached, keeping her movements slow despite the tension in her own body. Clover watched but didn't move away.

"Put your hand out. Flat, like this." Riley demonstrated, palm up, fingers together. "Let her smell you. That's how they figure out who you are."

Sage extended her hand, surprised by how much courage it took to reach toward the large animal. Clover stretched her neck out, nostrils flaring, and breathed across Sage's palm. The breath was warm and smelled of grass and something else, something fundamentally horse. Then the mare's whiskers brushed her skin, velvet-soft, and Sage gasped.

"She's just investigating," Riley said. "See her ears? Still forward. Loose eye. She's decided you're not particularly interesting but not threatening either."

"That's—" Sage struggled for words. "That's remarkable. I've never—I didn't know they were so..."

"Communicative? Aye, they are. Most people don't bother to learn the language." Riley moved along the fence to where two more horses stood waiting. "This is Thunder—he's pushy, see how he's crowding in front?—and that's Whisper behind him. She's timid. Notice how she's waiting back, letting Thunder go first?"

Sage watched. Thunder was indeed shouldering forward, ears pricked toward Riley but also occasionally pinned back at Whisper. The other mare—a chestnut with a white blaze—kept her head low, her body angled slightly away.

"Whisper's scared?" Sage asked.

"Not scared. Subordinate. It's different. Thunder's higher in the hierarchy, so she defers. But watch—when he moves away, she'll come up for her share." 

Riley poured grain for Thunder, who ate with evident satisfaction. As soon as he finished and stepped back, Whisper moved forward, quick but still cautious, and began eating. Her whole posture changed—less compressed, more confident.

"She was waiting for permission," Sage said, fascinated despite herself.

"In a manner of speaking. Waiting for the safe moment." Riley glanced at her. "If you're planning to be here any length of time, you should learn this. Horses are honest in a way humans rarely are. They show you exactly what they're thinking, if you know how to look."

Sage felt something catch in her chest. Honesty. These animals couldn't lie, couldn't pretend everything was fine while planning violence. What you saw was what existed.

"Could you teach me?" The words came out before she could think better of them.

Riley studied her for a long moment. "Rebecca said you might ask. Yes. I can teach you. But it means being out here, working with them. Getting close. Can you do that?"

Sage thought of Promise in his paddock. Thought of the way he'd watched her from the window, frozen in his own assessment of threat.

"I can try."

"Then we start with watching. Come on."

Riley led her to the barn, where four more horses stood in spacious stalls, bedded deep in straw. The space was clean, well-aired, nothing like the close darkness Sage had imagined stables to be. Each horse lifted their head as they entered, ears swiveling toward them.

"This is where they come at night, or when weather's bad," Riley explained. "See how they're all relaxed? Soft eyes, loose lips. If they were anxious, you'd see tension in the face. Whites of the eyes showing. Nostrils tight."

For the next hour, Riley walked her through the barn and paddocks, pointing out details Sage had never thought to notice. The way a horse shifted weight away from a sore foot. How the tail swished fast for annoyance, slow for contentment. The difference between a nicker of greeting and a squeal of warning.

"It's like learning another language," Sage said, watching Thunder and Clover interact at the fence line. Thunder approached with ears forward, Clover responded with her head slightly lowered—submission or friendliness?—and they touched noses briefly before returning to grazing.

"It is another language. Takes time. But once you see it, you can't unsee it."

They were walking back toward the house when Sage saw the black horse in the quarantine paddock. Promise. He was at the far end, grazing but with his head higher than the others, his ears constantly swiveling. Even at this distance she could see the tension in him, the way every muscle seemed coiled for flight.

"Could I—" She stopped herself. "May I go closer? To his paddock?"

Riley's expression shifted to something more serious. "You can. But carefully. Don't approach the fence directly—he'll see it as a threat. And don't expect anything from him. He's not like the others."

"I know. Rebecca told me about Thornton."

"Did she tell you what that means? What Thornton does to horses?" Riley's voice had gone flat. "Because understanding it in words and seeing it in an animal's body are different things."

"I want to see," Sage said, surprising herself with her certainty. "I want to understand."

Riley nodded slowly. "All right. But we go together, and we stay back from the fence. He's not safe, Sage. Not because he's mean. But because he's terrified, and terror makes animals—makes anyone—dangerous."

They walked across the field toward Promise's paddock. Sage kept her pace slow, her movements careful, applying what she'd just learned. Promise's head came up immediately, his whole body going rigid. She was close enough now to see the whites of his eyes, to see how his nostrils flared wide.

"Stop here," Riley said quietly when they were still fifteen feet from the fence. "Just watch him."

Sage stopped. Watched. Promise was staring at them, utterly frozen, but she could see now what Riley had taught her to see. His ears were flat back against his skull—not curiosity, not interest. Threat assessment. His weight was shifted onto his haunches, ready to explode into motion. His tail was clamped tight to his body.

"He's terrified," she whispered.

"Aye. Look at his neck—see how tight? How high he's carrying his head? That's pure adrenaline. He's deciding right now whether to run or fight."

As if to confirm Riley's words, Promise took three quick steps backward, then stopped, eyes rolling. His whole body was shaking.

"Can I—" Sage began, and took a single step forward.

Promise exploded.

He wheeled and charged at the fence, screaming—that was the only word for the sound, a high, terrible shriek of panic and rage. He hit the fence with his chest, the wood shuddering under the impact, and reared up, striking at the air with his front hooves. Sage stumbled backward with a cry, her heart slamming into her throat, and Riley's hand shot out to steady her.

"It's all right," Riley said, low and calm. "He's not going to come through the fence. But he needs us to move. Now."

They backed away quickly, and as the distance increased, Promise dropped back to all four feet. He was blowing hard, his coat dark with sweat, his body still trembling. But he wasn't charging anymore. Just watching them retreat with wild, rolling eyes.

"I'm sorry," Sage gasped. Her own hands were shaking. "I didn't mean to—"

"You didn't do anything wrong. You were just there. That's all it takes with him right now. Any human proximity is a threat." Riley guided her back toward the barn. "That's what Thornton did. Made him believe every human interaction ends in pain."

Sage looked back at Promise, who was still standing at the fence, watching them. Even from this distance she could see the tension, the fear. "Will he ever—can he be helped?"

"Rebecca thinks so. But it'll take time. And it'll take someone who understands what they're seeing." Riley stopped, met her eyes. "What did you see just now? Before he charged?"

Sage thought back, forcing herself past her own fear to remember those frozen moments. "His ears were back. His weight was shifted. He was—he was telling us he was frightened. He was asking us not to come closer."

"Exactly. And when we didn't listen—when you took that step—he did the only thing he knows how to do. He tried to make himself bigger, scarier, more dangerous than whatever was threatening him." Riley's expression was grave. "He's not vicious. He's not mean. He's just absolutely certain we're going to hurt him, and he's trying to survive."

***

The kitchen was warm when Sage pushed through the door, her hands still trembling. Jennifer looked up from the bread she was kneading, took one glance at Sage's face, and nodded toward the kettle.

"Tea's hot. Cups are where they always are."

Sage poured with unsteady hands, spilling a little on the worn wooden table. She mopped it up with her sleeve, not caring about propriety, and sank into one of the kitchen chairs. The familiar smell of yeast and woodsmoke wrapped around her like a blanket.

Jennifer continued kneading, her movements rhythmic and unhurried. She didn't ask what had happened. Didn't press for explanation. Just worked the dough while Sage's heartbeat slowly returned to something like normal.

"He charged at me," Sage said finally. "Promise. I wasn't even that close, and he just—" She couldn't finish.

"Riley mentioned he might, when you first tried to approach." Jennifer's voice was matter-of-fact, not unkind. "It's not about you. It's about what he's learned to expect from humans."

"I know. I know that. But knowing it didn't stop—" Sage pressed her hand flat against the table, watching it shake. "I thought I was past being frightened of things."

Jennifer was quiet for a moment, shaping the dough into a round. Then she crossed to the fire to check the oven's heat, passing behind Sage's chair. Her hand brushed Sage's shoulder in passing—light, incidental, gone before Sage could register it consciously.

But her body registered it. She felt herself flinch, a sharp involuntary jerk that came from somewhere beneath thought.

Jennifer continued to the fire as though nothing had happened. Adjusted the damper. Returned to the table by the longer route, giving Sage space.

"When I first came here," Jennifer said, settling back to her dough, "after William died, I startled at everything. Doors closing. People entering rooms. My own shadow, some days." She began shaping rolls, her hands steady and sure. "Rebecca never once remarked on it. Just moved a little slower around me. Gave me time to see her coming." She smiled slightly, a private expression. "It wasn't natural for her—she doesn't think about things like that instinctively. How her presence might affect someone. But she watched me the way she watches horses, learned what I needed, and then did it. Every time. Without ever making me feel studied."

Sage wrapped her hands around her teacup, anchoring herself to its warmth. "How long before it stopped?"

"The startling? Months. Maybe a year before I stopped bracing for every unexpected sound." Jennifer's hands worked steadily. "The feeling that I was waiting for something terrible? That took longer. Sometimes I think it never fully goes. It just... quiets. Takes up less space."

Sage thought about Promise in his paddock. The way he watched everything, every moment. The way his body never fully relaxed, even when he was grazing. How exhausting that must be—to live always waiting for the blow.

"I flinched," she said quietly. "Just now. When you touched my shoulder."

"I know." Jennifer's voice was gentle. "You also stayed in your chair."

Sage considered this. It was true. She had flinched, but she hadn't fled. Hadn't knocked over the tea. Hadn't pressed herself into a corner. Had simply startled, and then remained.

A small thing. But perhaps small things were how it began.

***

That evening, Sage sat with Rebecca in the study while rain drummed against the windows. She'd spent the rest of the day with Riley, watching the horses, learning their individual signals and patterns. But Promise's charge kept replaying in her mind—the violence of it, yes, but also the terror underneath.

Rebecca was at her desk when Sage came in, writing in what looked like a ledger—rows of small, precise handwriting, columns perfectly aligned. She finished her entry before looking up, added a period with careful deliberation, and closed the book. Then she turned her chair to face Sage, giving her the same complete, almost disconcerting attention she gave everything—as if, having decided Sage was the current subject, all other subjects had ceased to exist.

"Riley said Thornton broke him," Sage said finally. "I don't fully understand what that means. What... what exactly did he do?"

Rebecca set down her pen, placing it parallel to the ledger's edge. "I don't know all of it. But I know enough." Her voice had shifted into what Sage was beginning to recognize as her instructional register—clear, methodical, each point building on the last. "Thornton uses what he calls 'firm correction.' If a horse refuses a command, he uses a crop until it complies. If it resists the bit, he tightens the tackle until the pain of fighting is worse than the pain of submission. If it won't load into a wagon, he starves it until desperation overcomes fear."

Sage felt sick. "And that's... legal?"

"Entirely. A man's property is his to do with as he pleases." Rebecca's voice was acid. "Promise was three years old when Thornton bought him. Young, spirited, intelligent. Thornton intended to make him a racehorse. But Promise is too smart for that kind of work—he thinks too much, questions too much. And every time he questioned, Thornton punished him. Drew reins to force his head down so tight Promise could barely breathe. A standing martingale that cut into his neck. Beatings when he refused jumps. Beatings when he balked at the starting line."

She was not looking at Sage as she spoke. Her eyes had gone to the window, to the darkened paddocks beyond, and her voice had taken on a quality that was almost recitative—as if she were reading from a ledger of injuries, each item catalogued and recorded. Sage had the sense that Rebecca could have continued for some time, listing every method and its consequences in precise detail, that her mind held a taxonomy of cruelties as organized as her bookshelves.

"But he's alive," Sage said, and the interruption seemed to bring Rebecca back. She blinked, refocused on Sage. "Thornton could have killed him."

"He tried to. Sent him to the knacker's yard when beating him stopped working. The horse was too broken to be useful, so he sold him for meat." Rebecca's eyes were hard. "I bought him back three days before you arrived. I've been trying to save horses from Thornton for ten years. Promise is the thirty-fourth."

Thirty-four horses. Sage tried to imagine that number, each one tortured in the name of training, discarded when they broke under the pressure.

"Can you save him?" she asked quietly.

"I don't know. Some damage can't be undone. But I can offer him a choice. I can show him that not all humans are Thornton. And if he chooses to trust me—eventually, after months or years—then perhaps." Rebecca paused. "Or perhaps he'll simply live out his days here, eating grass and being left alone. That would be enough."

Sage thought of Promise watching her from his paddock. Thought of his frozen assessment, his desperate charge when she came too close. Thought of herself, pressed into the corner of the carriage, flinching from Jennifer's kindness.

"Riley said he's not mean," she said. "Just terrified."

"Riley's right. Terror and viciousness look the same from the outside. But they're not the same at all. One is cruelty. The other is survival." She said it with the conviction of someone who had spent a long time working out a distinction that mattered deeply to her—a distinction, Sage thought, that might apply to more than horses.

Outside, the rain drove harder against the windows. Somewhere in the darkness beyond, a horse breathed alone in his paddock, learning that not every night brought pain.
