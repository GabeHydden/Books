### Chapter 7: First Touch

The morning of the twenty-second day began like all the others. Sage rose to the familiar sounds of Hartwell waking—Riley's footsteps in the barn, the rustle and stamp of horses expecting breakfast—dressed in one of her serviceable gowns—the trousers were only for riding now, the dress her natural state for everything else—and took her tea standing at the kitchen window, watching the horses greet the day.

Promise was already at his fence when she looked toward his paddock. Waiting, maybe. Or just existing in that space he'd claimed as his own. The sight made her throat tighten. Three weeks ago he'd been pressed against the far fence, every muscle screaming flight. Now he stood where she could see him clearly, where he could see her, comfortable in proximity.

"You're smiling," Jennifer observed, bringing her own tea to stand beside Sage. "That's new."

Was she? Sage touched her face, found it was true. "I didn't realize."

"It's good to see. You've seemed lighter these past weeks. More yourself." Jennifer set down her cup and turned to face Sage fully. "I remember when you first came. You couldn't be in a room with anyone for more than a few minutes. Now here you are, drinking tea at my elbow, easy as anything."

Sage considered this. It was true—she hadn't noticed the change happening, but she could feel the evidence of it now. Standing close to Jennifer didn't make her skin crawl. Sharing space felt natural rather than threatening.

"Promise is teaching me," she said. "About patience. About how long things take. About how you can't force healing—you just have to show up, day after day, and let it happen."

"I think you're teaching each other." Jennifer reached out and touched Sage's hand briefly—warm, deliberate, gone. "You've both come so far. Both learning to trust again."

Sage thought about all the small moments that had built to this. Jennifer's hand brushing her shoulder and the startle fading. Riley's hands adjusting her seat and no flinch at all. The accumulation of touches that didn't hurt, presences that didn't threaten. Building a new understanding, one careful contact at a time.

"I think I'm finding out who I am when I'm not afraid," she said.

She finished her tea, collected her book—_Pride and Prejudice_ now, borrowed from Rebecca's library—and walked out to Promise's paddock.

He was there, at the fence, exactly as she'd seen him from the window. His head came up when she approached, ears forward, that now-familiar expression of interest. She settled into her spot on the ground near the fence, close enough that she could have reached through the rails to touch him if she'd wanted to.

But she didn't want to. Or rather, she wanted to, desperately, but wouldn't. This had to be his choice. All of it. Always.

She opened her book and began to read.

Promise watched her for several minutes, then lowered his head to graze. The sound of him pulling up grass, chewing, breathing—it had become the soundtrack to her mornings. Familiar. Comforting. The ambient noise of trust being built.

After perhaps twenty minutes, she heard hoofbeats approaching. Not Promise—he was still grazing near the fence. Someone riding across the field. She looked up to see Riley on Thunder, the gelding's bay coat gleaming in the morning sun.

Riley reined in at a respectful distance, staying mounted so as not to change the dynamic too much. "Morning. How's he doing today?"

"Same as yesterday. Close. Comfortable." Sage marked her place in the book. "Is everything all right?"

"Fine. Just wanted to check in. Rebecca's got a horse arriving this afternoon—one of Thornton's again. She'll need help settling him in." Riley's expression darkened slightly at Thornton's name. It always did. "But this morning is yours. Carry on."

Riley turned Thunder and rode back toward the barn, and Sage returned to her book. Or tried to. But she found herself reading the same paragraph three times, not absorbing a word, too aware of Promise's presence. Too aware of how close he was. Too aware of the weight of three weeks of patience and the strange, fragile thing that was building between them.

She set the book down and simply watched him instead.

He was beautiful. She could admit that now, could see past the scars and the wariness to the creature underneath. His coat had gone from dull to glossy with good feeding, the black catching blue highlights in the sun. His body had filled out, no longer all sharp angles and visible ribs. He moved with natural grace when he wasn't locked in fear, and even his wariness had a kind of dignity to it. He wasn't broken, she realized. Had never been broken, despite everything Thornton had done. Just hurt. Just terrified. Those weren't the same thing at all.

Promise raised his head, looked at her. Their eyes met.

Something passed between them in that moment. Some wordless understanding. She was seeing him. Really seeing him, not as a project or a parallel to her own trauma, but as himself. A creature with his own history, his own pain, his own slow journey toward whatever healing might mean for him.

And he was seeing her too. She could feel it. Could see it in the way his ears stayed forward, the way his body remained loose and easy. He'd learned her patterns, learned her scent, learned that she was safe. But more than that—he'd learned she was worth knowing. Worth approaching. Worth investigating.

He took a step toward the fence. Then another.

Sage held perfectly still, barely breathing. He was right at the fence now, his dark head hanging over the top rail, so close she could count the whiskers on his muzzle. Close enough that she could see her own reflection in his large, dark eyes.

Close enough to touch.

But she didn't move. Didn't reach out. This was his choice. Had to be his choice.

Promise lowered his head slightly, nostrils flaring wide, scenting her. She could feel the warmth of his breath on her face. Could smell him—grass and horse and sun-warmed coat. His ears flicked forward and back, processing, deciding.

Then he stretched his neck out further, reaching toward her, and very gently touched his muzzle to her shoulder.

***

The small-human goes very still when he touches her. Not the frozen-still of prey about to run, but different-still. Waiting-still. The kind of still that means paying attention without threatening.

Promise keeps his nose pressed to her shoulder for a long moment, breathing in her scent. It's familiar now—three weeks of mornings and evenings, the pattern he's learned means safe. But up close it's different. Stronger. More complex. There's the soap-smell she uses on her skin, the fabric-smell of her dress, the underneath-smell that is just her. Human-female-small-one. The one who sits and does nothing. The one who never demands. The one who showed him boring could mean safe.

She smells of the old-calm-mare too. Of leather and hay and the barn-scents he's learning are home-scents now, not trap-scents. She smells like the place that has food and water and no hard-stick. Like the place where pain doesn't come.

Under his nose, her shoulder is warm. Solid. Real. She's wearing the cloth-covering that humans wear, but he can feel the shape of her underneath—shoulder-bone, muscle, the small movements of breathing. She's alive and present and choosing to sit here with him, morning after morning, asking nothing.

Why?

The question has been growing in him for days now, pushing against the certainty that humans mean pain. Why does she come? Why does she sit? Why does she never hurt, never demand, never become the thing he's learned to fear?

The man-who-hits had reasons for everything. Pain-for-not-obeying. Pain-for-moving-wrong. Pain-for-being-what-he-was instead of what-the-man-wanted. Every action had purpose, and that purpose was always to make Promise into something else. Something broken enough to use.

But the small-human just... exists. Near him. With him. Not trying to make him into anything. Just being there, predictable and boring and safe.

He pulls his head back slightly, studying her. Her eyes are wet—water leaking from them, which he's learned means strong-feeling in humans, though whether pain-feeling or other-feeling he's still learning to distinguish. But her body is loose. Her breathing is steady. Her scent is bright-sharp, like the grass-smell after rain. Not fear-scent. Not pain-scent.

Happy-scent, maybe. If happy is even real. If it's something that exists outside of the before-before time with his dam and the endless grass.

The small-human's hand moves—slowly, so slowly—and comes to rest palm-up on the fence rail between them. Not reaching for him. Not grabbing. Just... there. Open. Available. Offering.

Promise looks at the hand. Fingers curled gently, not grasping. Skin pale and smooth, no scars like his own body carries. Small. Soft. Nothing like the man-who-hits's hands, which were always hard and closed and bringing pain.

He stretches his neck out again, touches his nose to her palm.

Warm. A little damp from the water leaking from her eyes. Still smelling of her, of soap and skin and the underneath-human-scent. The fingers stay still, curved but not closing. Just receiving his investigation. Just letting him choose how close, how long, how much.

This is new-learning. This is information his body doesn't know what to do with.

Touch that doesn't hurt. Proximity that doesn't trap. A human who lets him decide.

He snorts softly—confusion-sound, processing-sound—and pulls back. The small-human's hand stays where it is, not following, not demanding more. Just there, still open, still offered. But not required.

He takes a step back from the fence, then another. Creating distance because distance means thinking-space, means time to understand what just happened.

The small-human does nothing. Doesn't follow. Doesn't call out. Just sits there with the water still leaking from her eyes, hand still resting on the fence rail, waiting. Always waiting. Always letting him choose.

Promise turns and walks to the middle of his paddock, mind spinning with new information. He touched the human. The human let him. The human didn't grab, didn't trap, didn't turn the touching into pain-coming.

The touching was just... touching. Investigation. Information. A question asked and answered.

He lowers his head to graze, but his ears stay swiveled toward the small-human, tracking her. She's moving now—slowly, carefully—standing up. Not approaching. Just changing position.

The tall-human-who-moves-easy is there suddenly, appearing from the direction of the big-barn. Promise's body tenses—another human means more variables, means less predictable. But the tall-human stays back, doesn't approach his fence. Just goes to stand near the small-human, and they make sounds at each other. Human-words. He's stopped trying to understand those.

But their body-language he can read. Excitement-movements from the tall-human—energetic, pleased. The small-human is making the water-from-eyes more now, her shoulders shaking, but her scent is still that bright-sharp happy-scent. The tall-human touches the small-human's shoulder—the same shoulder Promise touched—and the small-human doesn't pull away.

Safe-touching. Gentle-touching. The kind he saw when the small-human was learning to ride the old-calm-mare.

Promise grazes, processing. The humans are leaving now, walking back toward the big-house together. The small-human keeps looking back at him, but she doesn't return. Doesn't try to make the touching happen again.

She's letting it be what it was. One moment. One choice. Not demanding it become more than that.

Promise feels something shift in his chest. Not the wondering-feeling he's been fighting. Something bigger. Something that might be the beginning of trust.

Not trust-complete. Not trust-certain. But trust-possible.

Maybe different is real. Maybe the small-human is teaching him that the before-before time—the time of dam-warmth and grass-endless and safe-feeling—maybe that wasn't the only good time. Maybe there could be an after-time. A time where humans don't all mean pain.

Maybe. Possibly. Carefully.

He tears up another mouthful of grass and chews slowly, watching the small-human and tall-human disappear into the big-house.

Tomorrow the small-human will come back. She always does. That's the pattern. That's the promise she's been keeping for three weeks.

And tomorrow, maybe, he'll let her investigate him the way he investigated her. Maybe he'll stand close again. Maybe he'll let her smell mean something other than fear.

Maybe.

The wondering is still dangerous. Hope is still the trap. But the touching was real, and it didn't hurt, and that's new information. Important information. Information that changes things.

Promise grazes in the spring sunshine and lets himself wonder.

Just a little.

Just enough.

***

Riley's hand on her shoulder was gentle but grounding, and Sage found she needed it. Her legs felt uncertain, her whole body trembling with an emotion she couldn't name. Joy? Relief? Something bigger than both?

"Did you see?" Her voice came out shaky. "He touched me. He just—he reached out and touched me."

"I saw." Riley's voice was quiet, reverent almost. "That's extraordinary, Sage. Three weeks, and he chose to reach out. That's—" Riley paused, seeming to search for words. "That's everything. That's the moment when a traumatized horse decides maybe, possibly, trust isn't the thing that will kill them."

Sage pressed her hand to her mouth, trying to hold in the sob that wanted to escape. She was crying—properly crying now, not just tears but full body-shaking sobs—and couldn't seem to stop.

"I know," Riley said, and pulled her into a hug. 

Sage went willingly—*willingly*, that was the word, she had *chosen* this—and felt Riley's arms close around her. The embrace was solid and warm, and for a moment Sage's body remembered other arms, other closeness, the way hands could trap and hold and hurt.

But this was different. This was Riley's familiar hay-and-leather scent, Riley's steady heartbeat against her own, Riley's arms that held without grasping. She could step back if she wanted. Could end this at any moment. The knowledge made it safe to stay.

*This is what touch can be,* she thought, pressing her face into Riley's shoulder. *Not taking. Not trapping. Not turning a person into a thing to be used. Just holding. Just being present with another person's body, offering comfort and receiving it.*

She had learned this. Slowly, incrementally, over three weeks of small touches and careful approaches. Jennifer's hand on her shoulder. Riley's hands adjusting her seat. The accumulation of evidence that touch could be kind.

And now Promise was learning it too. The horse who had charged at her in terror three weeks ago had chosen to reach out and touch her. Had learned what she was learning—that not all touch meant pain. That proximity didn't always become a trap.

They were teaching each other. Both of them. Both learning the same lesson from opposite directions.

"He chose me," Sage managed between sobs. "I didn't reach for him. He chose to reach for me."

"That's what makes it matter. That's what makes it real." Riley held her for another moment, then stepped back, keeping one hand on her shoulder. "Come on. Let's go find Rebecca. And then let's talk about what comes next."

What came next, Rebecca explained over tea in the kitchen, was patience. Still patience. Always patience.

"It's a remarkable breakthrough," Rebecca said, her eyes warm with genuine pride. "Three weeks is fast for a horse as traumatized as Promise. But it's just the beginning, Sage. One touch doesn't mean he's healed. It means he's opened a door. Now you have to honor that by not pushing through it too fast."

"I won't," Sage said immediately. "I wouldn't. This has to be his choice. All of it."

"Good." Rebecca poured more tea. "What we can start now is food reward. Positive association with your presence. Apples work well—most horses love them. You sit in your usual spot, and when he comes close, you offer an apple on your flat palm. Let him take it. Then you sit back. Don't ask for more. Just reward the choice he made to come close."

"How often?"

"Every time he comes to the fence during your sitting sessions. Eventually you can ask him to come by holding up the apple. But for now, let him approach on his own and reward him when he does." Rebecca's expression grew more serious. "And Sage? This is going to be slow. He might not touch you again for days. Might regress. Might have bad days where he stays in the corner again. That's normal. Healing isn't linear."

Sage thought of her own healing. The days when she could eat breakfast with the household and feel almost normal. The days when she woke from nightmares about Mr. Pemberton's hands and couldn't leave her room until noon. The way her progress looked from the outside like it had been steady, but felt from the inside like three steps forward, two steps back, another step forward, one step sideways.

"I understand," she said. "I understand completely."

That afternoon, Sage stood at the fence with an apple in her hand. Promise was in the middle of his paddock, grazing, watching her with evident curiosity. He'd touched her that morning. That morning, he'd chosen to close the distance and investigate. That morning, something had changed.

But this afternoon, he stayed where he was.

Sage held the apple for a few minutes, then set it on the fence post and sat down with her book. She wasn't disappointed. Or rather, she was disappointed, but she understood. One touch didn't mean everything was different. It meant one moment had been different. The rest remained to be seen.

She read for half an hour. Promise grazed. The apple sat on the fence post, and she didn't offer it or draw attention to it. Just let it exist there, a possibility. An option. A choice he could make if he wanted to.

While she sat, she thought about touch.

All the times she had been touched without choosing it. Her mother's performative embraces, staged for company. Dance partners whose hands wandered too close, too familiar. Mr. Pemberton's fingers on her arm, her waist, her throat.

But this morning—Promise's nose against her shoulder, Riley's arms around her—this had been different. This had been touch that was offered and received. Given and accepted. A conversation rather than an assault.

*Reclaiming.* Maybe that was the word. Not erasing what had happened—that wasn't possible—but reclaiming what touch could mean. Building new associations, new memories, new understandings.

Promise was learning the same lesson. Every time she sat here and didn't hurt him, he was building new evidence. Every time Rebecca approached and brought food instead of pain, he was revising his understanding of what humans meant.

They were both reclaiming something that had been taken from them. The belief that contact could be kind.

Finally, she stood up, collected the apple, and walked back to the house. Tomorrow she would bring another apple. And the day after. And the day after. As many days as it took for him to decide an apple was worth approaching. Worth trusting.

Healing isn't linear, Rebecca had said. Sage was learning that lesson in her bones.

***

That evening, after supper, Rebecca asked Sage to walk with her around the property. They strolled through the paddocks in the golden evening light, past horses grazing peacefully, past the barn where Riley was finishing evening feed. The air smelled of grass and apple blossoms and the particular sweetness of a spring evening in the Cotswolds.

"You've done well with him," Rebecca said. "Better than I dared hope."

"I haven't done anything. I just sit there."

"That's precisely what you've done, and it's everything. Most people can't tolerate that much patience. Can't sit with uncertainty that long. Can't honor another creature's pace when they're so desperate for progress they can measure." Rebecca paused by a fence, looking out over the grazing horses. "You've given Promise what he needed most: time. Space. Choice. Those are the greatest gifts you can give any traumatized creature."

Sage thought about the gifts Rebecca had given her. The bedroom with morning light. The household that refused to follow society's script. The time to heal without demands or deadlines. The space to discover who she was when she wasn't performing for anyone.

"You gave those things to me," she said quietly.

"Yes. And I hope you're learning to give them to yourself as well." Rebecca turned to look at her directly. "That's the harder lesson, Sage. Offering others compassion is one thing. Offering it to yourself—believing you deserve it—that's the work of a lifetime."

They walked on in comfortable silence. Sage's mind was full of the morning's moment—Promise's soft nose against her shoulder, the warmth of his breath, the way he'd studied her before choosing to reach out. The way it had felt to be chosen, to be investigated and deemed safe enough for closeness.

"Rebecca," she said hesitantly. "When will I be able to ride him? Is that—will that ever be possible?"

Rebecca was quiet for a long moment. "I don't know. Maybe never. He's a stallion, Sage. Young, traumatized, unpredictable. Even if he learns to trust you completely, riding is asking for a level of partnership he may never be able to give. Especially not with someone who's still learning to ride."

The words landed with weight, but she recognized their truth. Her desire to ride Promise had as much to do with her need to prove something—to herself, to Thornton, to every man who'd treated her like property—as it did with the horse himself.

"But," Rebecca continued, "if it does become possible, it will only be because you're becoming the kind of rider who deserves that trust. The kind who listens more than demands. The kind who can read a horse's fear and respect it. You're learning those skills. Whether you use them with Promise or another horse—that remains to be seen."

They reached the paddock where Promise grazed in the evening light. He raised his head when they approached, ears forward, watching them without fear. Just watching. Present and calm and existing in his space with something that looked almost like contentment.

"He's come so far," Sage said softly.

"You both have." Rebecca's voice was warm. "Three weeks ago you couldn't sit at our supper table. Now you're mucking stalls and riding horses and defining trust in ways that would make philosophers weep. That's no small thing, Sage. That's transformation."

Sage watched Promise watching her. Two creatures who'd been broken by men who believed in domination. Two creatures learning, slowly and carefully, that gentleness might be possible. That choice might be honored. That healing, while not linear and never complete, could still be real.

Tomorrow she would bring an apple. Would sit in her spot and read her book and offer the choice without demanding he take it. And the day after. And the day after.

However long it took.

She had learned patience. Had learned that rushing only broke what needed time to grow. Had learned that love—real love—meant being present, day after day, and letting someone else set the pace.

Promise lowered his head and returned to grazing, comfortable in their presence. Not approaching. Not retreating. Simply there.

For now, that was everything.
