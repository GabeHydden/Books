# Chapter 14: Rebuilding

Six days after Thunder threw her, Sage woke to ribs that ached rather than screamed. Progress. She sat up carefully, testing the movement, and found she could breathe deeply without wanting to cry. The bruising had faded from angry purple to greenish-yellow, and while bending still pulled, it no longer felt like knives.

She dressed slowly, choosing her work dress over the lighter cotton. The sturdy fabric felt like armor, something to protect her still-tender ribs. Outside her window, the heat had finally broken. The morning air carried a hint of coolness that promised autumn was coming, though it was still a month away.

When she reached the kitchen, Rebecca looked up from her tea with an appraising eye.

"How do you feel?"

"Better. Not healed, but better."

"Good. Because you're riding today."

Sage's stomach fluttered—anticipation and nervousness both. "Thunder?"

"Clover." Rebecca's tone left no room for argument. "You'll walk Clover around the ring for twenty minutes. Nothing more. We need to get you back in the saddle before fear has time to take root, but we're not rushing your recovery or asking more than you're ready to give."

Riley appeared in the doorway, already dressed for morning work. "I've got Clover tacked up whenever you're ready. Mounting block's set up too—you won't have to pull yourself up from the ground."

The consideration touched Sage more than she expected. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me yet. Getting back on after a bad fall is harder than it sounds."

***

Clover stood in the cross-ties looking half-asleep, one back hoof cocked in relaxation. When Sage approached, the old mare opened one eye, then closed it again. No judgment. No memory of Thunder's rear or Sage's mistakes. Just: *Oh, it's you. Are we working?*

Sage tacked her up slowly, every movement careful. Her ribs protested when she lifted the saddle, but she managed. By the time she led Clover to the exercise ring, Riley and Rebecca were both waiting.

"Use the mounting block," Rebecca said. "Step up slowly. If your ribs hurt too much, stop."

The mounting block made it easier, but Sage still had to pull herself up that final bit to swing her leg over. The movement pulled at her ribs sharply, making her gasp. For a heartbeat she thought she couldn't do it. Then she was in the saddle, settling carefully, finding her seat.

Clover stood perfectly still, patient as stone.

"How does it feel?" Riley asked.

"Like I've been away for a year instead of six days." Sage gathered her reins with hands that trembled slightly. Everything felt wrong—her balance off, her seat uncertain, her confidence shattered.

"That's normal," Rebecca said. "Walk her around the rail. Let your body remember what it knows."

Sage asked Clover to walk. The mare moved forward with her characteristic steady rhythm—one-two-three-four, one-two-three-four. For the first circuit, Sage couldn't relax. Every stride felt precarious, like she might fall off at any moment. Her ribs ached with the movement. Her hands were too tight on the reins.

"Breathe," Riley called. "You're holding your breath."

Sage forced herself to breathe, deep and slow despite the pull in her ribs. Around they went. One circuit. Two. Three. Gradually, incrementally, her body began to remember. Clover's walk was the same as it had always been. Steady. Predictable. Safe.

By the fifth circuit, Sage's hands had loosened. By the tenth, she could feel Clover's movement instead of just surviving it. By the fifteenth, something that felt almost like confidence began to creep back.

"How are your ribs?" Rebecca asked.

"Sore. But manageable."

"Good. Five more circuits, then dismount. That's enough for today."

Those last five circuits, Sage let herself actually ride instead of just sit. She asked Clover for a proper walk—purposeful, engaged. She practiced turning precisely, halting smoothly, walking again. Small things. Simple things. But they mattered.

When she finally dismounted—carefully, using the mounting block to minimize the drop—her legs shook and her ribs throbbed, but she felt something else too. Relief. She'd gotten back on. She'd ridden without falling. She'd proven to herself that fear didn't have to win.

"Well done," Rebecca said. "Tomorrow you'll ride her for thirty minutes. We'll work back up gradually. When you can ride Clover for an hour without pain, we'll put you back on Thunder."

The name made Sage's stomach clench. "How is he?"

"His mouth is healed. Riley's been riding him every day—keeping him exercised, proving to him that not all riders lose control. When you're ready, you'll start over with him. Walking only. Listening. Proving you learned your lesson."

"Will he forgive me?"

"He already has. Horses don't hold grudges. They just respond to who you are in each moment." Rebecca's grey eyes were steady. "The question is whether you've forgiven yourself."

Sage thought about that as she cooled Clover out, as she groomed her carefully, as she gave her carrots and thanks. Had she forgiven herself? She still felt shame when she thought about Thunder's cut mouth, about the trust she'd broken. But she also understood now, in a way she hadn't before, what the work really required. Not just skill. Not just patience. But constant, unrelenting control of her own emotional state.

The lesson had cost her bruised ribs and a week out of the saddle. It had cost Thunder pain and wariness. But she'd learned it. In her body, where lessons stuck deepest.

***

That afternoon, Sage made her way to Promise's paddock, moving easier now that she'd ridden. Promise was in the round pen with Rebecca—his daily training session. Sage stayed well back, not wanting to interfere, just watching.

Rebecca sent him around the circle with a gesture, her body language clear and consistent. Promise moved immediately, ears locked on her, stride even. Around and around, responding to "walk on" and "whoa" with increasing fluidity. This was routine now. Safe. Understood.

After ten minutes, Rebecca called him to halt and rewarded him with carrots and praise. Promise leaned into her scratching, comfortable.

Something in Sage's chest tightened with longing. She wanted that. Wanted to be the one working with him, teaching him, earning his trust in this direct way. Wanted to move past watching and waiting into actual training.

When Rebecca finished and led Promise out of the round pen, Sage approached.

"He's doing so well," she said.

"He is. We'll start introducing the halter soon. Just wearing it at first, getting comfortable with something on his head. It'll take time, but he's ready for the next step."

"Could I help? With the halter work? I could—"

"No." Rebecca's voice was gentle but firm. "Not yet."

The word landed like a stone. "But I'm riding again. I've learned—"

"You've learned an important lesson about emotional control. But you're still recovering, still rebuilding your confidence, still earning back Thunder's trust. You have enough on your plate without taking on Promise's training too."

"I'm not suggesting I ride him. Just groundwork. Just—"

"Sage." Rebecca's hand on her shoulder stopped the flow of words. "I know you want to work with him. I know you feel like you're ready. But wanting to be ready and being ready are different things. Promise's halter work will require absolute patience, absolute emotional control. If he fights it—and he might—you'll need to stay calm and consistent no matter what. Can you honestly say you're there yet?"

Sage thought about Thunder. About how frustration had made her hands hard, had made her hurt him. About how six days ago she'd let anger override everything she knew.

"No," she admitted quietly. "I can't say that."

"When you can—when you've rebuilt your confidence, when you've earned back Thunder's trust, when you can ride him through difficulty without losing control—then we'll talk about you working more directly with Promise. But not before." Rebecca's expression softened. "This isn't punishment. It's protection. For you and for him. You're not ready yet. And that's okay."

***

That evening, Sage sat at Promise's fence, feeling the ache in her ribs and the deeper ache of impatience deferred. Promise came over for his apple, took it with his careful precision, then stayed close while she scratched behind his ears.

"Rebecca says I'm not ready to work with you yet," she told him. "Says I need to rebuild my confidence first. Earn back Thunder's trust. Prove I can control myself under pressure."

Promise leaned into her hand, comfortable. His presence was solid and uncomplicated as always.

"She's right. I know she's right. Six days ago I hurt Thunder because I couldn't control my frustration. What if I did that to you? What if I undid all the trust you've built?" The thought made her stomach turn. "You deserve someone who's already mastered their emotions, not someone who's still learning how."

She watched Promise graze as evening settled golden over the fields. In the distance, Thunder grazed in his paddock, his mouth fully healed now. Tomorrow Riley would ride him again. The day after. However long it took until Sage was ready to face him again.

And Promise would continue his round pen work. Would learn to accept a halter. Would take the next small steps in his training. Without her direct involvement. Because she wasn't ready. Because wanting to be ready wasn't the same as being ready.

The distinction hurt. But it was true.

"I'll get there," she promised Promise. "I'll rebuild what I broke with Thunder. I'll prove I can control myself even when things are difficult. I'll become the kind of person who deserves to work with you directly. However long it takes. However much work it requires."

Promise lifted his head, looked at her for a long moment, then went back to grazing. Patient. Waiting. Trusting that she'd get there when she was ready.

The same way she'd waited for him, those early weeks at his fence.

Both of them learning. Both of them growing. Both of them understanding that trust was earned in increments, not demanded all at once.

Sage stayed at the fence until the light began to fail, watching Promise graze, feeling her ribs ache with each breath, holding the knowledge that she had more to learn before she could do the work she wanted to do.

Patience. Control. Humility.

The same lessons she'd been learning all summer, circling back deeper each time.

She would master them. Would become worthy of Promise's trust. Would earn the right to work with traumatized horses not just through skill, but through character.

However long it took.

Whatever it cost.

She would get there.

***

The next morning, Sage rode Clover for thirty minutes without incident. The morning after that, forty-five. By the end of the week, she could ride for an hour—walking and trotting, even a few minutes of easy canter—without her ribs protesting too badly.

On the eighth day after Thunder threw her, Rebecca told her it was time.

"Tomorrow morning," Rebecca said. "You'll ride Thunder again. Just walking. Twenty minutes. Riley and I will both be there. If at any point you feel yourself losing control—frustration, fear, anger—you stop. You dismount. You walk away. Do you understand?"

"Yes, ma'am."

"This isn't about proving you can ride him. It's about proving you can ride him with control. With patience. With awareness of what he's telling you. If you're not ready, there's no shame in saying so."

Sage thought about it. About Thunder's cut mouth, about her bruised ribs, about the lesson she'd learned in her body. About Riley's words: *Recognize the feeling when it starts. Breathe through it. Ask what the horse is telling you.*

"I'm ready," she said. "Or at least, I'm ready to try."

"That's all anyone can ask."

That night, Sage lay in bed staring at the ceiling, listening to Promise move in his paddock below. Tomorrow she would face Thunder again. Would find out if she'd truly learned the lesson, or if she'd just learned to be afraid.

She thought about Promise, about the slow patient work of rebuilding trust. About how healing wasn't linear, how setbacks taught as much as progress.

Tomorrow would tell her if she'd grown or just survived.

Either way, she'd show up. Would try. Would choose patience over force, control over reaction, listening over demanding.

One breath at a time.

One choice at a time.

Proving to Thunder—and to herself—that people could change.

That mistakes didn't have to define you.

That broken trust could be mended with time and care and honest effort.

She fell asleep with that thought, with Promise's quiet presence in the darkness below, with the knowledge that tomorrow would be hard but necessary.

Ready or not, she would face it.

And she would do better.
